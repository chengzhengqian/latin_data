When the consul learned that these preparations were being made—after posting guards as the circumstances and time dictated—he convened the senate and put the question what action they recommended to be taken with the men who had been delivered into custody. (Yet shortly before,110 a full meeting of the senate had decided that those men had committed treason). On the present occasion, Decimus Junius Silanus, being the first to be called upon for his opinion because he was consul-elect, had recommended that punishment111 be exacted from those who were being held in custody, as well as from Lucius Cassius, Publius Furius,112 Publius Umbrenus, and Quintus Annius if they were apprehended; later, influenced by the speech of Gaius Caesar, he had said113 that he would support the proposal of Tiberius Nero,114 who had recommended that the question be reopened after an augmentation of the guards.115 But Caesar, when his turn came and the consul asked him for his opinion, spoke in the following manner:

Woodman in his Penguin translation implausibly interprets dixerat as referring to an earlier, otherwise unattested debate at which Caesar spoke. The difficulty disappears if the plupf. is intended to convey the meaning that Silanus “had said” after Caesar’s speech, but before the final vote (discessio) of the senate (53.1), that he was planning to support Nero’s proposal. This interpretation of the plupf. finds support in Sallust’s use of this tense in narratives elsewhere: e.g., 18.6, 24.1; Jug. 39.3.
