I should be loth, therefore, for you not to know the immense difference made by one syllable. I should say Os colluere,1 but in balneis pavimentum pelluere,2 not colluere; I should, however, say lacrimis genas lavere,3 not pelluere or colluere; but vestimenta lavare,4 not lavere; again, sudorem et pulverem abluere5 not lavare; but it is more elegant to say maculam eluere than abluere; if, however, the stain had soaked in and could not be taken out without some damage, I should use the Plautine word elavere.6 Then there are besides mulsum diluere,7 fauces proluere,8 ungulam iumento subluere.9

5. So many are the examples of one and the same word, with the change of a syllable or letter, being used in various ways and meanings; just as, by heaven, I should speak with a nicer accuracy of a face, painted with rouge, a body splashed with mud, a cup smeared with honey, a sword-point dipped in poison, a stake daubed with bird-lime.
