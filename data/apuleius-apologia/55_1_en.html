Moreover, you said that a woman of free birth was brought to my home having the same illness as Thallus, I promised to cure her, and I bewitched her so that she too collapsed. 2I see: it’s a wrestler, not a magician, you have come to accuse, since you say everyone who came to me fell over. 3And yet, Maximus, when you questioned Themison the doctor, who brought the woman for me to examine, he said that nothing happened to her except that I asked whether if she had ringing in her ears and in which ear more: 4after replying that her right ear was very troublesome, she immediately left.

5On this point, Maximus, though for the moment I am taking great care to restrain my praises of you, since I do not wish to give the impression of having flattered you because of this trial, yet I cannot help praising your skill as an interrogator. 6Some while ago, when these matters were under discussion and those people said that I had bewitched the woman, while the doctor in attendance denied it, you asked with extraordinary shrewdness what I had to gain from bewitching her. 7They replied: “To make the woman fall down.” “So what?” you said; “Is she dead?” “No,” they said. “What are you saying then? What would Apuleius have gained if she had fallen?”
