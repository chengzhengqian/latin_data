I will talk only of aquatic creatures, not touching on other ones except when they share distinguishing features. 7Listen therefore to what I have to say. You will immediately clamor that I am listing words of magic in Egyptian or Babylonian style:86 8sharks, mollusks, creatures with soft shells, with hard shells, with cartilaginous skeletons, with sharp teeth, amphibious, with scales, with plates, with membranous wings, with webbed feet, creatures solitary and gregarious. 9I could go on, but it is not worth it to spend the day on these matters, so that I have time to get to other ones. (To a servant of the court:) In the meantime read out these few terms I mention as translated into Latin. (The servant reads out the Latin translations.)

Well then, do you think a philosopher, not one unlettered and ignorant like a shameless Cynic, but one who remembers that he follows Plato—do you think it a disgrace for him to know these things or not, to neglect them or to study them, to know how much of the design of Providence is visible even in these things, or instead to believe what his father and mother tell him about the immortal gods? 2Quintus Ennius wrote a poem called Delicacies, in which he lists innumerable kinds of fish, which he had no doubt studied carefully. I remember a few lines, and will quote them:87

3How the turbot from Clupea beats them all! There are mussels at Aenus, prickly oysters aplenty at Abydus. There’s the scallop at Mytilene and at Charadrus in Ambracia.
