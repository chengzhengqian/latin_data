13. So give Plato allowance for his love poems, or else I shall be forced to talk philosophy at length, against the advice of Ennius’ Neoptolemus;32 2but if you do not, I will be quite content to join Plato in being blamed for such poetry. 3To you, however, Maximus, I have the greatest gratitude for listening so attentively to these subsidiary parts of my defense, which I have to make in order to answer the charge. 4And so I make this further request, that you hear what I still have to say before coming to the actual charges in the way you have hitherto: with goodwill and attention.

5For next comes that long, censorious speech about a mirror, a subject so shocking that Pudens virtually burst himself with shouting, “A philosopher has a mirror! A philosopher owns a mirror!” 6Even were I to admit to having one (in case you think you have scored a point if I deny it), still it does not necessarily follow that I habitually dress myself before a mirror. 7Why, suppose I owned a theatrical wardrobe, surely you would not argue that I habitually wore the tragic actor’s robe, the dancer’s saffron dress, the buffoon’s motley, as well?
