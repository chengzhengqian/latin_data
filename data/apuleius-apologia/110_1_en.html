Just see, if you please, Maximus, how this news has made him suddenly go silent and turn his eyes to the ground. 6For what he had supposed was very different, and for good reason; he knew that the son had smeared the lady with insults, while I had endeared her by kindness. He had reason to fear me too: anyone, even one so indifferent to legacies as I am, might still not have shrunk from avenging himself on so undutiful a stepson. 7It was this worry above all that prompted them to prosecute me: their own greed made them guess that I had been left the whole estate. (To Aemilianus and his supporters:) As to the past, let me relieve you of that fear, for neither the chance of receiving a legacy nor of getting my revenge could have swayed me. 8As a stepfather, I fought for a wicked stepson against his angry mother, as a father would for the best of sons against a stepmother, and I did not rest until I had restrained my kind wife’s lavish generosity to me to a far greater degree than was fair.

(To a servant of the court:) You there, bring the will made by a mother whose son was now her enemy; they call me a burglar, and yet I dictated every word, mingling them with my pleas. 2Order that document unsealed, Maximus: you will find that she made her son her heir, while leaving me some modest legacy as a mark of respect, so that if anything happened to her in the course of nature, I as her husband should not go unmentioned in my wife’s will. 3(To Pudens:) Take your mother’s will there, which in one way really is undutiful, and that is this: she disinherited so indulgent a husband and made so hostile a son her heir—4or rather not her son, but Aemilianus’ hopes, Rufinus’ marriage, and that drunken fraternity of your hangers-on.
