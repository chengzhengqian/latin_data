Saturninus made here in Oea. 5(To a servant of the court:) You there, bring it and let them see, hold and examine it. (To the prosecution:) Look at what that scoundrel called a skeleton.118 Don’t you hear the outraged shouting of everyone present? Don’t you hear them condemning your lie? 6Is this a skeleton, is this a spook, is this what you kept calling a demon? Is this something magical or is it an ordinary image of the usual kind? (To Maximus:) Take it, please, Maximus, and look it over: it is right for innocent, pious hands like yours to be entrusted with a hallowed object. 7Just see how handsome and full of athletic bloom the face is, how cheerful the god’s expression, how becomingly the down creeps over his cheeks, how the curly hair on his head peeks out from under the brim of his cap, 8how prettily the pair of feathers rises up over his temples, and how gaily his cloak is tied around his shoulders. 9Surely someone who dares to calls this a skeleton has either never seen images of the gods or disregards them all. In short, only a fool can think this one a ghoul.119

But as for you, Aemilianus, may that god, the intermediary between the upper and lower worlds, repay you for that lie with the ill will of the gods above and below; may he ever bring dead men’s forms to meet your eyes, every shade, phantom, specter, ghost that ever was, 2every apparition of the night, every horror of the pyre, every terror of the graveyard, to all of which your age and just deserts have brought you near. 3By contrast, we followers of Plato know only what is festive, cheerful, sacred, exalted, heavenly.
