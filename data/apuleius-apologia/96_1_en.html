And why should I ply her with such absurd, barroom compliments when these same people call me quite clever at writing risqué love poems? 5In short, the fact is this, as anyone can see: this man, who could not read Pudentilla’s letter in idiomatic Greek, was more at ease reading one of his own and more skillful at communicating it.

6But I will soon have done with letters when I have added this one point. Pudentilla had written, “So come while I am still of sound mind” in a spirit of irony and jest; and after writing this letter she invited her sons and daughter-in-law to join her, and spent about two months with them. 7Now let her dutiful son mention anything untoward he saw his mother do or say at that time because of insanity. Let him deny that she most meticulously audited the account books of her stewards, herdsmen, and grooms. 8Let him deny that that she strongly advised his brother Pontianus to beware of Rufinus’ treacheries. Let him deny that she quite properly scolded him because he had publicly circulated a letter she had sent him, and even so had not read it in good faith.148 9Let him deny, after all I have said, that his mother married me at her country house, a place we had long since arranged. 10For we had agreed to prefer a house outside of town for our marriage, so that the citizenry would not flock there again in hope of wedding favors.149 Not long before, Pudentilla had paid out fifty thousand sesterces of her own money to the people on the day Pontianus married and the boy here assumed the toga. 11Moreover, we wanted to dispense with the many banquets and nuisances that newlyweds usually have to go through according to custom.

  • 148Pudens had read out his mother’s letter in court but (according to Apuleius) had represented it as a love letter (ch. 85.1).
  • 149Presents (sportulae, “little baskets”) were commonly given out on all kinds of occasions, either in kind or in cash, but big events such as weddings could involve such gifts to hundreds of people.
