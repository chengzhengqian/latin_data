If you recall the greatest crimes in human history, you will find no poor man involved in them, 5while by contrast scarcely any of the rich can be found among men of distinction: everyone we admire for some achievement has been raised by Poverty from the cradle. 6Poverty, I repeat, was the founder of every state in olden times, the inventor of every skill, with no faults to impart and every excellence to bestow, enjoying every kind of praise among all the nations of the earth. 7For this same Poverty is justice in Aristides, humaneness in Phocion, vigor in Epaminondas,46 wisdom in Socrates, eloquence in Homer. 8This same Poverty from earliest times secured the empire of the Roman people, which consequently to this day uses a ladle and an earthenware bowl to sacrifice to the immortal gods. 9Now, just suppose the judges presiding in this case were Gaius Fabricius, Gnaeus Scipio, Manius Curius, whose daughters were so poor that they received dowries from the public funds, and went to their husbands bringing only their household’s renown and the people’s money;47 10suppose the judges were Publicola, who expelled the kings, and Agrippa who reconciled the people,
