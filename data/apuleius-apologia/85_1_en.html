However, his wife, now almost a worn-out hag, at last gave up these insults to the family. 2Their daughter, though, they passed around among some rich youths, whom her own mother invited in, but to no effect, and they also gave her over to certain suitors for trial. If the easygoing Pontianus had not fallen in her way, she might still have been sitting at home, a widow before she was a bride.135 3Despite our urgent representations Pontianus gave her a false and imaginary claim to be married, even though knowing that before he married her, a certain young man of very good family, her previous fiancé, had had his fill and then left her. 4And so she came to him as a new bride, brazen and fearless, with her ruined honor, faded bloom, threadbare veil, a virgin again after her recent divorce, a girl in name rather than in purity. 5She was carried in an eight-man litter; all of you who were there must have seen how boldly she surveyed the men, how shamelessly she paraded herself. Who failed to recognize the mother’s training when they saw the daughter with painted face, rouged cheeks, seductive eyes? 6Her whole dowry down to the last penny had been got from a creditor the day before, and indeed was larger than necessary for a bankrupt household full of children.136
