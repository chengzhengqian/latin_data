20. I might also have a dispute with you about the very meaning of the word, saying that none of us is poor if he does not want the superfluities and owns the necessities, which by their nature are very few. 2For one who has the least desires will have the most resources, since by wanting the least he will have all he wants. 3That is why riches are better measured not by lands and revenues than by a man’s inner self. If avarice makes him needy and unsatisfied whatever his profit, he will not be content even with mountains of gold, but will be forever scrounging to increase his previous gains. 4And that indeed is truly to confess poverty: for all desire for gain comes from a sense of want, and it makes no difference how great something is if it seems to you too little. 5Philus did not have so large an estate as Laelius, nor Laelius as Scipio, nor Scipio as Crassus the Rich, but not even Crassus the Rich had as much as he wanted, 6so that though he surpassed everyone his own avarice surpassed him, and everyone thought him rich except himself.50 7But by contrast those philosophers I mentioned, who did not want more than they could command, and whose desires and resources were in balance, were justly and properly deemed rich and fortunate. 8For
