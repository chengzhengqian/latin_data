5(Turning to address Maximus.) Well, I now come to the actual charge of magic, which he set ablaze with a tremendous roar to make me disliked, and then, disappointing everyone’s expectation, doused it with a few old wives’ tales. 6Have you ever, Maximus, seen a fire starting from stubble? It crackles loudly, shines brightly, spreads quickly, but lacking fuel its flames die down and leave no trace.59 7Well, that is like this prosecution: begun with insults, heaped up with verbiage, short of proofs, but after your verdict fated to leave no trace of its slanders.

8Since Aemilianus rested it entirely on this one point, that I was a magician, I would like to inquire of his most learned advocates what a magician is. 9For if a magician in the Persian language is what a priest is in ours, as I have read in many authors, what kind of crime is it to be a priest and to have the right information, knowledge and mastery of the ceremonial rules, ritual requirements, and sacred laws? 10Provided of course that Plato understands what magic is when he recalls the lessons that the Persians use to initiate a youth in kingship.
