Furthermore, as I omitted to mention, some things by your own confession you did not know, and yet you denounce the same things as if you did. 2You say I kept certain objects in Pontianus’ house shrine wrapped up in a cloth; you admit that you did not know what things were wrapped up or what their nature was, and moreover that nobody was known to have seen them, but nonetheless you assert they were equipment for magic. 3Don’t let anyone flatter you, Aemilianus: your accusation shows no cunning or even impudence, as you perhaps imagine. What then does it show? The sterile ravings of a bitter heart, the pathetic madness of a harsh old man. 4For these are more or less the words you used when addressing so grave and perspicacious a judge: “Apuleius kept certain things wrapped in a cloth in Pontianus’ house shrine; what they were I do not know, and hence I insist that they were magical. Believe what I say, therefore, since I am saying what I do not know.” 5Splendid arguments, such manifest proofs of the charge! “It was this, though what it was I don’t know.” You are the first person in history, Aemilianus, to know even what he doesn’t know, so far do you surpass everyone in your stupidity; 6for the most intelligent and penetrating of philosophers say that we cannot trust even what we see, but you make assertions even about things that you have never observed and never heard.
