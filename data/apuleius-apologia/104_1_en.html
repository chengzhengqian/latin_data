APOLOGIA

94. The whole city learned about this. One and all cursed Rufinus and praised me to the skies. 2Even before my wife had completed her gift, Pontianus had come to us together with this brother who so little resembles him, thrown himself at our feet, and begged us to forgive and forget everything that had passed; he wept, kissed our hands, and apologized for listening to Rufinus and those like him. 3Next, he begged and prayed me to clear him also with his excellency Lollianus Avitus,161 to whom I had recently recommended him at the start of his speaking career; 4for he had found out that I had written a full and detailed statement of the facts to him a few days earlier. 5This too he persuaded me to do. And so after getting my letter he left for Carthage, where Lollianus, being almost at the end of his duties as proconsul, was awaiting yourself, Maximus. 6On reading my letter, as the extraordinarily kind person he is, he congratulated Pontianus on having promptly corrected his mistake, and through him he sent back such a letter to me—heavens above—so cultivated, so elegant, in language both so charming and so pleasant, absolutely like “the good man skilled in speech” he is.162 7I know, Maximus, that you will be glad to hear his letter, and indeed I will read it myself, I will recite it in my own voice. 8(To the servant of the court:) You there, Avitus’ letter, please, which has always been an honor to me and is now a vindication too. 9(To another [?] servant of the court:) You there, you may keep the clock going,163 since I am quite ready to read His Excellency’s letter three or four times over, whatever it costs of my time.

  • 161On Lollianus, see n. 55 and also Appendix E.
  • 162A definition of the true orator attributed to the elder Cato, e.g., by Quint. Inst. 12.1.1.
  • 163Literally, “allow the water to flow,” referring to the action of the water clock.
