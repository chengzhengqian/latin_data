8. (13) Was it really I who went onward from infancy and progressed to boyhood? Or was it rather that boyhood entered into me and took the place of my infancy? Infancy did not leave me—for where did it go to? Yet it was no longer there. I was not an infant incapable of speech,36 but now I was already a boy, able to talk. This I do remember; and later on I realized from what sources I had learned the art of speech. It was not that older people were teaching me, giving me words in a logical framework of instruction, such as happened soon after with the alphabet. Rather, I intended to give expression to the feelings of my heart by all kinds of groaning, and crying, and moving my limbs in different ways, to make people obey my will.
