Never will the wise man cease to be angry if once he begins. Every place is full of crime and vice; too many crimes are committed to be cured by any possible restraint. Men struggle in a mighty rivalry of wickedness. Every day the desire for wrong-doing is greater, the dread of it less; all regard for what is better and more just is banished, lust hurls itself wherever it likes, and crimes are now no longer covert. They stalk before our very eyes, and wickedness has come to such a public state, has gained such power over the hearts of all, that innocence is not rare—it is non-existent. For is it only the particular man or the few who break the law? On every hand, as if at a given signal, men rise to level all the barriers of right and wrong:

No guest from host is safe, nor daughter’s sire From daughter’s spouse; e’en brothers’ love is rare. The husband doth his wife, she him, ensnare; Ferocious stepdames brew their ghastly banes; The son too soon his father’s years arraigns.a

And yet how few of all the crimes are these!
