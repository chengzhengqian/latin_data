1 Varro, On Agriculture

Now as for grain crops, where the stem has brought forth the grain there is the spike. This, when it is not beardless, in barley and wheat, has three components: seed, husk, beard (and also at first, when the spike emerges, sheath). What is the hard part on the inside is called the seed, the husk what is its shell; the beard is what protrudes far out of the husk like a slender needle, as if husk and beard formed a peaked top for the grain. [2] “Beard” and “seed” are known to almost everyone, “husk” to few. Thus only in Ennius do I know this is written, in the books translated from Euhemerus. The word “husk” [gluma] seems to have its etymology from “removing the bark” [glubendo] because the seed is stripped [deglubitur] of this skin.
