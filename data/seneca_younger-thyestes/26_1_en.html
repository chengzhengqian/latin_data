THYESTES

What god has created sudden calm out of such uproar? Just now the weapons of civil war clattered throughout Mycenae; ashen-faced mothers clasped their children; wives feared for their husbands under arms, as the sword reluctantly came to hand dulled with the rust of tranquil peace. Some men struggled to repair the sagging walls, others to strengthen derelict towers, others to lock the gates with iron bars; guards crouched in dread on the battlements to watch the anxious night. Worse than war is the fear of war. Now the menace of savage steel is fallen, now hushed the trumpets’ blaring din, now mute the strident clarion’s scream; deep peace is restored to the joyful city. So, when waves swell out of the deep as Corus buffets the Bruttian Sea, Scylla roars at the pounding of her caves, and sailors in harbor dread the seas swallowed, then spewed by whirling Charybdis; and a bestial Cyclops, perched on a crag of seething Etna, fears his parent31 may violate with his flooding waves the fire that roars in undying forges; and as Ithaca trembles, Laertes thinks his meager kingdom could be submerged.
