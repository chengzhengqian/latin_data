XI. “It would also be utterly absurd if anyone(iii) love of other things, e.g. pleasure, referable to self-love; desired to maintain that, though the fact of self-love is admitted, this instinct of affection is really directed towards some other object and not towards the person himself who feels it. When this is said of friendship, of right action or of virtue, whether correct or not, it has some intelligible meaning; but in the case of ourselves it is utterly meaningless to say that we love ourselves for the sake of something else, for example, for the sake of pleasure. Clearly we do not love ourselves for the sake of pleasure, but 31 pleasure for the sake of ourselves. Yet what fact is(iv) self-love proved by dread of death, seen even in children in animals, more self-evident than that every man not merely of loves himself, but loves himself very much indeed?
