They have no tombs. For their corpses were cast into running water and their heads, while the mob capered, were burned in the Campus Martius.

XXXII. Aelius Sabinus1 has written, and we must not omit it, that such was the beauty of the son’s face that even in death his head, now black, and dirty, shrunken, and running with putrid gore, seemed still the shadow, as it were, of a beautiful face. And indeed, though there was great joy at seeing the head of Maximinus, there was almost equal grief when the son’s head was carried with it. Dexippus2 says that Maximinus was hated so thoroughly that when the Gordians perished the senate elected twenty men to oppose him.3 Among these were Maximum and Balbinus, and these two they made emperors against him. This same Dexippus says also that Maximinus’ prefect of the guard and his son were slain before his eyes, after his soldiers had deserted him. And there are not lacking historians who say that Maximinus also, after he had been deserted and had seen his son slain before his eyes, killed himself with his own hand,4 that nothing womanish might attach to him.
