F 17 Suetonius, Life of Caesar

I take no account of the edicts of Bibulus [M. Calpurnius Bibulus (122), F 5], in which he marked his colleague [C. Iulius Caesar (121)] as the queen of Bithynia, saying that previously he was enamored of a king, now of a kingdom. At that time, as Marcus Brutus reports,1 a certain Octavius even, a man of disordered mind and thus somewhat free in his speech, in a crowded assembly, after saluting Pompey [Cn. Pompeius Magnus (111)] as king, greeted him [Caesar] as queen.
