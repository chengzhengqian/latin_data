Against L. Aurelius Cotta (F 23–26)

BC), presumably for extortion; Cotta was defended by Q. Caecilius Metellus Macedonicus (18 F 1) and acquitted (TLRR 9).

​

F 23 Cicero, Pro Murena

P. Africanus had been consul twice and had destroyed the two threats to this empire, Carthage and Numantia, when he accused L. Cotta. In him [Africanus] there was outstanding eloquence, outstanding loyalty, outstanding integrity, and authority as great as in the empire of the Roman People that was preserved by that man’s service. I have often heard older men say the following, namely that this exceptional power and standing of the accuser was ofthe greatest advantage for L. Cotta. The very wise men who at that time were judging that case did not want anyman to be condemned in court so that he appeared crushed by the excessive strength of his adversary.
