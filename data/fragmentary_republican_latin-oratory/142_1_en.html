F 34 Gellius, Attic Nights

“For futurum does not,” he [an expert] said, “refer to rem,1 as it seems to hasty and careless readers, nor is it used as a participle; rather, it is an infinitive, what the Greeks call aparemphaton [‘infinitive mood’], not subject to either number or gender, but altogether free and not integrated, such a word as C. Gracchus used in the speech, [7] whose title is On P. Popilius around the places of assembly, in which it is written as follows: ‘I believe that my enemies will say this.’ inimicos dicturum,” he said, “not dicturos [agreeing in case, number and gender with inimicos]; [8] doesn’t dicturum in Gracchus seem to be used according to the same principle as futurum in Cicero?...”
