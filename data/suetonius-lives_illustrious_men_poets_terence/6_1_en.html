Terence

Afranius ranks Terence above all other writers of comedy, writing in his “Compitalia”:

You would not call anyone the equal of Terence.

But Vulcatius11 puts him not only below Naevius, Plautus, and Caecilius, but even below Licinius and Atilius. Cicero in his “Limo”12 gives him this much praise:

Thou, Terence, who alone dost reclothe Menander in choice speech, and rendering him into the Latin tongue, dost present him with thy quiet utterance13 on our public stage, speaking with a certain graciousness and with sweetness in every word.

Also Gaius Caesar:14

Thou too, even thou, art ranked among the highest, thou half-Menander, and justly, thou lover of language undefiled. But would that thy graceful verses had force as well, so that thy comic power might have equal honour with that of the Greeks, and thou mightest not be scorned in this regard and neglected. It hurts and pains me, my Terence, that thou lackest this one quality.

So the account of Suetonius Tranquillus.

  • 11In his celebrated “canon,” Gell. 15. 24.
  • 12“Meadow,” a fanciful title for a book of miscellaneous contents, like the “Silvae” of Statius, the “Pratum” of Suetonius, and the like.
  • 13Perhaps, “amid a hush of silence” in the audience. Because of the awkwardness of voce... vocibus Ritschl preferred motibus (animi).
  • 14Referring to Julius Caesar.
