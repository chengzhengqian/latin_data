But I am informed that, even at that early stage, he was serious-minded enough to blush at the thought that his physical appearance had commended him, for he would have been quite content2 to be loved for his intellectual worth. And indeed the best people are always appreciated far more for their beauty of character; moreover, the fugitive charms of the body wilt with the advance and decline of the rushing years. Later, when made Prefect of Gaul, Priscus Valerianus, his opinion still unchanged, associated his son-in-law with his deliberations3 and his tribunal, holding steadfastly to his old estimate of him, with the result that the man to whom he had married his daughter out of regard for his learning was now joined as partner in his dignity. 3.
