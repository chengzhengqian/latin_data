The audience listened in a spirit of eager rivalry; and the story-telling, though tinged with hilarity, was not on that account formless. By and by, having for some time felt sluggish for want of exertion, we resolved to do something energetic. 6. Thereupon we raised a two-fold clamour demanding according to our ages either ball or gaming-board, and these were soon forthcoming. I was the leading champion of the ball; for, as you know, ball no less than book is my constant companion. On the other hand, our most charming and delightful brother,1 Domnicius, had seized the dice and was busy shaking them, as a sort of trumpet-call summoning the players to the battle of the box.2 We on our part played with a troop of students, indeed played hard until our limbs deadened by inactive sedentary work could be reinvigorated by the healthful exercise. 7. Here the Illustrious Philomathius3 resolutely plunged into the ranks of the ball-players.

“Daring, even he, to essay vigorously the toil of youths,” as the Mantuan poet has it.4 He had been a fine player, but that was when still quite young.
