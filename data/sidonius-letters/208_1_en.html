His case concerns a will. He is not clear about the merits of his own documents and he has gone to seek skilled advice from the advocates who practise there. He will count it as good as winning the case if he finds that he is lawfully the loser; only, he does not want to be accused of having failed through indolence to guard the interests of himself and his family. I take it upon me to recommend him to this extent that, in case the counsel he calls in should disdain to favour him with instructions, the influence of your tonsure1 may exert itself to force a quick response from those negligent gentlemen. Deign to hold me in remembrance, my Lord Bishop.

IV Sidonius To The Lord Bishop Lupus,* Greeting
