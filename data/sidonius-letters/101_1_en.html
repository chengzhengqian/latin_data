I have left in the hands of the venerable Gaudentius1 money to pay both for the stone and for wages. The verseinscription, which will be appended, I composed last night, perhaps a not very polished piece, as I was too much occupied with my journey. 5. But, for what it is worth, I should like you to have it promptly cut on the slab: and see that the mason makes no blunders on the marble: because if such a thing is done, whether from perversity or from carelessness, the malignant reader is sure to put it down to me rather than to the engraver. If, however, you attend to my request with pious care, I shall thank you as warmly as if you also did not gain some praise and glory by the act; for if I, your uncle,2 had passed away, the entire responsibility for this duteous deed would have rested with you in virtue of your degree of relationship.
