III Sidonius To The Lord Bishop Leontius,* Greeting

1. Although so far I have received from you no word of exhortation to inspire me in my new profession, no shower of heavenly teaching to water the drought of my worldly ignorance, yet I do not so forget myself as to expect an equally balanced competition in attentions from your side and from mine;2 for my poor insignificance pales easily before the ripeness of your years, the seniority of your office, your exceptional standing, the eminence of your knowledge and your gift of self-knowledge; and so, if I expect your letters to keep pace with mine, I do not deserve to receive a single one. 2. In view of this suggested change in the text, I am not sure that Anderson caught the exact sense in § 1 of ut a meis praesumam partibus aequali officiorum lance certandum. Sidonius has been complaining that Bishop Leontius did not write him a letter on the occasion of his appointment to the See of Auvergne. “But,” says Sidonius, “I am not so conceited as to return tit for tat, and to repay your nothing with a like nothing. If I expected an answer from you for every letter of mine, such presumption would deserve no letter from you ever. So in this letter I am not blaming you for ignoring me, I am only trying to give a reason why I do now write—and the reason is this recommendation of the letter-carrier.”—W.H.S.
