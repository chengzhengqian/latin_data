Moreover, although weak in counsel, he was completely steady in fidelity; he was most incautious because trustful, and free from fear because he did no harm. No enemy was so bitterly unfriendly as to force a curse out of him; at the same time, no friend was so intimate as to escape abuse. He was difficult to approach, though easy enough to get a view of, and he had to be borne with but was not unbearable. 5. Further, if you estimate his speeches you find him vehement and smoothly rounded, quiet and energetic; if you examine his poems you find he was tender, a master of many metres, sonorous, and of finished artistry; for he composed verses perfectly constructed with a remarkable variety both of feet and of phrasing1—hendecasyllables gliding and smooth; hexameters resounding and majestic; elegiacs now echoic, now palindromic,2 now with end linked with beginning by duplication. 6. Further, following the dictates of whatever work he had taken in hand, he would vary the style of his character-drawing in accordance with the nature of the person, the time, and the place, and in so doing he used, not the first words that occurred, but elevated, graceful, and carefully studied expressions.
