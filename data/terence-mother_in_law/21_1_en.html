I gave my promise and I’m determined to honour my undertaking. As for taking her back, I don’t believe that would be at all honourable, and I won’t do it, in spite of the love and intimacy which bind me so strongly to her. I weep when I think of the life ahead of me and the loneliness. Oh fortune, how impermanent a gift you are! But my previous love affair has given me good practice in this sort of thing. I managed to get over that one through reason. I’ll see if I can do the same with this one.

(looking down the street) Here comes Parmeno with the slaves. He’s the last person we want involved in the situation. He’s the only person I let know at the time that I didn’t touch the girl when we were first married. I’m afraid that, if he hears these repeated cries, he’ll realise that Philumena’s in labour. I must send him away somewhere until the baby is born. (he stands aside)

Enter parmeno left from the direction of the harbour together With sosia and other slaves carrying baggage.

(to Sosia) You say it turned out to be an unpleasant journey?

sos

God knows, Parmeno, words really can’t express quite how unpleasant it actually is to travel by sea.

par

Is that so?

  • 19The exposure of unwanted babies is common in New Comedy and no doubt reflects real-life practice. Illegitimate babies were particularly at risk. If the father was unwilling to raise the child, the mother might, as here and often elsewhere in drama (compare The Self-Tormentor lines 626–643), give the baby to someone else to expose, in the hope that it might in fact be spared.
