He therefore maintained that he had no right to claim this, and that it ought not to be required. Verres told Habonius to hold his tongue, at the same time hinting that he would have some chance of sharing in the profits. He had no trouble in suppressing this unassuming and easy-going person, and stated definitely that he would make the requirement about the pillars as mentioned.

This new and, for young Iunius, unexpectedly135 disastrous development was promptly reported to the boy’s stepfather Gaius Mustius (recently deceased), to his uncle Marcus Iunius, and to another of his guardians, an honest man named Publius Titius. These three carried the matter to that eminent and most trustworthy and excellent man Marcus Marcellus, himself one of the guardians. Marcellus went to see Verres, and, like the honourable and conscientious man he was, urged Verres at considerable length not to think of so gross an injustice as to turn a boy like Iunius out of the property his father had left him.
