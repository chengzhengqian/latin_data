LXIII Does freedom, that precious thing, mean 163 nothing? nor the proud privileges of a citizen of Rome? nor the law of Porcius, the laws of Semproniusa? nor the tribunes’ power, whose loss our people felt so deeply till now at last it has been restored to them? Have all these things come in the end to mean so little that in a Roman province, in a town whose people have special privileges, a Roman citizen could be bound and flogged in the marketplace by a man who owed his rods and axesb to the favour of the Roman people? When the fire and hot metal plates and the like were brought to torture him, even if his agonized entreaties, his pitiful cries could not stay your hand, was your soul untouched even by the tears and the loud groans of the Roman citizens who then stood by? You dared to crucify any living man who claimed to be a Roman citizen?—Gentlemen, in the earlier part of this trial I refrained from speaking of this matter with my present vehemence; and I did so because, as you could see, the minds of the audience were being strongly excited against Verres by feelings of distress, of hatred, of fear for the general safety.
