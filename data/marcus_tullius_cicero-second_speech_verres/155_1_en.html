But Verres, to avoid both holding the election in an unlawful month and having Herodotus there when his office was taken from him (not that the latter point troubled Verres, but Climachias was much against it), devised a plan—there is, I said some time ago, no sharper man alive, nor ever was—a plan for holding the election in the lawful month and yet not having Herodotus there. It is the custom of the129 Sicilian as of all other Greeks, as they like to secure the agreement of their days of the month with the motions of the sun and moon, to correct an occasional discrepancy by shortening a month by some one day, or two days at most, days which they term “eliminated”; also they sometimes lengthen a month by one day, or by two days. When this was discovered by our newa student of astronomy, who was thinking more of the silver plate than of the silver moon, he gave orders, not for shortening the month by a day, but for shortening the year by a month and a half, so that (for instance) the day which ought to be reckoned as the 13th of January would by his orders be publicly announced to be the 1st of March; and this, to the discontentment and dismay of everyone, was what happened. The 1st of March was the lawful day for the election, and so it came about that Climachias was duly returned as high-priest elect. Herodotus on his return from130 Rome, fifteen days, as he imagined, before the election, found himself in the month following the election month, and the election over for a month already.
