I will not, believe me, gentlemen, exaggerate the facts to strengthen my case against Verres: I will put before you truthfully, and as vividly as I can, the impression my own eyes and understanding conveyed to me. When I arrived in Sicily after a four years’ 47 absence, it had to my eyes the look we associate with countries that have been the seat of a cruel and protracted war. The fields and the hill-sides that I had once seen green and flourishing I now saw devastated and deserted; the countryside seemed itself to feel the loss of the men who once tilled it, and to be in mourning for its old masters. The cornlands of Herbita and Henna, of Murgentia and Assorus, of Imachara and Agyrium, were for the most part so completely abandoned that we looked in vain not only for the cattle but for the proprietors who were once so numerous: and the land round Aetna that used to be so richly cultivated, and that headquarters of corn-farming, the plain of Leontini, whose aspect formerly was such that to see it under crop removed any fears that corn would be scarce and dear—these were so wild and miserable a waste that there, in Sicily’s most fertile regions, nothing reminded us of Sicily. The year before had dealt the farmers a staggering blow; this last year had ruined them altogether.
