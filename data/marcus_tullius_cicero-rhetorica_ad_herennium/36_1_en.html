For Subsequent Behaviour we investigate the signs which usually attend guilt or innocence.a The prosecutor will, if possible, say that his adversary, when come upon, blushed, paled, faltered, spoke uncertainly, collapsed, or made some offer—signs of a guilty conscience. If the accused has done none of these things, the prosecutor will say his adversary had even so far in advance calculated what would actually happen to him that he stood his ground and replied with the greatest self-assurance—signs of audacity, and not of innocence. The defendant’s counsel, if his client has shown fear, will say that he was moved,b not by a guilty conscience, but by the magnitude of his peril; if his client has not shown fear, counsel will say that he was unmoved because he relied on his innocence.
