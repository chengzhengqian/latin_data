XVIII. Reasoning by Contrariesb is the figure which, of two opposite statements, uses one so as neatly and directly to prove the other, as follows: “Now how should you expect one who has ever been hostile to his own interests to be friendly to another’s?”c Again: “Now why should you think that one who is, as you have learned, a faithless friend, can be an honourable enemy? Or how should you expect a person whose arrogance has been insufferable in private life, to be agreeable and not forget himself when in power, and one who in ordinary conversation and among friends has never spoken the truth, to refrain from lies before public assemblies?” Again: “Do we fear to fight them on the level plain when we have hurled them down from the hills? When they outnumbered us, they were no match for us; now that we outnumber them, do 26we fear that they will conquer us?” This figure ought to be brief, and completed in an unbroken period.
