(8) By means of the eighth commonplacea we show that it is not a common but a unique crime, base, nefarious, and unheard-of, and therefore must be the more promptly and drastically avenged.

(9) The ninth commonplace consists of a comparisonb of wrongs, as when we shall say it is a more heinous crime to debauch a free-born person than to steal a sacred object, because the one is done from unbridled licentiousness and the other from need.

(10) By the tenth commonplace we shall examine sharply, incriminatingly, and precisely, everything that took place in the actual execution of the deed and all the circumstances that usually attend such an act, so that by the enumeration of the attendant circumstances the crime may seem to be taking place and the action to unfold before our eyes.c
