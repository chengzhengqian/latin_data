Philippic 3

[8] But what did he do himself? Having published all 19these edicts, he issued another, summoning a full senate on 24 November: on the day appointed he himself was absent. And how did he phrase this edict? These, I think, are its concluding words: “If anyone fails to attend, all will be able to set him down as an instigator of my destruction and of the most desperate designs.” What are these desperate designs? Does he refer to those that have to do with the recovery of the Roman people’s freedom? To such designs I admit that I have instigated and encouraged Caesar and still do. Not that he needed anybody’s advice; all the same, I spurred a willing horse, as they say. As for your destruction, what decent man would not instigate it, when the safety and lives of the best citizens and the freedom and dignity of the Roman people depend on it? But having 20roused us with so grim a summons, why did he not attend himself? You don’t think it was for some solemn and sober reason, do you? No, he was detained by wine and banqueting, if this can be called banqueting rather than gorging; that was why he failed to appear on the day named in his edict, and he postponed the meeting until 28 November.24 He ordered that it take place on the Capitol and himself made his way up to the temple through some Gauls’ tunnel.25 People who had been personally requested assembled, including some of quality, unmindful, however, of their dignity.
