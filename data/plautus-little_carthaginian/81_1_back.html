At a relatively early stage, the Greeks took over the Phoenician alphabet. Several Phoenician consonant sounds had no equivalent in Greek, which allowed the Greeks to use the signs for these sounds in a different function, as vowel signs. A West Greek variety of the alphabet was subsequently adopted by the Etruscans, who passed it on to the Romans.

Much later, vocalism began to be indicated in biblical Hebrew. The vocalism of Phoenician (and Punic) can be reconstructed partly through comparison with Hebrew and other Semitic languages which marked vowels in their scripts, partly through the study of later developments within Phoenician and Punic, and partly through the analysis of borrowings and information arising through language contact. Nevertheless, a number of uncertainties remain.
