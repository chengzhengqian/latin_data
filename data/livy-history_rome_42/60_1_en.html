sheltered, so that it was obvious that you rejoiced, b.c. 172 to say nothing more, in the crime of those who had killed the prince most faithful of all the Illyrians to Rome; you went to Delphi with an army through Thessalian and Malian territory, contrary to the treaty;1 again, you sent help to the Byzantines, contrary to the treaty;2 you pledged by oath a secret alliance between yourself and the Boeotians, our allies, which was not permitted;3 as for the Theban envoys, Eversa and Callicritus, who were on their way to us, I prefer to ask who killed them than to make an accusation. In Aetolia, who, if not your men, can seem to have brought about the civil war and slaughter of leading men?4 The Dolopians were utterly ravaged by you yourself.5 King Eumenes, on his return from Rome to his kingdom, was almost slaughtered like a beast for sacrifice on consecrated ground before the altars of Delphi—and I am reluctant to mention whom he accuses6; about the secret crimes of which your guest-friend at Brundisium informs us,7 I am sure that everything has been written you from Rome and also that your own envoys have reported.
