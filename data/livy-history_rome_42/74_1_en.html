Book XLII

The stern order was given them to depart from the b.c. 171 walls of Rome at once, and within thirty days from Italy. Then to the consul Publius Licinius, to whom Macedonia had fallen as province, the order was given that he should appoint the first possible day for the army to assemble. Gaius Lucretius the praetor,1 whose duty was the command of the fleet, left the city with forty quinqueremes; for it had been decided to keep at the city several of the refitted ships for various purposes. The praetor sent his brother Marcus Lucretius on in advance with one quinquereme, with orders to meet the fleet at Cephallania with the ships received from the allies according to treaty. After taking on one trireme from Rhegium, two from Locri, four from the district of Uria,2 he coasted along Italy past the farthest cape of Calabria and crossed the Ionian sea to Dyrrhachium. There he came upon ten cutters3 of the Dyrrhachians themselves, twelve of the Issaeans, and fifty-four of King Gentius, and pretending that he believed they had been collected for the use of the Romans, took them all with him on the third day to Corcyra and thence crossed at once to Cephallania.
