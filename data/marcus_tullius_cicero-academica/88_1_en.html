Or Hercules in Euripides, when he was transfixing his own sons with his arrows as if they were those of Eurystheus, when he was making away with his wife, when he was attempting to make away with his father too,—was he not being affected by things false in the same manner as if the things by which he was affected had been true? Again, Alcmaeon himself whom you quote,a who says that ‘his mind agrees not with his eyes,’—does he not in the same passage spur on his frenzy and cry

Whence does this flame arise?

and then the words

They come, they come! Now, now they are upon me! ’Tis me they seek!

What when he appeals to the maiden’s loyalty for aid—

Help me, drive the venom off, the flaming violence that torments me! Girt with steely snake they come, they ring me round with burning torches?
