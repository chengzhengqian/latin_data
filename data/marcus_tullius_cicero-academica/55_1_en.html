XII. “Now that we are sufficiently acquainted(4) Certainty is needed for action: assent to phenomena underlies all rational conduct. with the matters already unfolded, let us say a few words on the subject of ‘assent’b or approval (termed in Greek syncatathesis)—not that it is not a wide topic, but the foundations have been laid a little time back. For while we were explainingc the power residing in the senses, it was at the same time disclosed that many things are grasped and perceived by the senses, which cannot happen without the act of assent. Again, as the greatest difference between an inanimate and an animate object is that an animate object performs some action (for an entirely inactive animal is an utterly inconceivable thing), either it must be denied the possession of sensation or it must be assigned a faculty of assenting as a 38 voluntary act.
