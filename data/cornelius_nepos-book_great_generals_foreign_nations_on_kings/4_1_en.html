And not very long after that Seleucus was treacherously killed by Ptolemaeus, surnamed Ceraunus or280 b.c. “the Thunderbolt,” to whom, when he was exiled by his father from Alexandria and was in need of help from others, Seleucus had given asylum. But Ptolemaeus himself, having made over his kingdom to his son while still living, by him, they say, was283 b.c. put to death.

Since I think that I have said enough about these kings, 1 it seems fitting not to pass over Hamilcar and Hannibal, who are generally admitted to have surpassed all men of African birth in greatness of soul and in sagacity.
