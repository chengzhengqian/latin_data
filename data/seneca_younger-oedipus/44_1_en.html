OEDIPUS

So he spoke, raging with anger. His eyes blazed threateningly with a ravening fire, and their orbs scarcely held fast in their seats. Full of audacious violence was his gaze, of ferocious anger, as he prepared to root it out. He groaned, and with a terrible cry he bent his hands toward his face. For their part his eyes stood out wildly, and tracked their own hands intently, meeting their wounds halfway. With hooked hands he greedily probed his eyes, and from their base, from their very roots he wrenched the eyeballs and let them roll out together. His hands stayed embedded in the cavities, their nails tearing deeply into the hollow recesses of his eyes, those empty sockets, with fruitless aggression and excessive rage: so great was the threat of the light. He raised his head, and scanning the sky’s expanse with hollow orbs he tested the darkness. He broke off the shreds hanging down where the eyes had been incompletely dug out, and in triumph called on all the gods: “Look, spare my fatherland, I pray you: I have done justice, have taken the penalty owed. At last I have found a night suited to my marriage chamber.” A hideous flurry of drops wetted his face, and his mutilated head spewed copious blood from the torn veins.
