11. He received the proper honours from you, first the tears which every son should shed, then the temples2 you raised to him. Others have done the same, but with different intent; Tiberius deified Augustus,3 but his purpose was to introduce the charge of high treason; Nero had done the same for Claudius4 in a spirit of mockery; Titus had similarly honoured Vespasian and Domitian Titus, but only for one to be thought the son and the other the brother of a god.5 You gave your father his place among the stars with no thought of terrorizing your subjects, of bringing the gods into disrepute, or of gaining reflected glory, but simply because you thought he was a god. This is an honour which means less when it is paid by men who believe themselves to be equally divine; unlike you, who set up his cult with altars, couches and a priest, yet created and proved his godhead still more by being the man you are. For there is no more certain proof of divinity in a ruler who has chosen his successor before he met his end than the worthiness of his choice.
