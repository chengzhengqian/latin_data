Lest he should think that rather than showing him the way to win honours in the future I have already won his honours for him, I am accustomed to give him the advice (though advice is somewhat beyond hima at his present years) which that kingb who was himself sprung from Jupiter gave to his sons:

Be wakeful aye; with snares the good are hemmed; For jealous hearts are rife . . .

No doubt you recall the rest of the passage. These lines were written by an earnest and gifted poet, whose object in writing them was to kindle the spirit of industry and ambition, not in those young princes who were merely the figments of his imagination, but in us and in our children.

What more, you ask, could Plancius have gained if he had been the son of Gnaeus Scipio?c He could not have been made aedile more easily, but he would have the advantage of being less an object of envy; for the steps of office are equal for the greatest and meanest of men, but in the glory they bring wholly unequal.
