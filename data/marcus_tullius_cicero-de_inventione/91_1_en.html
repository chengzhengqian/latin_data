This topic is what one might call the foundation or basis of this issue. For no one can be convinced that a deed has been done unless some reason is given why it was done. Therefore the prosecutor when he says that something was done on impulse, will be under the necessity of dilating upon that passion and, as it were, agitation and state of mind, with the full powers of his thought and expression, and of showing how great is the force of love, what powerful mental agitation arises from anger or from any of the causes by which he claims that the defendant was urged to commit this crime. Here pains must be taken that it may not seem strange that a mind disquieted by such passion should undertake some crime. This can be done by citing examples of those who have done something under a similar impulse and by collecting parallels and by explaining the nature of mental disturbance.
