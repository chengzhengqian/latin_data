XXXII. Necessity is brought in when the accused is defended as having done what he did because of some force beyond his control, as follows: There is a law at Rhodes that if any ship with a ram is caught in the harbour it is confiscated. There was a violent storm at sea, and the force of the wind compelled the sailors against their will to put into the harbour of Rhodes. The treasurera claims the ship as public property, the master of the vessel denies that it ought to be confiscated. The charge is: “A ship with a ram has been caught in the harbour.” The answer is confession and avoidance. The reason is: “We were driven into the harbour by force and necessity.” The prosecutor’s reply is: “Nevertheless the ship ought to be confiscated according to law.” The point for the judge’s decision is: “Granted that the law confiscates a ship with a ram caught in the harbour, and that this ship was driven into port by the force of the storm against the desire of the crew, should it be confiscated?”
