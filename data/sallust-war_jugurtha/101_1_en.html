He assigned all the cattle that had become booty on previous days to the auxiliary cavalry to drive; he ordered his lieutenant commander Aulus Manlius to go with light-armed cohorts to the town of Laris,272 where he had placed cash to pay the soldiers and supplies, and he said that in the course of his pillaging he would come to the same place a few days later. Having thus concealed his real undertaking, he proceeded to the river Tanais.273

Now every day on the march, Marius had equitably distributed cattle to his army by centuries and likewise by squadrons,274 and he took care that leather sacks should be made from the hides; thus at the same time he mitigated the lack of grain, and without anyone’s knowledge, he prepared objects that would soon to be useful.275 When they finally reached the river on the sixth day, a great quantity of leather sacks had been produced. Having pitched a lightly fortified camp by the river, he ordered the soldiers to take their meal and to be ready to march forth at sunset, and after discarding all their baggage, to load themselves and their pack animals with water only.
