Therefore, when he first sought election to a military tribunate, though the majority of voters did not know him by sight, yet because he was known by his deeds, he was elected by the vote of all the tribes.203 Then, from that beginning, he gained for himself one political office after another,204 and he conducted himself in exercising powering in such as way that he was regarded worthy of a higher position than that which he was holding. Nevertheless, despite being up until that point205 such an admirable man—for afterward he was driven headlong by ambition206—he did not venture to aspire to the consulship. Even in that period, while the commons bestowed other political offices, the nobles passed the consulate from hand to hand among themselves. No “new man”207 was so famous or so illustrious for his deeds that he was not considered unworthy of that honor and unclean, so to speak.208
