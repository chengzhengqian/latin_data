Comedies in Greek Dress

106–7

Cicero:—

(A) Tell me, how was it that you ruined such a mighty commonwealth as yours so quickly?

For that is the question men ask, as we find in The Game a of the poet Naevius. Among other answers that are given, this will be the chief one—

(B) There came forward new-fangled orators, silly little youngsters.

  • aNaevius is certainly alluding to the politics of his day, but whether the title of the play was Ludus, and if so, what type of play it was, we do not know. Ribbeck reads Lupo and assigns the fr. to the praetexta Lupus (see pp. 136–9). Mueller takes the name to be Lydus, and Norden (Sitz.-Ber. Berl. Ak., 1924, 229) believes Naevius translated Antiphanes’ comedy Λύδος. See also Moore, Am. Journ. Phil., XXIII, 437; M. Lenchantin de Gubernatis, ‘La leggenda Romana e le praetextae,’ Riv. di filol., XL, 444; Ribb., Trag. Fragm., p. 278; R.T., 66.
