A man buying out1 his own prostitute instead of his father’s

A man gave money to his loose-living son, who was in love with a prostitute, to buy out for him a prostitute whom he himself, the father, loved. He bought out a prostitute whom he himself, the young man, loved. He is disowned.

discussion

This old man will say that his son had been a loose-liver and loved a prostitute not without great detriment to patrimony and reputation. The father had wanted to remedy the evil. He often upbraided his son, often castigated him in private, often even publicly. When finally no limit could be set to the extravagance and shamelessness he took counsel with his relations on what he ought to do. Should he disown? But the loose living would be so much the more unbridled. Should he castigate? But castigating had got him nowhere. A way occurred to the relations and friends whereby they thought the young man might be reformed: if the father pretended that he was in love with a prostitute2 and gave money to the son himself of all people to buy her out. If he did that, when the other prostitute had been bought and bought out by the young man himself, the prostitute would be outraged and stop loving him. For it must be stated at the start that the young man had got involved by the prostitute’s fault, and for that reason they looked for something to take her mind off him3 and that was why money was given to the young man.
