He says: “But in the actual trial in which he was convicted I was not his advocate.3 Meanwhile, I propose to take the line that I did no harm to the commonwealth.”4 Even so, this is enough: you were a traitor’s advocate. Let us suppose that the trial was adjourned and that he was the advocate only in the first trial: would not conviction follow on the ground that he had been advocate at all? So we ought not to look at the time but at the case, if the man is the same, if the charge is the same, if the law is the same. Let us suppose he was convicted by somebody else. For this that you put to your credit5 will do you no good. For then you would have said: “I didn’t know and I was misled by the same thing that misled the jury.” As it is, you knowingly defended a traitor.6

I say nothing about the advocacy;7I have lost my witness.8 Such greed for gain? That is how traitors are corrupted. The guilty man talked to his advocate like this to be sure: “Yes, I was a traitor but I got a great deal of money—enough for you too.” Do you ask, gentlemen, how much he promised his client? The traitor was unable to pay.
