So the question is, gentlemen of the jury, whether I gave poison to my brother or that man to a non-relative. I am well aware of the name3 he uses, and I ask you to know, gentlemen, that the question today is just this, whether he was a friend. Without doubt none of you would overlook how much preference should be given to brother over friend, even if the good faith of both names were assured. For what friendship can be so happy as to imitate fraternity? Sure it is that when we wish to make up to those who appear to be our friends, no flattery can go beyond this name—we call them brothers. So <powerful> appears to be even the name and a sort of shadow of nature; it would be like putting the name of friendship on making a living (?).4 But since it is agreed that I am his brother whereas <that man>, if he gave the poison (as I shall prove), was certainly not his friend, what comparison can there be between the two personae? If the question were only about affection, gentlemen, nature would come far ahead; but as it is, something considerably more is before you. For the question is whether I could have killed5 my brother. So your enquiry is between homicide and parricide.
