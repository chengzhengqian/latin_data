XVIII. Now since I have promised1 to quote some of the letters which showed the joy of the senate when Tacitus was created emperor, I will append the following and then make an end of writing. The official letters:

“From the most noble senate to the council of Carthage, greeting. May it prove happy, auspicious, of good omen, and to the welfare of the commonwealth and the Roman world! The right of conferring the imperial power, of naming an emperor, and of entitling him Augustus has been restored to us. To us, therefore, you will now refer all matters of importance. Every appeal shall now be made to the prefect of the city,2 but it shall come up to him from the proconsuls and the regular judges. And herein, we believe, your authority also has been restored to its ancient condition, for this body is now supreme, and in recovering its own power it is preserving the rights of others as well.”
