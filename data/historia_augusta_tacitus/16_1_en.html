XVI. Tacitus scarcely gave a largess1 to the Roman people in six months’ time. His portrait was placed in the house of the Quintilii,2 representing him in five ways on a single panel, once in a toga, once in a military cloak, once in armour, once in a Greek mantle, and once in the garb of a hunter. Of this picture, indeed, a writer of epigrams made mock, saying: “do not recognise the old man in the armour, I do not recognise the man in the military cloak,” and so forth, “but I do recognise the man in the toga.” Both Florian and Tacitus left many children, whose descendants, I suppose, are awaiting the coming of the thousandth year. About them many epigrams were written, ridiculing the soothsayers who made the promise of the imperial power. This is all that I remember learning about the lives of Tacitus and Florian that is worthy of record.
