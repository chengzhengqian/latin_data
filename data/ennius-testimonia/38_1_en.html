T 89 Arnobius, Against the Pagans

And we can indeed show at this point that all those you mention to us and call gods were human beings, either by replicating Euhemerus of Acragas,1 whose books Ennius translated into the language of Italy to make them well known to all, or Nicagoras of Cyprus2 or Leon of Pella3 or Theodoros of Cyrene4 or Hippo5 and Diagoras from Melos6 or a thousand other authors, who, in the interest of scrupulous diligence, have brought hidden things to light with frank liberty.

  • 1Lat. Agrigentum, a town in Sicily. Only here and in Clement of Alexandria (Clem. Al. Protr. 2.24.2) is Euhemerus said to come from Acragas. Elsewhere he is associated with Messene (presumably also in Sicily).
  • 2A paradoxographer around 400 BC.
  • 3Allegedly an Egyptian priest promoting doctrines about the gods.
  • 4A Cyrenaic philosopher (ca. 340–250 BC).
  • 5An early natural philosopher.
  • 6A lyric poet of the fifth century BC (cf. F 738–39 PMG).
