TESTIMONIA

TESTIMONIA (T 1–114)

​

T 1 Terence, The Woman of Andros

When they [Terence’s critics] accuse him, they accuse Naevius, Plautus, Ennius, whom our man here regards as authorities, whose carelessness he would prefer to emulate than the obscure pedantry of those people.

​

T 2 (cf. T 41) Lucilius

Similarly, any epistle [in verse] that is not long is a poema [poem], but the above mentioned poesis [poetry] is a complete work (just as the complete Iliad is a single work and Ennius’ Annals makes one theme) and a single piece; it is a thing much bigger than what I earlier called poema. Therefore I say: nobody who criticizes Homer criticizes him in everything, nor that which I earlier called poesis: he criticizes a single verse, a word, a thought, a single passage.1

​

T 3 Pompilius

I am called a pupil of Pacuvius. He in turn was a pupil of Ennius, Ennius of the Muses. I am called Pompilius.1

  • 1 The Latin text is that of Marx.
  • 1As a pupil of Pacuvius, he was presumably a Roman tragedian. On the epigram see Lausberg 1984, 277–78.
