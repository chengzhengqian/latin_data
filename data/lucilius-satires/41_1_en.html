Book VI

261

Nonius: ‘Iubere’ means to wish . . . —

“To send welcome to a friend is to wish him well.”

262–3

Nonius: ‘Protelare’ (really ‘to drive forward’) means also to give help . . . Lucilius in bk. VI of the Satires uses ‘protelum’ (a driven team or line)—

whom neither bulls bred in the Lucanian moun- tains could draw away in a driven team on their sturdy necks.

264

Servius (supplemented): ‘Optare’ does not mean simply to choose . . . but even to want . . . —

“. . . what you may want me myself to do.”

265

Nonius: The genitive put for the ablative . . . —

“You encourage me, if I can gain the good will of yonder men,”

266

A last resort saves Scipio:

Nonius: The ablative form put for the genitive a . . . —

That alone is left of his bad luck in this bad business.

267–8

Porphyrio, on ‘Thus did Apollo preserve me ‘in Horace: He took this in that well known clause found in Homer; which Lucilius also has quoted word for word in the sixth book of the Satires, when he says as follows—

so that it may be all the same and become a case of ‘and him Apollo rescued.’

  • aBut re is more probably a dative—“This alone resists bad luck and a bad business.” Cf. lines 294–5.
