Book XXVI

Sat. III. On writing tragic poetry. a 665

Nonius: ‘Averruncare,’ to turn aside (literally ‘to sweep and hoe away’?) . . . —

“May the gods advise you better things, and root out this your madness!” b

666

Lucilius admits his fault?:

Nonius: ‘Contemnificum’ . . . —

For I become contemnifical and despise Aga- memnon. c

667–8

Nonius: ‘Prosperari’ . . . —

Nor was Minerva’s favour propitiated; for he dragged Cassandra away from the statue. d

669

Nonius: ‘Efferre,’ to submit (or substitute) e . . .—

They will fight it out in person, will die and actually make their own funerals for your sake.

  • aCp. Cichor., 127 ff.
  • bLucilius’ critic here parodies the tragic diction of Pacuvius (in Chryses) by quoting a line (one word short); for this, see Remains of Old, Latin, Vol. II, pp. 206–77. The true meanings of the old words verrunco and averrunco are not known. But the words mean something stronger than Nonius implies. Perhaps from verrere to sweep and runcare to hoe or plane off.
  • cLucilius retorts in like style. In Chryses of Pacuvius there was perhaps a quarrel between Achilles and Agamemnon over Chryseis. Cf. Fiske, T.A.P., XL, 131.
  • d i.e. Ajax, son of Oileus, dragged Cassandra away from the statue of Pallas. Again probably a hit at Pacuvius’ Chryses: see last two notes. If Minerva is right, the reference is to the anger of Athene (Homer, Od., IV, 499, Virg., Aen., I, 40).
  • eNonius is wrong, unless subdere can mean ‘to bury,’ ‘lay out,’ or offerent is read for efferent in the quotation from Lucilius. But efferre in the lemma (in a group of which all begin with e) forbids this; see the next item.
