III. On Gaius Cassius:

Nonius: ‘Damnare’ means to disinherit . . . —

Here we have Gaius Cassius, an odd-job man whom we call Cephalo, this cut-purse and thief. Him does Quintus Tullius the informer make the heir, and all the others lose their cases. b

448–9

IV. On Quintus Granius the auctioneer:

Gellius: From book XI of Lucilius there are the lines . . . the same poet lower down—

I was wanting to put into verse a smart saying of Granius the auctioneer. c

In this preposition also—that of the first word (conicere). they lengthen the vowel.

Cicero: I remember how Titus Tinea of Placentia, a very witty fellow, was having a contest of wordy wit with our friend Quintus Granius the crier. “Do you mean,” said Brutus, “with him about whom Lucilius has much to say?” That’s the very man; but Granius by some homebred show of taste overwhelmed Tinea who was not behind-hand in making many a good joke.

  • bCichor., 314–15 (he suggests C. Cassius Sabaco); cp. id., Rom. Stud., 83. Cephalo = Capito; there is a pun on sector, cut-purse, and sector, purchaser of confiscated goods, the real vocation of Capito; and probably two meanings in operarius,’ man of business.’ iudex, judge, may well be right.
