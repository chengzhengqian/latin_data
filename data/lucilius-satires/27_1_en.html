Book IV

162–3

A nasty-looking dish?:

Nonius: Grease and ointment have this difference—grease is made from the lard-fat of cud-chewing animals . . . —

“Tisiphone, most holy Fury among the Gracious Ones, brought along oil boiled out of the lungs and fat of Tityos.” a

164

Revelry:

Priscianus: ‘Turgeo,’ perfect tursi . . . —

‘I swelled up drunk.

165

Host speaks of a sponger?:

Priscianus: The oldest writers used to say even ‘edo, edis, edit,’ with the first syllable shortened . . . —

“This fellow, who has gobbled his own substance, now gobbles up me.”

166

Matters of sex:

Nonius: ‘Puellos,’ the same as ‘pueros’ . . . —

“and when he is a person so shapely and a boy worthy of you.”

  • aTisiphone was one of the Furies. The giant Tityos, for trying to outrage Latona, was punished by being condemned to have his liver eaten by a vulture in the Lower World; his liver constantly renewed itself.
