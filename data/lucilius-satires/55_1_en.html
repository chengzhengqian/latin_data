Book IX

352–3

a brick-maker:

Nonius: ‘Acerosum’ (wholemeal bread) and ‘aceratum’ (clay mixed with chaff) are both put as unusual words. . . . ‘Aceratum’ is a clay mixed with chaff, according to the custom of brickmakers . . . —

He who makes bricks never has more than common natural clay and chaff, mixed mud and grain-husks. b

354–5

an old wreck of a man:

Nonius: ‘Ramites’ (blood-vessels of the lungs) is a term used for lungs, or for a rupture . . . —

because he is a deformed, rheumaticky, gouty old man, because he is a poor maimed lanky wretch with a big rupture.

356

Priscianus: ‘Scabo,’ perfect ‘scabi’ . . .—

He had scratched as a pig does by rubbing its ribs against a tree.

357–8

an old glutton with spoilt palate:

Nonius: ‘Ostrea’ . . . of the neuter gender . . . —

What then if Cerco c finds that oysters taste of the very mud and mire of the rivers?
