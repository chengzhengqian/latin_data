Book XXVI

702

Nonius: ‘Apisci,’ the same as ‘adipisci’. . . —

so that I recoil from that which, I understand, you wish especially to attain. a

703

We go different ways: Nonius: ‘Niti’ . . . —

You press on to the best of your powers . . . but I on the other hand strive to be quite unlike that.

704

And this is natural:

Nonius: ‘Bulga’ means a little bag . . . —

just as when each of us was given forth to light from a mother’s bag. b

705

Pleasure not the chief aim of life:

Nonius: ‘Vegrande,’ very large c . . . —

That is not why a man is brought up and not for that is he given over to a short life.

706

Nonius: ‘Petere,’ to want, to grasp after . . . —

But if they see this, and think that the wise man always aims at things that are good,

707

Lucilius ‘friend believes in the utility of ancient history:

Nonius: ‘Cordi est’ . . . —

Further this matter lies at your heart because you think it is useful.

  • aThese last three fragments may be joined together.
  • bMarx rightly thinks Lucilius here expresses the Epicurean idea that all living things aim at pleasure from birth. Bulga is here a vulgar word for womb.
  • cNonius, misled by the double negative in his example, has taken vegrandis wrongly; it means ‘not large.’
