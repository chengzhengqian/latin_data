575–6

Boxing-match between Ulysses and Irus?:

Macrobius: Lucilius in the seventeenth book—

His big bones and muscles show up.

Sat. II.? On business life in Rome? 577–8

A business man:

Nonius: ‘Proprium’ (one’s own) also means perpetual . . . —

He wagers his all in a little money-lending, a and spurns all other professions; for to be sure no one has anything for ever his own.

579–80

Serving a warrant:

Nonius: ‘Calvitur’ is a term used for ‘frustratur’ . . . —

“If he does not go,” said he, “and if he shirks, arrest him.” “Shall the thief arrest the owner then?” b

Book XVIII

581–2

From a satire on gluttony?

Nonius: ‘Cadi’ are jars in which wines are stored. Lucilius in the eighteenth book—

You take away two hundred thousand bushels of corn and a thousand jars of wine. c

  • aMarx takes usura as enjoyment of life’s gifts. ‘He lays out his all in enjoyment.’ This may well be right.
  • bLucilius here adapts a law of the Twelve Tables preserved in Porphyr., on Hor., S., I, 9, 76 ‘ni it, antestamino; igiturem capito,’ and Fest., 452, 6 ‘Si calvitur pedemve struit, manum endo iacito.’ See below, pp. 424–7. Lucilius calls attention to ambiguity in the wording of the law.
  • cImitated by Horace, S. II, 3, 111 ff. Fiske, 234.
