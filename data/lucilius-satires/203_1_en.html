Unassigned Fragments

1242–3

Varro: They think that a further proof that there is no regularity is that Lucilius writes—

They are priced a ten-as, or perhaps the right expression is ‘at ten-awes.’ a

They are wrong because Lucilius ought not to have doubted; for both forms are right.

Ills and diseases:

Paulus: ‘Vitiligo’ a white spot on the human body, which the Greeks call ἀλφόν. Lucilius—

“This white spot disgusts me.” “It doesn’t hurt, does it?” said he.

1245

Paulus: It is certain that the term cold ‘querquera’ with shiverings is derived from the Greek κάρκαρα, whence also ‘carcer.’ Lucilius—

tossing me about like a feverish ague.

1246

Festus: ‘Tama’ is a term used when from the toil of travel the blood moves down into the legs and makes a swelling. Lucilius—

lest a groin-swelling appear, lest pimples, a lump, b a blister should give pain.

1247

Charisius: ‘Pedetemptim.’ Lucilius—

safety to come hither step by step. c

  • aa decussis was a piece of ten asses; the word could be treated as indeclinable in the singular. Lucilius was perhaps referring again to the Lex Fannia which allowed the expense of ten asses as indicated on p. 405, note d.
  • bin medical language, a varicose vein. (Linds., C.Q., XX, 103.) The line perhaps belongs to book III and gave a reason for not making the journey on foot. Some (Fiske, T.A.P., XL, 146) take inguen here as ‘membrum virile.’
  • cThe true reading and the meaning are unknown.
