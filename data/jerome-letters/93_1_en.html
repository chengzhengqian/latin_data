Letters

I write this in haste, dear lady Asella, as I go on board ship, grieving and in tears; and I thank my God that I am held worthy of the world’s hate. Pray for me that from Babylon I may return to Jerusalem, and that Joshua, son of Josedech, may have dominion over me rather than Nebuchadnezzar,1 and that Ezra, whose name means ‘helper,’ may come and bring me back to my own country. Foolish was I, who wished to sing the Lord’s song in a strange land,2 and left Mount Sion to seek the help of Egypt. I forgot the Gospel story,3 how that he who goes out from Jerusalem immediately falls among robbers, is stripped, wounded, and left for dead. But though priest and Levite pay no heed, there is the good Samaritan, who, when he was told, ‘Thou art a Samaritan and hast a devil,’ denied having a devil, but did not deny that he was a Samaritan, that name in Hebrew being equivalent to our ‘guardian.’ There are some men who style me a sorcerer: I, who am but a servant, recognize the word as a title of faith. The Jews called my master a magician, and the great apostle was spoken of as a deceiver. ‘There hath no temptation taken me but such as is common to man.’4 How few troubles have I endured, I who am a soldier of the cross? Men have laid upon me the disgrace of a false charge, but I know that the road to the kingdom of heaven leads alike through good report and through evil.
