Letters

Lord that from a bitter seed of learning I am now plucking sweet fruits.

I will tell you of another thing that I saw in Egypt. There was a young Greek in a community there, who could not quench the fires of the flesh by any continence or any labour however severe. In his danger the father of the monastery saved him by the following device. He instructed a grave elder to pursue the young man with revilings and abuse, and after having thus insulted him to be the first to lay a complaint. When witnesses were called they always spoke in favour of the aggressor. The youth could only weep at the false charge, but no one believed the truth. The father alone would cleverly put in a plea on his behalf, lest ‘our brother be swallowed up by overmuch sorrow.’1 To cut a long tale short, a whole year passed in this way, and at the end the youth was asked about his former imaginings, whether they still troubled him. ‘Good heavens,’ he replied, ‘how can I want to fornicate, when I am not allowed even to live?’ If he had been alone, by whose help could he have overcome temptation?

Worldly philosophers are wont to drive out an old passion by a new one, as you drive out an old nail by hammering in another. This is what the seven princes of Persia did to Ahasuerus, when they assuaged his regret for queen Vashti by suggesting an amour with other maidens.2 They cure fault by fault and sin by sin: we must overcome vice by love of virtue.’ Depart from evil,’ says the Scripture, ‘and do good; seek peace and pursue it.’3 If we do not hate evil we cannot love good.
