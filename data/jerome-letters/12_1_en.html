Letters

Julian. He planted, you must water, the Lord will give the increase. Jesus has given her to me as compensation for the wound which the devil inflicted. He has brought her back from death to life. But, as the heathen poet says, for her

‘All things, though safe in semblance, I do fear.’1

You know yourselves how treacherous is the path of youth, a path where I fell and which you are now traversing not without fear. At this moment, when she is entering upon it, she needs to be supported by all men’s encouragement, confirmed by all men’s advice; in other words, strengthened by such frequent letters as your saintliness will suggest. Love endureth all things; and I therefore beg you to get a letter from Pope Valerian2 also, so that her courage may be increased. You know that a girl’s spirit is often fortified by the thought that her elders are interested in her.

As for my own country, it is enslaved to barbarism, and men’s family God is their belly. People live only for the day, and the richer you are the more saintly you are held to be. Furthermore, to use a well-worn popular saying, the cover there is worthy of the dish; for Lupicinus3 is their priest. It bears out the proverb which, as Lucilius tells us, made Crassus laugh for the only time in his life4: ‘When an ass eats thistles up, his lips have lettuce like themselves.’5 I mean that in my country a crippled helmsman steers a leaking ship, a blind man leads the blind into a pit; as the ruler is, so are the ruled.
