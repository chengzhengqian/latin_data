He saw to it that the doorkeeper was constantly at his post, that the curtains were hanging at the entrance, that the sanctuary was neat, and the church-vessels brightly polished. His careful reverence extended to every form of ceremonial, and no duty, small or great, was neglected. Whenever you looked for him in his church, there you found him.

In Quintus Fabius1 antiquity admired a man of rank, who not only wrote a history of Rome but won even greater fame from his paintings than from his books. Our own Bezaleel also and Hiram,2 the son of a Tyrian woman, are spoken of in Scripture as men filled with wisdom and the spirit of God, because one made the furniture of the tabernacle, the other that of the temple. As rich crops and fertile fields are at times one great luxuriance of stalk and ear, so great talents and minds that are filled with virtue overflow into a variety of elegant accomplishments. So among the Greeks the great philosopher3 was praised, who boasted that he had made with his own hands everything which he used, including his cloak and his finger-ring. We can say the same about Nepotian, for he adorned the church-buildings and the halls of the martyrs with different kinds of flowers and with the foliage of trees and clusters of vine leaves. Indeed, everything in his church that pleased by its arrangement or its appearance bore witness to the labour and the zeal of its presbyter.
