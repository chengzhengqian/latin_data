Letters

How many matrons, how many of God’s virgins, ladies of gentle birth and high position, have been made the sport of these beasts! Bishops have been taken prisoners, presbyters and other clergymen of different orders murdered. Churches have been overthrown, horses stabled at Christ’s altar, the relics of martyrs dug up.

‘Sorrow and grief on every side we see And death in many a shape.’1

The Roman world is falling, and yet we hold our heads erect instead of bowing our necks. What, think you, are the feelings of the Corinthians, the Athenians, the Lacedaemonians, the Arcadians, and all the other Greeks over whom barbarians now are ruling? I have only mentioned a few cities certainly, but they were once the seats of no small powers. The East seemed to be immune from these dangers and was only dismayed by the news that reached her. But lo! last year2 the wolves—not of Arabia, but from the far north—were let loose upon us from the distant crags of Caucasus, and in a short time overran whole provinces. How many monasteries did they capture, how many rivers were reddened with men’s blood! They besieged Antioch and all the other cities on the Halys, Cydnus, Orontes, and Euphrates. They carried off troops of captives. Arabia, Phoenicia, Palestine and Egypt in their terror felt themselves already enslaved.

‘Had I a hundred tongues, a hundred mouths, A voice of brass, I could not tell the names Of all those punishments.’3

  • 2a.d. 395.
  • 3Virgil, Aeneid, VI. 625.
