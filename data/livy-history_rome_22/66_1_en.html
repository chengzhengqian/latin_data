BOOK XXII

He was as fully aware of how matters stood on the enemy side as on his own: he knew that the commanders differed in character and were at loggerheads, and that nearly two thirds of their army consisted of raw recruits. Accordingly, thinking that the place and time were right for an ambush, he set off the following night with his men carrying nothing but their weapons, and he left the camp behind him filled with all manner of riches, both state and privately owned. Behind some nearby mountains he stationed his men out of view and drawn up for battle, infantry on the left, cavalry on the right, and then led the baggage train along a valley between the hills. His aim was to take the enemy by surprise while they were completely preoccupied with ransacking a camp that they would assume had been abandoned when its occupants fled. Numerous fires were left burning in the camp to make the Romans believe that Hannibal had tried to keep the consuls in place with the illusion of a functioning camp while he got a good head start in his flight—the same ploy with which he had duped Fabius the previous year.146

42. At daybreak, first the fact that the Carthaginian outposts had been withdrawn and then the strange silence as they approached took the Romans by surprise. Then, when quite sure the camp was deserted, they converged on the headquarters of the consuls reporting that the enemy had fled in such panic as to leave the camp with thetents still standing and, to cover up the flight, with numerous fires left burning. The cry then went up for the consuls to order the advance, lead the men in pursuit of the enemy, and pillage their camp immediately; and one of the consuls was acting just like one of the crowd of common soldiers.
