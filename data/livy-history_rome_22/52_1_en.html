For he was left with no hope of provisioning his army in those parts should the incoming consuls follow those same tactics for the war.

When the war around Gereonium had already ground to a halt with the difficulties of winter, ambassadors from Naples came to Rome. They brought into the senate forty heavy golden bowls of considerable weight and delivered a speech stating that they were aware that the treasury of the Roman People was being depleted by the war, one being fought as much for the cities and farmland of the allies as for the city of Rome—the capital and fortress of Italy—and its empire. Accordingly, the people of Naples had voted that it was only right for them to assist the Roman people with the gold that had been to left them by their ancestors both for beautifying their temples and as a fund to aid them in adversity. Had they believed their personal assistance would help, they would have just as readily offered it. They would be grateful, they concluded, if the Roman senators and people regarded as their own all the Neapolitans’ possessions, and deigned to accept a gift whose value lay more in the goodwill of those gladly giving it than in its intrinsic worth. The ambassadors were formally thanked for the generosity and concern; and only the lightest of their bowls was accepted.119
