BOOK XXII

The region—the Etruscan plains lying between Faesulae20 and Arretium—was among the most fertile in Italy, rich in grain, livestock and all other commodities. The consul had become headstrong as a result of his earlier consulship, quite disrespectful not just of the laws and of the senate, but even of the gods, and this ingrained recklessness had been further nourished by the good luck that had secured him success in his civilian and his military career.21 It was thus perfectly clear that, since he had no regard for god or man, his conduct would throughout be aggressive and reckless, but to have him more readily yield to his natural defects the Carthaginian was preparing to stimulate him and stir him to action. Accordingly, leaving his enemy to his left, he made for Faesulae to raid central Etruria, and with slaughter and burning he provided the consul with a distant view of as much devastation as he could.

Flaminius who, even if his enemy remained inactive, was not going to remain inactive himself, now saw allied property being hauled off and pillaged almost before his eyes. He took it as a personal affront that, while the Carthaginian was now meandering through central Italy and advancing unopposed to assault the very walls of Rome, everybody else was advocating in council a cautious rather than a dramatic response: they should await his colleague, they said, in order to fight with combined forces and unified strategy, meanwhile curbing the enemy’s unchecked raiding with cavalry and light infantry.
