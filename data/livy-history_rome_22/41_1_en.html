Hannibal, also fearing that it might be some sort of trap, withdrew his men, and the Romans, giving chase with the help of the Samnite, took two strongholds by storm that day. Six thousand of the enemy were reportedly killed, and a good five thousand Romans. But despite losses on both sides being so close, a baseless report was brought to Rome, along with an even more baseless dispatch from the master of horse, of an outstanding victory.

25. These events were very frequently discussed in both the senate and the assembly of the people. While the community was full of joy, the dictator alone refused to believe the report or the dispatch, adding that even if it were all true he feared success more than he did a reverse. Then the tribune of the plebs, Marcus Metilius, declared that this really was intolerable: the dictator had not only opposed a promising operation when he was present but now refused to recognize success achieved when he was absent. In carefully drawing out the war he was temporizing in order to stay in office longer and be the sole holder of imperium99 both in Rome and in the armed forces. For one of the consuls had fallen in action and the other taken far from Italy, ostensibly in pursuit of the Punic fleet.
