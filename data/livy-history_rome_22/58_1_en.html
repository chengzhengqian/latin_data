In addition, a number of people had been fatally struck by lightning on the vaulted street137 that used to lead to the Campus Martius. The prodigies were expiated as prescribed by the books.

Ambassadors from Paestum brought golden bowls to Rome. Like the Neapolitans, they were thanked, but their gold was not accepted.

37. At about this time, a fleet that had been sent by Hiero reached Ostia with a large cargo of provisions. Ushered into the senate, his ambassadors announced that King Hiero had been so distressed by the news of the consul Gaius Flaminius’ death and the destruction of his army that he could not have been more upset by any misfortune overtaking himself or his own realm. So, they said, while Hiero well knew that the greatness of the Roman people was almost more remarkable in bad times than in good, he had nevertheless sent everything usually provided by good and faithful allies to assist in prosecuting wars; and these things he earnestly begged the senators not to refuse. First of all they were bringing a 220-pound golden Victory to serve as a good omen. The senators were to accept, hold, and keep it as theirs in perpetuity.
