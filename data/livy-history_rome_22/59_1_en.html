Hiero knew that the Roman People employed only infantry and cavalry that were Roman or of Latin status, they added, but he had also observed foreign light-armed auxiliaries in the Roman camp. He had therefore sent them a thousand archers and slingers, a force well suited to combat Baliaric and Moorish troops and other peoples who fought with projectiles.

To these gifts the ambassadors also added some advice: the praetor allotted charge of Sicily should take a fleet across to Africa so that the enemy, too, would have war in his own land and be granted less leeway for sending assistance to Hannibal.

The reply given to the king by the Senate was as follows. Hiero was a good man and an outstanding ally who had shown unflagging loyalty ever since he had established friendship with the Roman people, and who had at all times and in all places generously supported the Roman cause. For that the Roman people were grateful to him, as they should be. Gold had also been brought to them by a number of communities, they said, but the Roman people had not accepted it, appreciative though they were of thekind gesture.
