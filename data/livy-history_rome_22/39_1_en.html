Hannibal was now encamped before the walls of Gereonium.94 He had captured the city and put it to the torch but had left a few buildings standing to serve as grain depots. From there he would send two thirds of his force on foraging expeditions; and he himself would remain on watch with the other third (which was kept ready for action), both to protect the camp and also to keep a lookout for attacks on his foragers from any direction.

24. The Roman army was then in the territory of Larinum;95 in command was the master of horse, Minucius, since the dictator had left for the city, as noted above. The camp, which had been pitched in a safe location on a lofty hill, was now brought down into the plain and, in line with the commander’s temperament, more hotheaded plans were being mooted, namely attacking either the scattered Carthaginian foragers or their camp, which had been left only lightly guarded.

It had also not escaped Hannibal’s notice that the strategy of the war had changed with the commander and that his enemy’s moves would be impulsive rather than cautious. However, Hannibal himself now made a move that was hard to credit when the enemy were quite near: he sent a third of his men on a foraging expedition, keeping two thirds in camp. He then moved the camp itself closer to the enemy, to a hillock visible to them about two miles from Gereonium, so they would know that he was ready to protect his foragers should they come under attack.
