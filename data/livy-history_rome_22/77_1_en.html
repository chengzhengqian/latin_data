50. Such was the battle of Cannae. Its fame rivals that of the disaster at the Allia,160 and while it was less critical in its later impact (since the enemy stalled thereafter), it was in casualties for the army even more serious and appalling. For while the flight at the Allia meant the loss of the city, it still saved the army; at Cannae there were barely fifty men with the fleeing consul, and almost the entire army remained with the other one who lost his life.

In both Roman camps there was now a poorly armed crowd of leaderless men, and those in the larger camp sent a message to the smaller urging their comrades to join them while the enemy, exhausted from the battle and the jubilant feasting that would follow, would at night be deep in sleep. They would then leave for Canusium161 in a single column, they said. Some rejected the suggestion out of hand, asking why those issuing the invitation could not come themselves, that being just as satisfactory a way to join up.
