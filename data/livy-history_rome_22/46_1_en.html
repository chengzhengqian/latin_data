There was a hill between Minucius’ camp and that of the Carthaginians and whoever took it first would clearly make his enemy’s position less secure. Hannibal did not so much want to take it without a fight (though that in itself was a worthwhile objective) as to use it to bring on a battle with Minucius, since he well knew that the man would always sally forth to stop him.

All the terrain between the two forces seemed at first sight to be of no use for laying a trap: it had not only no woodland but even no covering of bushes. But for that very reason it was just the spot for concealing an ambush as there could be no fear of such a trap in a bare valley; and there were, in fact, rocky hollows in its ravines, some able to hold two hundred men. Five thousand infantry and cavalry were concealed in these hiding places, distributed according to the number each hollow could conveniently hold. However, to prevent the movement of someone carelessly stepping out or a glint of armor revealing the trap in such an open valley, Hannibal diverted his enemy’s attention by sending a few men out at dawn to take the hill I mentioned above.

At the very first sight of them there was only contempt for their paltry numbers and everyone demanded for himself the task of dislodging the enemy and taking the position; and then the leader himself, one of the most foolish and reckless of them all, called the men to arms and assailed his enemy with empty threats.
