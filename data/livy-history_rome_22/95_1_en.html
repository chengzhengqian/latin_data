BOOK XXII

“But why am I accusing those people of cowardice when I can accuse them of a crime? Not only did they refuse to follow a man giving them sound advice but they actually tried to stand in his way and hold him back—and would have but for some very courageous men drawing their swords and pushing the cowards aside. Publius Sempronius, I tell you, had to break through a column of his countrymen before breaking through the enemy column! Will our country miss such citizens? Had the rest been like that she would today have not one citizen left of all those who fought at Cannae.

“Out of seven thousand soldiers, six hundred were found with the pluck to break out, men who returned to their native land free and still armed; and these six hundred faced no resistance from the enemy. How safe a passage do you think a column comprising almost two legions would have had? Members of the senate, you would today have twenty thousand brave and loyal soldiers in service at Canusium. As it is, however, how can these men be good and loyal citizens (for ‘brave’ is a word not even they would apply to themselves)? Unless one can really believe that they had helped their comrades191 with the breakout, when in fact they tried to stop them making it! Or unless one can believe they do not begrudge their comrades the safety and renown they earned by their valor, when they know that it was their fear and cowardice that brought about their degrading captivity! They preferred to skulk in their tents awaiting the arrival of dawn and the enemy, though they had the chance to break out in the still of the night.
