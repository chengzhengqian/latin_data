BOOK XXII

Both sides drove straight ahead but, as horses came to a halt and were then massed together, rider grappled with rider, each trying to drag the other from his mount. For the most part it had now become fighting on foot; but it was more ferocious than long-lived, and the Roman cavalry, driven back, turned to flight.

As the cavalry engagement was ending the infantry battle got under way,154 fought at first with equal strength and equal confidence by the two sides as long as the Gallic and Spanish ranks remained unbroken. But these stood ahead of the rest of the line in a wedge formation that was too thin and thus lacked strength, and while the Roman front equaled theirs in length it was also densely ordered; and so, after persistent and frequent efforts, it eventually drove back the enemy. Then, when their enemy was repelled and retreated in confusion, the Romans forged ahead and in one surge drove through the terror-stricken column of fleeing men and into the Carthaginian center. Finally, meeting no resistance, they reached the African reserves, who had taken up a position on the two wings. The wings had drawn back from the main body on both sides, while the center, formed of Gauls and Spaniards, protruded some way ahead of them. This convex formation had now been driven back, first of all straightening out the Carthaginian front and then, as their retreat continued, even forming a depression in the center. The Africans had now completed the crescent formation at both ends and, as the Romans charged recklessly into the center, they outflanked them and soon extended the crescent to enclose them to their rear as well.
