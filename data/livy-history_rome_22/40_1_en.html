The following day, contemptuous of their small numbers, the Romans dislodged them and transferred their own camp to the spot. Thus, there was only a short distance96 between one rampart and the other, and that space itself had been almost entirely filled by the Roman troops; and at the same time cavalry and light infantry sent forth from the rear of the camp against the Carthaginian foragers killed or chased off the scattered enemy over a wide area. Hannibal, however, did not dare commit himself to pitched battle because, with numbers so small, he could barely defend the camp if it came under attack. And now he proceeded to fight the war with Fabian tactics, sitting and waiting,†97 and he had drawn his troops back to their earlier camp, which lay before the walls of Gereonium.

Some sources also have it that a pitched battle, with lines drawn up, was fought that day. They claim that the Carthaginians were driven in disarray right back to their camp at the first clash but subsequently counterattacked and suddenly terrified the Romans in turn, but that the fortunes of the battle were then restored by the arrival of the Samnite Numerius Decimus.
