There, in the open, he established camp, where he himself would take up position with only his African and Spanish troops. The Balearic troops and the other light infantry he led around behind the hills; and the cavalry he stationed right at the entry to the defile, where some hillocks conveniently provided cover so that after the Romans entered they would, with the cavalry blocking them, be entirely shut in between the lake and the mountains.

Flaminius reached the lake at the sunset of one day, and on the next, when it was barely light, he went through the defile without reconnoitering. When the column began to spread out as it reached the more open terrain, all he saw of the enemy was what was right before him; the ambush to his rear and above his head escaped his notice. Now that the Carthaginian had his enemy where he had wanted him, squeezed between lake and hills and surrounded by his troops, he gave all his units the signal for a simultaneous attack. They charged down, each taking his shortest route to the enemy, the attack being all the more surprising and unexpected for the Romans because of a mist that had risen from the lake and settled more densely on the level ground than on the heights. The enemy columns had been quite visible to each other from the various hills and had thus more accurately synchronized their downward charge.
