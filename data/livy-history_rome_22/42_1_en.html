And so, for heaven’s sake, he continued, it was not just Samnium that had been totally devastated, its territory ceded to the Carthaginians as if it lay the other side of the Ebro, but so, too, had the territory of Campania, Cales and Falernum, while the dictator sat in Casilinum, defending his own lands101 with the legions of the Roman People. An army eager to fight and the master of horse had been virtually kept imprisoned within the rampart; they had been deprived of weapons, like enemy prisoners. At last, when the dictator left, they had emerged from behind their rampart like men delivered from a siege and had driven back and routed the enemy. In view of which, added Metilius, he would have confidently proposed the annulment of Quintus Fabius’ imperium—if the Roman plebs still had its mettle of days gone by. As it was he would bring forward only a modest bill to give the master of horse and the dictator equal constitutional powers. That notwithstanding, he said, Quintus Fabius must not be sent back to his army until he had seen to the election of a replacement consul for Gaius Flaminius.

The dictator avoided assemblies of the people on a policy that was very unpopular. Not even in the senate would he receive an impartial hearing when he paid tribute to his enemy, blamed defeats suffered over the past two years on commanders’ recklessness and incompetence, and said that the master of horse should be called to account for having engaged against his orders.
