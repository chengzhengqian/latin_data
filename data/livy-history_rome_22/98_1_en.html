One may be surprised at the discrepancy between these accounts; less easy is telling where the truth lies.

How much greater this defeat was than earlier ones is indicated by the fact that the loyalty of the allies, which up to that day had remained unshaken, now began to waver, for no other reason, clearly, than loss of hope for the empire. The following peoples defected to the Carthaginians: the Atellani, the Calatini, the Hirpini, some of the Apulians, all the Samnites but the Pentri, all the Bruttii, and the Lucanians. Beside these there were also the Uzentini, almost all the Greeks on the coast (the peoples of Tarentum, Metapontum, Croton and Locri193) and all the Cisalpine Gauls. And yet these defeats and allied defections prompted no talk of peace anywhere among the Romans either before the consul’s arrival in Rome or after he returned and brought back to their minds the disaster they had suffered. Such was the strength of character of the citizenry at that very time that, on his return from the debacle for which he was primarily responsible, people of all classes streamed out to meet him and thanked him for not having lost hope for the republic. Had he been a Carthaginian leader there is no manner of punishment that he would not have faced.
