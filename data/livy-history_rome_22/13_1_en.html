Eventually, not long before sunset, the praetor Marcus Pomponius declared: “We have been defeated in a great battle.” And though no further details were forthcoming from him, they were fully supplied with snippets of gossip from each other and took home the news that the consul and most of his troops had been killed and that there were few survivors, men who had either scattered in flight throughout Etruria or been captured by the enemy.

The anxieties besetting the minds of those whose relatives had been serving under the consul Gaius Flaminius were as numerous as the defeated army’s misfortunes; for they were ignorant of their loved ones’ fate, and no one knew what to hope for or what to fear. The next day, and for a number of days thereafter, a crowd stood at the gates—almost more women than men—waiting for one of their relatives or news of them. They would crowd around people they met, plying them with questions, and could not be torn away, especially from acquaintances, until they had fully interrogated them on every detail. After that, one might have seen the different expressions on their faces as they left their informants, depending on whether the news each received was good or bad, and could also have seen people gathering round them as they went home, offering congratulations or consolation. In the case of the women, displays both of joy and of sorrow were especially striking. They say that one, right at the city gate, suddenly met a son who returned safely and expired in his arms. A second, having received an erroneous report of her son’s death, sat grieving at home; but when the son returned she died from excessive joy at the first sight of him.
