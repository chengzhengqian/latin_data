And so, on the day of his first meeting with Quintus Fabius, he told him that what needed to be established first of all was how to put their shared command into effect. The best idea, in his opinion, was for each to have absolute authority and power in turn, either on alternate days or, if longer periods seemed preferable, for apportioned blocks of time. In this way they would be equal not only in strategy but also in strength in facing the enemy, if given the opportunity for action.

Quintus Fabius did not like this at all: everything would depend on the luck that came his headstrong colleague’s way. His imperium had been divided between himself and another but had not been taken from him; and he would therefore never willingly relinquish whatever discretion in directing the campaign he could still retain. He would not agree to a division of command shared with him by periods or days, but he would divide the army and with his own strategy save what he could, since he was not permitted to save everything. He thus gained the division of the legions between them, as was the normal practice for consuls. The first and fourth fell to Minucius, and the second and third to Fabius; and they similarly split equally the cavalry and the auxiliary troops of the allies and those with Latin status. The master of horse also wanted them to be in separate camps.
