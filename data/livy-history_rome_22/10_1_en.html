But it was not the usual ordered engagement fought with principes, hastati and triarii,28 or with the men of the front rank fighting before the standards and the rest behind, or with soldiers taking their places in their own legion, cohort or maniple.29 Chance was throwing them together and individual courage determining whether each man stood in front or behind in the fight. So whetted was their spirit and so focused their attention on the fight that, even though there was an earthquake that demolished large areas of many Italian cities, turned swift streams from their course, brought tidal waters up rivers, and leveled mountains with huge landslides, none of the combatants noticed it.30

6. The fighting continued for some three hours and was savage everywhere; but around the consul the battle was even more fierce and violent. He had his strongest troops with him and was himself energetically bringing assistance at any point where he had noticed his men under pressure and in difficulties. Being conspicuous in his armor, the enemy were furiously attacking him and his own men just as furiously defending him until an Insubrian31 horseman named Ducarius recognized the consul also by his face. “Look,” he said to his countrymen, “Here’s the man who cut our legions to pieces and pillaged our lands and city!
