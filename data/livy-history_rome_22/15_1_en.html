9. Hannibal marched straight through Umbria as far as Spoletium.38 After wreaking havoc on the countryside, he then proceeded with an assault on the city, but was repulsed with heavy losses. Assessing from the strength of a single colony attacked with little success the difficulty presented by the city of Rome, he then turned off into the territory of Picenum,39 an area not only rich in all manner of produce but also replete with spoils, which Hannibal’s rapacious and impoverished troops began to seize without restraint. There he maintained a stationary camp for several days and his soldiers recovered from the rigors of their winter marches, from their trek through the swamps, and from the battle, which, successful though it proved to be, had been no minor or easy encounter. When they had been given sufficient rest—although they actually enjoyed looting and pillaging more than inactivity and rest—he pressed on and laid waste the land of the Praetutii and Hadria,40 and after that the territory of the Marsi, the Marrucini and the Paeligni, and the area of Apulia closest to Arpi and Luceria.41

The consul Gnaeus Servilius had fought some minor battles with the Gauls and taken one insignificant town; and when he heard of his colleague’s death and the destruction of his army, he now feared for his native city’s defenses and headed for Rome so as not to be absent in her hour of extreme danger.
