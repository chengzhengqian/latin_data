Statilius rode up to the camp gates, told the rest of his men to remain outside the fortifications, and passed beyond the palisade with two cavalrymen. After carefully examining the whole camp, he reported that it had to be a trap: the fires that had been left alight were on the side of the camp facing their Roman enemy, the tents were open and all their precious objects left exposed to view, and in places he had seen silver carelessly strewn about in the laneways147 as if inviting plunder.

This report, which was made to discourage their rapacity, only served to excite them, and when the cry went up from the men that unless the signal were given they would go without their leaders, a leader was certainly not lacking—for Varro immediately gave the signal to advance. Paullus was hesitant in any case, but when in addition the chickens also refused their approval,148 he ordered the news to be taken to his colleague, who was already marching the troops out through the camp gate. Although Varro was angry, Flaminius’ recent disaster and the consul Claudius’ famous calamity at sea in the first Punic war149 filled him with religious fears.
