BOOK XXII

Moors! We who recently protested against the blockade of Saguntum, appealing not only to men but also to treaties and the gods—we are now happily watching Hannibal scale the walls of a Roman colony! The smoke from burning farms and fields comes wafting into our eyes and faces; our ears ring with the weeping and wailing of our allies, who invoke our aid more often than they do heaven’s. And here we are, hidden in clouds and forests, leading an army like a flock of sheep through summer pastures and along remote trails.

“Imagine if this was how Marcus Furius72 had proposed to recover the city from the Gauls—by trailing over hilltops and mountain passes, the way this latter-day Camillus, this superlative dictator that we have chosen in our hour of need, is planning to recover Italy from Hannibal! In that case, Rome would now be in the hands of the Gauls—Rome, which many times our ancestors saved, though I fear that through this delaying of ours they did so only for Hannibal and the Carthaginians! But Camillus was a man and truly a Roman who, on the day that news was brought to Veii that he had been appointed dictator by the authority of the senate and order of the people, came down into the plain (although the Janiculum was high enough for him to sit and watch his enemy!) and that very day cut down the Gallic legions in the city center—the site now of Gallic graves73—and the following day cut them down this side of Gabii.
