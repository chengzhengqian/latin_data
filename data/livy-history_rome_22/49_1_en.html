BOOK XXII

30. The signal given, the call for breaking camp108 went up. They set off and proceeded in marching order to the dictator’s encampment, taking the dictator himself and all around him by surprise. When they came to a halt before the tribunal,109 the master of horse marched forward ahead of the others and addressed Fabius with the title “father,” and his entire army saluted as “patrons” those of the dictator’s men who were around him. “I have just made you equal to my parents in the name I applied to you, Dictator,” said Minucius, “the best I can do with language, but to them I owe only my life whereas to you I owe both my own salvation and these men’s, too. And so, that decree of the people,110 onerous to me rather than an honor, I am the first to reject and repeal. I put myself once more under your command and auspices111 and restore to you these standards and legions, praying that this might turn out well for you, for me and for these armies of yours, the one rescued and the rescuer alike. Please forgive us, and order me to hold the position of master of horse and these men to retain their various ranks.”

Handshakes followed, and at the meeting’s conclusion the soldiers received warm and cordial hospitality from acquaintances and strangers alike; and so a festive day emerged from one that shortly before had been particularly dismal and almost under a curse.

When news of the episode was brought to Rome and then corroborated not only by correspondence from the two commanders but also by the rank and file in both armies, everybody now praised Maximus to the skies.
