Everybody, whether in Rome or in the army, and Fabius’ friends and foes alike, took the passing of the bill as a deliberate slap in the face for him—everybody but the dictator himself. He bore the injustice of the people’s anger with him with the same sangfroid as he had the slanderous remarks his enemies made against him before the masses. He received the senate’s letter informing him of the equal division of the command when he was still on the road but, confident that there was no equalization of a commander’s skill along with the equalization of authority, he returned to his army with his spirit unbroken either by his fellow citizens or by the enemy.

27. Minucius’ success and his support from the masses had already made him quite insufferable, but he now lost all restraint, boasting impudently and arrogantly that Quintus Fabius had, no less than Hannibal, come off second best to him. Fabius, he said, had been chosen in difficult circumstances as a sole commander who could be a match for Hannibal, but now the superior officer had by order of the people been put on a level with the junior, the dictator on a level with the master of horse. This was something unparalleled in history, and had happened in the same society105 in which masters of the horse would tremble and shake at the sight of a dictator’s rods and axes.
