14. When Hannibal’s camp was pitched at the River Volturnus69 and the most beautiful countryside of Italy was being put to the torch, with smoke rising everywhere from burning farmhouses, Fabius was marching along the ridges of Mt. Massicus70 when mutiny almost flared up again. For a few days the men had been silent because they had believed that, since the column had been taken along at a faster than usual pace, the aim of their rather speedy advance was to keep Campania from enemy depredations. But when they came to the end of the heights of Mt. Massicus and before their eyes the enemy were putting the torch to the buildings in the Falernian fields and those of the colonists at Sinuessa, and there was still no word of combat, Minucius asked, “Have we come here as spectators—to enjoy the sight of our allies being butchered and their property burned? Even if we feel no shame before anyone else, do we not feel it before these fellow citizens of ours, men whom our fathers sent to Sinuessa ascolonists71 to make this area secure from the Samnite foe? Now it is not the neighboring Samnite putting it to the torch but a Carthaginian foreigner who has made his way here from the very ends of the earth thanks to our indecision and inertia. Ah, is this how far we fall short of our forefathers?
