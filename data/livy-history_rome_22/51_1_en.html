The consul himself set off on foot through Sicily and crossed to Italy by the strait,116 since he and his colleague Marcus Atilius had been sent for in a dispatch from Quintus Fabius to take over the dictator’s armies as his six-month imperium had almost expired.

The annalistic accounts nearly all record Fabius’ position as that of dictator for his campaign against Hannibal; and Coelius117 also recounts that he was the first dictator appointed by the people. However, Coelius and the others have lost sight of the fact that the right to appoint a dictator lay with the one surviving consul, Gnaeus Servilius, who was at that time far off in his province of Gaul. Panic-stricken after the third defeat it had suffered, the state could not brook the delay this would involve and resorted to the measure of having a provisional dictator appointed by the people. Subsequently, Fabius’ achievements and outstanding reputation, plus the additions his descendants made to the inscription on his bust,118 all made it easy to believe that a man who had been made a provisional dictator had actually been dictator.

32. The consuls Atilius and Geminus Servilius assumed command of the armies of Fabius and Minucius respectively. They constructed winter quarters early and, in complete harmony, spent what remained of the autumn conducting the war with Fabius’ tactics.
