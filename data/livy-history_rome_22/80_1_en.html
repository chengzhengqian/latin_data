52. After spending most of the day gathering up spoils, Hannibal led an attack on the smaller camp and first of all cut its occupants off from the river by constructing a barricade; but as the men were all also fatigued from their efforts, sleep deprivation and wounds, their capitulation came even sooner than he had expected. They negotiated terms whereby they surrendered their weapons and horses and fixed a ransom price of three hundred quadrigati163 per head for Romans, two hundred for allies and one hundred for slaves, with each allowed to leave with one article of clothing once payment was made. They then admitted the enemy into their camp and were all put under guard, citizens and allies in separate groups.

While time was being frittered away there, a number with sufficient strength and courage—about four thousand infantrymen and two hundred cavalrymen—had left the larger camp and escaped to Canusium, some in a body and others randomly drifting through the fields, which proved no less safe. The camp itself was surrendered to the enemy on the same terms as the other by the wounded and fainthearted.164

The spoils taken were enormous, and apart from horses, prisoners and such silver as there was (mostly in horse trappings, as they used very little silverware at table, at least on campaign), everything else was given over for plunder.
