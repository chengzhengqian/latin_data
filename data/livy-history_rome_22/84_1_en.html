BOOK XXII

There is surely no other people that would not have been crushed by such an overwhelming disaster. One might try comparing the defeat of the Carthaginians in the sea battle off the Aegates Islands, which broke their resolve and made them abandon Sicily and Sardinia and accept taxpaying and tributary status.170 Or perhaps one might think of the defeat later sustained in Africa by Hannibal himself. In no way can these be compared with Cannae—except to say that they were borne with less strength of character.

55. The praetors Publius Furius Philus and Marcus Pomponius summoned the senate to the Curia Hostilia171 to discuss defensive measures for the city; for they had no doubt that, the armies having been destroyed, the enemy would come to attack Rome, which was all that remained for him to do in the war. Facing a critical situation whose gravity was still unknown, they could not even come up with a plan, while in the meantime their ears were ringing with the noisy lamentations of women and, with the truth not yet known, mourning was going on for the living and the dead alike in almost every house. At this point Quintus Fabius Maximus proposed sending some light cavalry along the Appian and Latin Ways to make inquiries of people they met—some were sure to be men who had scattered after the rout—and report back on what had befallen the consuls and their armies.
