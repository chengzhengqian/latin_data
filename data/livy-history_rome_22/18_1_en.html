When the vows had been duly formulated a period of supplication51 was proclaimed, and to the supplication went not only the urban populace with their wives and children but also such country people who, possessing some property, consequently took some thought for the public good. Then a three-day lectisternium was held under the supervision of the decemvirs for sacrifices. Six couches were on display: one for Jupiter and Juno, a second for Neptune and Minerva, a third for Mars and Venus, a fourth for Apollo and Diana, a fifth for Vulcan and Vesta and the sixth for Mercury and Ceres. Vows of temples were then made. The dictator Quintus Fabius Maximus made a vow of a temple to Venus of Eryx because it was prescribed by the Books of Fate52 that the vow be formulated by the man holding the highest office in the state; and the praetor Titus Otacilius53 made a vow of the temple to Mens.

11. Religious observances thus completed, the dictator next opened discussion of the war and the state of the republic, seeking the senators’ opinion on which and how many legions they should use to face their victorious foe. It was decided that he should take over the army of the consul Gnaeus Servilius as well as enlist from the citizens and allies as much cavalry and infantry as he thought appropriate; and he was to take all other measures and decisions that he considered to be in the state’s interest.
