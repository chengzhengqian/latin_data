BOOK XXII

The military tribune Publius Sempronius Tuditanus then declared: “So, you prefer to be taken by a greedy and ruthless foe, do you? To have a price set on your heads and payment demanded by men who ask you if you are a Roman citizen or a Latin ally, so that another may gain some honor from your being humiliated and taunted?162 No, you do not, at least not if you are fellow citizens of the consul Lucius Aemilius—who chose to die well rather than live in disgrace—and of all those courageous men who lie heaped about him. Before dawn comes upon us and larger troops of the enemy block our path, let us break through these men clamoring in chaotic disorder before our gates. With a sword and enterprise a way can be found through the thickest enemy line. In fact, with a wedge formation you could scatter this sprawling and disorganized bunch as though nothing blocked your path. Go with me, then—those of you who want to save both yourselves and the republic!”

So saying, he drew his sword, formed up a wedge, and then marched through the enemy’s midst; and when the right flank was exposed and at this the Numidians were directing their javelins, the men transferred their shields to their right side and some six hundred made good their passage to the other camp. After that they were immediately joined by another large group and safely reached Canusium. This was done by defeated men, the result ofimpulse that came from each man’s character or pure chance; it was not planned by them or conducted under anyone’s command.
