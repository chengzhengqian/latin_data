BOOK XXII

In addition to these terrible disasters, the Romans were also alarmed by a number of prodigies and especially by the conviction that year of two Vestals, Opimia and Floronia, on charges of sexual misconduct. One had received the traditional penalty of being buried alive near the Colline Gate and the other had committed suicide. The man guilty of the misconduct with Floronia, Lucius Cantilius, was the secretary of a pontiff—one of the officers that they now call minor pontiffs176—and he was so badly flogged in the comitium by the Pontifex Maximus177 that he expired under the lash.

Occurring as it did along with all the other calamities, this piece of sacrilege was, as usual, interpreted as a portent. The decemvirs178 were therefore instructed to consult the books and Quintus Fabius Pictor179 was sent to the oracle in Delphi to find out with what prayers and acts of supplication they could appease the gods, and to ask what end there would be to their great disasters. Meanwhile a number of extraordinary sacrifices were conducted following instructions from the Books of Fate.180 These included a Gallic man and woman and a Greek man and woman being interred alive in the Forum Boarium, in a spot enclosed with stones that had already been stained by human sacrifice, which was very alien to Roman religious observance.181

When it was felt that enough had been done to placate the gods, Marcus Claudius Marcellus sent fifteen hundred soldiers—men whom he had under his command as recruits for the fleet—from Ostia to Rome for the city’s defense.
