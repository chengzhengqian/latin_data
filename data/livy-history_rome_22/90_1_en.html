We were surrounded and cut off from water by a victorious army and had no hope of breaking through the dense enemy lines; and since fifty thousand men from our army had been slaughtered, we thought it no crime for a few Roman soldiers to survive the battle of Cannae. So, the next day, we finally negotiated a price for our ransom and release and surrendered to the enemy the weapons that could help us no more.

“We had been told that our ancestors had also secured their release from the Gauls with gold185 and that your own fathers, though very much opposed to the peace terms, had nevertheless sent representatives to Tarentum to ransom prisoners of war.186 And yet both battles, at the Allia with the Gauls and at Heraclea with Pyrrhus, were notorious less for the losses incurred than the cowardly flight there, whereas the plains of Cannae are covered with piles of Roman corpses and we survived the battle only because the enemy ran out of both weapons and strength in cutting us down.

“Besides, there are in our number some who were not even involved in the fighting but were left to defend the camp and simply fell into the enemy’s hands when the camp was surrendered. Now I begrudge none of my fellow citizens or comrades-in-arms his good fortune or his circumstances, nor would I wish to promote myself by discrediting another. But consider those men who ran from the field, most without weapons, and did not stop until they reached Venusia or Canusium!
