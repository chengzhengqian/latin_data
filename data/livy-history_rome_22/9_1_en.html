5. Despite the chaos all round, the consul showed considerable composure in such a precarious situation.27 The ranks were in disarray as his men all turned toward the confused shouts, but he formed them up as well as time and the location permitted, and wherever he could go and could be heard he encouraged them and commanded them to stand and fight. The way out of there was not by prayers and petitioning the gods, he said, but by force and valor; a path could be made through the midst of the enemy lines with the sword, and in general less fear meant less danger.

But over the din and turmoil neither advice nor orders could be heard. So far from recognizing their own standards or their ranks and their places within them, they barely had the presence of mind to seize their armor and put it on for the fight, and some were cut down when their weapons proved more a burden than a protection. Furthermore, in such dense fog, ears were more useful than eyes. It was to sounds that they would turn their faces and eyes—to groaning from wounds being dealt, blows falling on bodies and armor, and mingled cries of confusion and panic. Some, in flight, would be brought to a halt running into a group still fighting; others, returning to the fray, were driven back by a crowd running off. Then when attempts to force a passage in any direction proved futile and they were hemmed in on the flanks by the hills and the lake and to front and rear by enemy troops, the only hope of survival appeared to lay in their own right hands and swords.
