BOOK XXII

12. Assuming command of the consul’s army from the legate Fulvius Flaccus, the dictator marched through Sabine territory to Tibur, where he had fixed the rendezvous for his new recruits. He then moved on to Praeneste and taking various byways emerged on the Latin Way.61 From there, reconnoitering the roads with the utmost care, he marched toward the enemy, determined to trust to luck at no point unless necessity dictated it.

On the day that Fabius first pitched camp, not far from Arpi62 and within sight of the enemy, the Carthaginian leader immediately led his troops out for action and offered battle. But when he saw everything still on the enemy side and no signs of activity in their camp, he returned to camp uttering disdainful comments: the Romans’ famed military spirit had at last been crushed, they were a conquered people and they had openly admitted they were no match for him in valor and glory! But a silent worry bothered him: his dealings would henceforth be with a commander completely unlike Flaminius or Sempronius, and the Romans, finally taught by hard experience, had chosen a leader that was Hannibal’s equal. He also felt immediate apprehension about the dictator’s wariness; but having not yet put the man’s determination to the test he proceeded to harass and provoke him, frequently moving camp and laying waste the allies’ lands before his eyes. At one moment he would accelerate his march and disappear from view and at the next he would suddenly halt, concealed at some curve in the road, hoping to catch him unawares if he came down into the plain.
