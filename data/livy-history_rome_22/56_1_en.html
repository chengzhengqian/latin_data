35. The plebeians were inflamed by such speeches. There were three patrician candidates, Publius Cornelius Merenda, Lucius Manlius Volso, and Marcus Aemilius Lepidus, and two plebeian candidates from already-ennobled families,132 Gaius Atilius Serranus and Quintus Aelius Paetus (one a pontifex, the other an augur133). But only one consul, Terentius Varro, was elected, so the elections for the appointment of his colleague were in his hands.

Discovering that those running against Varro had lacked the necessary electoral strength, the nobles now forced Lucius Aemilius Paullus to stand, though for a long time he steadfastly refused to do so. Paullus had served as consul with Marcus Livius and had been a bitter enemy of the plebs ever since the condemnation of his former colleague, an affair in which his own reputation almost went up in flames.134 On the next election day all those who had been in competition with Varro withdrew, and Paullus joined the consul as a rival to oppose him rather than as a colleague.

The praetorian elections were held next. Marcus Pomponius Matho and Publius Furius Philus were returned, and Philus was allotted the urban judicial administration and Pomponius jurisdiction between citizens and foreigners.
