The following day, in addition to everything else, they were also facing severe hunger; but when Maharbal, who had had overtaken them during the night with all his cavalry, promised that he would let them go with one article of clothing each if they surrendered their weapons, they capitulated. The promise was honored by Hannibal with typical Punic scruple32 and they were all clapped in irons.

7. Such was the famous battle of Trasimene; few disasters suffered by the people of Rome have been as memorable. Fifteen thousand Romans were killed in action; tenthousand scattered in flight throughout Etruria and headed for the city by various routes; two thousand, five hundred of the enemy died in the battle, and many others later succumbed to their wounds. Statistics for the fallen on both sides are many times greater in other authors; beyond avoiding idle exaggeration, to which historians are all too prone, I have also accepted Fabius33 as my main source since he was contemporary with this war.

Hannibal released captives of Latin status without ransom but Romans he put in irons; and after separating the corpses of his own men from the heaps of the enemy dead he ordered them buried. He also made a great effort to seek out Flaminius’ body for burial but did not find it.

In Rome, at the first news of the defeat, people rushed to the forum in sheer terror and panic. Married women roamed the streets and queried those they met about the unexpected disaster that had been reported and the fortunes of the army.
