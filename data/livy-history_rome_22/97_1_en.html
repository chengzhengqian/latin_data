When the grim reply came back that the prisoners were not to be ransomed, and fresh anguish was added to the old over the sacrifice of so many citizens, the people accompanied the delegates to the gate with floods of tears and lamentations. One of the delegates left for his home, believing he had released himself from his oath by his deceitful return to the camp. When this became known and was reported the senate, the members unanimously voted that the man be arrested and taken back to Hannibal with an official escort.

There is also another version of the story of the prisoners: ten of their delegates came first and, when doubts were expressed in the senate over whether or not they should be allowed into the city, they were allowed in only on condition that they not be granted a senatorial audience. Then, as they stayed longer than anyone expected, three further delegates arrived: Lucius Scribonius, Gaius Calpurnius and Lucius Manlius.192 It was only then that the motion to ransom the prisoners was finally put to the senate by a plebeian tribune who was a relative of Scribonius, and the senate voted against the ransom. The three recently-arrived delegates then returned to Hannibal but the ten earlier ones remained behind.
