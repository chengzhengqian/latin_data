“Hannibal, by contrast, is in a strange and hostile environment, facing opposition and peril on every side, far from his home and country. For him there is no peace either on land or sea; there are no cities, no protective walls to welcome him; he sees nothing anywhere that is his. He lives on what he pillages day by day, and he has barely a third of the army that he took across the River Ebro. More have died from starvation than in battle; and for the few that remain food is in short supply. Can you doubt, then, that it is by inaction that we shall overcome a man who grows weaker by the day and has no provisions, no reinforcements and no money? How long has he been sitting before the walls of Gereonium,142 a feeble Apulian town, as though he were before the walls of Carthage? But I shall not brag about my own success, not even before you; just see how Servilius and Atilius, the last consuls, toyed with him!

“This is the one road to safety, Lucius Paullus, and it will be your fellow citizens rather than your enemy who will make it difficult and treacherous for you. For your soldiers will want the same thing as the enemy’s, and the Roman consul Varro will hanker after the same thing as the Carthaginian Hannibal. You must on your own resist two generals. But resist them you will if you stand quite firm in the face of people’s gossip and tittle-tattle, and if neither your colleague’s hollow reputation nor your own ill-deserved bad name will shake your resolve.
