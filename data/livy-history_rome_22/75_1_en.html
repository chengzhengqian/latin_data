BOOK XXII

49. In the other sector of the fighting Paullus had been seriously wounded by a projectile from a sling at the very start of the engagement, but even so by making frequent charges against Hannibal with his men in close formation he restored the battle at several points. Roman horsemen were giving him cover, but eventually they abandoned their mounts because the consul was losing the strength even to control his horse. Then, when someone informed Hannibal that the consul had ordered his cavalrymen to dismount, they say Hannibal commented: “I would have preferred him to hand them over to me in irons!”156 The cavalry now being on foot, their fighting was as might be expected when the enemy victory was no longer in doubt: though defeated, they preferred to die where they stood rather than flee, and the victors, furious with them for delaying their victory, massacred those they could not dislodge. They did, however, drive off a few survivors, men exhausted from their efforts and wounds. The rest all scattered, and those who could were chasing after their horses to make their escape.

The military tribune Gnaeus Lentulus was riding by when he saw the consul, covered with blood, sitting on a rock. “Lucius Aemilius,” he said, “the only person on whom the gods look with favor as being free of blame for today’s debacle, take this horse while you still have some strength. I can remain at your side, raise you up and protect you. Do not add tragedy to this battle with the death of a consul—even without that there are enough tears and grief!”
