The senators felt it more appropriate that a dictator be appointed by one of the consuls for holding the elections.127 Lucius Veturius Philo was appointed, and he appointed Marcus Pomponius Matho as his master of horse. But there was an irregularity in the appointment process, they were ordered to resign their posts after thirteen days, and the state reverted to an interregnum.128

34. The consuls had their imperium prorogued for a year. The interreges appointed by the senators were Gaius Claudius Cento, son of Appius, and then Publius Cornelius Asina. The elections were held during the latter’s interregnum amid bitter antagonism between the senatorial party129 and the plebeians.

The plebeians were striving to raise to the consulship Gaius Terentius Varro, a man of their own class who had ingratiated himself with them by attacking leading citizens and by his crowd-pleasing methods. Indeed, thanks to the attack he had mounted on Quintus Fabius’ influence and his authority as dictator, Varro had gained some distinction through another man’s unpopularity. The senators were making every effort to block him; they wanted to prevent it becoming a common occurrence for people to rise to their level by scurrilous attacks on them.
