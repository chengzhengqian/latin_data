8. Before any firm plans could be formulated, news suddenly arrived of another reverse: four thousand cavalry sent by the consul Servilius to his colleague under the command of the propraetor Gaius Centenius had changed course after hearing about the battle of Trasimene and been cut off in Umbria by Hannibal. News of the event produced different reactions in people. Some, their hearts gripped by a greater anguish, thought the recent loss of cavalry trivial compared with the earlier losses. Others would not consider what had happened in isolation, saying that when a body was sick any upset, however slight, was felt more deeply than a graver one when in good health; and likewise, when the commonwealth was ailing and weakened, any stroke of misfortune should be measured not on an absolute scale but with reference to its diminished capacity, which could tolerate no further aggravation.

As a result, the citizen body resorted to a measure longneither needed nor employed, namely the naming ofa dictator. Because the consul35 was absent from the cityand it was thought that only by him could a dictator be named, and since sending a messenger or letter throughItaly, now occupied by the Carthaginian army, was no easy matter, the people took upon themselves thehitherto unprecedented step of appointing a dictator.
