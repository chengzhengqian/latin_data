That happened to be the day on which Minucius had rejoined Fabius. He had been earlier sent by the dictator to secure with a garrison the pass that contracts to a narrow gorge above Tarracina and overlooks the sea—so that the Carthaginian could not have access to Roman territory from Sinuessa by the Appian Way.78 The armies now joined up, and the dictator and his master of horse brought their camp down to the road that Hannibal was going to take. The enemy was two miles away.

16. The next day the Carthaginians filled the entire road between the two camps with their column. Although the Romans had stationed themselves right at the foot of their own rampart, clearly in a more favorable position, the Carthaginian none the less moved forward with his light infantry and cavalry to challenge his enemy. The Carthaginians made sporadic attacks, charging and then retreating, but the Roman line remained in position. It was a sluggish battle, more to the dictator’s liking than Hannibal’s. Two hundred fell on the Roman side, and eight hundred of the enemy.

Hannibal then appeared to be cut off since the road to Casilinum was blocked. Capua, Samnium and large numbers of produce-rich allies to their rear could provision the Romans, while the Carthaginians would be wintering amid the rocks of Formiae and the sands and swamps of Liternum,79 and throughout inhospitable woodlands; and it did not escape Hannibal’s notice that his own strategy was being used against him.
