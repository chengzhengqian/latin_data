38. The troop levy completed, the consuls waited a few days for the soldiers from the allies and men of Latin status to arrive. Then the oath was administered to the enlisted men by the military tribunes, something hitherto unprecedented; for until that day there had been nothing more than the oath the men took to mobilize when ordered by the consuls and not to disband without their order. When they had mobilized for division into decuries or centuries, the cavalry (by decuries) and the infantry (by centuries)139 would simply swear an informal oath among themselves not to leave or break ranks for flight or from fear except to fetch or retrieve a weapon, to strike an enemy, or to rescue a fellow citizen. This discretionary pact, made among the men themselves, was now superseded by an official administering of the oath by the tribunes.

Before the troops marched from the city there were many arrogant addresses from the consul Varro. The war. he claimed, had been brought on Italy by the nobility and would remain stuck in the vitals of the state if it had more generals like Fabius, whereas he would end it on the day he set eyes on the enemy. From Varro’s colleague Paullus there was only one speech, made on the day before he left the city, and it had more truth to it than popular appeal.
