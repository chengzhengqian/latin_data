Next with well-worked clay mixed with chaff daub the whole of the wound and the space between the grafts so that the scions project two inches, and put moss on the top of the clay and bind it on so that rain may not penetrate. If the tree which you wish to engraft is small,6 cut it off near the ground so that it projects a foot and a half above the soil; then, after cutting it down, carefully smooth the wound and split the stock in the middle a little way with a sharp knife, so that there is a cleft three inches deep in it. Then insert a wedge in this cleft as far as it will allow and thrust down into it scions which have been pared away on both sides in such a way as to make the bark of the scion exactly meet the bark of the tree. When you have carefully fitted in the scions, pull out the wedge; then bind and daub the tree in the manner described above. Next heap up earth round the tree right up to the graft itself; this will give the best protection from wind and heat.
