We shall also make distinctions of taste as follows:20 from that part of the field which displeases us most, clods should be dug and soaked in an earthen vessel, then thoroughly mixed with fresh water and, after careful straining in the manner of dreggy wine, examined by tasting;c for, whatever is the taste transmitted from the clods to the water, such we shall take to be the taste of that soil. But, apart from this experiment, there are many signs which show that ground is sweet and suitable for grain—for example, the rush, the reed, grass, trefoil, the dwarf-elder, bramble bushes, wild plums, and many other things which are well known also to searchers for springs,d and which are not nourished except by veins of sweet water in the ground. And we should not be content21 with the first appearance of surface soil, but should take pains to investigate the character of what lies beneath—whether it is earthy or not.
