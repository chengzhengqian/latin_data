My hopes rose, as I expect yours did as well, from the report that gloom reigned in Antonius’ house and that his wife was in tears. Here too I saw his backers, whose faces my eyes keep under constant observation, looking glum. If3 this is not the case, why the mention of peace by Piso and Calenus of all people, why at this time, why so unexpectedly and suddenly? Piso says he knows nothing, has heard nothing. Calenus says that nothing new has come his way. They make these denials now, after they think we have entangled ourselves in a peacemaking embassy. Well, where is the need for a new policy, if there is nothing whatever new in the circumstances?

[2] We have been misled, Members of the Senate, misled, I say. Antonius’ friends have pleaded his cause, not that of our country. I saw it indeed, but I saw it through a fog as it were; my vision was blurred by my anxiety for Decimus Brutus. If the giving of substitutes were customary in war, I would gladly let myself be shut in instead of him so as to secure Decimus Brutus’ release. Moreover, we4 were taken in by these words of Quintus Fufius: “Shall we not listen to Antonius even if he withdraws from Mutina, even if he promises to be under the senate’s control?” It seemed obstinate of us; consequently we softened, we gave in. Well, does he withdraw from Mutina? “I don’t know,” is the reply.
