May it blow in all its fury, till it bends the tops of our masts, and drive us all the way to the cities of Greece; else Pompey’s sailors, issuing from all the coast of Phaeacia,1 may overtake our flagging sails by the stroke of their oars.2 Cut the cables which detain our victorious prows; we have long been wasting the chance given us by cloudy skies and angry waves.”

The sun sank beneath the sea, the first stars had come out in the sky, and the moon had begun to throw shadows of her own, when they cast loose their ships all together. The ropes shook out the sails at full stretch; the sailors bent the yards and slanted the canvas, keeping the sheet to the left, and spread the high topsails to catch the breeze that would otherwise be lost. Hardly had the light air begun to drive the sails till they puffed out a little, when they fell back on the mast and drooped towards the centre of the ship; and, when land was left behind, the very breeze that had carried them could not keep pace with the vessels. The sea lay motionless; chained in dead calm, the waves had less movement than a stagnant pool.—Thus the Bosporus lies idle and binds the Northern Sea, when the Danube, arrested by frost, no longer urges on the deep, and the vast sea is covered with ice; the water holds in a vice every ship it has grasped; the rider strikes the solid floor that no sail may traverse; and the wheel-track of the Bessian nomad furrows the Maeotian mere,3 while the surge groans beneath.
