She, scarce lifting her eyes and with modest look, replied: “O goddess (for I may not tell who thou art, and yet ’tis plain thou art a goddess), lead, oh, lead me on, and show me my husband’s face. If only the fates grant me but once to see him, then shall I say I have gained heaven indeed.” Straightway she fared along with Thaumas’ daughter to the hill of Romulus. There a star from high heaven came gliding down to earth, and Hersilia, her hair bursting into flame from its light, goes up together with the star into thin air. Her with dear, familiar hands Rome’s founder receives, and changes her mortal body and her old-time name. He calls her Hora, and now as goddess is she joined once more to her Quirinus.
