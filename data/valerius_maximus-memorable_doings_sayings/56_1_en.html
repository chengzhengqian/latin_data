Book I

And should we not take the following for Nature’s jests in human bodies? We can bear them, since there is no cruelty in them, but they too are to be numbered among wonders. The son of Prusias king of Bithynia,38 whose name was the same as his father’s, instead of an upper row of teeth had a single bone evenly extended. It was not unsightly nor in any way inconvenient to use.

The daughter of king Mithridates, Drypetine, child of queen Laodice, had a double row of teeth, very ugly. She accompanied her father’s flight after his defeat by Pompey.39

Deserving too of no small wonder are the eyes of a person who is known to have had such keen and sure vision that from Lilybaeum he saw fleets leaving Carthage harbour.40

More wonderful than his eyes is the heart of Aristomenes of Messene. Because of his extraordinary craftiness the Athenians cut it out and found it full of hair, having captured him after he had escaped by cunning from several earlier captivities.41

The poet Antipater of Sidon fell sick of a fever every year for one day only, the day of his birth. And having reached extreme old age, he died on his birthday of the disease as it came round in its regular course.42

At this point it is appropriate to recall the philosophers Polystratus and Hippoclides.43 They were born on the same day and followed the system of the same teacher
