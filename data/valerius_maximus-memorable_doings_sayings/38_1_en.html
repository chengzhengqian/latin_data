This too must not be wrapped in silence.8 Driven out of Rome by a gang of his enemies, M. Cicero was lodging for the night at a farm in the plain of Atina. There relaxed in sleep he thought he was wandering in a desert place, pathless regions, when C. Marius, wearing the insignia of the Consulship, met him and asked him why he was travelling uncertain of his route with so sad a countenance. Then, having heard the misfortune which afflicted him, he took Cicero’s right hand and gave him to his Proximate Lictor9 with orders to take him to Marius’ own monument, saying that hope of better things for him lay there. Nor did it turn out otherwise. For in the Marian temple of Jupiter10 the senate passed its decree for his return.

C. Gracchus11 was warned plainly and openly in a dream of the terrible fate in store for him. For deep in slumber he saw the likeness of his brother Tiberius, who told him that he could by no means avoid perishing by the same death as himself.
