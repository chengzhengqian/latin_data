Nor am I unaware how doubtful a view may be taken of any judgment concerning the motions or utterances of immortal gods perceived by human eyes and ears. But since I am saying nothing new but recalling things handed down, let the originators prove their credibility and let it be my part not to shy away from items consecrated by famous literary memorials as though they were mere fiction.

After mention of the city from which our community drew its origin, her auspicious offspring12 the divine Julius presents himself before us. C. Cassius, never to be named without prefix of public parricide, was standing firm and full of ardour at the battle of Philippi when he saw Caesar, majestic beyond human aspect, robed in a purple commander’s cloak, charging at him with threatening countenance and horse at the gallop. Terrified at the apparition, Cassius turned in flight from his enemy, first uttering these words: “What more is a man to do if killing be not enough?” No, Cassius, you had not killed Caesar, for no divinity can be extinguished; but by violating him while he was still in his mortal body you deserved to have the god thus hostile.13
