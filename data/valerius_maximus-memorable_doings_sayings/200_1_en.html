7. Of Friendship

Now let us look at the tie of friendship. Powerful it is and very strong, no whit inferior to the force of blood, even more reliable and safe in that the latter is contracted by the accident of birth, a work of chance, the former by will beginning in solid judgment. So one would more easily escape blame in turning away from a kinsman than from a friend, because the one severance is not necessarily open to the charge of injustice, whereas the other is necessarily open to that of levity. For since a man’s life will be deserted if not surrounded by the protection of any friendship, so needful a recourse ought not to be rashly taken up, but once properly grasped, ought not to be spurned.
