L. Reginus must be torn by the reproaches of posterity if he be judged by the canon of fidelity due to the public service; but if he be assessed according to faithful pledge of friendship, he must be left in the best of havens, a laudable conscience. As Tribune of the Plebs, mindful of an old and close friendship, he liberated from public custody Caepio, who had been thrown into prison because he was held to blame for the destruction of our army by the Cimbri and Teutones. And not content to play the friend so far, he also became Caepio’s companion in exile. Ah Friendship, mighty and insuperable is your strange power! From one side the commonwealth laid its grasp upon him, from the other your right hand drew him. The commonwealth demanded that he choose to be inviolable, you (so gently you exercise your authority!) condemn him to exile. He chose the punishment before the office.4
