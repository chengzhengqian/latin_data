Once more Ptolemy Physcon crops up, recently13 a foul example of lustful madness, but also to be mentioned among the prime revelations of cruelty. For what could be more brutal than this act of his? He had his acknowledged son by Cleopatra, both his sister and his wife, a handsome boy of the fairest promise, Memphites by name, put to death before his eyes and then sent his severed head and hands and feet covered with a mantle in a box as a birthday gift to his mother14—as though he himself had no share in the calamity that he was inflicting upon her, as though he was not the more unhappy of the two in that in their common bereavement he had made Cleopatra an object of compassion to all, himself an object of hate. Such is the blind madness in which the ultimate in cruelty boils over, finding its bulwark in itself. For since he perceived how much his country hated him, he sought a remedy for fear in crime, and so that he might reign more safely after massacring the populace he surrounded a gymnasium full of young men with arms and fire and killed all who were inside partly by steel, partly by the flames.
