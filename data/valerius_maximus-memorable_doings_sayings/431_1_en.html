He filled a space fenced in by high walls with ashes and placed above them a protruding beam on which he put his victims after plying them with food and drink. From it they fell in their sleep on to the treacherous heap.16

More open and abominable was the cruelty of the other Ochus, surnamed Artaxerxes,17 who buried his sister (also his mother-in-law) Atossa alive head downward and killed with darts his uncle along with more than a hundred sons and grandsons, left at his mercy in an empty space; not provoked by any injury but because he saw that they had a great name among the Persians for uprightness and bravery.

Prompted by a similar sort of jealousy the Athenian community by a decree unworthy of its glory cut off the thumbs of the young men of Aegina, so that her people, which had a powerful navy, should not be able to come to a contest of maritime strength with themselves.18 I do not recognize an Athens that would borrow a remedy for fear from cruelty.
