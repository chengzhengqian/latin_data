And while he there kept vigil beyond his bodily strength for gaining victory, his camp was captured by Brutus.2 What else then do we think but that by the work of divine power the head already destined to immortality felt no violence of Fortune unmeet for a celestial spirit?

Augustus now, besides his mind’s natural vigour in subtle perception of all matters, was admonished by a recent domestic example to comply with Artorius’ dream. For he had heard that Calpurnia, his father the divine Julius’ wife, on the last night3 her husband spent on earth saw him in her sleep lying in her lap prostrate with many wounds, and that badly frightened by the horror of the dream she kept on begging him to stay away from the senate house next day. But unwilling to let it be thought that he did so in alarm at a woman’s dream, he persisted in holding a senate, in which the hands of the parricides were raised against him.4 Between father and son no sort of comparison is to be made, especially as they are joined together on the summit of divinity; but the one had already raised for himself an access to heaven by his works, while a lengthy round of earthly achievements still remained for the other. Therefore the immortal gods chose that the former should only be made aware of the coming change in his condition, while the other should also postpone it, so that the one glory be given to heaven, the other promised.
