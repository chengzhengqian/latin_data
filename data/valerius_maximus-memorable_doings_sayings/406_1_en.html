Xenophilus of Chalcis, the Pythagorean, was two years younger but in good fortune not inferior; for as Aristoxenus the musician says, he died in the full brilliance of the most consummate learning, without any experience of human adversity.10

Arganthonius of Gades reigned for so long a period that to have lived that long would be more than enough to produce satiety. For he ruled his country for eighty years, having ascended the throne at the age of forty. For that fact there are reliable authorities. And Asinius Pollio, not the least part of Roman literature, relates in the third book of his histories that he completed a hundred and thirty years. Pollio himself was no mean example of vigorous longevity.11
