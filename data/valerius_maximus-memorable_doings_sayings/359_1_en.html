But the bond of procreation, the most binding between human beings, overbore a father’s wishes and a great man’s authority at the same time.2

C. Tettius was disinherited in infancy by his father, who had taken his mother Petronia in wedlock during her lifetime. The divine Augustus ordered him by his personal fiat to take possession of his father’s goods, acting in the spirit of the Fatherland’s father. For it was quite unjust of Tettius to cancel his name of father in the case of a son whom he had begotten as of personal right.3

Septicia too, mother of the Trachali of Ariminum,4 got angry with her sons and to insult them, being no longer capable of bearing children, she married Publicius, quite an old man, and also left both of them out of her will. They approached the divine Augustus, who reprobated both the woman’s marriage and her last dispositions.
