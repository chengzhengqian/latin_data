Strangers too received generous hospitality in both his town and his country residences, and were sent on their way the richer with various gifts. On one occasion he fed and clothed five hundred Geloan horsemen at once, driven by a storm onto his land. In short one might have called him no mortal man but the kindly pouch of propitious Fortune. So Gillias’ possessions were so to speak the common patrimony of all and for his welfare and aggrandisement not only the citizens of Agrigentum but the neighbouring regions too prayed through the nights. Place against this strongboxes barred with inexorable bolts. Would you not think Gillias’ expenditure somewhat preferable to such safe custody?7
