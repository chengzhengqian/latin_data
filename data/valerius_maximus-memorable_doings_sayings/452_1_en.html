The deed and saying of C. Fimbria is less atrocious, but considered in themselves both are of the most audacious. He had intended that Scaevola should be murdered at C. Marius’ funeral. Learning that he had recovered from his wound, Fimbria set about prosecuting him before the people. Then, asked what he was going to say against one whose blameless character could not be praised highly enough, he replied that he would charge him with receiving the weapon in his body too gingerly. Extravagance of madness, to be accompanied by the groans of the ailing commonwealth!2

As M. Cicero was talking in the senate about the fire started by L. Catilina, “I feel it,” said the latter, “and if I can’t put it out with water, I’ll do it with the falling house.”3 What are we to think but that driven by the goads of conscience he proved himself guilty of the parricide he had begun?

Magius Chilo’s mind too was confused by madness. An old friend of M. Marcellus and his companion in the Pompeian service, with his own hand he snatched away the life Caesar had granted, out of resentment that some other friends were preferred by Marcellus to himself. As Marcellus was returning to Rome from Mitylene, where he had betaken himself, in the harbour of Athens, Magius stabbed him with a dagger and went straight on to slaughter the provocations of his lunacy: an enemy of friendship, an interceptor of the divine benefaction, a sad defilement of a sacred public obligation so far as concerned the restoration of a most illustrious citizen.4
