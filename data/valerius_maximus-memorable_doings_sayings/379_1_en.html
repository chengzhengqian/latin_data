Likewise when Fulvius Flaccus was on trial, his slave Philippus, on whose evidence the whole case depended, was tortured eight times and uttered no word prejudicial to his master. Nevertheless the defendant was found guilty, although one man tortured eight times provided a more reliable proof of innocence than eight men tortured once would have furnished.3

5. Of Witnesses

I next proceed to examples pertaining to witnesses. Cn. and Q. Servilius Caepio, born of the same parents and advanced to the highest eminence by progress through all stages of official rank, likewise the brothers Metelli, Quintus and Lucius, ex-Consuls and ex-Censors, one of them a triumphator, spoke very strongly in evidence against Q. Pompeius, son of Aulus, accused of extortion. Pompeius was acquitted, not because they were disbelieved but to counter the impression that they had crushed an enemy by their influence.1
