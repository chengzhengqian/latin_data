XXVI. “I come next to the auspices and auguries which you have laboriously collected and cited, to prove retribution attending on neglect, and success upon observance. Clodius, you say, Flaminius, and2 Junius lost their armies because they refused to wait till the chickens ate heartily.a But what of Regulusb?3 Did he not observe the auguries? yet was he not taken captive? Mancinus did nothing irreligious, yet he passed under the yoke, and was surrendered to the enemy.c Paulus’s chickens were in good feed, yet at Cannaed he and the greater part of the republic were laid low. When auguries and auspices were4 adverse to his crossing into Africa before winter, Gaius Caesar disregarded theme: as the result both voyage and victory were made easy.

  • aSee ch. 7 and notes.
  • bM. Atilius Regulus, the typical patriot, who suffered defeat in the First Punic War, 255 b.c., and surrendered himself into the hands of the Carthaginians to meet his fate.
  • cC. Hostilius Mancinus, consul 137 b.c., was defeated before Numantia; the Senate refused to ratify the terms to which he had agreed, and he was handed over to the enemy.
  • d216 b.c.
  • eIn 47 b.c. C. Julius Caesar crossed from Sicily to Africa, to prosecute the war against the Pompeians.
