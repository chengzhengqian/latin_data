4. Some have held that there are four winds: the Solanus1 from the equinoctial east, the Auster2 from the south, Favonius3 from the equinoctial west, and Septentrio from the north. But those who have inquired more diligently lay down that there are eight: especially indeed Andronicus of Cyrrha,4 who also, for an example, built at Athens5 an octagonal marble tower, and, on the several sides of the octagon, had representations of the winds carved opposite their several currents. And above that tower he caused to be made a marble upright, and above it he placed a bronze Triton holding a rod in his right hand. He so contrived that it was driven round by the wind, and always faced the current of air, and held the rod as indicator above the representation of the wind blowing. 5. Therefore there are placed between the Solanus and the Auster, the Eurus from the winter sunrising; between the Auster and the Favonius, the Africus from the winter sunset; between the Favonius and the Septentrio, the Caurus (which most people call Corus); between the Septentrio and the Solanus, the Aquilo.
