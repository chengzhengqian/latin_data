At first, owing to the slight amount, the ceiling does not drip, but as soon as it has collected more moisture, it cannot keep the water up because of its weight, but sprinkles it on the heads of the bathers. In the same way the air of the sky, receiving heat from the sun, draws moisture from all directions, lifts it and assembles it into clouds. 5. This is proved by the winds. For those which originate and blow from the coldest quarters, the north and north-east, bring currents of air which are rarefied by their dryness; but the south wind and the rest, which attack us from the south, are very moist and always bring rains, because they come warmed from the hot regions; and as they fall, they take up the moisture everywhere and so pour it forth towards the north.
