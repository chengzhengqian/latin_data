3. Therefore if buildings are planned with a view to the status of the client, as was set forth in the first book under the head of decor,1 we shall escape censure. For our rules will be convenient and exact in every respect. Moreover, we shall take account of these matters, not only when we build in town, but in the country; except that, in town, the halls adjoin the entrance, in the country the peristyles of mansions built town-fashion come first, then the atria surrounded by paved colonnades overlooking the palaestra and the promenades.

I have set forth as I am able the general methods of building in town. I will now state the methods of building in the country,2 with a view to convenience in use, and especially to the disposition of the site.
