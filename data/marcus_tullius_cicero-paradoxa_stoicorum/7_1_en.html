Paradox II

That the possession of virtue is sufficient for happiness.

16Nor indeed have I myself ever thought Marcus Regulusa either wretched or unhappy or miserable; for the tortures of the Carthaginians did not affect his greatness of mind or his dignity or loyalty or constancy or any of his virtues, nor finally his mind itself, for this, defended as it was by so great a retinue of virtues, certainly could not possibly itself be taken prisoner, although his body was. But we have actually seen Gaius Marius, who appeared to me in his prosperity to be one of fortune’s favourites among mankind and in his adversity one of the supreme heroes, and that is the highest happiness that can befall a mortal.

17You know not, madman, you know not how great is the strength that virtue possesses; you merely utter the name ‘virtue,’ you do not know what virtue itself means.
