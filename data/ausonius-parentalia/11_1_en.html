XIII.—Avitianus, my Brother

Muse, do thou enrich Avitianus, my brother, with a mournful lay. In years below me, but in gifts of mind above, he learned our father’s art. But Atropos, his foe, forbade him fully to enjoy the gladsome bloom of youth or to pass beyond the bounds which mark the end of boyhood. Ah, what grief for his playmates! Ah, from how glorious a life, and what rich hopes you turned away while yet a lad, my brother—my brother by the law ot flesh and parentage of blood, in love almost my son!

XIV.—Valerius Latinus Euromius, my Son-in-Law

O glory of an illustrious race, O untimely death to me, Euromius, my son-in-law chosen from the company of youths, you perished snatched from me in the very bloom of early youth, a father scarce fully recognized by your son at his mother’s breast.
