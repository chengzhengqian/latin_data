Nevertheless the year in this calendar begins on the 1st of January, which shows that Caesar had local as well as official precedent when he made such a beginning to his normal year of 365 days a universal rule; the custom probably arose locally after 153 b.c. when the Roman consuls began to enter office on the 1st of January. The spelling and style of this calendar point to a date early in the first century b.c. It was published by G. Mancini in Notiz. d. Sc., 1921, pp. 73 ff. (see folder there facing p. 140).
