from Plotius health, body, complexion, strength, faculties. Consign him to Pluto thy husband. May he be unable to avoid this by devices of his. Consign that man to the fourth-day, the third-day, the every-day fever. 1 May they wrestle and wrestle it out 2 with him, overcome and overwhelm him unceasingly until they tear away his life. So I consign him as victim to thee, Proserpina, unless o Proserpina, 3 unless I ought to call thee Goddess of the Lower World. Send, I pray, 4 someone to call up the three-headed dog 5 with request that he may tear out Plotius’ heart. Promise Cerberus that thou wilt give him three offerings—dates, dried figs, and a black pig 6 —if he has fulfilled his task before the month of March. All these, Prosperina Salvia, will I give thee when thou hast made me master of my wish. 7 I give thee the head of Plotius, slave of Avonia. O Proserpina Salvia, I give thee Plotius’ forehead.   • 1malaria is the fever meant; ‘fourth-day’ = returning every third day; ‘third-day’ = returning every other day.
  • 2or possibly ‘fight him down.’ deluctent is a guess; all the five tablets have a gap here. quas is nominative.
  • 3So the tablet cursing Avonia, the only tablet intact at this point. But probably we should read Proserpinam: ‘victim to thee, Proserpina, whether I ought to call thee Proserpina or Acherusia.’
  • 4 me—mihi, as attested by Festus.
  • 5Cerberus, watch-dog of Acheron or of the house of Pluto and Proserpina. The victims mentioned in the next sentence are to be three so as to provide one for each month; besides, three was a magical number.
  • 6These offerings are all characteristic of the worship of underground deities.
  • 7 compote = compotem. The complete sense is cum me voti compotem feceris.
  • 8 palpetrae for palpebrae was used also by Varro (L.L., Fr. 23 Kent, L.C.L). Some Romans said palpetrae means really eyelids, palpebrae eyelashes.
