All the farmhands came and went from time to time, and none of them perceived the stag; the overseer also passed by, and he, too, failed to notice anything. Joyfully then the wild animal began to express his thanks to the oxen, who had remained quiet and given him hospitality in time of trouble. One of them replied: “Indeed, we wish you well, but if he who has a hundred eyes comes here your life will be in great danger.” Meanwhile the master himself returned from dinner; and, since he had noticed of late that the cattle were in poor condition, he went up to the manger. “Why isn’t there enough fodder here?” says he. “You’re short of bedding. How much trouble would it have been to remove these spider-webs?” As he probes each little thing in turn, he catches sight of the stag’s lofty horns; whereupon, having called together his servants, he orders the stag to be killed and carries off the spoil.

The point of this fable is that the master’s eye sees more than any other where his own interest is at stake.
