Heraclea lies at the foot of Mt Oeta;108 it is in a plain itself but has a citadel that rises above it on a lofty elevation, sheer on all sides. After thoroughly reconnoitering its salient features, Acilius decided to attack the city at four points simultaneously. On the side of the River Asopus, where there is also a gymnasium, he put Lucius Valerius in charge of the siege works and the assault. The attack on the area outside the walls, which was almost more densely populated than the city itself, he entrusted to Tiberius Sempronius Longus.109 On the side of the Malian Gulf, an area that did not afford easy access, he placed Marcus Baebius;110 and on the side of the other small river called the Melas, and opposite the temple of Diana, he set Appius Claudius. Thanks to the keen competition between these men, the towers, battering rams and all the other machinery for besieging cities were completed in a matter of days. Moreover, the land around Heraclea, being all marshland, thickly forested with tall trees, offered a wealth of timber for every kind of construction; and in addition, because the Aetolians had sought refuge within their walls, the houses that they had abandoned at the entrance to the city provided not only beams and planks that could be put to various uses but also tiles, rubble111 and rocks of various sizes.
