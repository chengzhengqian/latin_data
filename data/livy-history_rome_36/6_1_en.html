He also asked if the fetiales enjoined a separate declaration of war for the Aetolians and whether the alliance and friendship with them should be formally renounced before that declaration was made. The fetiales replied that they had already given their decision (when they were consulted about Philip)24 that it was immaterial whether the declaration was made directly to the person or to a military outpost. As for the friendship, that was evidently renounced because, they said, they had decided that despite repeated demands made by Roman ambassadors no restitution or equitable compensation had been made. The Aetolians had themselves declared war on the Romans when they forcefully occupied Demetrias,25 a city of their allies, launched an attack by land and sea on Chalcis,26 and brought King Antiochus over to Europe to make war on the Roman people.

All the preparations now satisfactorily completed, the consul Manius Acilius issued a proclamation for a general mobilization at Brundisium on May 15th of those troops that Lucius Quinctius had enlisted or requisitioned from the allies and holders of Latin rights27—that is, the troops that were to go with him to his province—as well as the military tribunes of the first and third legions. Acilius himself set out from the city wearing his general’s cloak28 on May 3rd, and the praetors also left for their provinces at about the same date.
