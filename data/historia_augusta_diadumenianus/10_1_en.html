This should not have been done, nor will it prove of any avail. For, in the first place, they cannot love you now, rendered sore, as they are, by suspicion; in the second, those who have forgotten their ancient friendship and have joined your bitterest enemies will prove to be all the more cruel foes. Consider also the fact that they still have armies.

‘Even should you yourself regard not the fame of such actions, Think of the youthful Ascanius, the hopes of Iulus your scion; Fated for him is Italy’s realm and the land of the Romans.’1

These men must be executed, if you wish to live in safety, for, thanks to the evil ways of mankind, there will be no lack of other foes, if the lives of these be spared.” This letter, attributed by some to Diadumenianus himself, by others to his teacher Caelianus,2 formerly a rhetorician in Africa, shows how cruel the young man would have been, had he lived.
