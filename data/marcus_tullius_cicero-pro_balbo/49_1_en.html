Sections 14–16.

The prosecutor, however, suggests that Pompey acted unwittingly. But it is incredible that a man in Pompey’s position should show ignorance of the treaty with Gades. Pompey in fact must be well informed of the treaty relations between Rome and foreign states, for his experience abroad must have taught him fully what study imperfectly teaches us at home. That finishes my case. But the ills of our time urge me to continue. Were Pompey a hero of early Rome, and were he now accused before us of breaking a treaty, the charge would be dismissed as absurd.

Sections 17–19.

The speeches of Crassus and Pompey have left nothing new to be said, but from a sense of duty I am complying with their wish that I should speak last for the defence. Since the source of the charge against Balbus is envy of his social success, I ask the jury to regard my client’s distinctions as a help not a hindrance to his case.

Sections 19–22.

The source of this case is the lex Gellia Cornelia which confirmed a grant of Roman citizenship made by Pompey to certain individuals among whom was Balbus, a citizen of Gades, a civitas foederata.
