Letters to Atticus

faute de mieuxwe must make the best of him.1 As for the other man, whom you say you think not unsuitable, I doubt whether my girl could be brought to consent, and you say there’s not much to choose. For my part I have no wish to be awkward, but you will be away and in my absence the thing will hang fire (?). If both of us were on the spot something acceptable might be made of Servius2 through Servilia. As it is, even if I were to approve in principle, I see no way of going to work.

I now come to the letter I got from Tullius. Thank you for your efforts in connexion with Marcellus. If then a decree3 is passed, you’ll let me know; if not, you’ll get the thing through all the same, as I shall be entitled to a payment from the Treasury, Bibulus4 also. But I am sure the decree presents no difficulty, particularly as it means a saving to the public. Thank you about Torquatus. We’ll see about Maso5 and Ligus when they arrive. As for Chaerippus’ request—since here too you have ruled out ‘consent’6—oh, who would be a governor? Must I really look after him? I suppose so, to the extent of making sure that nothing comes up before the Senate (?)—‘Refer’ or ‘Count.’7 As for the rest—but I’m glad you had a word with Scrofa. You are right about Pomptinus. The position is that if he is going to be at Brundisium before the Kalends of June M. Anneius and Tullius won’t need to be hurried so

  • 1Tullia, now divorced from her second husband, needed a replacement. ‘Him’ is probably a young man of Arpinum (Pontidia’s son) mentioned in Letters 114.14 and 115.10. The ‘other man’ is unidentified.
  • 2The younger Ser. Sulpicius Rufus.
  • 3Authorizing a grant to Cicero as Proconsul.
  • 4Recently appointed governor of Syria.
  • 5This and the other matters cursorily mentioned here cannot be elucidated.
  • 6Atticus might be said to have taken Cicero’s consent out of the question by committing him in advance.
  • 7By ‘refer’ members of the Senate demanded a discussion as opposed to a vote; by ‘count,’ a count to show the presence or absence of a quorum. The usual object was to block a decree.
