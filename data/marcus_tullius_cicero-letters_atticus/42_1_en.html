Letters to Atticus

In public life the great thing just now is the Gallic war scare. Our Haeduan brothers1 have recently taken a beating, and there is no doubt that the Helvetii are up in arms and raiding the Province. The Senate has decreed that the Consuls should cast lots for the two Gauls,2 that a levy should be held with all exemptions cancelled, and ambassadors with full powers sent to visit the Gallic communities and try to stop them making common cause with the Helvetii. The ambassadors are Q. Metellus Creticus, L. Flaccus, and (‘perfume on lentils’3) Lentulus son of Clodianus. While on the subject I cannot help mentioning that when I drew the first lot among the Consulars a full House unanimously declared that I must be kept in Rome. The same thing happened to Pompey after me, so it looked as if we two were being kept as gages one might say for the Republic. Why after all should I wait for other people’s applaudissements when I am so good a hand at the game myself?

Home affairs stand thus: Tribune Flavius is vigorously pushing his agrarian law, with Pompey’s backing. There is nothing popular about it except the mover. With the approval of an assembly I advocated the deletion from the bill of all provisions detrimental to private interest. I was for releasing from its operation such land as was in state ownership in the Consulship of P. Mucius and L.
