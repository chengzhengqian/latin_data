Letters to Atticus

174A (IX.7A)

Rome, 10 or 11 March 49

Balbus and Oppius to M. Cicero

Advice, even the advice of great men, let alone of humble folk like us, is generally judged by the event, not by the intention. None the less we shall rely on your charitable heart and give you the advice which seems to us the soundest on the matter about which you have written to us. It may or may not be wise, but at any rate it is offered in all sincerity and good will.

If we had learned from Caesar himself that he would do what in our judgement we think he ought to do, namely start negotiations for a reconciliation between himself and Pompey as soon as he gets to Rome, we should not cease to urge you to consent to take part in them, so that through you, who have ties with both, the whole matter might be settled with greater facility and dignity. Alternatively, if we thought on the contrary that Caesar would not do this and even knew that he wanted war with Pompey, we should never persuade you to bear arms against your benefactor, just as we have always besought you not to fight against Caesar.
