The men, splendid ornaments of the earth, the horses, ornaments no less splendid, both of race divine, stand behind one barrier, and with them hope and audacious fear and anxious confidence. In their hearts nothing is firm-fixed. They fight to go forth and are afraid; courageous chill courses all through their limbs. The horses are as ardent as their masters. Their eyes swell fiery, their mouths loudly champ, foam and blood corrode their bits, the posts and bars cannot withstand their push, the pant of stifled rage smokes through. To stand still is torture; a thousand paces are wasted before the start, the heavy hoof strikes the absent flat. Trusty attendants straighten the tangled patches in their manes, encourage them, give many words of counsel. 45 Opposite sounded the Tyrrhenian blare and all leapt from their stations. What canvass on sea, what weapons fly so in battle, what clouds in the sky? Not so swift the rush of winter rivers, not so swift the rush of fire. More slowly fall stars, more slowly balled rains, 46 more slowly run torrents down from a mountaintop.

The Pelasgi saw them as they shot out and recognized; and already, snatched from vision and mingled in blinding dust, they are hidden in a single cloud and as confusion obscures their faces they barely know each other by shout of names.
