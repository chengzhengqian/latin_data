Further, when sleep has fast bound our limbsin sleep we seem to move, see, hear, speak. with sweet drowsiness, and our whole body lies in profound quiet, yet we seem to ourselves then to be awake and to move our limbs, and in the blind darkness of night we think that we see the sun and the light of day, and we seem to exchange our narrow room for sky and sea, rivers and mountains,a and traverse plains afoot, and to hear sounds though the stern silence of night reigns everywhere, and to utter speech while saying nothing.

We see in marvellous fashion many things besidesIn all such cases, the sense are true, but the mind is deceived. of this kind, which all try as it were to break the credit of our senses; but all in vain, since the most part of them deceives because of opinions of the mind which we bring to them ourselves, so that things are held to be seen which have not been seen by our senses. For nothing is more difficult than to distinguish plain things from doubtful things which the mind of itself adds at once.
