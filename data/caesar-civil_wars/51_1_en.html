74. With their departure, the soldiers had a clear opportunity for negotiations, and they came out in a body, each man looking and calling for his acquaintances and townsmen in Caesar’s camp. 2First they gave collective thanks to everyone for having spared them the previous day, when they were terrified. “Your kindness kept us alive.” Then they asked about the general’s reliability, and whether it would be a good move to entrust themselves to him, lamenting the fact that they had not done so from the beginning and had fought against men who were their friends and relatives. 3Encouraged by this discussion they asked for the general’s promise concerning the future of Petreius and Afranius, so that people would not think that they had incurred guilt or betrayed their own side. Once these matters were settled the men guaranteed that they would transfer their standards immediately, and sent to Caesar a peace delegation of chief centurions. 4Meanwhile, some led friends into their own camp to entertain them, and others were led off by friends, with the result that the two camps seemed to have become one. Several staff officers and centurions went and commended themselves to Caesar. 5Spain’s leading men did the same. (The enemy had summoned these men and kept them in camp with them as hostages.) These sought out their acquaintances and guests, hoping to get from them the opening of a recommendation to Caesar. 6Even Afranius’ adolescent son began negotiations with Caesar, through Caesar’s officer Sulpicius, about his own welfare and that of his father.
