This increased Varro’s haste to make for Cadiz as soon as possible with his legions, to avoid being cut off while marching or crossing; the province’s attitude toward Caesar was found to be strongly favorable. 2After going a little further, he received a letter from Cadiz. “As soon as they learned about Caesar’s edict, the city leaders came to an agreement with the officers of the cohorts garrisoning Cadiz to expel Gallonius and hold the city and island for Caesar. 3After adopting this plan they announced to Gallonius that he must leave Cadiz voluntarily while it was possible to do so without danger. If he didn’t do so, they said, they were going to act for themselves. Gallonius, induced by this fear, left Cadiz.” 4At this news one of Varro’s two legions, the one called “indigenous,” removed its standards from Varro’s camp while he stood and watched; the legion went back to Hispalis and established itself in the forum porticoes without causing any trouble. 5Their action was so well thought of by the town’s association of Roman citizens that each member wanted above all to welcome them as guests into his own home. 6When Varro, thoroughly alarmed by this, changed his route and sent word that he would be coming to Italica, he was informed by supporters that the gates had been closed against him. 7Then, finally, cut off in every direction, he sent to Caesar: “I am ready to surrender my legion to anyone you order.” Caesar sent Sextus Caesar to him and ordered the legion to be surrendered to him. 8After the surrender Varro went to Caesar at Corduba.
