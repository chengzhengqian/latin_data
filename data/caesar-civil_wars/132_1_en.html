They remembered that after suffering great scarcity at Alesia, and even more at Avaricum, they had left the field as conquerors of supremely great peoples.66) 6When given barley they did not object, nor beans. As for herd animals, of which there was a large supply in Epirus, they valued them highly.

48. There was also a kind of root vegetable, found by those who had been . . .67 This was called “chara,” and mixed with milk it did much to alleviate the scarcity; 2the supply was abundant. They made loaves from it, and when the Pompeians, in crosstalk, referred mockingly to their hunger, our men generally threw these at them to lessen their hopes.

49. In addition, the grain crops were already beginning to ripen, and hope itself parried the scarcity, since the men were confident that they would soon have plenty. There was a saying among the soldiers on watch and in crosstalk: “We will eat the bark from the trees before we let Pompey out of our grasp.” 2They were also pleased to learn from deserters that, while the enemy’s horses were being kept alive,68 the rest of their pack animals had perished, and the men themselves were not in good health, suffering both from the limited space and the foul odor of numerous corpses and their daily labors—they were unaccustomed to working—and from an extreme shortage of water, 3for Caesar had either diverted or obstructed with large earthworks all of the rivers and streams that ran to the sea.
