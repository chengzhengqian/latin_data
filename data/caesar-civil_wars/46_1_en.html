When the enemy made another attempt to advance, he pursued, delaying them. 3They necessarily made camp sooner than they had intended, for the mountains were coming close, and difficult and narrow paths awaited them five miles away. 4They were trying to retreat beyond84 these mountains in order to get clear of Caesar’s cavalry, prevent the passage of his army by placing garrisons in the narrows, and take their own forces across the Ebro without danger or fear. 5They needed to try and accomplish this whatever the cost, and85 they put the matter off until the following day, tired from the day-long fight as they were, and weary from the exertion of the march. Caesar too made camp, on the next hill.

66. Around midnight the cavalry caught some men who had gone too far from their camp for water. The prisoners informed Caesar that the enemy commanders were moving their troops out of camp in silence. When he learned this he ordered men to give a signal and call out “pack up!” military fashion. 2When the enemy heard the shouting, worried that they would have to fight by night and encumbered with baggage, or would be trapped by Caesar’s cavalry in the narrows, they stopped the march and kept their troops in camp. 3On the next day Petreius set out undercover with a few cavalry to explore the region. The same was done from Caesar’s camp: Lucius Decidius Saxa was sent with a few men to reconnoiter the character of the terrain. 4Each announced the same thing to his side: the first five miles of the route to the mountains were level, then rugged and mountainous terrain took over.
