38. With these arrangements in place and his plans approved, Curio heard from some townspeople deserting Utica that Juba, recalled because of a border war and a dispute with Leptis, had stayed behind in his kingdom. Saburra, they said, a general of his, had been sent with a modest force and was approaching Utica. 2Rashly believing these informants, Curio changed his plan and decided to commit the issue to battle. Many factors helped make this plan appealing: youth, ambition, past outcomes, confidence in success. 3Urged on by such considerations, he sent his entire cavalry at nightfall to the enemy camp at the Bagradas River; Saburra, about whom he had heard earlier, was in command there. But Saburra had behind him the king and his entire force; Juba was encamped six miles away. 4The cavalry Curio sent finished their journey during the night; they attacked an enemy off guard and unsuspecting, for the Numidians in their barbarian fashion had camped here and there in complete disarray. 5By attacking while these were fast asleep and scattered, Curio’s cavalry killed a large number of them; many fled in terror. Thereafter the cavalry headed back to Curio with prisoners in tow.
