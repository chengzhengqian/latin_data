13. Learning of Caesar’s approach the town councilors at Auximum met as a body with Attius Varus. They told him that the affair was not something for them to decide. “Neither we nor the rest of our townspeople can tolerate that Gaius Caesar, a commander who has such important public achievements to his credit, be barred from the town and its fortifications. Furthermore, you should consider the future and your danger.” 2Disturbed by their words Varus led out the garrison he had installed, and fled. 3A few of Caesar’s advance-guard soldiers caught up and forced him to stop. 4A fight began, but Varus was deserted by his men. A portion of the soldiers went home, the rest reached Caesar, bringing the chief centurion Lucius Pupius along as a prisoner. (He had earlier held that same rank in one of Pompey’s armies.) 5Caesar for his part praised Attius’ soldiers, dismissed Pupius, and thanked the people of Auximum, promising to remember their action.

14. When this news reached Rome the panic that suddenly hit was so great that, although the consul Lentulus had come to open the treasury to provide money for Pompey as per the senate’s decree, he fled the city directly after opening the treasury reserve.31 For—so went the false report—Caesar was already approaching, indeed his cavalry was already at hand. 2Lentulus was followed by his colleague Marcellus and most of the magistrates. 3Pompey, having left Rome the day before, was en route to the legions he had received from Caesar and distributed in winter
