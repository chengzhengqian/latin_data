Emerging from the rocky heights, he came to a flat area and drew up his line there facing the enemy. Afranius, with his rearguard under pressure from the cavalry and the enemy visible ahead, halted when he came to a hill. 4From there he sent four native cohorts to the highest mountain in sight.89 He ordered them to move at a fast run and seize this, thinking that he would head there with his entire force and so reach Otogesa by a new route along the ridges. 5When the native troops deviated toward this objective they were observed and attacked by Caesar’s cavalry. Unable to withstand the charge even for a moment, they were all surrounded by the cavalry and killed with both armies watching.

71. This was an opportunity for decisive action. It did not escape Caesar that after taking—and watching—such damage the terrified army could not hold out, particularly when surrounded on all sides by cavalry, since the battlefield was level and open. From every direction he heard demands for an engagement. 2Legionary commanders, centurions, and staff officers quickly gathered: “Do not hesitate to engage. The morale of all of our soldiers is at its peak. 3Afranius’ men, by contrast, have signaled their alarm in many matters: in not sending help to their own men, not coming down from the hill, barely withstanding the cavalry attacks, and not preserving their formations or units, given that they have collected their standards in a single spot and are crowded together. 4But if you are worried about the location’s drawbacks, there will be an opportunity to fight somewhere, because Afranius certainly has to withdraw from this area; he cannot stay long without water.”
