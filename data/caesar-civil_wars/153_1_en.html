80. With his forces united, Caesar went to Gomphi, which is the first town in Thessaly for those coming from Epirus. This community had spontaneously sent a delegation to Caesar a few months earlier, inviting him to make use of all of their resources; they had asked him for a garrison of soldiers. 2But the aforementioned report about the Dyrrachium battle had already outstripped him, and they had augmented it considerably. 3So Androsthenes, the top man in Thessaly, preferring to be a companion to Pompey’s victory than Caesar’s ally in adversity, drove the whole population of slaves and free men from the countryside into the town, shut the gates, and sent messengers to Scipio and Pompey with a request for help: “I have confidence in the town’s defenses if help comes quickly. We cannot withstand a long siege.” 4Scipio, upon learning of the armies’ departure from Dyrrachium, had led his legions to Larisa; Pompey was not yet near Thessaly.95

5After fortifying his camp Caesar ordered ladders and galleries built and brushwood bundles readied for an immediate assault. 6When these were finished he rallied his troops, explaining how useful it would be for relieving the general scarcity to take control of a town that was well stocked and wealthy, and at the same time to strike terror into other cities with the example of this one, and to do so quickly, before reinforcements could arrive. 7Taking advantage of the soldiers’ remarkable determination, he began the attack in the middle of the afternoon the day he arrived.
