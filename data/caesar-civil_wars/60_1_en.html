This pleased and gladdened the soldiers, as could be understood simply from their demonstrations, inasmuch as those who, being the defeated party,102 had expected some hardship, were being given, without having asked for it, the reward of discharge. 2For when its location and timing came under discussion, they all began shouting and gesticulating from the rampart where they stood to signify that they should be discharged immediately. No pledge given in the meantime, they said, could make their discharge a sure thing if it was deferred to another time. 3After a brief statement on each side of the question, the matter ended thus: those who had a home or property in Spain would be dismissed immediately, the rest at the Var River.103 4Caesar guaranteed that they would not be harmed and that no one would be forced to enlist unwillingly.

87. Caesar promised to supply food from then until they reached the Var. He also added that whatever items any of them had lost in the war, if they were in his soldiers’ possession, would be returned to those who had lost them. To his soldiers he gave monetary compensation for these items after a fair appraisal. 2Thereafter the soldiers of their own accord approached Caesar for adjudication of whatever internal disputes they had. 3As for Petreius and Afranius, when the legions demanded—in what nearly became a mutiny—their pay, and they claimed that it was not yet due, people requested that Caesar hear the case, and both sides were content with his settlement. 4After dismissing about a third of the army within two days, he ordered two of his legions to go ahead and the rest to follow; they were to make camp at no great distance from one another.
