A large supply of rowers and pilots was available.2 Plus, fishing boats had been added, newly furnished with decks so that the rowers would be safe from the impact of projectiles. They filled these with archers and catapults. 3With the fleet thus equipped the men embarked, roused by the pleas and tears of a whole crowd of older men, matrons, and girls urging them to rescue the city in its moment of crisis. Their spirits and confidence were as high as in the earlier battle, 4for by a common defect of our nature it happens that we put more reliance in unseen and unknown things,6 as occurred then. (The arrival of Lucius Nasidius had filled the city with a very great sense of hope and purpose.) 5When they got a suitable wind they left the harbor and went to Nasidium at Tauroeis, a stronghold belonging to Marseilles. There they got their ships ready and again encouraged one another for battle and exchanged plans. The right wing was assigned to Marseilles, the left to Nasidius.

5. Brutus made for the same spot with an augmented fleet, for to the ships made on Caesar’s orders at Arles he had added six captured from Marseilles.7 In the preceding days he had repaired these and equipped them fully. 2Therefore after encouraging his men—“You have nothing to worry about from beaten men whom you defeated when they were unscathed!”—he set out against the enemy ships full of good hope and spirit.
