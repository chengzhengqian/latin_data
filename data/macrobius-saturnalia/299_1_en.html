15. ‘Hyperbole—that is, overstatement28—produces an emotional response in giving expression to anger or pity: anger, as when we say, perhaps, “He should have died a thousand times,” a thought found in Virgil (A. 10.854),

would that I myself had given up my guilty life in any and every kind of death!;

pity, when he says (E. 5.27–28*),

Daphnis, even lions of Carthage lamented your passing. . . .

16. In addition, overstatement produces an emotional response typical of a lover or someone of the opposite sort (E. 7.43),

If this day now doesn’t seem to me longer than a whole year . . .,

and quite differently (A. 9.115–16),

. . . Turnus will sooner be allowed to burn up the seas than my sacred pines,

and (A. 12.204),

. . . not if it should send the earth tumbling into the sea.
