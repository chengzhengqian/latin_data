16. Then there’s Homer’s Lycaon, caught amid the rout and reduced (no surprise) to begging, though Achilles did not spare him in his grief for the slain Patroclus (Il. 21.34–135). He’s replaced by Magus, who finds himself in similar circumstances in the midst of the fray (A. 10.521):

thereafter he had aimed from afar his hostile spear at Magus,

and when he grasped Aeneas’ knees and begged for his life as a suppliant, Aeneas replied (A. 10.532–33),

. . . Turnus did away with that sort of barter in war, back then when he killed Pallas.
