he landed in the other’s tracks before the dust settled around them.

The meaning of this line is as follows: if a race happens to be run over dusty ground, a runner of course leaves a track when he raises his foot from the earth, and yet quicker than thought the dust raised by a footfall settles back over the track.

The godlike poet’s point, then, is that the pursuer is so close that he plants his foot in the other’s track before the dust settles upon it. But what does your poet say when he wants to convey the same thing? (A. 5.324)
