2. ‘Indignation must begin to be expressed without preamble, since a slow and gradual start is not appropriate for those experiencing the emotion fully. That is why Virgil’s Juno begins as follows (A. 10.63–64):

. . . why do you force me to shatter my deep silence?

and elsewhere (A. 1.37),

. . . am I to stand down from what I’ve begun, beaten?

and elsewhere (A. 7.293–94),

Ah, that hated race, and the Phrygians’ fates dead set against my own. . . .

and Dido (A. 4.659–60),

“I will die unavenged,” she said, “but let me die. . . .”

and again (A. 4.590–91*),

“By god!,” she said, will he just leave . . .?,”

and Priam (A. 2.535),

“But for you,” he cries out, “in return for your crime and brazen deeds. . . .”
