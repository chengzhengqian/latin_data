9. Julius Modestus says (fr. 2 GRF 2:9) that sacrifice is made to this goddess because the Roman people were freed of the disease called angina after making a vow to her.166

10. The eleventh day before the Kalends is the holy day dedicated to the Lares, to whom the praetor Aemilius Regillus, in the war against Antiochus, vowed that he would raise a temple in the Campus Martius.167

11. ‘The tenth day before the Kalends is the holy day of Jupiter called the Larentinalia,168 about which—since it’s fun telling tales—I can offer this range of views.

12. They say that in the reign of Ancus the temple warden of Hercules was enjoying his holiday leisure and challenged the god to a game of dice (he would handle the dice for both of them), adding the stipulation that the loser would be penalized the cost of a meal and a prostitute.169

13. So when Hercules won, the warden locked up Acca Larentia, the most notorious prostitute of the day, in the temple with a meal; the next day she put about the rumor that after sleeping with her the god told her, as a kind of tip, that she should not fail to benefit from the first opportunity that presented itself when she returned home.

14. And so it happened that soon after leaving the temple she was propositioned by Carutius, who had been taken by her beauty. She did as he desired and married him, then after his death came into all his possessions: when she died, she named the Roman people her heir.170
