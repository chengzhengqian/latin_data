Book III

You calmed the winds with a slaughtered maiden’s blood, when first you came, Greeks, to Ilium’s shores: with blood you must seek your return, an Argive’s life-spirit must be offered in propitiation . . ., for he used both “life-spirit,” to denote the victim, and “offer in propitiation” [litare], which indicates that he placated a god by making the sacrifice.37

5. ‘Among these victims, whether the “spirit” sort or those used to seek the gods’ will, there are some, called “unyoked,” that have not been tamed or put to the yoke.38 Our poet makes mention of them in these terms (A. 6.38–39*),

Now it would be better to slay seven bullocks from an untouched herd, and as many two-year-old sheep chosen according to custom;

and to make more apparent that he means “unyoked” animals, he added (G. 4.540* = 551),

. . . and as many bullocks with necks untouched.

6. ‘The word “choice” [eximii], too, when used of offerings, is not a poetic epithet but a term of priestly art.39 In his Pontifical Investigations Veranius tells us (fr. 4 GRF 1:431 = fr. 8 IAH 2.1:8 = fr. 4 IAR6) that victims are called “choice” when they’re marked out for sacrifice and removed from the herd, or because they’re selected for their “choice” appearance, as worthy of being offered to the gods.
