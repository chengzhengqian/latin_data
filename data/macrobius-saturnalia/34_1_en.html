That second clause forbids us from asking anything of the gods that we could not confess to wanting without disgrace.87

7. But we aim both to honor this religious holy day and to avoid the typical holiday lethargy by turning our leisure to some useful employment: hence we’ve agreed to give the whole day over to sharing learned tales, like contributions to a common meal.

8. If no religious scruple forbids clearing stream-beds on a holiday, and if divine and human laws allow sheep to be given a curative dip,88 why should religious scruple not be thought to receive due honor by dedicating to sacred days the sacred study of literature?

9. But since one of the gods wanted the three of you to join our number, let’s spend the day in shared talk and meals, if you’re willing: I know that all those here today agree with me in this suggestion.’

10. Evangelus said: ‘For my part I think there’s nothing disgraceful for the uninvited to overtake a conversation underway, but to willingly crash a party prepared for others—for that even a brother cannot escape censure, according to Homer. And seeing that on that occasion the great king Agamemnon had only one Menelaus come his way, watch out that you not claim too much for yourself in wanting three.’89

11. Then all, seconding Praetextatus, entreated and cajoled them to join the common undertaking,
