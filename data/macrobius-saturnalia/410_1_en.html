Book V

Hernici and Anagnia, their best known city (as it was then), are listed in the catalogue (A. 7.684–90):

. . . those whom rich Anagnia sustains, or you, father Amasenus: none has arms that clash and clatter, no shields or chariots, but most spray shot of dull dark lead, some carry light spears, two in each hand; tawny caps of wolf’s hide cover their heads, they plant the tracks of their left feet unshod, a rawhide boot covers the right.

14. So far as I know, it is nowhere attested that there was in Italy the custom of going to war with one foot shod, the other bare; but I shall now adduce abundant authority to show that some Greeks had this custom.

15. Here indeed we can marvel at the care the poet took while making no display of it at all: having read that the Hernici, whose city is Anagnia, were Pelasgians by origin and derived their name from a certain Pelasgian king named Hernicus, he attributed to the Hernici, who were an ancient colony of the Pelasgi, a custom that he had read about in connection with Aetolia.

16. Julius Hyginus, in Book 2 of his Cities, demonstrates at length that a certain Pelasgian named Hernicus was the leader of the Hernici (fr. 8 HRR 2;74 = fr. 13 GRF 1:533–34).
