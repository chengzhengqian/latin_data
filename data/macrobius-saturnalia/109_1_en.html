Book I

21. They call the same god who presides over the sources of health Oulios, or “author of well-being,” as Homer says (Od. 24.402): Health to you, and much joy. Maeandrius writes (no. 491 fr. 2 FGrH) that the people of Miletus sacrifice to Apollo Oulios for their health.412 Pherecydes reports (no. 3 fr. 149 FGrH = fr. 149 EGM) that when Theseus was brought to Crete to face the Minotaur, he made a vow to Apollo Oulios and Artemis Oulia for his safe return.

22. Nor is it surprising if the god’s two actions are celebrated under different names, since we learn that the other gods who have opposite effects in the same sphere are judged to have a twofold power and a double name, as in the case of Neptune, whom people call now Enosichthôn, or “earth-shaker,” now Asphaliôn, or “he who steadies.”413 Similarly, Mercury both arouses and lulls to sleep men’s minds or eyes, as the poet says (Il. 24.343–44):

He took up his wand with which he beguiles men’s sight, <those whom he wishes, and rouses them too when they sleep>.

23. ‘So too we come to worship Apollo, that is, the sun, under names that signify survival and destruction by turns; yet the plague that the god lets loose upon the guilty still shows that he fights on behalf of the good.
