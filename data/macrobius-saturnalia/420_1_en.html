‘Nor will we fail to touch on the following lines from20 Book 1 of the Georgics (100–3):

Pray for damp solstices and calm winters, farmers: emmers grow best in the dust of winter, the fields are fertile. Mysia can make no such boast about its tillage nor Gargara itself so marvel at its harvests.

2. Not only is the meaning here rather obscure and the expression a bit more tangled than usual for this poet, but the passage involves a question derived from ancient Greece that deserves notice: what is that “Gargara itself” that Virgil held out as a model of fertility?

3. The Gargara in question is in Mysia, a province on the Hellespont, but the name has two different meanings, referring to two different places: Gargara is the name of both the top of Mount Ida and the town at foot of the mountain.90

4. Homer uses the name of the mountain-top here (Il. 8.47–48): He came to Ida, rich in springs, mother of beasts, Gargaron.
