10. Eratosthenes, an extremely learned man, mentions this cup in a letter to the Spartan Hagetor, saying (Philosophical Books fr. 14):96 “They set up a mixing bowl for the gods, not silver nor set with precious stones but of clay from Kôlias.97 As often as they filled it, they made a libation to the gods and then poured servings one after the other, using a kymbion as a ladle.”

11. There have been those who thought that cymbium was derived from kissybium, a syllable having fallen out.98 But many writers mention the kissybium—to say nothing of Homer, who says that Ulysses gave this cup to the Cyclops (Od. 9.347)—and some claim that the kissybium is properly a wooden cup made of ivy, that is, kissos.

12.
