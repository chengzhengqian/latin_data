Flavianus then said, ‘I’ve always heard other doctors6 too making this same point, that wine should be counted among the things that are naturally warm; and just now Eustathius, too, in touching on the causes of drunkenness, mentioned wine’s warmth. But when I’ve pondered the question, as I often have, I’ve formed the impression that wine is by nature more nearly cool than warm: I’ll put before you the reasons that lead me to this assessment, so that you can pass judgment on it.50

2. As far as I can see, wine is on the one hand cool by nature but is on the other hand capable of taking on warmth, or even seeks it out when it has been brought near sources of heat. For though metal too is cold to the touch (Il. 5.75)— he gripped the cold bronze with his teeth— it nonetheless grows warm when it’s received the sunlight, and the warmth, though a new arrival, drives out the native chill. Let’s ask, then, whether reason urges that the same is true of wine.

3. Wine is either introduced to the body’s inner regions by drinking or is applied as a poultice to treat the body’s surface. Not even physicians are going to deny that it is cool when it is poured on the skin, yet they assert that it is warm inside the body—though it is not warm as it goes down but becomes warm when it mingles with sources of heat.
