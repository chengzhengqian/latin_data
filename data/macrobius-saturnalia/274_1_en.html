4. Let’s take, then, a single speech of Virgil’s as an example (A. 7.293):7

Ah, that hated race . . .,

starting with an exclamation, then two short questions follow (A. 7.294–96),

. . . Couldn’t they fall on the fields of Sigeum, couldn’t they stay captive once captured? Couldn’t Troy in flames have consumed her heroes?

Then an overstatement follows (A. 7.296–97),

. . . through the midst of battle and the midst of the Flames they found a way. . . .,

then irony (A. 7.297–98),

. . . No doubt my godhead at last lies prostrate, exhausted, no doubt I’ve found peace, my hatred slaked.

5. Then she complains that her own bold moves were foiled (A. 7.299–300):
