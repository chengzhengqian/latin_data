6. ‘There are pleasing repetitions that he doesn’t avoid:

. . . things that a maiden and young boy, a maiden and young boy whisper to one another. (Il. 22.127–28) If Pan should compete with me, even before Arcadia as judge, Pan will admit he’s been beat, even before Aracadia as judge. (E. 4.58–59)

7. ‘He acknowledged, through his imitations, how much he admired Homeric epithets: . . . God-blessed child of destiny . . . (Il. 3.182) . . . men of the bronze cuirass. But the boss-studded shields . . . (Il. 4.448 = 8.62) . . . and of new-polished cuirasses . . . (Il. 13.342) . . . dark-haired Poseidon . . . (Il. 13.563 = 14.390) . . . Zeus cloud-gatherer . . . (Il. 5.631 = 5.736 et al.) . . . both shadowy mountains and resounding sea . . . (Il. 1.157) . . . dark-skinned beans . . . (Il. 13.589) and a thousand other such words that, like stars, make the godlike poem glitter in its variegated majesty.
