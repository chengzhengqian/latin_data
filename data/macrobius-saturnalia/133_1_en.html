Book I

15. The square figure’s four sides are fashioned according to the same principle that attributes the four-stringed lyre to Mercury. Indeed, this number signifies the four quarters of the world, or the four seasons that define the year, or the two equinoxes and two solstices that articulate the system of the zodiac, just as Apollo’s seven-stringed lyre represents the seven motions of the heavenly spheres.509

16. That the sun is worshipped in Mercury’s guise is also apparent from his wand, the caduceus, which the Egyptians fashioned in the shape of two intertwined serpents, male and female, and consecrated to Mercury.510 These serpents are bound together at the middle by the so-called knot of Hercules;511 their heads and necks, curving round in a circle, are joined in a kiss to complete the circle; and below the knot their tails rejoin the caduceus’ hilt and are equipped with wings that sprout from the same part of the wand.

17. The Egyptians also use the caduceus’ significance to explain people’s horoscope (“genesis” it’s called), saying that four gods attend a human being as it’s born, Deity [Daimôn], Chance [Tykhê], Love [Erôs], and Necessity
