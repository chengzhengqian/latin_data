Yet those who keep on saying 16 that Catiline is going to Marseilles are more afraid than sorry. None of them is tender-hearted enough to wish him to go there rather than join Manlius. If he had never before even dreamt of doing what he is doing now, he would still rather die a bandit than live an exile. As it is, everything has so far gone for him according to plan and just as he wished—except that I was still alive when he left Rome—let us hope, then, that he is going into exile, and not complain about it.

Why, though, am I talking so long about one 17 enemy, an enemy at that who now admits that he is an enemy and one of whom I am not afraid because—and this is what I have always wanted—the city wall lies between us, and yet do not mention those who pretend that they are not enemies, who remain in Rome, and are still among us? I do not wish so much to take revenge upon these men as to bring them to their senses in any way that I can, and to reconcile them to the Republic, a task that should not prove impossible if only they are now willing to listen to me. I shall list for you, citizens, the types of men from which the revolutionary forces are recruited and I shall then offer to each of them in my speech the remedy of any advice that I can give.
