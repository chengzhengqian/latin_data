I

On the evils that are all that the wicked fear and that the world always suffered while it worshipped the gods.

Enough has now been said, I think, to show that in respect to social and personal vices, which are particularly to be avoided, the false gods did nothing to assist the people that worshipped them to diminish their oppression by the weight of those evils but instead took action to increase such oppression to the utmost.

It is now clear that I must speak about the evils which alone they are unwilling to endure, such as famine, disease, war, pillage, captivity, slaughter and similar calamities that have already been mentioned in the first book. Evil men regard as evils only those things which do not make men evil, and they do not blush to remain evil, in the midst of the good which they praise, though themselves evil while they praise. It upsets them more if their countryseat is faulty than if their life is so, as though the greatest good for a man were to have everything of his good save himself.
