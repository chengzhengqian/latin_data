The fact is that souls do not pay the penalty in this way by returning once again to this life, and there is no creator of any living thing, whether in heaven or in earth, but him by whom the heaven and earth were made. Indeed, if our only reason for living in this body is to suffer punishment, how can Plato also say that the world could not have been made supremely beautiful and good except by being filled with all kinds of animate beings, that is both immortal and mortal?1 On the other hand, if our creation even as mortals is a divine gift, how can the return to these bodies, that is, to God’s good works, be a punishment? And if God, as Plato repeatedly mentions,2 held in his eternal intelligence the forms not only of the entire universe but also of all animate beings, how comes it that he did not create them all himself? Could it be that he was unwilling to be the craftsman of some things, though the requisite craft for producing them existed in his mind, which no tongue can describe or sufficiently praise?
