On the limits to be used in refutation.

If the infirmity of sense usually found in man did not make bold to resist the clear reasoning of truth, but yielded its lethargy to wholesome doctrine as to medicine until with Godâ€™s help it obtained healing by the intercession of religious faith, those who have right ideas and who express them effectively in words would need no long discourse to refute no matter what error of futile conjecture. As things are, however, foolish minds are affected by this distemper in a severer and more loathsome form, so that, even after the account has been paid in full and every debt of reason that one human being owes to another has been satisfied, they defend their own unaccountable vagaries as if they were the very accounting of truth, either because they are too blind even to see what is obvious, or because they are utterly obstinate in a stubborn refusal to accept the truth though they see it. Hence we are forced, as often as not, to give eloquence more play though the matter is plain enough. It is as if we had, not so much to present such matters for the inspection of men who fix their eyes on them, as for the examination by feeling of men who keep their eyes closed and use their fingers.
