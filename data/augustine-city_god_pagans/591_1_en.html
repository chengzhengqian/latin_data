V

The sacrifices that God does not require but that he chose should be offered as symbols of those that he does require.

But who would be so foolish as to think that the objects offered in sacrifices are needed by God for any particular purposes? The folly of this is attested by holy Scripture in many passages, but, not to be tedious, let it suffice to cite that brief text from the Psalm: “I have said to the Lord,’ Thou art my God, for thou needest not my goods.’”2 We are to believe, then, that God has no need either of man’s flocks and herds or of any other thing that is corruptible or earthbound. Not only that, but he has no need even of man’s righteousness, so we must believe; and everything done in the due worship of God benefits man, not God. For no man would say that he intended to help a spring when he drank from it, or a light when he saw by it.
