This was the kind of life the Romans led under the kings in that much praised epoch of the state that expired with the expulsion of Tarquin the Proud after two hundred and forty-three years. At that moment all those victories, bought with so much bloodshed and such great disasters, had scarce expanded the dominion twenty miles from the city. God forbid we should compare so small a domain with that of even any Getulian city of our time.

On the first Roman consuls, of whom one expelled the other, and presently perished by a wound from a wounded enemy, following a series of blackest murders.

To that epoch let us add also the epoch in which, as Sallust says, equitable and just law still prevailed, that is, until fear of Tarquin and a serious war with Etruria were relieved. For, as long as the Etruscans gave assistance to Tarquin in his attempt to regain the throne, Rome was shaken by a serious war. For this reason, he says, equitable and just law prevailed rather from the pressure of fear than from the influence of justice.
