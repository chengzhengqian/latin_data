Book XIX

And if some say that the Romans in their state served not unclean spirits but good and holy gods, must we repeat again and again the same things that we have already said enough times, nay, more than enough? For what reader who has read the earlier books of this work and has reached this point can doubt that the Romans served evil and impure demons, unless he is either exceedingly stupid or unblushingly argumentative? To say nothing of the character of the gods whom they worshipped with sacrifices, it is written in the law of the true God: “He that sacrifices to any god, save to the Lord only, shall be uprooted.”1 It was not his will that sacrifices should be offered either to good gods, then, or to evil, who laid down this precept with so great a threat.

XXII

Whether the God whom the Christians serve is the true God, to whom only it is right to offer sacrifice.

But the reply may be made: “Who is this God, or how is he proved worthy, and no other god besides, of the worship and sacrifices of the Romans?” He must be very blind who still asks who this God is. He is the very God whose prophets foretold the things that we behold. He is the very God from whom Abraham received the declaration: “By your seed all people shall be blessed.”2 And this was fulfilled in Christ, who arose from that seed after the flesh, as even those acknowledge, whether they will or not, who have remained hostile to this name.
