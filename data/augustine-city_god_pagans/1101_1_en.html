He proved that he had guarded the honour of his wife Sarah in his dealings with Hagar, and had not gratified his own pleasure, but his wife’s will. He had taken Hagar, but had not sought her, mingled with her but not been entangled, made her the receptacle of his seed but not of his love. For he said: “Lo, your maid is in your hands, do with her as you wish.”1 What a man was this, using women in a true manly fashion, his wife soberly, the handmaiden obediently, no woman immoderately!
