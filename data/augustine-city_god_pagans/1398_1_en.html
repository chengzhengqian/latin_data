No man ought to be so completely inactive as not to think of his neighbour’s advantage, nor so active as to neglect the contemplation of God. Inactivity should not mean delight in dull vacancy of mind, but investigation or discovery of truth, so conducted that each man makes some contribution and does not withhold from his fellow what he has discovered.

And in action it is not honour or power in this life that should be prized, since all things under the sun are vanity,1 but the task itself that is accomplished through that same honour and power, if rightly and helpfully performed; that is, if it contributes to the welfare of those who are set below us. This is according to God’s law, with which we have already dealt.2 So the Apostle says: “He that desires the episcopate desires a good work.”3 He wished to show what the word episcopate means: a task, not an honour. In fact, it is a Greek word, derived from the fact that he who is set over others superintends or cares for them; for skopos means attention, and therefore we may if we wish translate episkopein into Latin as “superintend.”4 Thus a bishop who loves preeminence, not good works, should understand that he is no bishop.
