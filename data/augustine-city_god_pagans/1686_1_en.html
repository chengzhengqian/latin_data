Book XXII

XXIV

Of the good things with which the Creator has filled even this condemned life. 1

Now we must consider how even this wretched state of mankind, by which the justice of his punishment is glorified, has been blessed, and how rich and numerous are the blessings due to the same God’s goodness, as he guides all things which he created. First, he chose not to withdraw after the sin that blessing which he granted before the sin, when he said: “Increase and multiply and fill the earth.”2 So the fecundity once granted remained in the condemned race, nor could the flaw of sin by which even the necessity of death is imposed upon us take away the marvellous power of seed, or rather the still more marvellous power by which seeds are made, that was conferred on human bodies and somehow interwoven in our texture. But in this rushing stream of human life two currents, as it were, flow together, the evil derived from Adam and the good bestowed by the Creator. In the original evil there are two parts, sin and punishment.
