Man could not possibly upset the divine plan by his sin, as if he could have compelled God to change what he had decreed, for God through his foreknowledge had anticipated both the coming events, that is, both how bad the man whom he himself had created good would become and what good he himself would use him to effect even so.

God, it is true, is said to change his decrees, and hence we read in Scripture the statement in figurative speech even that God repented.1 But such a statement is based on man’s expectation or on the prospect implicit in the orderly course of natural causes, not on the Almighty’s foreknowledge of what he will do. Thus, as Scripture tells us, God made man upright2 and consequently of good will, for man would not have been upright without a good will. Good will then is the work of God, since man was created in possession of it by him.

On the other hand, the first evil act of will, preceding, as it did, all evil works in man, was rather a falling away from the work of God to the will’s own works than any one work; and those works were evil because they followed the will’s own pattern and not God’s. Thus the will itself, or man himself in so far as he was possessed of an evil will, was the evil tree, as it were, that bore the evil fruit3 that those works represented.
