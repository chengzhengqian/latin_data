XVII

On the rape of the Sabine women and other iniquities that flourished in the Roman state even in the era of her good fame.

Perhaps the divinities did not establish laws for the Roman people because, as Sallust says, “equity and virtue prevailed among them no more by laws than by nature.”1

This “equity and virtue,” I suppose, produced the rape of the Sabine women! What is more just and virtuous than to take other men’s daughters, lured by the trick of presenting a show, without their fathers’ consent, and to carry them off by force, as each man was able? For if the Sabines were wrong to refuse their daughters on request, how much more wrong to kidnap them on refusal! Moreover, it would have been a more righteous war, if they had waged war against a people who refused to give their daughters in marriage to those who belonged to the same country and were their neighbours, than it was when they waged war against the same people seeking return of their daughters after abduction.
