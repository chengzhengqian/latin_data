VI

On the opinion of Varro that God is the world soul, which nevertheless in its various parts has many souls which are of divine nature.

The same Varro, then, still in his introductory remarks about natural theology, says that he thinks that God is the soul of the universe, which the Greeks call cosmos, and that this universe itself is God. But just as a wise man, he says, though consisting of body and mind, is called wise because of his mind, so the universe is called God because of its mind, though it likewise consists of mind and body. Varro here seems somehow to confess that there is one true God, but in order to bring in more he adds that the universe is divided into two parts, heaven and earth, and the heaven is two fold, divided into aether and air, and the earth in turn is divided into water and land. Of these the highest is the aether, the second air, the third water and the fourth earth. All these four parts, he says, are full of souls, immortal souls in the aether and the air, mortal souls in the water and on land. From the highest circle of heaven to the circle of the moon are etherial souls, the constellations and the stars, and these are not only known by our intelligence to exist, but are also visible to our eyes as heavenly gods.
