But above all—and this especially brings me virtually to an admission that Plato was not without knowledge of those books—there is the fact that, when the angel brought the words of God to Moses, and the holy man asked the name of the one who charged him to go and deliver the Hebrew people out of Egypt, the answer was given: “I am he who is, and you are to say to the children of Israel: ‘He-who-is has sent me to you,’”1—signifying that, in comparison with him who really is because he is unchangeable, things which have been created subject to change have no being. This tenet Plato strenuously upheld and most earnestly urged upon others. Yet I do not know whether this statement can be found anywhere in the writings of those who preceded Plato, except where it is said: “I am he who is, and you are to say to them: ‘He-who-is has sent me to you.’”
