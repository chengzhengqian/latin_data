XXVII

On the belief of the Platonists who, granting that the angels were created by God, held that the angels themselves were the creators of human bodies.

When Plato held that lesser gods, who were created by the supreme God, were the makers of the other living beings, he doubtless meant that, while they received the immortal part from God himself, they themselves attached to it the mortal part.2 His view therefore was that they were creators not of our souls but of our bodies. Now Porphyry says that we must avoid all touch of body for the purification of the soul,3 and at the same time he shares with Plato and the other Platonists the belief that those whose lives were licentious and disgraceful return by way of atonement to mortal bodies—even of beasts according to Plato, but only of men according to Porphyry.4 Hence it follows that, if these philosophers are to bestow the name of gods upon those whom they would have us cherish as though they were our parents and creators, these gods are nothing but the artificers of our fetters or prisons, and not our creators but our gaolers, shutting us up in grievous workhouses and binding us fast in most burdensome chains.
