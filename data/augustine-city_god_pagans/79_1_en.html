Certainly he would choose such happiness for his mother, if it were possible.

If we should then put a further question and ask whether he would want such shameful exhibitions among her divine honours, would he not cry out that he preferred to see his mother lying there dead and feeling nothing, rather than see her live on as a goddess for the purpose of relishing such obscenities? God forbid that a Roman senator so serious of purpose that he forbade a theatre to be built in a city of brave men, should want his mother to be honoured as a goddess with such services that as a matron the words would scandalize her. He would hardly believe that the virtuous modesty of a woman of so praiseworthy a character could be so completely reversed by the process of deification that her worshippers would seek her favour in such terms that, if she had while alive among men heard them employed against someone as terms of abuse without stopping her ears and withdrawing, her relatives and husband and children would have blushed for her.
