Finally, when at last, broken by so many and such great plagues, the Egyptians had let the Israelites go, and were pursuing them across the Red Sea, they were themselves wiped out. For the sea had divided and opened a path for the fleeing Israelites, but as the Egyptians pursued, the waves turned back and overwhelmed them.1

After this the people of God wandered for forty years in the wilderness under Moses’ leadership. During this period the tabernacle of testimony was given this name,2 as the tent in which God was worshipped with sacrifices by which future events were foretold. This was, of course, after the law had been given on the mountain in most awe-inspiring fashion, for the divine presence was most clearly attested by marvellous signs and spoken words. Now this took place soon after the departure from Egypt and the beginning of the people’s sojourn in the wilderness, on the fiftieth day after the Paschal feast was celebrated by the sacrifice of a lamb,3 which is so clearly a type of Christ, foretelling that he should pass over from this world to the Father through the sacrifice offered in his passion—for Pascha in the Hebrew language means Passover—that, when the new covenant was revealed after Christ our Passover was sacrificed, it was on the fiftieth day that the Holy Spirit, which is called in the Gospel the finger of God,4 came down from heaven.
