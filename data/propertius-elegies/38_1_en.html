Did not a propitious Love, my darling, in your first days sneeze a clear-sounding omen 11 on your birth? These celestial gifts were surely bestowed by the gods; do not imagine that your mother gave you them. No, no, such endowments are no part of human parentage: ten months never produced those gifts of yours. You were born to be the unique glory of Roman maidens: Helen had this beauty once, and now it comes to earth again. Nor will you always visit human couches in our midst: you will be the first Roman maiden to lie with Jove.

Can I now wonder if all our youth is set on fire by her? Better, O Troy, had it been for her that you perished. Time was I used to marvel that a girl caused the mighty war at Pergamum between Europe and Asia: I see that you, Paris, were wise, and you, too, Menelaus; you for demanding, you for reluctance to comply. 12 Her beauty merited that even Achilles should die for it; even Priam had to approve the cause of the war. 13 If any desires to surpass the ancient painters in renown, let him take my mistress as a model for his art: whether he exhibits her to the lands of the West or the East, he will set on fire both East and West.

  • 11A sneeze was regarded as a good omen (cf. Catullus 45.9, etc.).
  • 12Cf. Ronsard, Sonnets à Hélène: ‘Ménélas fut bien sage et Paris, ce me semble, / L’un de la demander, I’autre de la garder.’
  • 13Cf. Homer, Iliad 3.154-8.
