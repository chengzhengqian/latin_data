Book III

Pegasid Muses, delicate be the garlands you bestow on your poet: an epic crown will not suit my brow.

But what the envious crowd disallows me in my lifetime, Fame will repay with double interest after my death. Time makes all things greater after death: after his funeral a man’s name sounds greater on people’s lips. 3

Else who would know the fortress battered by the firwood horse, 4 the rivers that fought in combat with Thessaly’s hero, Simois of Ida together with Jove’s offspring Scamander, and the chariot that thrice befouled Hector’s body on the plain? And Deiphobus and Helenus and Paris in Polydamas’ armour, sorry figure though he cut, 5 their own country would scarcely know about. Ilion, you would now be little talked of, as you too, Troy, twice taken by the power of Oeta’s god.

Homer also, the chronicler of your fate, has found his reputation grow with the passage of time. I, too, will be praised by late generations of Rome: I myself predict that after I am ashes such a day will come. Not neglected shall be the grave where the tombstone marks my bones: so decrees the Lycian god, who approves my prayer.

  • 3Ezra Pound has ‘I shall have, doubtless, a boom after my funeral, / Seeing that long standing increases all things regardless of quality.’
  • 4I.e. the Trojan Horse, cf. Virgil, Aen. 2.16 sectaque intexunt abiete costas.
  • 5No such incident is elsewhere reported, but the poet was probably thinking of Paris’ inglorious combat with Menelaus (Homer, Il. 3.324ff) and misremembered that it was Lycaon’s armour, not Polydamas’, that he borrowed.
