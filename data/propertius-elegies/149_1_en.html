Book IV

What then was Rome when the trumpeter of Cures made quake with his lingering blast Jove’s neighbouring cliffs? For a wall there were hills; where buildings now hedge in the Senate House, the war-horse drank from the spring there, and where justice is now dispensed to a subject world, Sabine javelins stood in the Roman forum.

A lush grove there was, ensconced in an ivy-mantled glen, and many a tree rustled in answer to the splash of natural rills, the leafy abode of Silvanus, whither in refuge from the heat melodious pipes directed the sheep to drink. Opposite this grove Tatius fences his camp with a stockade of maple and, for more assurance, rings it with a rampart of earth.

Here Tarpeia drew water for her goddess; and an urn of earthenware pressed squarely on her head. She saw Tatius manoeuvring on the sandy plain and uplifting his blazoned arms over his horse’s golden mane: she was stupefied by the king’s looks and his kingly armour, and the urn dropped from between her heedless hands.

Often she feigned some untoward aspect of the blameless moon and declared that she must bathe her locks in running water; often she brought silvery lilies to the winsome nymphs that Romulus’ spear not mar the looks of Tatius: and in climbing the Capitol misted with the first smoke of dusk, she brought home arms torn by the rough brambles and, sitting down, thus from her 42 citadel
