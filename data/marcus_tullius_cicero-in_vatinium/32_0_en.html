Both otium and dignitas could have more than one meaning. When used of individuals otium meant privacy or retirement as opposed to activity. It could be used of a man who eschewed public life, as of one who had retired from it after playing a full part. When used of public life otium stood for tranquillity and freedom from civil discord. For example, Cicero’s letter to Pompey of 62 b.c. (Ad fam. v. 7) expresses gratification that his dispatches from the East hold out a hope of freedom from political disturbance (otium) on his return. Balsdon (op. cit. p. 47) thinks that, as a natural extension of this, otium could be used for acceptance of the existing political and social conditions and of the traditional working of the Republican government, of which the fundamenta and membra were listed in Pro Sestio (§ 98), “religiones . . . aerarium.”

There was a dignitas not only of individuals but also of the State. When used of individuals it meant the rank, prestige and honour of a Roman public man, what its possessor claimed as his due.
