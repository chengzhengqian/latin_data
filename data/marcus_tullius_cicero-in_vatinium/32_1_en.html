This dignitas had been outraged by Caesar’s methods in 59 b.c., and by the gangsterism of Clodius not only when he was tribune (58 b.c.) and aedile (56 b.c.) but also when out of office in 57 b.c.

The earliest association of otium and dignitas in what survives of Cicero’s works is found in his public speech of thanks made immediately after his return from exile in September, 57 b.c. In praising Pompey for the part which he had played in securing his return, he said that he had conferred not only on Cicero himself but also on the whole State, salutem, otium, dignitatem (Post reditum ad populum, § 16). The appearance of the phrase cum dignitate otium is rare and is limited to the years 56-54 b.c. First, in the Pro Sestio we find it and its variant, otiosa dignitas, on three occasions (§§ 98, 99, 104). Secondly, in the De Oratore, finished in 55 b.c., we find otio cum dignitate (§ 1). Lastly, cum dignitate otium appears in the celebrated letter (Ad fam. i. 9. 21) which Cicero wrote in 54 b.c. to P. Lentulus Spinther to explain not only the political change which he would find on his return from his proconsulship in Cilicia, but also that in Cicero’s own position since Lentulus’ departure at the end of 57 b.c.
