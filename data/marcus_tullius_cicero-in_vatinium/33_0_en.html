The literal rendering offered on p. 169 and elsewhere, “peace with dignity,” may mean a salutary condition for the benefit of the whole State: peace and quietness for the masses, freedom from civil discord, “a responsible, effective, and respected government” (Balsdon, op. cit. p. 49). No programme of political or social reform was put forward in the Pro Sestio, for the speech was not basically a defence of the status quo, but a call for “the defence of lex and ius against vis“ (Lacey, op. cit. p. 68), and a plea for the justification of armed resistance in defence of the State against terrorism.

Cicero then turns to the Senate as the keystone of “the constitution so wisely established by our ancestors” (§ 137). His words contrast the transient magistrate and the permanent Senate; they insist that the Senate is to be composed of ex-magistrates who have been chosen by the whole electorate for merit (industria et virtus); and they imply a belief that the Senate was open to plebeians from the beginning of the Republic.a Thus the Senate was to be no narrow oligarchy, but representative of all that was best in the citizen-body, merit and not birth being the qualification for it. It is noteworthy that such a Senate bears a striking resemblance to the Senate which Sulla planned to set up in supreme control of the State and to which he proposed to give representative character by popular election.b
