Since Cicero repeatedly accuses Clodius of having repealed, by a law passed during his tribunate, the lex Aelia Fufia, the earliest known statute regulating the observance of auspices (auspicatio), some account may be attempted of the theory and practice of the auspices in the Ciceronian Age, as a preliminary to a statement of the difficulty presented by the law of Clodius.

The use of auspicia, which was possibly in part of oriental origin, was, as evidence shows, widely practised from early times in Italy, among Etruscans, Umbrians and Romans. The Romans divided auspicia into two classes, impetrativa and oblativa.b Auspicia impetrativa were omens which were definitely sought; auspicia oblativa came unsought. In theory these two classes comprised several varieties: signs from the flight of birds, from the movement or sounds of animals, from thunder or lightning, or from the behaviour of special chickens.
