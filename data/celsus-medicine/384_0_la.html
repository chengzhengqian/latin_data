praepositisque is, quae in qualibet corporis parte fiunt (capp. i–v), ad ea, quae proprias sedes habent, transibo.

1. Luxata igitur, in quacumque parte corporis sunt, quam primum sic curari debent, ut, qua dolor est, ea scalpello cutis crebro incidatur, detergeaturque eodem averso profluens sanguis. Quod si paulo tardius subvenitur iamque etiam rubor est, qua rubet corpus si tumor quoque accessit, quacumque is 2 est, id optimum auxilium est. Tum superdanda reprimentia sunt, maximeque lana sucida ex aceto et oleo. Quod si levior is casus est, possunt etiam sine scalpello inposita eadem mederi; et si nihil aliud est, cinis quoque maxime ex sarmentis (si is non est, quilibet alius) ex aceto vel etiam ex aqua coactus.

2. Verum hoc quidem promptum est: in iis autem negotium maius est, quae per se vitio intus orto intumescunt et ad suppurationem spectant. Ea omnia genera abscessum esse alias (V. 28. 11) proposui. medicamentaque his idonea executus sum: nunc superest, ut dicam in iisdem quae manu fieri debeant.   • cLuxata. This word is regularly used in Latin writers to mean displacements or dislocations (Cato, R.R. 157; Seneca, Ep. 104. 18; Pliny, N.H., 31. 6. 37, etc.); but Celsus, who only uses the word here, never applies it to dislocations when describing these lesions in Book VIII. 11, and the description given here suggests that he has in view rather injuries from blows as in boxing, where the use of the caestus caused abrasions of the skin as well as subcutaneous haemorrhage (ecchymosis), and where immediate incision prevented widespread suppuration. See Hippocrates III. 30 (Head Wounds, XIII. 21). V. d. Linden emended to vexata which would have the more general meaning of parts injured.
