Epigrams Book IX

94

Hippocrates 157 gave me cups drugged with Santonian twig 158 and (the impudence of the fellow!) asks me for mead. Glaucus, methinks even you were never so stupid, who presented gold armor to him that gave you bronze. 159 Does anybody ask for a sweet gift in return for a bitter one? Let him take it, but only if he drinks it in hellebore. 160

95

Athenagoras was Alfius formerly, but has now begun to be Olfius since he married a wife. 161

95 b

You ask 162 Athenagoras’ real name, Callistratus. Damned if I know who Athenagoras is. But suppose I am giving a real name, Callistratus. The fault is not with me but with your 163 Athenagoras.

  • 157Name of the founder of medicine. See Appendix B.
  • 158Absinth.
  • 159Diomedes, in the Iliad (6.234). Homer says that Zeus deprived Glaucus of his wits.
  • 160A supposed cure for madness.
  • 161His supposed name was Alfius Athenagoras, presumably a freedman, Alfius being a recognized gentile name. The point of the epigram is in doubt, but the best guess, as I now think, is Calderini’s: Olfius = cunnilingus (olfacere = “smell”).
  • 162 Quaeris is the right choice. The question is answered in verse 2. “Actually,” says M., “I was not writing about a real person. There is no Athenagoras. But suppose there was, and that I attacked him, not (as you think) under a pseudonym, but under his real name: it would be his own fault for doing what I said he did.” Credis makes little sense. Why should C. believe that A. is a real name if he did not know who A. was?
  • 163The plural vester implies that others beside Callistratus had made the enquiry.
