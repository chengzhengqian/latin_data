Epigrams Book XII

60

Day, child of Mars, on which I first saw the rosy lamp and the mighty countenance of the starry god, 87 if you are ashamed to be celebrated in the country and at green altars whom once I worshipped in Latium’s city: 88 pardon me if I do not choose to be a servant on my own Kalends and wish to live 89 on the day I was born. Am I to turn pale on my birthday for fear Sabellus doesn’t get enough hot water 90 and to filter turbid Caecuban through an anxious bag so that Alauda drinks his wine clear? Am I to go to and fro among my tables receiving this guest and that, getting to my feet all through the meal and treading marble colder than ice? 91 Where is the sense of enduring and suffering all this voluntarily, things which you would refuse if your patron and lord 92 ordered you?

61

You are afraid, Ligurra, of my writing verses against you, a brief, lively poem, and you long to seem worthy of such an apprehension. But idle is your fear and idle your desire. Libyan lions roar at bulls, they do not trouble butterflies. I

  • 88M. has chosen to celebrate his birthday Kalends of March at his Nomentan villa (rure, v. 3) instead of in town. He is not in Spain, where he would not have had the choice.
  • 89M. constantly returns to this idea: cf. 2.90.3; 5.20.11.
  • 90To mix with his wine.
  • 91Diners reclined barefoot, but M. could have asked for his slippers (cf. 3.50.3; 8.59.14). Perhaps he was in too much of a hurry. I take vv. 7—12 as a series of questions; cf. Cic. Att. 12.44.2 quid enim? sedere totos dies in villa?
