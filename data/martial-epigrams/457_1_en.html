Epigrams Book XII

32

I saw your movables, Vacerra, you disgrace of July’s Kalends, 44 yes, I saw them. They were not impounded in lieu of two years’ rent, so your wife with her seven red curls and your white-headed mother, and your burly sister were carrying them. I thought the Furies had come out from the darkness of Dis. These three went in front and you followed, parched with cold and hunger, paler than old boxwood, the Irus of your times. One would have thought that Aricia’s slope 45 was moving house. Along went a three-footed truckle bed and a two-footed table, and a broken chamberpot was pissing from a chip in the side together with a lamp and a cornel-wood bowl. The neck of a flagon was lying under a green brazier. An obscenely stinking jug confessed that it had contained salt pickerel or worthless sprats, a smell such as the reek of a marine fishpond farts. Nor was there wanting a slice of Tolosan cheese, nor a four-year-old wreath of black pennyroyal, nor ropes bare of garlic and onions, nor your mother’s pot full of foul turpentine, with which Summemmius’ 46 wives depilate themselves. Why do you look for a house and make mock of superintendents 47 when you could lodge for nothing, Vacerra? This procession of movables is fit for the bridge. 48

  • 44Quarter-day, as it were.
  • 45Where beggars took their stand: cf. 2.19.3.
  • 46Cf. 1.34.6n.
  • 47By asking to rent a lodging without money to pay for it.
  • 48I.e. a bridge where beggars resort; cf. 10.5.3.
