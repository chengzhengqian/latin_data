On the Spectacles

8 (6b)

Illustrious Fame used to sing of the lion laid low in Nemea’s spacious vale, Hercules’ work. Let ancient testimony be silent, for after your shows, Caesar, we have now seen such things done by women’s valor.

9 (7)

As Prometheus, bound on Scythian crag, fed the tireless bird with his too abundant breast, so did Laureolus, 20 hanging on no sham cross, give his naked flesh to a Caledonian bear. His lacerated limbs lived on, dripping gore, and in all his body, body there was none. Finally he met with the punishment he deserved; the guilty wretch had plunged a sword into his father’s throat or his master’s, or in his madness had robbed a temple of its secret gold, or laid a cruel torch to Rome. The criminal had outdone the misdeeds of ancient story; in him, what had been a play became an execution.

10 (8)

Daedalus, when you are being thus torn by a Lucanian bear, how you wish you now had your wings! 21

  • 20A robber who had been crucified and torn to pieces by wild beasts. A mime on the subject was performed under Caligula (Juv. 8.187; Suet. Cal. 57.4). The death was now enacted “for real” by a criminal in the amphitheater.
  • 21Daedalus will have been a criminal or beast-fighter; cf. 1.43.14.
