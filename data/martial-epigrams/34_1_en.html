Epigrams Book I

52

Quintianus, I commend you my little books—that is, however, if I can call them mine when your poet friend recites them. If they complain of harsh enslavement, come forward to claim their freedom 65 and give bail as required. And when he calls himself their owner, say they are mine, discharged from my hand. 66 If you shout this three or four times, you will make the kidnapper ashamed of himself.

53

Fidentinus, there is one page of yours in my little book, only one, but stamped with the sure likeness of its owner; 67 and it holds your poems up to scorn as a manifest theft. So a Lingonian overcoat put among city tyrianthines 68 contaminates them with its greasy wool; so crocks from Arretium 69 dishonor crystal glasses; so a black raven, wandering mayhap on Cayster’s banks among Leda’s swans, becomes a laughingstock; so, when a sacred wood is alive with many toned nightingales, an impudent magpie grates upon their Cecropian laments. My books need no informer, no judge: your page confronts you and says “You are a thief.”

  • 65As assertor in libertatem, who takes up their claim to freedom, not allowing the plagiarist to claim them when manumitted by M.
  • 66“To send forth from the hand” was to make free a slave. So, in another sense, a book on publication is sent forth from the hand.
  • 67Copies of a book sometimes carried a portrait of the author (14.186).
  • 68“Cloths dyed first violet and then Tyrian purple (a kind of scarlet)” OLD.
  • 69Cf. 14.98.
