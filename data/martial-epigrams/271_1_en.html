Epigrams Book VIII

1

Book, about to enter the laurelled 1 dwelling of our Lord, learn to speak more chastely with modest utterance. Nude Venus, avaunt! This little book is not yours. Do you come to me, you, Caesar’s Pallas.

2

When Janus, progenitor and parent of our annals, 2 lately saw Hister’s conqueror, he thought his many faces 3 too few and wished to have more eyes. And speaking with all his tongues in unison, he promised the Lord and God of the world four times the Pylian 4 length of days. 5 We ask you, Father Janus, to add your own.

3

“Five had been enough; six or seven little books is already too much. Why, Muse, 6 do you wish to frolic further? Let there be some shame, and an end. Fame can confer nothing more on me now. My books are thumbed everywhere;

  • 1Because of the Emperor’s recent victories on the Danube.
  • 2The god Janus presided over the year and the public records.
  • 3Four, representing the four seasons (sometimes only two); cf. 10.28.6.
  • 4Nestor’s.
  • 5So 10.38.14. But here M. may have Janus’ four tongues in mind.
  • 6Thalia, Muse of comedy and light verse (v. 10).
