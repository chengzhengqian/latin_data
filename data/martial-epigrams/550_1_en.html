Epigrams Book XIV

169. Same

Why do noisy rings 150 wander round the wide circle? So that the crowd in their path may yield to the tinkling hoops.

170. Gold statue of Victory 151

She is given without drawing of lot to him to whom Rhine has given a true name. Ten times pour the Falernian, boy. 152

171. “Brutus’ Boy” in clay

Not dim is the glory of so small a figurine. 153 This is the boy Brutus loved.

172. Lizard slayer in Corinthian bronze 154

Spare the lizard, insidious boy, as she creeps toward you; she wants to die by your fingers.

  • 150Rings were often strung round the orbit of a boy’s hoop: see a picture taken from a sepulchral bas-relief at Tivoli reproduced in Rich’s Dict. Ant. s.v. “Anulus.” How the hoop was able to run is very obscure (K).
  • 151This, and the following, describe sigilla (statuettes) which were frequently given at the Saturnalia, i.e. on the last two days, which were called sigillaria: cf. Auson. De Fer. Rom 32 (festa sigillorum nomine dicta colunt) (K).
  • 152To represent the letters of Germanicus (Domitian): cf. 9.93.
  • 153Cf. 2.77.4; 9.50.5. There may be a play on two senses of amator, which can also mean “fan” (cf. “amateur”). Cf. Pliny N.H. 34.82 puerum, quem amando Brutus Philippiensis cognomine suo illustravit.
  • 154On a replica of a work of Praxiteles representing the young Apollo with an arrow watching a lizard. It was called Σανρόκτονος (“Lizard-killer”): Pliny N.H. 34.70.
