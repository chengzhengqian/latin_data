Epigrams Book V

50

Whenever I dine at home, Charopinus, if I haven’t invited you, immediately there’s a deadly feud, and you could skewer me with your drawn sword if you know that my fireplace has warmed up without you. So am I not to be allowed to cheat just once? That gullet of yours, Charopinus, is the most exorbitant thing in creation. I beg you, give up watching my kitchen and let my cook bamboozle you once in a way.

51

This fellow whose left hand is loaded with documents, who is surrounded by a smooth-faced band of stenographers, who lends a grave face to the notebooks and letters proffered from all sides, like to Cato or Tully or Brutus—though the fiddle strings 108 constrain him, he can’t say “have” in Latin or“chaire” in Greek. If you think I am making this up, let’s say good morning. 109

52

I remember all you have done for me and shall ever keep it in mind. Why then do I say nothing about it, Postumus? You talk. Whenever I start to tell somebody of your generosity, he exclaims at once: “He told me that himself.” Some things are not nicely done by two. One is enough for this work. If you want me to talk, you must hold your tongue.

  • 108A method of torture.
  • 109Naturally it is not because he does not know the words that he does not answer (as M. pretends to believe), but out of surliness; cf. 4.83.3.
