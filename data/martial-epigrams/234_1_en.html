Epigrams Book VII

13

Hearing that the ivory of an ancient tusk turns white in the suns of Tibur, 18 dusky Lycoris went to Hercules’ hills. How potent is the air of lofty Tibur! In a short time she returned black. 19

14

An unspeakable villainy has afflicted my girl, Aulus: she has lost her plaything and pet—not such as tender Catullus’ mistress wept for, Lesbia, bereaved of her sparrow’s naughty tricks, or such as Ianthis, sung by my Stella, bewailed, 20 whose black dove flies in Elysium. My beloved is not won by trifles or by such loves 21 as those nor do such losses move my lady’s heart. She has lost a boy numbering twice six 22 years, whose cock was not yet eighteen inches long.

  • 18The sulphurous exhalations of the springs at Tibur (cf. 4.42) were supposed to have the property of bleaching things, especially ivory.
  • 19M. had probably forgotten that he had published a prototype of this epigram (4.62) using the same name, Lycoris. Cf. also 1.72.6.
  • 20I.e. on whose grief for her dead dove Stella wrote his poem (cf. 1.7).
  • 21The manuscript reading neque moribus istis (“nor by such ways”) makes poor sense, and M. never has neque before a consonant in dactylic meter (always nec).
  • 22Some editors keep the absurd denos, making the boy twenty years old.
