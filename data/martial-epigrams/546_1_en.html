Epigrams Book XIV

150. Damask blankets (cubicularia) 130

The land of Memphis gives you this present; Babylon’s Needle 131 is surpassed now by the comb 132 of the Nile.

151. Girdle

I am long enough at present; but if your stomach should swell with a sweet burden, I should then be too short a girdle for you.

152. A square frieze rug

The country of poet Catullus will send you blankets (lodices); we are from the region of Helicaon. 133

153. Apron

Let a rich man give you a tunic; I can gird you in front. If I were rich, I should give you both presents.

154. Amethystine wool

Since I am drunk with the blood of a Sidonian shellfish, I do not see why I am called sober wool. 134

  • 130Damask is a variety of twill (cf. 14.143), and depends upon the number of warp threads (generally four) intersected by the weft (K).
  • 131Babylon was celebrated for embroidery in color; the art of many-threaded work (polymita) came from Alexandria: cf. Pliny N.H. 8.196 (K).
  • 132A weaver’s reed [or sley] (K).
  • 133Paduan: cf. 10.93.1.
  • 134“Amethyst” etymologically means “unintoxicated,” either, as Pliny says (N.H. 37.121), because it did not approximate to the color of wine, or because its possession was supposed to be an antidote against inebriety. There are similar Greek epigrams in Pal. Anth. 9.748 and 752 (K).
