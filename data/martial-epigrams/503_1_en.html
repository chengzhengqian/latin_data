Epigrams Book XIII

86. Sea urchins

Though the sea urchin 84 pricks the fingers with its sharp shell, once the carapace is put aside, it will be soft.

87. Murexes

Ingrate, you don cloaks dyed in our blood 85 and that is not enough: we are something to eat. 86

88. Gobies

In Venetian parts, though the dinner may be elegant, the Goby 87 is generally the start of the meal.

89. Sea bass

The woolly bass 88 breasts the mouths of Euganean Timavus, fed on fresh water with sea salt

  • 84It was eaten with vinegar and honey sauce, parsley, and mint (Athen. 91 a).
  • 85Heinsius’ conjecture bis (“twice”) for de, adopted in my edition, is attractive but not necessary.
  • 86Pliny says that luxury had made the fish as precious as pearls. Travellers speak of a hill still standing at Tarentum of the debris of the murex. Cf. Col. 8.16.7; Hor. Sat. 2.4.32 (K).
  • 87Small fish, ordinarily little esteemed, but common in the lagoons of Venice. Inferior to a blenny: Diog. L. 2.67. Juv. (11.37) treats the price of a gobius as an insignificant sum, as compared with the price of a mullus. The name gobio includes also the freshwater gudgeon (K).
  • 88The most prized lupi were called lanati or lanei from the whiteness and softness of their flesh: Pliny N.H. 9.57, 61, 169. The lupus is the sea bass, one name of which is the sea wolf, from its rapaciousness (K).
