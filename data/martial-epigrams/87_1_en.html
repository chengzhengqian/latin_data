Epigrams Book II

answer, 85 neither does pretty Attis dictate me a weakly effeminate galliambus. 86 That does not automatically make me a bad poet, Classicus. Would you tell Ladas to mount against his will the narrow path of a trapeze? It’s demeaning to make difficulties out of trifles, and labor over frivolities is foolish. Let Palaemon write poems for the crowd. I like to please uncommon ears.

87

You say that pretty girls are on fire with love for you, Sextus, you that have the face of a man swimming under water.

88

You recite nothing and want to be considered a poet, Mamercus. Be what you like, so long as you recite nothing.

89

You enjoy stretching the evening with overmuch wine. That I forgive; you have Cato’s bad habit, 87 Gaurus. You write verses without Apollo and the Muses. You deserve praise; this habit you have of Cicero’s. Your vomiting: a way of Antony’s. Your extravagance: of Apicius’s. Your sucking: tell me, whose bad habit is that?

  • 85 Versus echoici seem to be elegiac verses in which the first words of the hexameter are repeated at the end of the couplet, as in Anth. Lat. 226–227 (Bailey = 234–235 Riese).
  • 86The Attis of Catullus is in this meter.
  • 87On Cato the Younger’s drinking see Pliny Ep. 3.12.
