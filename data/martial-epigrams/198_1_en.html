Epigrams Book VI

13

Julia, who would not think you molded by Phidias’ chisel or a work of Pallas’ artistry? The white lygdus 11 matches 12 with a speaking likeness, and living beauty shines in your face. Your hand plays, but not roughly, with the Acidalian knot 13 that it snatched from little Cupid’s neck. To win back Mars’ love and the supreme Thunderer’s, 14 let Juno and Venus herself ask you for the girdle.

14

You declare you can write good verses, Laberius. Why won’t you then? Let somebody capable of writing good verses not write them, Laberius, and I’ll think him a stout fellow. 15

15

As an ant was wandering in Phaethontic shade, 16 a drop of amber enfolded the tiny creature. So she that was despised but lately while life remained has now been made precious by her death.

  • 11Parian marble from the Cyclades.
  • 12 respondet = “corresponds to the original”; usually understood as “answers” (the questions in vv. 1–2; but these are rhetorical, not calling for any reply).
  • 13The girdle (cestus) of Venus, which inspired love.
  • 14In the Iliad (14.214ff) Hera (Juno) borrows Aphrodite’s (Venus’) girdle before consorting with Zeus (Jupiter). For Mars’ vagaries cf. 6.21.5.
  • 15I.e. I don’t believe such a persons exists (SB 3).
  • 16I.e. in the shade of a poplar.
