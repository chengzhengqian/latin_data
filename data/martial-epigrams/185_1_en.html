Epigrams Book V

64

Callistus, pour in a double double 121 of Falernian. Alcimus, melt summer snow over it. Let my soaked hair be sleek with excess of unguent and my temples wearied by stitched roses. The Mausoleum 122 so close at hand tells us to live, teaching that the very gods can perish.

65

The stars and sky were given to Alcides in his stepmother’s despite by the terror of Nemea, and the Arcadian boar, and the chastised mud 123 of the Libyan wrestling ring, and massy Eryx stretched in Sicilian dust, and Cacus, scare of the woods, who by a stealthy trick used to draw oxen backwards 124 into his lair. How small a part, Caesar, is all this of the show in your arena! Each new day that dawns provides greater combats. How many masses heavier than Nemea’s monster fall! How many Maenalian pigs does your spear lay low! If the triple fight of the Spanish herdsman were reenacted, you have one 125 who can vanquish Geryon. Though the beast of Grecian Lerna be counted often, 126 what is the abominable Hydra to the wild creatures of the Nile? For such deserts, Augustus, the gods gave Alcides heaven speedily; but to you they will give it late. 127

  • 121Four cyathi, the sextans being equal to two cyathi.
  • 122The Mausoleum of Augustus (described by Strabo 5.3), which M. could see from his home on the Quirinal: cf. 2.59.
  • 123Cf. 4.19.5n. It stands here for the defeated wrestler Antaeus.
  • 124I.e. by their tails, to elude pursuers.
  • 125Carpophorus.
  • 126When one of the Hydra’s heads was cut off by Hercules, two grew in its place.
  • 127I.e. that you may live long to benefit earth.
