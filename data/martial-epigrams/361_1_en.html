But mind you don’t knock tipsily on the eloquent door at the wrong time. He gives all his days to sour Minerva, at work on compositions which future ages will be able to compare even with Arpi’s pages, 31 for the ears of the Hundred Men. 32 It will be safer for you to go when the late lamps are lit. This is your hour, when Lyaeus runs wild, when the rose is queen and hair is damp. Then let even stiff Catos read me. 33

21

Sextus, why, may I ask, do you like to write stuff that Modestus himself or Claranus would have trouble understanding? Your books need, not a reader, but an Apollo. 34 In your judgment Cinna was greater than Maro. On that basis by all means let your poems be praised; but let mine, Sextus, please commentators so far as, having none, they may.

22

Do you ask, Philaenis, why I often go out with a plaster on my chin or my healthy lips painted with white lead? I don’t want to kiss you.
