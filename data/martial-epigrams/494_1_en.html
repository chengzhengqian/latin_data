Epigrams Book XIII

44. Sow’s udder

You would think it not yet udder; 44 in such abundance flows the pap, swelling with living milk.

45. Chickens

If I had Libyan birds 45 and pheasants, you should receive them. But as it is, receive fowl of the farmyard.

46 Early peaches 46

On our mother’s branches we had been peaches of little worth; now, on adoptive boughs, we are peaches of price.

47. Picene loaves

Picene bread grows bigger with its white nectar, as a light sponge swells when it has taken water. 47

  • 44I.e. not yet the cooked dish but part of the living animal.
  • 45Guinea fowl.
  • 46According to Friedländer, peaches grafted on an apricot tree; cf. Calp. 2.42, of peaches grafted on a plum tree. Rather perhaps on a superior kind of peach (Steier, RE XIX.1.1025).
  • 47According to Pliny, Picenian bread was made of spelt (alica), steeped for nine days, then mixed with raisin juice, and kneaded into the shape of a spool of wool (in speciem tractae), and then baked. He adds that it was not fit to eat till it had been moistened with milk mixed with honey (mulsum). Pliny N.H. 18.50, 71, 106, 109, 112, and elsewhere (K).
