Epigrams Book XII

47 (46)

Gallus and Lupercus sell their poems. Now, Classicus, tell me that poets are insane.

48

If you serve mushrooms and boar as everyday fare and don’t imagine that these are the object of my prayers, well and good; but if you think I am getting rich and want to be made my heir because of five Lucrine oysters, good-bye. “But it’s a fine dinner”: very fine, I confess, but tomorrow it will be nothing, or rather today, or rather a moment from now it will be nothing; a matter for a luckless sponge on a doomed mop stick 65 or some dog or other 66 or a crock by the roadside 67 to take care of. 68 That is what mullet and hares and sow’s udder come to—that and a pasty complexion and feet that play torturer. No Alban revel 69 would be worth it to me, no Capitoline or Pontifical banquets. 70 Should a god himself debit me with nectar, it would turn to vinegar and the treacherous, flat content of a Vatican jar. Feastmaster, look for other guests to be captivated by the proud despotism of your table. As for me, let a friend invite me to potluck morsels. I like a dinner I can return.

  • 65Used for sanitary purposes.
  • 66 Qui ad vomitum accurrit—Schrevelius.
  • 67Set by the roadside as a urinal. Cf. 6.93.2.
  • 68Lit. “know about,” here used idiomatically if the text is sound (see SB 3).
  • 69Such as Domitian gave at his Alban villa.
  • 70Twice annually a “banquet of Capitoline Jupiter” was held for the three resident deities, Jupiter, Juno, and Minerva. The sumptious dinners of the College of Pontiffs were proverbial (Hor. Od. 2.14.28; cf. Macrob. Sat. 3.13.12).
