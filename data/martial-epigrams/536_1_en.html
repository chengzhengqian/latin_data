Epigrams Book XIV

104. Bag for straining through snow

My linen also knows how to reduce snow: no colder spurts the water from your strainer.

105. Small jugs for table service

Cold water will not be lacking, nor warm either, when you ask for it. But forbear to dally with a pernickety thirst. 91

106. Earthenware jug

This red jug with arched handle 92 is presented to you. With this Fronto the Stoic used to ask for cold water.

107. Goblets 93

The Satyrs love us, Bacchus loves us, and so does the tipsy tigress that has been taught to lick her master’s 94 wine-drenched feet.

108. Saguntine cups

Take cups made of Saguntan clay which your servant may hold and keep without anxiety. 95

  • 91I.e. do not be fussy about the precise temperature.
  • 92Cf. OLD pandus “of things with downward turned extremities.”
  • 93Cups or jugs called calathi (“baskets”).
  • 94Bacchus.
  • 95M. speaks disparagingly of Saguntine clayware; cf. 8.6.2; Pliny praises it, at least for cups: N.H. 35.160 (K).
