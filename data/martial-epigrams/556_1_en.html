Epigrams Book XIV

196. Calvus 172 on the use of cold water

These pages that tell you of fountains and the names of rivers were better swimming in their own waters. 173

197. Dwarf mules

You need not be afraid of falling off these mules. You are almost higher up sitting on the ground.

198. Gallic lapdog

If you want to hear of the pretty tricks of the little lapdog, a whole page is too short for me in the telling. 174

199. Jennet

This little Asturian horse 175 that picks up its fleet hooves in rhythm came from gold-bearing peoples.

200. Greyhound 176

The keen greyhound hunts not for himself but for his master; he will bring you a hare unharmed by his tooth.

  • 172Presumed to be C. Licinius Calvus, Catullus’ friend and fellow poet, with whom he is regularly associated. The work in question is unknown, and M.’s uncomplimentary judgment surprises. If this were a different Calvus, the juxtaposition with Catullus could be a joke on M.’s part.
  • 173From which the papyrus came.
  • 174Cf. M.’s description of Issa (1.109).
  • 175This small horse had a special sort of trot (Pliny N.H. 8.166).
  • 176 Vertragus, a Celtic word, probably meaning “fast runner.”
