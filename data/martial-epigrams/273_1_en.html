Epigrams Book VIII

5

In giving rings to girls, Macer, you have ceased to possess rings, Macer. 11

6

Nothing is so boring as old Euctus’ originals (I had rather have cups shaped from Saguntine clay), when he rehearses the smoky pedigrees 12 of his silver and turns the wine moldy with his chatter: “These goblets once belonged to Laomedon’s table: Apollo built the walls with his lyre to get them. With this mixing bowl fierce Rhoecus commenced battle with the Lapiths; you see how the piece was damaged in the struggle. These two bases are valuable because of long-lived Nestor; the dove shines, polished by the Pylian thumb. Here we have a bowl in which Aeacus’ grandson 13 bade more and livelier wine to be mixed for his friends. In this dish fairest Dido pledged Bitias, when she gave dinner to the Phrygian hero.” 14 After you have much admired the antique embossments, in Priam’s vessels you will drink—Astyanax. 15

  • 11I.e. you have lost your qualification as a knight: cf. Juv. 11.43. The ius anulorum (right to wear a gold ring) was possessed by senators, knights, and magistrates.
  • 12The pedigrees (lists of previous owners) of his treasures hung in Euctus’ hall like the family tree with death-mask portraits in the hall of a Roman nobleman (cf. 2.90.6 and OLD fumosus).
  • 13Achilles: cf. Hom. Il. 9.203.
  • 14Aeneas: cf. Virg. Aen. 1.738.
  • 15I.e. something immature.
