Epigrams Book II

29

Rufus, you see that person lounging in the front rows, 28 whose hand with its sardonyx gleams even from here, and his cloak that has so often drunk up Tyre, and his gown, commanded to outdo untrodden snow, whose oily hair can be smelt all over Marcellus’ theater 29 and whose plucked arms shine smooth, on whose crescent-bearing shoe rests a shoestrap not of yesterday, 30 whose unchafed foot is decked with scarlet leather, whose starred brow is plastered by many a patch. 31 Don’t you know what he is? Remove the patches: you will read.

30

I happened to ask a loan of twenty thousand sesterces, no burdensome present even as a gift. He of whom I asked it was a faithful old friend, 32 whose coffer whips up his ample wealth. 33 Says he to me: “You’ll be a rich man if you plead cases.” Give me what I ask, Gaius; I’m not asking advice.

31

I have often fucked Chrestina. You ask how well she gives? To the limit of the possible, Marianus.

  • 28Reserved for senators.
  • 29South of the Circus Flaminius. Begun by Julius Caesar, and finished by Augustus, who dedicated it in 11 b.c. in the name of Marcellus, his nephew who died young.
  • 30That is, brand-new, not twenty-four hours old. The man was pretending to be a senator.
  • 31Often used to set off beauty (cf. 8.33.32), here to hide the marks of the branding iron.
  • 32I.e. he had been; cf. 2.43.15; 5.19.9. The alternative reading felix, “prosperous,” may be right, but it anticipates v. 4.
  • 33I.e. makes it increase. The coffer would contain records of money lent at interest as well as coin and other valuables (SB 3).
