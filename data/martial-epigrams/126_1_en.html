3

See how dense a fleece of silent waters flows down on Caesar’s countenance and bosom. Yet he humors Jove, and without moving his head laughs at the moisture congealed by paralyzing frost. For he is wont with dripping locks to weary the stars of Hyperborean Bootes and ignore Helice. Who sports with dry waters and plays games from heaven? I suspect these snows come from Caesar’s child. 6

4

The odor given off by a marsh from its dry bed, 7 or the vapors of raw Albulae, or the stale reek of a salt-water fishpond, or a sluggish billy goat on top of his nanny, or the boot of a weary veteran, or a fleece twice stained with purple dye, 8 or the Sabbath fastings of Jewish women, or the sighs of unhappy men on trial, or the sputtering lamp of dirty Leda, or wrestlers’ mud from Sabine dregs, or a fox in flight, 9 or a viper’s lair—I would sooner smell of any of these than of your smell, Bassa.

  • 6Who died in infancy and is assumed to have been deified.
  • 7Or, with the vulgate siccae . . . lacernae, “by the rushes of a dry hole.”
  • 8The purple dye extracted from a shellfish (murex) gave garments a disagreeable smell; cf. 1.49.32; 9.62.
  • 9Foxes in flight were believed to emit a stench to check their pursuers.
