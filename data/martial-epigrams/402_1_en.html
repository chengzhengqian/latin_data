Epigrams Book XI

4

Sacred emblems and household gods of the Phrygians, which Troy’s heir 8 chose to rescue leaving Laomedon’s wealth 9 to the flames, and Jupiter, now for the first time depicted in everlasting gold, 10 and you the sister and you the daughter, 11 wholly his, of the supreme Father, and you, Janus, who now for the third time bring back the name of Nerva to the purple annals, 12 to you in piety I pray: preserve you all this our Leader, preserve the senate; let it live by its prince’s code, he by his own.

5

Caesar, your reverence for right and justice is no less than Numa’s was; but Numa was a poor man. It is a hard thing not to sacrifice morals to wealth and to be Numa when you are richer than many a Croesus. If the fathers 13 of old, those mighty names, were to return and the Elysian grove could be emptied, Camillus, unconquered champion of freedom, 14 would be your courtier, and Fabricius 15 would accept your proffered gold; Brutus would rejoice to be under your leadership, bloodstained Sulla when about to resign his power would hand it over to you, Magnus 16 would love you along with Caesar, a private citizen, and Crassus

  • 8Aeneas.
  • 9I.e. the wealth of Troy.
  • 10Some representation of Jupiter placed by Nerva in the temple on the Capitol. Aeterno = never again to be destroyed by fire.
  • 11Juno and Minerva, the latter being “wholly his” as having sprung from his head.
  • 12Nerva being consul for the third time. The consular records were kept in the temple of Janus: cf. 8.66.11. “Purple” either = consular, from the consul’s purple gown, or referring to actual purple lettering in the volume; cf. 12.29.5.
  • 13I.e. senators.
  • 14Cf. 1.24.3.
  • 15Who refused the presents of Pyrrhus, king of Epirus.
  • 16Pompey the Great (Cn. Pompeius Magnus).
