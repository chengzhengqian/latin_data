Epigrams Book XIV

164. Quoit (gong)

When the shining weight of the Spartan quoit is flying, keep your distance, boys. Let it be guilty only once. 145

165. Lyre

It gave Eurydice back to the bard; but he lost her himself, not trusting himself nor loving in moderation. 146

166. Same

It has often been thrown out of Pompey’s theater 147 —the instrument that drew forests and held wild beasts. 148

167. Quill

Lest a hot blister arise on your chafed thumb, let a white Quill 149 equip the docile lyre.

168. Hoop

The wheel must be fitted with a tire. You give me a useful present. This will be a hoop to boys, but a tire to me.

  • 145Apollo accidentally killed the Spartan boy Hyacinthus with a quoit; cf. ep. 173. But discus also means “gong,” hence its placing after the bell.
  • 146Orpheus loved “not wisely, but too well”—the same sense as in 5.7.8.
  • 147I.e. hissed off by the audience.
  • 148When played by Orpheus.
  • 149An instrument for striking the strings.
