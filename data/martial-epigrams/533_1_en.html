Epigrams Book XIV

89. Citrus table 81

Receive the fertile forests, gifts of Atlas. Who gives golden gifts, will give less.

90. Maple table

I am not veined, it is true, nor the daughter of a Moorish forest, 82 but my timber too knows luxurious repasts.

91. Ivory tusks

They can carry the great bodies of bulls; 83 do you ask whether they can support Libyan tabletops?

92. Five-foot rule

A piece of oak pricked with marks and ending in a sharp point is wont often to reveal a contractor’s frauds.

93. Antique cups

Not of recent origin nor of Roman chisel is this glory. Mentor was first to drink from these cups while he was making them.

  • 81The citrus (the tree called thyine wood, Callitris quadrivalvis, the Greek θύα or θύον) came from Mauretania, in northwest Africa: Pliny N.H. 13.96 seqq. Round tabletops (orbes) were made of it, for which incredible sums were often paid (K).
  • 82I.e. neither veined (a feature greatly valued: Pliny loc. cit.) nor citrus. Maple was second to citrus: Pliny N.H. 16.66 seqq.; and one species was also peacock-veined: ibid. (K).
  • 83Cf. Sp. 18 and 20.
