Epigrams Book XIII

67. Ringdoves

Ringdoves retard and dull the loins. Let him who would be salacious not eat this bird.

68. Yellow birds

The yellow bird 65 is deceived by rods 66 and nets when the young grape is swelling with wine still green.

69. Catta birds

Umbria never gave us Pannonian catta birds. Pudens prefers to send these gifts to his “master.” 67

70. Peacocks

Do you admire him whenever he spreads his jewelled wings, and can you hand him over, callous man, to the cruel cook?

71. Flamingoes

My ruddy wing 68 gives me a name, but my tongue is a treat to epicures. What if my tongue were to tell tales? 69

  • 65The identity of the bird, here called yellow, is very obscure. It is generally supposed to be the same as the ἴκτερος, and has been variously identified with the golden oriole, the greenfinch, the stone-curlew, and the green woodpecker (K).
  • 66Limed canes: cf. 9.54.3; 14.218 (K).
  • 67The catta must be a species of bird native to Pannonia. Pudens, who came from Umbria, sent a brace or more to a favorite boy slave (cf. 1.31; 5.48; 8.63). For “master” see 5.57.2n.
  • 68Phoenicopterus = “scarlet-wing.”
  • 69It could tell how impure was the mouth of the consumer (so Housman, 738).
