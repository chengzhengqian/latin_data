Epigrams Book II

75

A lion, accustomed to bear the blows of his fearless keeper and mildly suffer a hand thrust into his mouth, unlearned his pacific ways in a sudden return of savagery such as should not have existed on the hills of Libya. For the cruel wretch with his raging fangs killed two boyish bodies out of the youthful band that was refurbishing the bloody ground with rakes. Mars’ sand never saw a worse atrocity. One would fain exclaim: “Cruel, treacherous brigand, learn mercy to boys from our shewolf!” 69

76

Marius has left you five pounds of silver, a man to whom you yourself used to give nothing. He’s cheated you!70

77

Cosconius, you that think my epigrams too long, you would do fine for greasing axles. 71 At that rate you would find the colossus 72 too tall and call Brutus’ boy 73 too short. Let me tell you what you don’t know: two pages of Marsus and accomplished Pedo often cover a single item. Things from which you cannot make any deduction are not long. But you, Cosconius, make long couplets.

  • 69Who fostered Romulus and Remus.
  • 70Lit. “Gave you words,” a common expression. The man’s expectation (that having given nothing, he would get nothing)was falsified. The humor lies in turning upside down the stock situation in which a testator “cheats” a legacy-hunter by leaving him out of his will (SB 1).
  • 71I.e. you are pinguis, “greasy,” which can mean “stupid.”
  • 72Cf. Sp. 2.1n.
  • 73Statuette of a boy, said to have been loved by M. Brutus (cf. 9.50; 14.171; Pliny N.H. 34.82).
