. . . Those wars are unjust which are undertaken without provocation. For only a war waged for revenge or defence can actually be just. . . .

. . . But our people by defending their allies have gained dominion over the whole world. . . .

The following fragments, as St. Augustine explains (De Civ. Dei XIX, 21), are part of the argument for the justice of slavery and imperialism, in which it is maintained that certain nations and individuals are naturally fitted for and benefited by subjection to others.1
