The Republic I.

Fragments of Book I

1. . . . nor for very learned men.

That Manius Persius read these words I care No whit; let Junius Congus read them all.1

2. Therefore, as our fatherland is the author of more benefits, and is an earlier parent than the father who begot us, surely greater gratitude is due to it than to a father.

3. Nor could Carthage have prospered so greatly for about six hundred years without good counsel and strict training.

4. . . . that I certainly am acquainted, he said, with this habit of yours, and with your eagerness for discussion . . .

5. Surely all the discussions of the men you mention, though they contain abundant springs of virtue and knowledge, nevertheless, if compared with what the others have actually performed and accomplished, would appear, I am afraid, to have provided men with more entertainment than stimulus to practical work.
