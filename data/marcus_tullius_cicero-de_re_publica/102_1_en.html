XXVII. . . . In which I agree that an anxious and hazardous justice is not appropriate to a wise man. . . .

XXVIII. . . . Virtue clearly desires honour, and has no other reward. . . . yet though she receives it gladly, she does not exact it rigorously. . . . . What riches, what power, what kingdoms can you offer such a man? For he thinks these things are human, but deems his own possessions divine. . . . . But if universal ingratitude, or the envy of many, or the hostility of the powerful, deprive virtue of its proper rewards, yet it is soothed by many consolations, and firmly upheld by its own excellence. . . .
