CONSOLATION ADDRESSED TO CLAUDIUS ETRUSCUS

Piety,1 highest among deities, whose godhead beloved of heaven looks but rarely upon desecrated earth, come hither with fillets on your hair, shining in snow-white robe, as when present to aid nor yet expelled by sinners’ wrongdoing, you cared for primitive peoples and a golden reign. Come to a gentle funeral2 and behold the pious tears of sorrowing Etruscus, praise his eyes and wipe them. For who that saw him bursting his breast with insatiable lament and embracing the pyre and bending over the ashes but would think his mourning was for a young wife or that the flames were devouring the face of a son just growing to manhood? It is a father he weeps for. Gods and men, come to the rites. Far, far from hence you sinners, if any bear a crime unspoken in his heart and thinks his weary parent’s old age too long, if any guilty wretch fear his beaten mother’s snake3 and stern Aeacus’ infernal urn!
