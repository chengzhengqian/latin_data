THE BATHS OF CLAUDIUS ETRUSCUS

My frenzied lyre does not strike Helicon with weighty quill1 nor do I invoke the Muses, the deities whom I have so often wearied. I dismiss you, Phoebus, from the choir, and you, Euhan. You too, winged one of Tegea, hide in silence the back of the tuneful beast.2 My song calls on other assemblies. ’Tis enough to have drawn out the Naiads, mistresses of the waves, and the king of flashing fire,3 still weary and ruddy with Sicilian anvil. Thebes, lay your guilty arms aside for a while.4 I would frolic for a dear friend. Boy, pour cup on cup (but take no care to count them) and string the tarrying lyre. Begone Toil and Care, as I sing the Baths bejeweled with glistening marbles, and my Clio, wantoning in fillets and ivy and free of modest leafage,5 sports for Etruscus. Come, green goddesses, and
