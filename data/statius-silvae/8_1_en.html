BOOK I

No long delays drew out the time. The god’s present likeness17 itself makes labor sweet and the men intent upon their task are surprised to find their hands more powerful. The lofty scaffolding18 is loud with hammer strokes and an incessant din runs through Mars’ seven hills, drowning the vagrant noises of great Rome.

The guardian of the place in person,19 whose name the sacred chasm and the famous pool preserve in memory, hears the countless clashes of bronze and the Forum resounding with harsh blows. He raises a visage stark in holy squalor and a head sanctified by well-earned wreath of oak.20 At first he took alarm at the huge accouterments and flashing light of a mightier horse as thrice his lofty neck sank affrighted in the pool. Then, happy at sight of the ruler: “Hail, offspring and begetter of great gods, deity known to me by distant report. Blessed is now my swamp, venerable now that it is vouchsafed me to know you close at hand and behold your immortal radiance from my neighboring seat. Once only did I make and find salvation for the people of Romulus; whereas you in length of fighting quell the wars of Jove,21 the battles of the Rhine,22 the civil outrage,23 the mountain slow to treat.24 But if our times had given you birth, you would have made to plunge into the deep pool, when I dared not venture, but Rome would have held your reins.”

  • 17The statue itself (not “beauty”), or possibly the mold from which it would be cast.
  • 18Rather than “crane.”
  • 19The legendary M. Curtius, who plunged himself and his horse into a chasm that had opened in the Forum, for country’s sake.
  • 20A military decoration bestowed for saving the life of a fellow soldier (corona civica).
  • 21The fighting on the Capitol in AD 69 between partisans of Vitellius and Vespasian. Domitian was on the spot, but his contribution was minimal.
  • 22Campaign against the German C(h)atti.
  • 23The rebellion of Antonius Saturninus, governor of Upper Germany, in 89.
  • 24See v. 7.
