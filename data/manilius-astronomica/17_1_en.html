It stirs up war and restores peace, and returning in different guise affects the world with the glance it gives it and governs with its mien. Sure proof that the star has this power are its colour and the quivering of the fire that sparkles in its face. Hardly is it inferior to the Sun, save that its abode is far away and the beams it launches from its sea-blue face are cold. In splendour it surpasses all other constellations, and no brighter star is bathed in ocean or returns to heaven from the waves. Then come Procyon and the swift Hare. Then famed Argo, raised to the skies from the sea which it was the first to cross, sails upon the depths of the heaven it earned by its brave exploits, made a god for having given safety to gods. Next to it is the Water-snake, whose stars are so arranged as to represent its scaly skin. Then shines the bird that is sacred to Phoebus b and with it the Bowl beloved of Bacchus and the Centaur of twofold form; he is half man, joined at the waist to the body of a horse. Next has heaven a temple of its own, where, its rites now paid, the Altar gleams after victory gained when Earth in rage bore forth the monstrous Giants against the skies.
