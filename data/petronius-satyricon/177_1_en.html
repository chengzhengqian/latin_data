Satyricon

“Why have you come into my room as if you were visiting a fresh-made grave?” she said.“ Especially on a holiday, when even mourners smile.” …“ Ah, 1 Oenothea,” said the woman,“ this young man was born under a bad star; he cannot sell his treasure to boys or girls either. You never beheld such an unlucky creature: he is a piece of wash-leather; he has no genitals. Just to show you, what do you think of a man who can come away from Circe without a spark of pleasure?” When Oenothea heard this she sat down between us, shook her head for some time, and then said,” I am the only woman alive who knows how to cure that disease. And, lest you think I’m twisting, I ask that the young man sleep a night with me … if I don’t make that thing stiffer than horn:

“Whatever thou seest in the world is obedient to me. The flowery earth, when I will, faints and withers as its juices dry, and, when I will, pours forth its riches, while rocks and rough crags spurt waters wide as the Nile. The great sea lays its waves lifeless before me, and the winds lower their blasts in silence at my feet. The rivers obey me, and Hyrcanian2 tigers, and serpents, whom I bid rise upright. But I will not tell you of small things; the shape of the moon is drawn down to me by my spells, and Phoebus3 trembles and must turn his fiery steeds as I compel him back in his course.
