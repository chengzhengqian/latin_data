XIV. Epilogue—to an unnamed Friend

Cherisher and revered protector of learned men, what doest thou—thou that hast ever befriended my genius? As thou once wert wont to extol me when I was in safety, now too dost thou take heed that I seem not wholly absent? Dost thou assemble my verse except only that “Art” which ruined its artificer? Do so, I pray, thou patron of new bards; so far as may be, keep my body2 in the city. Exile was decreed to me, exile was not decreed to my books; they did not deserve their master’s punishment. Oft is a father exiled on a foreign shore, yet may the exile’s childpen live in the city. Pallas-fashion3 were my verses born from me without a mother; these are my offspring, my family. These I commend to thee; the more bereft they are, the greater burden will they be to thee their guardian.
