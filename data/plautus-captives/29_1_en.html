The Captives

heg

(to Aristophontes) Seriously, I’m not surprised at all if545 he’s avoiding you, or eye-contact with you, or if he hates you, since you address him as Tyndarus instead of Philocrates.

tyn

Hegio, this man was considered a lunatic in Elis, so don’t take what he says seriously; at home he chased his mother and father with spears, and there he sometimes gets the illness that is spat upon. 22 So go far away from him.

heg

(to slaves) Away with him from me!

ari

(to Tyndarus) Do you say so, you thug? Are you telling him that I’m a lunatic and chased my own father with spears? That I have the illness that makes it necessary to spit on me?

heg

Stop being afraid, that illness tortures a lot of people for555 whom being spat on was helpful and beneficial.

ari

(to Hegio) How’s this? You too? Do you actually believe him?

heg

Believe him in what?

ari

That I’m mad?

tyn

(to Hegio) Can you see how he’s looking around with such a hostile expression on his face? It’s best for you to leave, Hegio: what I told you is happening, his frenzy is increasing; look out for yourself.

heg

I immediately believed that he’s mad when he called you Tyndarus.

tyn

Well, from time to time he doesn’t even know his own560 name and has no idea who he is.

heg

But he also said he’s your friend.

  • 22People spat out when they saw an epileptic fit; this apotropaeic rite was meant to protect from infection (cf. Plin. nat. 28. 35). Hegio goes one step further and humorously treats being spat on as a cure.
