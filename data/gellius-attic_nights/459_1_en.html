in the Protesilaodamia I think, used claustritumum1 of one who had charge of the fastenings of a door, evidently using the same formation by which he saw that aeditumus, or “one who guards the temples,” is made. In the most reliable copies of Marcus Tullius’ Fourth Oration against Verres I find it written:2 “The custodians (aeditumi) and guards quickly perceive it,” but in the ordinary copies aeditui is read. There is an Atellan farce of Pomponius’ entitled Aeditumus. In it is this line:3

As soon as I attend you and keep your templedoor (aeditumor).

Titus Lucretius too in his poem4 speaks of aedituentes, instead of aeditui.5

That those are deceived who sin in the confident hope of being undetected, since there is no permanent concealment of wrongdoing; and on that subject a discourse of the philosopher Peregrinus and a saying of the poet Sophocles.
