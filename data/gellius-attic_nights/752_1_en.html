To the practice of the “acroatic” training which I have mentioned he devoted the morning hours in the Lyceum,2 and he did not ordinarily admit any pupil to it until he had tested his ability, his elementary knowledge, and his zeal and devotion to study, The exoteric lectures and exercises in speaking he held at the same place in the evening and opened them generally to young men without distinction. This he called δειλινὸς περίπατος, or “the evening walk,” the other which I have mentioned above, ἑωθινός, or “the morning walk”;3 for on both occasions he walked as he spoke. He also divided his books on all these subjects into two divisions, calling one set “exoteric,” the other “acroatic.”
