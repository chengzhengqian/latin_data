But the expression purum putum occurs, not only in the treaty with Carthage, but also in many other early writings, including the tragedy of Quintus Ennius entitled Alexander,1 and the satire of Marcus Varro called Δὶς Παῖδες οἱ Γέροντες,2 or Old Men are Children for a Second Time.

VI

That Julius Hyginus was hasty and foolish in his criticism of Virgil for calling the wings of Daedalus praepetes; also a note on the meaning of aves praepetes and of those birds which Nigidius called inferae.

From Minos’ realms in flight brave Daedalus On pinion swift (praepetibus), ’tis said, did dare the sky.

In these lines of Virgil3 Julius Hyginus criticizes the use of pennis praepetibus as an improper and ignorant expression. “For,” says he, “those birds are called praepetes by the augurs which either fly onward auspiciously or alight in suitable places.” Therefore he thought it inappropriate in Virgil to use an augural term in speaking of the flight of Daedalus, which had nothing to do with the science of the augurs.
