The argument of the one who thought that we should say egregi was of this sort: “Whatever nouns or words,” said he, “end in the nominative singular in the syllable us preceded by i, in the vocative case terminate in the letter i, as Caelius Caeli, modius modi, terlius terti, Accius Acci, Titius Titi, and the like; so then egregius, since it ends in the syllable us in the nominative and the letter i precedes that syllable, must in the vocative singular have i for the final letter, and therefore it is correct to say egregi, not egregie. For divus and rivus and clivus do not end in the syllable us, but in that which ought to be written with two u’s, and in order to indicate that sound a new letter was devised, which was called the digamma.”1

When the other heard this, he said: O egregie grammatice, or if you prefer, egregissime, tell me, I pray you, what is the vocative case of inscius, impius, sobrius, ebrius, proprius, propitius, anxius, and contrarius, which end in the syllable us and have the letter i before the final syllable?
