Book XV. iii.

III

What Marcus Cicero thought and wrote about the prefix in the verbs aufugio and aufero; and whether this same

preposition is to be seen in the verb autumo.

I read a book of Marcus Cicero’s entitled The Orator. In that book when Cicero had said that the verbs aufugio and aufero were indeed formed of the preposition ab and the verbs fugio and fero, but that the preposition, in order that the word might be smoother in pronunciation and sound, was changed and altered into the syllable au,1 and aufugio and aufero began to be used for abfugio and abfero; when he had said this, I say, he afterwards in the same work wrote as follows of the same particle:2 “This preposition,” he says, “will be found in no other verb save these two only.”

But I have found in the Commentary of Nigidius3 that the verb autumo is formed from the preposition ab and the verb aestumo (estimate) and that autumo is a contracted form of abaestumo, signifying totum aestumo, on the analogy of abnumero.4 But, be it said with great respect for Publius Nigidius, a most learned man, this seems to be rather bold and clever than true.
