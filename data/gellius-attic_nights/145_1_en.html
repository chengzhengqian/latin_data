I myself too a little while ago, when reading the Fretum—that is the name of a comedy which some think is not Plautine—had no manner of doubt that it was by Plautus and in fact of all his plays the most authentic. From it I copied these two lines,1 with the intention of looking up the story of the Arretine oracle:2

Now here we have at the great games3 the Arretine response: I perish if I don’t, and if I do, I’m flogged.

Yet Marcus Varro, in the first book of his Comedies of Plautus,4 quotes these words of Accius:5 “For not the Twin Panders nor the Slave-ring nor the Old Woman were the work of Plautus, nor were ever the Twice Violated or the Boeotian Woman, nor were the Clownish Rustic or the Partners in Death the work of Titus Maccius.”6

In that same book of Varro’s we are told also that there was another writer of comedies called Plautius. Since his plays bore the title “Plauti,”7 they were accepted as Plautine, although in fact they were not Plautine by Plautus, but Plautinian by Plautius.
