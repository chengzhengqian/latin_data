“By this time it is plain that the power to argue Argument to be met by argument, appeal to emotion by exciting the contrary. both sides of every question is abundantly furnished from the same commonplaces. But your opponents’ proof must be countered, either by contradicting the arguments chosen to establish it, or by showing that their desired conclusion is not supported by their premisses and does not follow there from; or, if you do not so rebut it, you must adduce on the opposite 216side some proof of greater or equal cogency. Lastly appeals, whether mild or passionate, and whether for winning favour or stirring the feelings, must be swept aside by exciting the opposite impressions, so that goodwill may be done away with by hate, and compassion by jealousy.
