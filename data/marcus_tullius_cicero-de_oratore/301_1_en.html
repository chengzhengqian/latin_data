For even country people speak of ‘jewelled vines,’ ‘luxurious herbage,’ ‘joyful harvests.’ The explanation is that when something that can scarcely be conveyed by the proper term is expressed metaphorically, the meaning we desire to convey is made clear by the resemblance of the thing that we have expressed by 156the word that does not belong. Consequently the metaphors in which you take what you have not got from somewhere else are a sort of borrowing; but there is another somewhat bolder kind that do not indicate poverty but convey some degree of brilliance to the style. However there is no need for me to give you a lecture on the method of inventing these or on their classification.
