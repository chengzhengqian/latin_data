LXII. At the conclusion of these observations of Adjournment of the debate. Antonius, Sulpicius, and Cotta too, appeared to be in grave doubt as to which of the two speakers’ discourses bore the closer resemblance to the truth. 263Presently Crassus replied: “Antonius, you are making our orator something of a mechanic; and I rather suspect you are really of a different opinion, and are gratifying that singular liking of yours for contradiction, in which no one has ever outdone you; the exercise of this power belongs peculiarly to orators, though nowadays it is in regular use among philosophers, and chiefly those who make a practice of arguing at extreme length either for or against 264any proposition whatever laid before them.
