Consequently your view, Catulus, is correct, for I feel that the young man in question lacks no gift of 230nature or of education. And this makes it all the more necessary for you, Cotta, and you, Sulpicius, to be watchful and diligent. For it is no ordinary orator who is growing up in the person of Hortensius to follow on after your generation, but one gifted with a very keen intelligence, ardent devotion to study, exceptional learning and an unrivalled memory; and though I favour him, nevertheless, while I am eager that he shall outstrip his own generation, still it would be hardly creditable for you that one so much your junior as he is should outstrip you.

“But now let us rise,” he said, “and take some refreshment; this has been a very keen debate, and it is time to give our minds a rest from the strain.”
