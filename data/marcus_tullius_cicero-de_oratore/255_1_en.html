Now as to the two first-mentioned qualities, I do not suppose that I shall be expected to give an account of purity and lucidity of language, as it is not our task to teach oratory to a person who does not know the language, nor to hope that one who cannot speak correct Latin should speak ornately, nor yet that one who does not say something that we can understand can possibly say something that we shall admire. Let us therefore leave these qualities, which are easy to learn and indispensable; for one of them is conveyed by books and by elementary education, and the other is employed for the purpose of making an individual’s statements understood, which obviously while indispensable is at the same time the 39merest minimum.
