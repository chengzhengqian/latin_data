VI. “But if this appears to be too vast a theory for the senses or the thought of human beings to be able to grasp it, there is also the truth enunciated by Plato, which you, Catulus, have undoubtedly heard, that the whole of the content of the liberal and humane sciences is comprised within a single bond of union; since, when we grasp the meaning of the theory that explains the causes and issues of things, we discover that a marvellous agreement and harmony underlies all branches of knowledge. 22But if this truth also seems too lofty for the sight of us lowly earthlings to be able to rise to it, nevertheless it is unquestionably our duty to know and to hold to the system that we have embraced and profess and have undertaken to maintain.

“For, as I said yesterday, and also Antonius indicated in some passages of his discourse this morning, eloquence is one, into whatever shores or realms of discourse 23it ranges.
