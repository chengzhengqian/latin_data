And first he put to death Rufina, with all who were implicated in, or aware of, the adultery that she had committed, whose case (as we have previously said)2 Ursicinus had referred to the Court; and then many others, regardless of whether they were guilty or innocent.

46. For vying in bloody rivalry with Maximinus, as his leader,3 he strove to outdo him in cutting the sinews of distinguished families, imitating Busiris of old, and Antaeus and Phalaris4 to such a degree that he seemed to lack only the Agrigentine bull of the last-named.

47. Amid these and such acts so perpetrated a matron called Hesychia, who because of an attempted crime was committed to an official’s attendant to be guarded at his house, and was in fear of much cruel treatment, pressed her face in the feather bed on which she was lying and so stopped her nose and her breath and gave up the ghost.

48. There was added to these another no less cruel evil.
