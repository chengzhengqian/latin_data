MEDEA (EXUL) (F 89–100)

Tragedies with the title “Medea” were written by Euripides, Neophron (15 F 1–3 TrGF), Euripides II (17 T 1 TrGF), Melanthius I (23 F? TrGF), Morsimus (29 F 1? TrGF), Dicaeogenes (52 F 1a TrGF), Carcinus II (70 F 1e TrGF), Theodorides (78A T 1 TrGF), Diogenes Sinopensis (88 F 1e TrGF), Biotus? (205 F 1 TrGF) in Greek, and by Ovid (p. 230 R.2 = p. 267 R.3 = Vol. I, pp. 156–57 TrRF) and Seneca in Latin. Sections of the Medea myth were dramatized in Pacuvius’ Medus (Trag. 218–43 R.2–3) and Accius’ Medea sive Argonautae (Trag. 391–423 R.2–3). The part of the myth presented in Ennius (as can be inferred from the fragments) is what happens in Corinth (Medea’s revenge on Jason, who is leaving her for another wife), as in Euripides’ Medea and Seneca’s Medea.

The model for Ennius’ tragedy is probably Euripides’ Medea (T 28; F 89): many fragments can be compared to similar lines in Euripides (even if altered by Ennius). Since the plots of the two tragedies seem similar, the order of the Ennian fragments according to the plot is likely to be as follows: F 89, 96, 91, 90, 93, 92, 99, 97, 94; the position of the remaining fragments is uncertain.
