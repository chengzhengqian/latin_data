And this Ennius <says>, who a few lines earlier voices the opinion that there are gods, “but” believes that “they do not care what the human race does.” But I, who believe that they care and also admonish and predict many things, approve of divination when unreliability, vanity, and wickedness have been excluded.

c Cicero, On Divination

For first you [Stoics] embrace this: “if there are gods, they are beneficial toward humans.” Who will grant you this? Epicurus? He who denies that the gods care for any matter of someone else or their own; or our Ennius? He who says to great applause, with the agreement of the people:

I have always said and will say that there is a race of gods and heaven dwellers, but I believe that they do not care what the human race does.1

And indeed he adds a reason why he thinks so; but there is no need to quote what follows; this much is sufficient to have it understood, that they embrace as certain what is doubtful and controversial.
