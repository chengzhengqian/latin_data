If you deny that He is visible to mortals, then tell me who it is that from Babylon’s throne the king sees at a distance walking unharmed through the flames, and trampling on the consuming fires with his brethren unscorched.a He says, you know, “Ye nobles, three men the devouring fire received in the panting furnace, and lo! one more parts asunder with a smile the flame’s hot gust. That is the Son of God. I confess it, and yield and worship Him. Take ye away the brands, for they are laughed to scorn; draw off the dying logs of pitch-pine; the brimstone ye set fire to is chilled. It is the Son, no doubt of it, that works these wonders; He is before my eyes, God himself, God’s most assured Son, commanding the measureless heat and taming its wrath, triumphing over the fire, his servant; He subdues the raging pitch and compels the fierce flames to grow cold. The hot breath is forbidden to touch the folds of their orientalb garments; it passes them by, and runs hissing past their fine raiment. The very heat fears to penetrate their Assyrian turbans, lest the hair that falls on their shoulders be dirtied by the acrid smoke.” So saying, he bids the varied sounds of music cease, all the instruments, sackbuts, harps, reed-pipes and cornets. Foolish superstition is silent, stilled are the festal notes that were sounding in honour of the golden image wickedly set up to view.
