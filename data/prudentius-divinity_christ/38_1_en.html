It is worth while to make mention of the phantom that belongs to a misty doctrine. It consists of fine particles in minute structure, but it fails for lack of body, vanishes away like the wind, and is too fleeting and unsubstantial to maintain itself.a There moved about, says the Manichean, a phantasmal God without real body, a false appearance, an empty likeness of body, having nothing tangible. Now see first whether it is fitting to ascribe aught that is counterfeit to God, whose pure glory admits of nothing false. Would such a God furnish himself with unreal members, and with cunning make-believe feign himself man, lying whenever He said “I have mercy on deep-rooted diseases, and in the same act I remit sins. It is the Son of man who is able to drive out the plague of the flesh and to loosen and undo the bonds of wickedness. Arise in health, arise in innocence, take up thy bed. I, the Son of man, command it.”b Does He not appear worthy to bear witness of himself, to know himself and his body of flesh?
