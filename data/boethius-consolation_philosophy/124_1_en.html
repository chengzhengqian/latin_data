“How so?” she asked.

“Because the common talk of men usually says, and indeed often, that some men have ill fortune.”

“Then do you want us,” she asked, “to have recourse for a while to terms like those of the common people, in case we seem to have withdrawn too far, as it were, from the practice of men?”

“As you think best,” I said.

“Then do you not judge that which is profitable to be good?”

“That is so,” I said.

“But that which either exercises or corrects is profitable?”

“I agree,” I said.

“And therefore good?”

“How could it not be?”

“But this is the case with those who, either being established in virtue wage war against adversity, or turning aside from their vices take the path of virtue.”

“I cannot deny it,” I said.

“Now what about the pleasant fortune which is granted as a reward to the good: do the common people judge that to be bad?”

“By no means, but as indeed it is, so also they judge it to be, very good.”

“What of the other, which although it is hard, constrains the wicked with just punishment; the people do not think that good, do they?”

“Indeed not,” I said, “they judge it to be the most wretched of all conceivable things.”
