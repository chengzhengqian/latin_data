Consolation III

III

And you also, earthly creatures that you are, have some image, though hazy, in your dreams of your beginning; you see, though with a far from clear imagination yet with some idea, that true end of your happiness. Your natural inclinations draw you towards that end, to the true good, though mistaken notions of many kinds lead you away from it. For consider, can men come to the end they set themselves through those things by which they think they will obtain happiness? For if money or honours or the rest do produce any such thing, from which no good seems to be lacking, even we should admit that some men are made happy by getting them. But if they cannot perform what they promise, but lack many goods, is not the appearance of happiness that they produce clearly false? First then, I ask you yourself, you who were not so long ago very wealthy, were you never, among all your vast riches, troubled in mind by some anxiety, arising from some wrong or other?”

“I certainly cannot remember,” I answered, “that my mind was so free that it was not always in some way tormented.”

“Was it not either because something was missing that you wanted, or because something you did not want was present?”

“Yes,” I said.

“So you desired the presence of the one, and the absence of the other?”

“That is so,” I said.

“Now any man must lack that thing which he desires?”

“Yes, he must,” said I.
