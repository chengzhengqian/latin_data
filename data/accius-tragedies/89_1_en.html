Plays

513

Gellius: ‘Squalere’. . . is a term derived from dense quantity and roughness of ‘squamae.’. . . Accius in Pelops’ Sons . . .—

. . . That serpent’s scales With scaly gold and purple interwoven

514

Martianus: ‘Que’ is a copulative conjunction; ‘ve’. . . is disjunctive, a for example . . . in a passage of Accius, in Pelops’ Sons—

or I goad my heart.

515

Nonius: ‘Laetare’ and ‘laetiscere,’ to make ‘laetus’ . . .—

and that you I might gladden with a threefold blessing.

516–17

Nonius: ‘Satu’ (sowing) put for seed . . .—

. . . for your proofs stir my mind And bring me to believe that I am a man Conceived from your own sowing.

518

Nonius: ‘Lucti’ for ‘luctus’ . . .—

Why do I dally to go hence and take In my wife’s death a garb of mourning?

519

Servius supplemented, on ‘gratatur’ in Virgil: Some take ‘gratatur’ not in the sense of ‘wishes joy to,’ but ‘delights in’. . .—

Nor is it fit I should be pleased with you In this.

  • aThe right word is disiunctiva; expletivae was the term used of words such as autem, equidem.
