Plays

345

Nonius: ‘Anxitudo’ . . .—

Grief, wandering, anxiety, and pain Prevailed on me.

346

Nonius: ‘Disertim’ (plainly, expressly) is a term we can, on the authority of Titinius, use for clearly, openly . . .—

In plain words this alone Is wanting from the sum of my discomforts.

347–50

Nonius: ‘Miserari’ and ‘misereri.’ The old writers saw fit to take these words in different senses, as follows: ‘miserari’ being to weep and lament, ‘misereri’ to show commiseration at another’s calamities; and they held that the verb in the active form (sc. ‘miserare’) applied to the former emotion, in the deponent to the latter. Accius in Eurysaces—

Ah me! So there is even this distress That adds grief to my griefs . . . who sorrows for me?

In another person: a ‘Why do you sorrow?’ (see p. 599). The same poet in Eurysaces—

Your loneliness I talked of, full of fear On your account; for you I sorrowed more Than I was sorry for myself.
