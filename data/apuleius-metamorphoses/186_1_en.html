And first, as the boldest dogs ran up within close range, lunging this way and that with his tusks he slashed them to pieces and killed them. Then he trampled down the net where he had halted his first onset and escaped right through it.

As for us, we were all terrified with5 fright, accustomed as we usually were to harmless hunting expeditions, and besides we had no weaons or means of defence. So we ran off and hid under cover of foliage or behind trees. Thrasyllus, however, having hit on a perfect cover for his treacherous designs, slyly taunted Tlepolemus.

“‘Why are we standing here confused and dumbfounded, needlessly afraid like our lowborn slaves there, or cowed like frightened women? Why are we letting such a rich prize slip through our hands? Why not mount our horses and quickly catch up with him? You take a hunting spear, and I will get a lance.’

Immediately, without a moment’s hesitation, they jumped on their horses hot-foot in pursuit of the animal, which, relying on its natural strength, stopped and wheeled round. Ablaze with the fire of its savagery, it whetted its tusks and glared, hesitating whom to attack first. But Tlepolemus began the action by hurling his spear down on to the animal’s back from above. Thrasyllus, however, spared the beast, but struck with his lance and hamstrung the hind legs of Tlepolemus’ horse. The steed fell where the blood had spurted and, sprawling full on his back, involuntarily threw his master to the ground. It did not take the maddened boar long to attack him where he lay. Again
