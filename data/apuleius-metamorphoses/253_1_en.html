This account, which evinced an all too plausible semblance of truth, was delivered by that scoundrel without a trace of nervousness, and the trial came to an end.

None of the councillors had remained so impartial toward8 the young man as not to pronounce him manifestly guilty and sentence him to be sewn in the sack.5 Their votes, which all agreed, since all had written the same word—guilty, were about to be thrown into the bronze urn in accordance with immemorial custom. Once the ballots were lodged inside, the defendant’s fortune was done with and nothing could be changed thereafter; power over his life was delivered into the hands of the executioner. But one of the older councillors, a man above all others of proven honesty and an exceptionally respected physician, covered the mouth of the urn with his hand to prevent anyone from thoughtlessly inserting his ballot, and then made the following speech to the court:
