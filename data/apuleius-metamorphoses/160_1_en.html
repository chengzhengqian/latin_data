Metamorphoses vii

Apuleius of Madauros Metamorphoses

Book VII

When darkness had been cast away at day’s first whitening,1 and the sun’s bright chariot illumined all the world, a member of the robber band arrived—his identity was revealed by their exchange of mutual greetings. He sat down just at the entrance to the cave, and when he had stopped panting and recovered his breath, he made the following announcement to his guild:

“As for the house of Milo of Hypata, which we recently plundered, our worry has been dispelled and we can now rest easy. After your valiant might had carried everything off and gone back to our camp, I mixed in with the crowds of people. Pretending to be pained and indignant, I tried to find out what sort of plan was being formed to investigate the crime, whether they had decided to search for the robbers, and how extensive the search would be. My intention was to report all this back to you, as you had ordered. They had decided, not on the basis of dubious evidence, but on very likely grounds and by unanimous agreement of the whole crowd, to prosecute somebody named Lucius as the obvious perpetrator of the crime.
