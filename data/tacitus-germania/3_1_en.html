The first tribes in fact to cross the Rhine and expel the Gauls, though now called Tungri,3 then bore the name Germans: so little by little the name—a tribal, not a national, name—prevailed, until the whole people were called by the artificial name of “Germans,” first only by the victorious tribe Tungri in order to intimidate the Gauls, but afterwards by themselves also.4

  • 1The location of the Marsi (Tac. Ann., I. 51) and of the Gambrivii (Strabo, VII. 1.3—a central tribe) is not known. The Vandilii or Vandalii (the later Vandals) of east Germany included, according to Pliny, the Gutones (Goths—see below), the Burgondiones (Burgundians), and others; in Tacitus the Vandalii seem to be the same as the Lugii (see below, p. 202). For the Suebi, see pp. 192–3. He did not know of the Burgundians (between the middle Oder and the Vistula) who had come from Bornholm island.
  • 2For this much discussed subject, see J. G. C. Anderson’s edition of the Germania, 42 ff. Germani as the national name of all Germans was certainly an extension, as usually happens, of a native tribal name. So Germani must not be derived from a Celtic word; and the Romans were quite wrong in connecting it with germani “brothers.” There was an inroad of Germans, about 500 b.c., into south Holland and east Belgium and to the foot of the Ardennes, which we can ignore in this context. The name Germani arose later among the Gauls after a second invasion of Germans not much later than 250 b.c. These Germans included the Tungri and others—five such tribes are said by Julius Caesar to have the collective name Germani who had settled in north-eastern Gaul. The Gauls, and from them the Romans later, called all these peoples Germani. Tacitus wrongly says that it was the German Tungri themselves who first called all Germans “Germani” to scare the Gauls into believing that other peoples east of the Rhine were likewise Germani who might support them. There is no sign that all Germans were calling themselves Germani. The subsequent German invaders of Gaul were the Cimbri and the Teutoni or Teutones and the Ambrones c. 120–102 b.c.; the Suebi and other Suebic tribes the Vangiones, Triboci, and Nemetes c. 100 b.c. (see p. 174); the followers of the Suebic king Ariovistus 71–58 b.c.; and the Usipetes and Tencteri in 55 b.c. It was the Romans who came to call all the tribes of Germans by the generic name Germani; the Germans themselves did not call their whole race by any name until about a thousand years later. See note 4.
