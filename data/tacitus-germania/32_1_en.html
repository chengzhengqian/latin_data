Germania

But the Germans routed or captured Carbo and Cassius1 and Aurelius Scaurus2 and Servilius Caepio and Maximus Mallius,3 and wrested five consular armies in one campaign from the people of Rome, and even from a Caesar wrested Varus and three legions with him.4 Nor was it without paying a price that Marius5 smote them in Italy, and Julius of happy memory in Gaul,6 and Drusus, Nero, and Germanicus in their own homes.7 Soon after, the prodigious tragedy threatened by Gaius Caesar turned into a farce:8 then came peace,9 until, on the opportunity offered by our dissensions and by civil war, they carried the legions’ winter quarters by storm and even aspired to the Gallic provinces;10

  • 1Gnaeus Papirius Carbo was defeated by the Cimbri at Noreia, 113 b.c.; in 109 M. lunius Silanus was defeated in the Rhône valley—this adversity is not mentioned by Tacitus; in 107 the Helvetian Tigurini as allies of the Cimbri crushingly defeated the consul L. Cassius Longinus in the valley of the Garonne.
  • 2M. Aurelius Scaurus was not a consular commander of any of the five lost armies, but was consular legatus of Mallius in 105 b.c. (see below).
  • 3In 105 b.c. Q. Servilius Caepio (consul in 106) and Cn. Mallius Maximus (consul in 105) each in charge of an army on the Rhône, did not co-operate and suffered a most terrifying defeat at Arausio (Orange) by the Cimbri.
  • 4In a.d. 9 when Cherusci under Arminius won a great victory. Quintilius Varus took his own life. The “Caesar” was Emperor Augustus.
  • 5C. Marius had already in 102 b.c. defeated the Teutoni or Teutones and the Ambrones at Aquae Sextiae in Gaul; then in 101 he with Q. Lutatius Catulus destroyed the Cimbri at the Battle of the Raudine Plain near Vercellae.
  • 6He defeated the German king Ariovistus in 58 b.c., exterminated the Usipetes and the Tencteri from Gaul in 55, and furthermore crossed the Rhine more than once.
  • 7During the years 12–9 b.c. Drusus according to Augustus’ plan reached the Elbe; Nero—the later Emperor Tiberius—made a number of campaigns in Germany between 9 b.c. and a.d. 11; and Germanicus, son of Drusus and adopted by Tiberius, made several undecisively, a.d. 14–16, reaching the Elbe.
  • 8In a.d. 39 Emperor Gaius Caligula did something on the Rhine which may have been a serious effort to restore efficiency there.
  • 9There were in fact some minor operations.
  • 10During the year a.d. 69, which saw four Emperors (Galba, Otho, Vitellius, and Vespasian), the Batavian Iulius Civilis led (ostensibly in support of Vespasian) a rising (a.d. 69–70) supported by some of the tribes of Gaul who wanted a separate “Empire of the Gallic countries.” He probably wanted this Empire for himself. He captured the fortress Vetera near Xanten on the lower Rhine but was put down by Petilius Cerealis in a.d. 70.
