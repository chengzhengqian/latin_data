Germania

Cornelius Tacitus

The Earliest Beginnings and the Land of the Germans

1. Germany1 as a whole is separated from the Gauls and from Raetians2 and Pannonians3 by the rivers Rhine and Danube: from the Sarmatians4 and Dacians5 by mutual misgivings or mountains: the rest of it is surrounded by the ocean,6 which enfolds wide peninsulas and islands7 of vast expanse, some of whose peoples and their kings have but recently become known to us:8 war has lifted the curtain.

The Rhine, rising from the inaccessible and precipitous crest of the Raetian Alps, after turning west for a reach of some length is lost in the North Sea. The

  • 1Germany east of the Rhine, the Germans west of that river being Celticised and Romanised peoples of the Roman provinces Germania superior and Germania inferior.
  • 2In Tirol and part of eastern Switzerland. The Roman province Raetia, created in 15 b.c., included Vindelicia between the north eastern Alps and the upper Danube.
  • 3Of the country between the eastern Alps and the Danube, the Roman province Pannonia including the valleys of the Save and the Drave and bordering on the Danube from Vienna to Belgrade.
  • 4Iranians from Central Asia who had moved during the period after 200 b.c. over South Russia; in Tacitus also those east of the Eipel and of the Danube after its bend to the south, and south-east of the Erzgebirge, called Sarmatae Iazyges, separated from the rest of the Sarmatae by the Dacians.
  • 5Thracians (often included among the Getae) of the Transylvanian upland. After Trajan’s wars of 101–102 and 105–106 the Roman province Dacia extended from the Theiss eastwards to the Pruth and from the Danube northwards to a Carpathian mountain frontier.
  • 6The Baltic and the North Sea, as parts of one outer Ocean.
  • 7The Danish islands, and south Sweden thought to be an island.
  • 8Because Tiberius had reached the River Elbe in a.d. 5 and Roman ships the Skagerrak.
