I should suppose therefore that, just as in the secluded places of the East, where frankincense and balsam are exuded, so in the islands and lands of the West there are groves and glades more than ordinarily luxuriant: these are tapped and liquefied by the rays of the sun, as it approaches, and ooze into the nearest sea, whence by the force of tempests they are stranded on the shores opposite: if you try the qualities of amber by setting fire to it, it kindles like a torch and feeds an oily and odorous flame, and afterwards dissolves into something like pitch and resin.

Adjacent to the Suiones come the tribes of the Sitones,1 resembling them in all other respects, and differing only in this, that among them the woman rules: to this extent they have fallen lower not merely than freeman but even than slaves.

46. Here Suebia ends. As for the tribes of the Peucini,2 Venethi,3 and Fenni,4 I am in doubt whether

  • 1Where these were is unknown: possibly they were a Finnish people living in level lands in north-eastern Scandinavia and known as Kvaens who still exist in north-west Finland—Finns are still called Qven in Swedish.
  • 2The Peucini were part of the Bastarnae with whom Tacitus equates them. Here Tacitus has shifted southwards to the regions including the eastern Carpathian Mountains and regions farther south also, where the Peucini proper were in the Roman province of Lower Moesia, that is, Bulgaria from the Zibritz to the Black Sea (see below, note on Bastarnae).
  • 3The Venethi or rather Venedi or Venedae, ancestors of the Slavs, are to be located beyond the middle Vistula and east and north-east of Warsaw. Their name Winidā, later Wenden, was used for all Slavs known to the Germans, and has not yet become completely obsolete.
  • 4Finns, who seem to have lived originally round Moscow whence they had spread to the Gulf of Finland about 1500 b.c., those in Tacitus being part of them, along the eastern coast of the Baltic and north of the Aestii. But Tacitus’ actual description (§§ 3–5) seems to suit the people called Scrithifinni (“skiing Finns”) described in the sixth century a.d. by Procopius, Bell. Goth. 11.15—that is Lapps of northern Scandinavia, still called Finns by the Norwegians.
