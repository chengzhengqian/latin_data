Germania

ocean—a small state today, but rich in memories. Broad traces of their ancient fame are still extant—spacious encampments1 on each bank (of the Rhine), by the circuit of which you can even today measure the multitudes and manual strength of the tribes and the evidences of that mighty “trek.”

Our city was in its six hundred and fortieth year2 when the Cimbrian armies were first heard of, in the consulship of Caecilius Metellus and Papirius Carbo. If we count from that date to the second consulship3 of the Emperor Trajan, the total amounts to about two hundred and ten years: for that length of time has the conquest of Germany been in process. Between the beginning and end of that long period there have been many mutual losses: neither Sammite4 nor Carthaginian,5 neither Spain6 nor Gaul,7 nor even the Parthians have taught us more lessons. The German fighting for liberty has been a keener enemy than the absolutism of Arsaces.8 What taunt, indeed, has the East for us, apart from the overthrow of Crassus9—the East which itself fell at the feet of a Ventidius10 and lost Pacorus?

  • 1Probably some of the many forts (Ringwälle) of which remains have been found in the valley of the Rhine and were Celtic not “Cimbric.”
  • 2114–113 b.c.; later in 113 Gnaeus Papirius Carbo was defeated at Noreia.
  • 3a.d. 98.
  • 4There were three Samnite wars with Rome during the last part of the fourth century b.c. and the early part of the third. Tacitus is thinking specially of the Roman disaster in 321 b.c.
  • 5The Carthaginians in three wars 264–241, 218–201, and 150–146 b.c., Tacitus alludes especially to the victories of Hannibal at Lake Trasimene (217) and Cannae (216).
  • 6During the second war between Rome and Carthage and when Rome was dealing with Spain in the second century to 133 b.c.
  • 7In 387 (or 390) b.c. Gauls invaded Italy and sacked Rome. There were other events adverse to Rome before Julius Caesar’s campaigns in Gaul (59–51).
  • 8Alleged founder in 248 b.c. of the dynasty in Parthia which lasted till a.d. 226.
  • 9The Romans suffered several adversities in warfare against the Parthians, the most shocking being the destruction of M. Licinius Crassus and his army in 53 b.c. at Carrhae in Mesopotamia.
  • 10P. Ventidius Bassus, of the Piceni in Italy, was a mule-driver who rose from the ranks to be consul in 43 b.c. In 38 he expelled the Parthians from Asia Minor and Syria after a two years’ occupation and killed Pacorus, son of Orodes the Parthian king.
