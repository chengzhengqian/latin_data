Germania

41. These sections of the Suebi extend into the more secluded parts of Germany; nearer to us—to follow the course of the Danube, as before I followed the Rhine—comes the state of the Hermunduri:1 they are loyal to Rome, and with them alone of Germans business is transacted not only on the river-bank, but far within the frontier in the most thriving colony2 of the province of Raetia. They cross the river everywhere without supervision; and while we let other peoples see only our fortified camps, to them we have thrown open our houses and homes, because they do not covet them. Among the Hermunduri rises the River Albis3—a river once known and famous; now a name only.

  • 1The Hermunduri as a whole extended from Regensburg on the Danube over Franconia to part of Thuringia. On his way from the Danube to the Elbe during the years 7–2 b.c. L. Domitius Ahenobarbus settled the southern part of them as friends of Rome, in lands left by the Marcomani (see note 5, p. 199); the northern part of the tribe fought against Tiberius in a.d. 5 when he crossed the Elbe. The southern portion supported the Marcomani in the warfare waged against the latter by Emperor Marcus Aurelius, a.d. 167–180. The Thuringians of today are descended from the Thuringi, the later name of the Hermunduri.
  • 2Augusta Vindelicum (Augsburg) founded by Drusus after the conquest of Raetia in 15 b.c. It was not a colonia.
  • 3The Elbe, here apparently confused with the Saale—a tributary of the Elbe. [There is another Saale, a tributary of the Main.] The Elbe became known to the Romans through the expedition of Drusus in 9 b.c.; the activities of Domitius Ahenobarbus 7–2 b.c.; and the second (a.d. 5) of Tiberius’ campaigns of a.d. 4 and 5. Emperor Augustus had intended the Elbe to be the frontier; but after the destruction of Quintilius Varus in a.d. 9 the frontier remained on the Rhine and the Danube.
  • 4In the Oberpfalz between the Bohemian Forest and the Franconian Jura.
  • 5The Marcomani, a Suebic people, a little before 200 b.c. came through the Hercynian Forest and overran the regions of the Main and the Rhine and settled along the valley of the Main in Franconia and southwards to the Danube. They helped Ariovistus against Julius Caesar. After their defeat by Drusus in 9 b.c. Maroboduus during the last few years of the century led them into Bohemia to be the core of a mighty league (see below). The Quadi (Suevi in Caesar’s account?) went with them and settled in Moravia. In a.d. 19 Emperor Tiberius established the Quadian prince Vannius over Marcomanic peoples, whom T. settled, after Maroboduus’ flight, by the Danube. Both tribes were now clients of Rome until the Marcomanic wars, a.d. 167 onwards, of M. Aurelius.
