Germania

Mercury,1 to whom on certain days they count even the sacrifice of human life lawful. Hercules2 and Mars3 they appease with such animal life as is permissible. A section of the Suebi4 sacrifices also to Isis:5 the cause and origin of this foreign worship I have not succeeded in discovering, except that the emblem itself, which takes the shape of a Liburnian galley, shows that the ritual is imported.

Apart from this they deem it incompatible with the majesty of the heavenly host to confine the gods within walls, or to mould them into any likeness of the human face: they consecrate groves and coppices, and they give the divine names to that mysterious something which is visible only to the eyes of faith.

  • 1Wotan (Wodan, Woden; Othin, Odin of Scandinavia). Thus the Roman fourth day of the week, dies Mercurii, became for Germans “Wodanstag,” our “Wednesday,” French “Mercredi,” Italian “Mercoledi.”
  • 2Donar, Norse Thor, strong god with hammer for Hercules’ club. Later he was equated with Jupiter, and the fifth day of the week was called “Donnerstag ”(Norse “Thorstag”), our “Thursday,” French “Jeudi,” Italian “Giovedì.”
  • 3Ziu or Tiu. The Romans’ third day of the week, dies Martis (French “Mardi,” Italian “Martedì”) became Scandinavian “Tysdagr,” our “Tuesday,” while the older German “Dingstag,” now “Dienstag,” comes from Mars Thingsus, that is Tiu as god of the (armed) Assembly (see Tacitus, Germania, 11 and 12), in old German “Thing” or “Ding.” Many religious minds no doubt equated Tiu with the sky-god, Sanskrit Dyaus, Greek Zeus, Latin Iuppiter, thus equating what we call Tuesday with what we call Thursday (see above).
  • 4See Chapter 38.
  • 5Tacitus means the “real” Egyptian goddess, protectress of shipping (she was other things also). But near Augsburg a goddess Cisa or Ziza was worshipped.
  • 6Tacitus means not only hazel but also beech, oak and other trees.
  • 7Too early for runes. There is no certain evidence that Germans could write in Tacitus’ time.
