XI.—Ausonius to Tetradius,2 Greeting

O thou, who with copious eloquence enrichest our ancient stores of wit, Tetradius, and takest heed that thy tart compositions be not gloomy and bereft of sweetness; who, blending gall and honey in thy verse, sufferest not thy Muses to grow dull, and flavourest alike what is insipid to the taste and what bitter to the palate; thou who outstrip’st the unpolished Muses of Suessa,3 yielding in age to them but not in style; why dost thou shun me, neighbour to the walls of Saintes, as of old the Roman youth fled from the Lucanian oxen4 who renewed the battle with exceeding fury? Not like a tiger, not with lion’s spring, but in fond love I seek thee out. I yearn to see my pupil’s countenance and to enjoy the longed-for fruits of his mind. Reluctant hitherto I have gulped down the necessity which parted us in bygone days when Iculisma5 kept thee hidden, once fettered with the heavy chains of teaching,

  • 2Otherwise unknown.
  • 3Now Sessa, in Campania; the birthplace of Lucilius the satirist.
  • 4“Lucanian Oxen” was the name given by the Romans to elephants as first seen in Lucania in the army of Pyrrhus.
