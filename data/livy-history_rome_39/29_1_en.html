They were further instructed by senatorial decree to raise two new legions; and they levied from the allies and holders of Latin rights 20,000 infantry and 800 cavalry and enrolled 3,000 Roman infantry and 200 cavalry. It was decided that this entire armament, the legions excepted, should be taken to reinforce the army in Spain. The consuls had therefore put Titus Maenius65 in charge of the mobilization of the troops while they were tied up with their investigations.

On the completion of the investigations it was Quintus Marcius who set out first, against the Ligurian Apuani.66 While he was pursuing these deep into some secluded forests, which had always served them as a place of retreat and shelter, Marcius was surrounded on difficult ground in a pass that the enemy had occupied ahead of him. Four thousand men were lost; and three standards of the second legion as well as eleven banners of the allies and holders of Latin rights fell into enemy hands together with a large quantity of weapons (abandoned at various points because they impeded flight along the woodland paths). The Ligurians gave up the chase before the Romans did their flight. As soon as he emerged from the enemy’s land, the consul demobilized his army in pacified territory so that the extent of his troop losses would not be evident.67 He could not even so erase the infamy of the debacle as the wood from which the Ligurians had chased him was then dubbed the “Marcian Wood.”
