Manlius distributed forty-two denarii to each of his infantrymen, double to each centurion and triple to each cavalryman; and he also doubled their pay. Many men of all ranks who had been awarded military decorations followed the chariot. And the songs sung by the soldiers to their general were such that it was readily apparent that they were addressed to an undemanding leader who courted popularity and that the triumph was more warmly welcomed by the troops than by civilians. But Manlius’ friends succeeded in winning over the support of the people, too: under pressure from them a senatorial decree was passed stipulating that the as-yet unrepaid portion of the tribute contributed by the people to the public purse be discharged with some of the money carried in the triumph. The urban quaestors scrupulously paid twenty five and a half asses on each thousand of census assessment.29

At about the same time two military tribunes came from the two Spains with a letter from Gaius Atinius and Lucius Manlius, who were governors of those provinces. From this letter it was ascertained that the Celtiberians and Lusitanians30 were up in arms and raiding the territory of Roman allies. Consideration of the issue the senate left entirely to the new magistrates.
