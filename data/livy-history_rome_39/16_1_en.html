He had been despoiled of his assets by those who should have been the last people to do that, she said, and he was now at her house. He had been thrown out by his mother because, being a decent young man, he refused to be initiated in what were rumored to be disgusting rites (and may the gods forgive her for saying so!).

12. Feeling that he had learned enough about Aebutius to see that he was no unreliable informant, the consul let Aebutia go and asked his mother-in-law to send for Hispala, a freedwoman also from the Aventine who was not unknown in the quarter—he had questions to put to her, too.

On receiving the message, Hispala was frightened—without knowing why, she was being summoned by a woman of such distinction and dignity—and when she caught sight of the lictors in the forecourt, the crowd around the consul and then the consul himself, she almost fainted. She was taken to the interior of the house where the consul, in his mother-in-law’s presence, informed her that she need not be alarmed if she could bring herself to tell the truth—on this she would have the guaranty of such a lady as Sulpicia or indeed himself. He then bade her divulge to him what usually went on at the nocturnal rite of the Bacchanalia in the grove of Stimula.41 When she heard this, such panic and such trembling overtook the woman throughout her body that she was long unable to open her mouth. Finally pulling herself together, she said that she had undergone initiation with her mistress when she was just a young slave girl but for several years now, ever since her manumission, she had had no idea of what went on there.
