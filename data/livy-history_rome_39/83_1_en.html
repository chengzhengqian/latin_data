But for all his distress and groaning—intensified by seeing his son spending almost more time with the Romans than with him—he was none the less punctilious in meeting all the Romans’ demands in order not give them reason to open hostilities against him right then. Feeling also that he should divert their thoughts from such plans,214 he led his army through the middle of Thrace on a campaign against the Odrysae, Dentheleti and Bessi.215 He took the city of Philippopolis,216 whose inhabitants had fled and retreated with their families to the neighboring hilltops, and he accepted the surrender of the barbarian plainsmen after ravaging their territory. Then, leaving a garrison at Philippopolis—it was shortly afterward expelled by the Odrysae—he proceeded to found a city in Derriopus (an area of Paeonia), close to the Erigonus, a river that flows from Illyricum through Paeonia and empties into the River Axius not far from the ancient city of Stobi. He had the new city named Perseis as a mark of honor for his elder son.217
