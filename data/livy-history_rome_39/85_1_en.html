Accordingly, they would send ambassadors back with them to the consul with orders for all their possessions to be restored to them if they returned to their place of origin; and then these men would continue straight on over the Alps to warn the Gallic peoples there to keep their teeming population at home. The Alps lay between them as a virtually impassable frontier, they said, and they certainly would have no more success than the Gauls who had originally created a way over them. The deputation comprised Lucius Furius Purpureo, Quintus Minucius and Lucius Manlius Acidinus. When all the goods that they possessed without detriment to anyone had been restored to them, the Gauls left Italy.

55. The Transalpine peoples were courteous in their response to the Roman deputation. Their elders took the Roman people to task for being too lenient in letting off scot-free men who had, without authorization from their people, attempted to occupy territory under Roman rule and build a fortified settlement on other people’s land—a heavy penalty should have been fixed for their effrontery. As for the Romans restoring those men’s property, they added, they feared that others might be encouraged to similar presumptuous acts by such soft-heartedness. The elders both welcomed them and bade them farewell with gifts.

After driving the Gauls from his province, the consul Marcus Claudius next began to engineer a war with the Histrians, having first sent a letter to the senate to ask permission to take his legions across to Histria.220 The senate opposed this.
