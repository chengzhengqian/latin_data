Gaius Atinius, who had left for that province as praetor two years earlier, had engaged the Lusitanians in pitched battle in the territory of Hasta.68 As many as 6,000 of the enemy were killed and the rest were scattered and put to flight, with their camp taken from them. Atinius then took his legions forward to assault the town of Hasta and captured this, too, with no more of a struggle than he had the camp; but as he approached the city walls with too little caution he was struck by a weapon and died from the wound a few days later. When the dispatch reporting the death of the propraetor was read out, the senate voted to send a man to overtake the praetor Gaius Calpurnius at the port of Luna69 and advise him that the senate thought it appropriate for him to hasten his departure so the province should not be without a governor. The man who was sent reached Luna in three days; Calpurnius had left a few days earlier.

In Hither Spain, too, Lucius Manlius Acidinus, who had gone to his province at the same time as Gaius Atinius, clashed with the Celtiberians. The combatants parted with the victory still undecided but for the fact that the Celtiberians moved their camp from there the next night and the Romans were thus given the opportunity both to bury their dead and also scoop up spoils from their enemy. The Celtiberians then put together a larger army and a few days later went on the offensive against the Romans at the town of Calagurris.70 No explanation has been left on record as to what made them weaker when their numbers were increased.
