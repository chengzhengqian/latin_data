“A short while ago the envoys of Eumenes took it for granted (for heaven’s sake!) that it was fairer for Eumenes rather than me to have Antiochus’ former possessions. I have a very different view on this. You see, Eumenes could not have remained in his kingdom if the Romans had not undertaken the war in the first place, not just won it.104 Thus he has received a favor from you, not you from him. As for my own kingdom, so far from any part of it being at risk, I in fact rejected the overtures of Antiochus who, to bribe me into an alliance, actually came to me with a promise of 3,000 talents, fifty decked ships and all the city-states of Greece that I had formerly held.105 I chose to be his enemy even before Manius Acilius brought his army over to Greece. With that consul, moreover, I took on whatever part he assigned to me in the war and, when the succeeding consul, Lucius Scipio, decided to take his army to the Hellespont by land, I did not simply grant him passage through my kingdom but even built roads, constructed bridges and provided supplies for him. And I did this not only throughout Macedonia but also throughout Thrace where, apart from everything else, peace had to be kept with the barbarians.106 In view of my support—let me not say ‘my services’—for you, which course should you have followed, you Romans?
