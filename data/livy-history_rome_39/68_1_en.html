At Placentia, Antias writes, a woman of some notoriety with whom Quinctius was completely infatuated had been invited to dine with him. There Quinctius was showing off to the whore and in the course of his remarks commented on the severity with which he had handled his court cases and on the large number of condemned men he then had in irons and whom he was going to behead. The woman, reclining below him on the couch, observed at that point that she had never seen anyone conduct a beheading and that was something she really would like to see. The indulgent lover thereupon gave orders for one of those poor wretches to be dragged before him and beheaded him with an ax.

This was a ruthless and heinous crime, whether perpetrated in the manner alleged by the censor or as described by Valerius: a human victim sacrificed and a table bespattered with blood to provide a spectacle for a shameless whore lying in the consul’s arms! And that in a setting of drinking and feasting when it was customary to make libations to the gods and offer fitting prayers. At the end of Cato’s speech a proposal is made to Quinctius. If he denied that the incident happened and rejected all the other charges against him, he should defend himself by putting down a judicial wager;172 but if he admitted the charges, why should he think anyone would feel sympathy for his disgrace when he had made a party game out of a man’s lifeblood while deranged with drink and lust?
