Then, carried away by anger, Philip added that the sun had not yet set on all his days, a threat that not only the Thessalians but the Romans, too, took as being personally directed at them. When the uproar that followed his remarks eventually subsided, Philip made his reply to the envoys of the Perrhaebians and Athamanians. The situation of all the city-states they were discussing was the same, he told them. The consul Acilius and the Romans had granted them to him as being cities of the enemy. If those who had made the gift wished to withdraw it, he knew he must comply, but they would only be wronging a better and more loyal friend to please allies who were fickle and worthless. Nothing, he said, produced gratitude of shorter duration than the granting of freedom, especially in the case of people who would spoil it by misuse.

When the hearing concluded, the Roman commissioners announced their decision: the Macedonian garrisons were to be withdrawn from the cities in question and Philip’s kingdom was to be limited to the old boundaries of Macedonia.97 Concerning the wrongs forming the basis of the two sides’ complaints against each other, the commissioners declared a legal procedure would need to be established for resolving the contentious issues between the peoples involved and the Macedonians.
