The mother approached the young man and told him that she had made a vow for him when he had been ill, promising to initiate him in the rites of Bacchus as soon as he recovered. She was duty bound to fulfill the vow, thanks to heaven’s kindness, she said, and now wished to discharge it. There was need of a ten-day period of sexual abstinence;37 on the tenth day Aebutius was to dine and then ritually purify himself and she would take him to the shrine.

There was a well-known prostitute, a freedwoman called Hispala Faecenia.38 She deserved more than that occupation; she had taken it up as slave girl and had continued to keep herself by the same line of work even after her manumission. A sexual relationship with Aebutius arose from their being neighbors but this in no way damaged the young man’s finances or his good name: it was he who was the object of Hispala’s affections and he who had been sought out by her, and while his own family was niggardly in every respect toward him he was actually supported by the courtesan’s generosity. In fact, her affair with him had progressed to the point where, on her patron’s death, she, being under nobody’s legal authority,39 petitioned the tribunes and the praetor for a guardian, made a will and appointed Aebutius her sole heir.
