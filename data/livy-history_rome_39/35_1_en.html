What had particularly concerned the senate was that they had heard that Philip’s attempt to seize Aenus and Maronea86 was now in progress—they were less worried about the Thessalians. Athamanian representatives had also come before them complaining not that they had lost part of their lands or some piece of territory but rather that all Athamania had fallen under the control and authority of the king.87 In addition, Maronean exiles had come, men who had been driven out for championing the cause of freedom against the king’s garrison, and they brought word that not just Maronea but Aenus, too, was in Philip’s hands.

Representatives had also come from Philip to clear him of these charges, and they claimed that the king had taken no action without permission from the Roman commanders. They argued that the Thessalian, Perrhaebian and Magnesian city-states and the Athamanian people, along with Amynander, had been in the same position as the Aetolians. After defeating King Antiochus, the consul had been preoccupied with attacking the cities of Aetolia and had sent Philip to recover the states in question; crushed by force of arms, they were now subject to the king. To avoid taking a decision in the king’s absence, the senate dispatched Quintus Caecilius Metellus, Marcus Baebius Tamphilus and Tiberius Sempronius as its representatives to resolve the disputes.
