The enemy had numbered more than 35,000, of whom only this tiny fraction survived the battle. One hundred and thirty-two standards were captured. Not many more than 600 Romans and allies fell, plus about 150 provincial auxiliaries. The loss of five military tribunes and a few Roman cavalrymen made the victory appear particularly bloody. The Romans remained in the enemy camp because they had not had time to fortify their own. At an assembly the following day the cavalry men were praised by Gaius Calpurnius and presented with decorations for their mounts,117 and Calpurnius declared that it was thanks mostly to them that the enemy had been routed and the camp then occupied and taken by storm. The other praetor, Quinctius, presented his own cavalry with ornamental chains and clasps. Presentations were also made to a large number of centurions from the armies of both praetors, particularly those who held the center.

32. On the conclusion of their troop levies and other duties in Rome, the consuls led the army against the Ligurians (who were their province). Sempronius118 set off from Pisae against the Ligurian Apuani,119 and by laying waste their fields and burning their villages and fortresses he opened up a pass all the way to the River Macra and the port of Luna.120 The enemy occupied a hill that had been the ancient seat of their ancestors, but the difficulty of the terrain was overcome and in a battle they were also forcefully dislodged.
