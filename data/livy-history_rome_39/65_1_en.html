This was not only to increase their own chances of attaining the position or because they objected to seeing a “new man”162 as censor, but also because they anticipated a strict censorship (and one that would put at risk the reputation of many) from a man harmed by numerous people and eager to inflict harm himself. And, indeed, during his campaign at this time he was making threats, alleging that opposition to him came from men who feared a forthright and tough censorship. At the same time he also gave his support to Lucius Valerius; only with him as his colleague could he chasten the new wave of vice and revive traditional morality, he said. People were fired by these words, and over the objections of the nobility they not only elected Marcus Porcius censor but also gave him Lucius Valerius Flaccus as his colleague.

After the election of the censors, the consuls and praetors set off for their provinces, all except Quintus Naevius. His departure for Sardinia was delayed for no less than four months by his investigations into the poisonings,163 most of which he conducted outside Rome in the towns and market centers, because that seemed more convenient. If one chooses to believe Valerius Antias, Naevius found as many as 2,000 people guilty. In addition, the praetor Lucius Postumius, to whom Tarentum had fallen as his province, punished some widespread conspiracies by shepherds and also painstakingly mopped up what remained of the Bacchanalian investigation.
