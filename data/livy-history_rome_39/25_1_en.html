After that they proclaimed an edict outlawing anyone selling or purchasing anything for the purpose of fleeing justice, and forbidding anyone to harbor, conceal or help fugitives by any means.

When the meeting adjourned there was great trepidation throughout the city and it did not stop at the walls or the bounds of Rome; panic began to spread the whole length of Italy as letters were received from friends on the subject of the senatorial decree, the meeting and the edict of the consuls. During the night following the day on which the affair was made public at the meeting, a large number of fugitives were caught and brought back by the triumvirs, who had posted guards at the gates; and the names of many were reported by informers. Some of them, both men and women, committed suicide. More than 7,000 men and women were said to have been involved in the intrigue. However, it was established that the ringleaders of the conspiracy were Marcus and Gaius Atinius from the Roman plebs, a Faliscan Lucius Opicernius and a Campanian Minius Cerrinius.50 It was said that all the criminal and immoral activity started with these men and that they were the most important priests and organizers of the cult. Steps were taken to have them arrested at the earliest opportunity. Brought before the consuls, they confessed their own guilt and did not hesitate to furnish information.
