11. When he came home and his mother brought up the steps involved in the rites that he needed to take on that and the following days, Aebutius replied that he would take none of them and had no intention of being initiated. His stepfather was party to the conversation. The woman immediately exclaimed that Aebutius could not go without sleeping with Hispala for ten nights and that it was because he had been infected by that snake’s poisonous wheedling that he had no respect for his mother, his stepfather, or the gods. Pouring reproaches on him, mother from one side, and stepfather from the other, they drove him from the house along with four slaves.

The young man went from there to his aunt Aebutia,40 and told her why he had been thrown into the street by his mother. Then, at Aebutia’s urging, he reported the affair to the consul Postumius in a private conversation the following day. The consul sent him off under orders to return in two days, and himself asked his own mother-in-law Sulpicia, a dignified lady, whether she knew an old woman from the Aventine called Aebutia. When Sulpicia answered that she did know her and that she was a decent woman with traditional values, the consul said that he needed to meet her—Sulpicia should send her a message asking her to come.
