Employing a certain Cassander, one of the king’s party and a longtime resident of Maronea, Onomastus let some Thracians into the town at night and produced such carnage as occurs when a city is taken in war. When the Roman commissioners protested against such ruthless treatment of the unoffending Maroneans and such high-handed behavior toward the Roman people—men being butchered as enemies when the senate

had voted that they should have their independence restored—the king’s response was to deny any involvement on the part of himself or any of his supporters. There had been fighting among the Maroneans because of political dissension, he said, with some trying to bring the state to support him and others to support Eumenes. The Romans would easily establish this—they should question the Maroneans themselves (Philip had no doubt that the Maroneans were all terror-stricken after the recent bloodbath and that no one would dare open his mouth against him). Appius replied that such an open-and-shut case required no investigation, as though there were some doubt about it. If Philip wished to exculpate himself, he should send to Rome Onomastus and Cassander, by whom it was said the crime had been committed, so the senate could interrogate them. This suggestion at first so shook the king that his color drained and he pulled a face. Finally composing himself, he said that if that was absolutely the commissioners’ wish he would send Cassander, who had been at Maronea, but he wondered how Onomastus could be involved when he had not even been anywhere near Maronea, much less in it.
