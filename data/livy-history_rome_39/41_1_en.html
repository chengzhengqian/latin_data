All decent men, those who cared about liberty and the laws, had either been driven from their country and were living in exile or were holding their tongues, disrespected and at the mercy of their inferiors. The delegates also added a few remarks about territorial rights. When Quintus Fabius Labeo had been in the region, they said, he had established as Philip’s boundary the old royal road that led to Parorea in Thrace, which at no point went down to the sea; but Philip had later made a new road that veered off to take in the cities and farmland of the Maroneans.101

In answer, Philip took a very different line from that which he had just used in dealing with the Thessalians and Perrhaebians. “My argument is not with the Maroneans or Eumenes,” he declared. “It is now with you, Romans, since I have long been aware that I am receiving no fair treatment from you. That the Macedonian communities that had defected from me during the truce102 should be restored to me I considered only fair, not because they would have been a great addition to my realm—they are small towns and they also lie on our farthest frontiers—but because it was an important precedent for keeping the other Macedonians under control. My request was denied.
