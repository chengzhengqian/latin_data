BOOK XXXIX

Scipio Asiagenes173 had his horse taken from him. The censorship was also rigorous and severe on all classes in its property assessments. The iuratores174 were instructed to assess at ten times their market value decorative articles, women’s dresses and carriages worth more than 15,000 asses.175 Slaves under twenty years of age who had fetched 10,000 asses or more since the last lustrum he likewise ordered to be assessed at ten times their market value. All these items were to be taxed at a rate of three asses per thousand.

The censors shut off all public water flowing into any private building or private land;176 and structures owned by private individuals that had been built or erected on public land they demolished within thirty days. They next contracted out works that were to be financed by moneys officially designated for the purpose: the paving of cisterns with stone, the cleaning of sewers where necessary, and the construction of new ones on the Aventine and other areas where none yet existed. They also completed projects independently: Flaccus built a causeway at the Neptunian waters177 to provide a public footpath, and a road

  • 173Brother of Africanus, whom he wished to emulate by taking the name Asiagenes (later Latinized to Asiaticus) to commemorate his victory over Antiochus (cf. 37.58.6 and note). His horse may have been removed on the grounds of his being physically unfit for military service, but as this did not usually happen to elderly or unfit ex-consuls (who were not expected to serve as cavalrymen), Cato’s real motive was to insult the man.
  • 174iuratores (assessors) were tax officials who received the declarations of property made by citizens, and given under oath, for taxation purposes.
  • 175The Latin has been supplemented here (see textual note), but the content is fairly certain. Plutarch (Cat. Mai. 18.2–3) states that Cato “had all clothing, carriages, women’s jewelry and household appurtenances, whose value exceeded 1,500 drachmas in each case, assessed at ten times their value, since he wished there to be larger tax revenue deriving from the larger assessments.”
  • 176People had clearly been illegally diverting water for their own private use.
  • 177Location uncertain; possibly the “so-called Fons Neptunius” (Vitr. 8.3.15) at Tarracina on the Via Appia.
