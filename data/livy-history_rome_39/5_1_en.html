Aemilius wanted to speak against the proposal, he said, and on his departure for his province had instructed Aburius to see that opening a discussion of the issue be held back until his arrival. For Fulvius it simply meant a loss of time, he said—the senate would follow its own inclinations in the vote even in the consul’s presence.

Then Fulvius declared that if people had been unaware of Marcus Aemilius’ feud with him or the extent to which Aemilius kept up that quarrel with an obsessive and almost tyrannical passion16—even then, he said, it would have been intolerable for an absent consul to obstruct honor being paid to the immortal gods and delay a triumph well and truly deserved. It would even then have been intolerable for a general, who had conducted a superb campaign, and his victorious army to be kept standing before the city gates with their plunder and prisoners of war until the consul—who was stalling for this very reason—should choose to return to Rome.
