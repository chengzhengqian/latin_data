And as he had now brought tranquility to the province after the hostilities, he built a road from Bononia to Arretium in order not to have his men kept idle.5

The other consul, Marcus Aemilius, burned and pillaged the fields and villages of the Ligurians living in the plains and valleys while the inhabitants were occupying two mountains, Ballista and Suismontium. He then attacked those who were on the mountains and, at first wearing them out with skirmishes, he finally forced them into a regular engagement and defeated them in pitched battle, during which he also vowed a temple to Diana.6 After crushing all the Ligurians south of the Apennines, Aemilius next attacked those beyond the mountains—including those Ligurian Friniates that Gaius Flaminius had not reached—and brought them all to their knees, disarmed them and moved them en masse from the mountains to the plains.

With the Ligurians pacified, Aemilius led his army into Gallic territory and constructed a road from Placentia to Ariminum7 to join up with the Flaminian Way. In the last pitched battle in which he clashed with the Ligurians he made a vow of a temple to Queen Juno. Such were that year’s operations in Liguria.
