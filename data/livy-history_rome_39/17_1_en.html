At this point the consul commended her for not denying that she had been initiated but pressed her to make full disclosure of everything else, with the same guaranty. When Hispala said she knew no more, the consul declared that if she were refuted by the testimony of another she would not receive the same indulgence or favor as she would if she herself confessed. And he concluded by saying that the man who had heard the story from her had told him everything.

13. Believing that it was undoubtedly Aebutius who had divulged her secret (as was, indeed, the case), the woman threw herself at Sulpicia’s feet. At first she began to beg her not even to take seriously, and certainly not turn into a capital charge, a freedwoman’s chatter with her lover; it was only to scare Aebutius that she had said these things, not because she really knew anything. At this Postumius flew into a rage and said that Hispala evidently thought she was even then exchanging pleasantries with her lover Aebutius instead of conversing in the home of a lady of great dignity, and with a consul. Sulpicia raised up the frightened woman and offered her words of encouragement while simultaneously calming her son-in-law’s anger. Eventually Hispala pulled herself together and, after a long tirade against the disloyalty of Aebutius for thus repaying her for her devoted service to him, she declared that while she greatly feared the gods whose secret rites she would expose, she feared much more the men who with their own hands would tear her apart42 for informing on them.
