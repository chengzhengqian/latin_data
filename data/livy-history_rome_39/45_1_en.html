BOOK XXXIX

30. In Spain that same year, the praetors Gaius Calpurnius and Lucius Quinctius113 brought their troops out of winter quarters at the beginning of spring and joined up in Baeturia. They then advanced into Carpetania,114 where the enemy camp was situated, ready to prosecute the war together with a joint plan of action. Not far from the cities of Toletum and Dipo115 fighting broke out among the foragers, who were given support from their camps on their respective sides, and gradually all the troops were brought out into the line of battle. In this irregular encounter both the home ground and the nature of the fighting favored the enemy. The two Roman armies were routed and forced back into their camp. The enemy did not press their advantage against the demoralized Romans. The Roman praetors, fearing the camp would be attacked the next day, gave a silent signal in the still of the night that followed and led the army away. At first light the Spaniards deployed their line of battle and advanced to the rampart. Entering a camp that was unexpectedly deserted they pillaged the things left behind in the confusion of the night and then returned to their own camp and spent a few days inactive there at their base. About 5,000 Romans and allies were killed in the battle and the retreat, and the enemy armed themselves with the spoils taken from the dead. They then moved off toward the River Tagus.116

The Roman praetors meanwhile spent all this time bringing together auxiliary forces from the allied Spanish communities and rebuilding the confidence of the men, who were terrified after the defeat.
