In combat he was a valiant soldier with a brilliant record in many famous battles, and he was also an outstanding general after he reached the higher offices. In peacetime, too, he was a great expert if consulted on the law and he displayed great eloquence if he had to plead a case. Nor was he simply one who, though his eloquence bloomed while he was alive, left no record of his speaking ability; in fact, his eloquence remains alive and vigorous, immortalized in all manner of written texts. There are numerous speeches written in defense of himself160 and others both in defense of and against other people. He ground down his enemies not simply by prosecuting them but also in the process of making his own defense. The vendettas that harassed him—and which he mounted against others—were all too numerous, and one could not easily say which was the greater, the pressure put on him by the nobility or his baiting of the nobility. He was undoubtedly a fractious personality with a sharp and too free a tongue; but he had a character immune to the base appetites, an unshakable integrity, and no regard for influence and wealth. In frugality and ability to face hardship and danger he was physically and mentally almost hard as iron and even old age, which breaks down everything, did not crush him. At eighty-six he defended himself in court, delivering in person a speech of his own composition, and in his ninetieth year he arraigned Servius Galba before the people.161
