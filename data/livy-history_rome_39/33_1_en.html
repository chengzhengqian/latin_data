The consul Spurius Postumius had earlier reported that when he traveled along both coasts of Italy for his investigations he had found two colonies abandoned: Sipontum on the Adriatic Sea and Buxentum on the Tyrrhenian.81 So, at the end of the year, in accordance with a senatorial decree, a board of three—Lucius Scribonius Libo, Marcus Tuccius and Gnaeus Baebius Tamphilus—was set up by the urban praetor Titus Maenius to enroll colonists for these places.

The war that was now imminent with King Perseus and the Macedonians arose not from the causes that most now accept and did not lie with Perseus himself.82 Its origins go back to Philip and had he lived longer he would have fought the war himself. There was one thing that particularly galled him when conditions were imposed on him after his defeat: he had been deprived by the senate of the right to vent his fury on those Macedonians who had defected from him during the war, and he had not been without hope of being granted this because, in the peace terms, Quinctius had left the entire matter in abeyance. Later on, when King Antiochus had been beaten in the field at Thermopylae, there had been a division of responsibilities, with the consul Acilius attacking Heraclea and Philip attacking Lamia at the same time.
