The king had there seized merchant vessels that could bypass Thebes and make for Demetrias, and he had thereby diverted all maritime trade to that port. And now he did not even stop short of violating ambassadors, who were sacrosanct under international law—he had set an ambush for them when they were going to see Titus Quinctius.92 The result was that all Thessalians had been thrown into such panic that nobody dared open his mouth in his own community or in the communal meetings of the nation. The Romans, authors of their freedom, were far

off, and they had a stern master sticking close to them who was preventing them from enjoying the benefits provided by the Roman people. What use was being free if there was no freedom of expression, they asked. Even now, despite their confidence in, and the protection of, the envoys, they were uttering laments rather than speaking out. Unless the Romans made some provision for reducing the fear of Greeks living close to Macedonia and the audacity of Philip, his defeat and their liberation had both been pointless. Like an obstinate horse, they said, the king should be chastened with harsher reins if he did not obey. Such were the bitter words of those who spoke last, whereas earlier speakers had gently tried to sooth Philip’s anger, begging his pardon for speaking up for their freedom and asking him to renounce the severity of an overlord, accustom himself to behave as their ally and friend, and emulate the Roman people, who preferred to win allies for themselves though affection rather than intimidation.
