I had long feared this gathering storm, seeing the anything-but-brotherly looks passing between you two and hearing some of your language. But the hope would occasionally come to me that the flames of your anger could subside and your suspicions be cleared away. Even enemies, I mused, had put down their weapons and made peace, and the private feuds of many men had been brought to an end. I thought that at some stage the recollection that you are brothers would come to you, and the

recollection of the open and friendly relationship you had as boys and, indeed, of the things I taught you—though I fear these lessons may have been fruitless and fallen on deaf ears. How often in your hearing have I expressed my abhorrence of those examples of brothers quarreling, and recounted the terrible results of them—how they completely destroyed themselves and their families, their homes and their kingdoms! I also produced for you better examples on the other side: the harmonious partnership between the two kings of Sparta, which for many generations provided security for them and their fatherland, and the overthrow of that same state when the practice of individuals seizing personal tyranny grew up. Then I pointed to the still living brothers Eumenes and Attalus, whose beginnings were so modest23 that one would almost blush at using the title ‘king,’ but with nothing more than brotherly concord these built a kingdom to rival mine and that of Antiochus or any other king today.
