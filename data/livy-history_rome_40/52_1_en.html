They brought word of the two successful battles, the surrender of Celtiberia and the completion of Fulvius’ mission, and added that neither the payment normally sent to the province nor the grain transported to the army was needed for that year. They made two requests of the senate, first that the immortal gods be honored in the light of the successful operations and secondly that Quintus Fulvius be permitted to take with him, when he left the province, the army of whose gallant service both he and many praetors before him had availed themselves. Apart from being the correct procedure, this was almost an unavoidable one, they said. The soldiers were so set on leaving that keeping them any longer in the province appeared impossible and they would go without authorization if they were not demobilized or would break into a catastrophic mutiny if anyone coerced them into staying.

The senate ordered that Liguria should be the province of the two consuls, and the praetorian sortition then went as follows: the urban jurisdiction came to Aulus Hostilius, the foreigners’ jurisdiction to Tiberius Minucius, Sicily to Publius Cornelius and Sardinia to Gaius Maenius.

The Spanish provinces were drawn by Lucius Postumius (Farther) and Tiberius Sempronius (Hither).
