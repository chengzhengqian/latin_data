The move so surprised the Ligurians that they were as panic-stricken as if they had been caught in an ambush. For a brief moment there was some semblance of a battle; then there was only disordered flight and a massacre of fleeing Ligurians in all quarters, since the cavalry had been given the signal to mount up and allow none to escape. All of them were driven into their camps in a frenzied rout and then the camps were also taken from them.

More than 15,000 Ligurians were killed on that day, and 2,500 were taken prisoner. Three days later the whole nation of the Ligurian Ingauni gave hostages and capitulated. The pilots and crews that had manned their pirate ships were hunted down and all thrown in prison. Thirty two such ships were also captured on the Ligurian coast by the duumvir Gaius Matienus.

Lucius Aurelius Cotta and Gaius Sulpicius Galus were sent to Rome to report on these events and deliver a dispatch to the senate; and they were also to request permission for Lucius Aemilius to quit his sphere of command, its duties having been discharged, and bring back and demobilize his men. Both requests were granted by the senate and a three-day period of supplication at all the couches86 was also authorized. Instructions were issued to the praetors as well; Petillius was to demobilize the city legions and Fabius to terminate his conscription among the allies and those with Latin rights.
