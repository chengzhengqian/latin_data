30. A major war broke out in Hither Spain that summer. The Celtiberians had mustered some 35,000 men, a number almost unprecedented before that time. Quintus Fulvius Flaccus was governor of the province.93 He had heard that the Celtiberians were putting their men of military age under arms and he had himself mobilized as great a force of auxiliaries as he could from the allies, but he in no way rivaled the enemy in numbers. At the beginning of spring Flaccus led his army into Carpetania and encamped near the settlement of Aebura94 after installing a garrison in the town. A few days later the Celtiberians pitched camp about two miles from him at the foot of a hill. When the Roman praetor became aware of their arrival, he dispatched his brother Marcus Fulvius with two squadrons of allied cavalry toward the enemy camp on a reconnoitering expedition. Fulvius was told to get as close as possible to the enemy rampart in order to inspect the dimensions of the camp, but to avoid combat and fall back if he saw the enemy cavalry making a sortie.

Fulvius followed his instructions and for a number of days there was no action apart from the two squadrons letting themselves be seen and then retreating whenever the enemy cavalry charged out of their camp. Eventually, the Celtiberians left the camp with all their infantry and cavalry forces at the same time and took up a position in battle formation midway between the two camps. The plain was completely level and well-suited for combat. The Spaniards stood there waiting for their enemy but the Roman commander kept his men within his rampart.
