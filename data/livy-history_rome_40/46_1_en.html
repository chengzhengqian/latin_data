31. When he was satisfied that so many days of inactivity had led his enemy to expect that he would not initiate any action, the Roman praetor ordered Lucius Acilius to take the left division and 6,000 provincial auxiliaries95 and move around the hill that lay to the rear of the enemy. When he heard the shout, Acilius was to swoop down from there on the enemy camp.

The force set off at night to avoid detection. At dawn, Flaccus sent a commander of the allies, Gaius Scribonius, to the enemy rampart at the head of the supplementary cavalry of the left division. When the Celtiberians saw them coming closer than usual and also in larger numbers, their cavalry came pouring en masse from their camp, and at the same time the signal to march out was given to the infantry. Following his instructions, Scribonius wheeled round his horses as soon as he heard the noise of the enemy cavalry and headed back to camp. This prompted an even more reckless pursuit by the enemy. First came the cavalry and soon after them the infantry line, confidently expecting that they would take the camp by storm that day. They were now no more than half a mile from the rampart; and so when Flaccus, who had deployed his force within the rampart, judged that the enemy had been sufficiently drawn away from the shelter of their camp, he burst forth with his troops from three points simultaneously, raising a war cry not only to fire enthusiasm for the fight but also so the men in the hills could hear it.
