When he saw this, Flaccus calmed the initial commotion in the column by issuing instructions through his centurions for all to remain in place and make ready their weapons. He then brought the baggage and pack animals to one spot and, either in person or through his legates and military tribunes, deployed all the troops as the emergency and terrain required and without a hint of panic. Meanwhile he reminded them that their engagement was with men who had twice surrendered; that the new qualities that these had found were villainy and duplicity, not courage or fortitude; and that they had made a lackluster return to the fatherland into a glorious and memorable one.129 They would, he said, be carrying back to Rome for the triumph swords spattered with the fresh gore of the enemy, and spoils dripping with blood. Time did not permit a longer address; the enemy were starting their attack and there was already fighting at the extremities of the field. Then the lines clashed.

40. It was a ferocious battle in all quarters but fortunes varied. The legions were putting up a magnificent fight and the two allied units were no less energetic. The foreign auxiliaries were under pressure from their counterparts, who were similarly armed but a somewhat better class of soldiers than they, and could not hold their ground. When the Celtiberians realized that they were no match for the legions in ordered battle with lines drawn up against each other, they made a thrust forward in wedge formation, a style of fighting in which they are so strong that resistance is impossible wherever they have directed their charge.
