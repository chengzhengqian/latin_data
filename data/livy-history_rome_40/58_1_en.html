39. In Spain that same year, because his successor was slow to arrive in his province, the proconsul Fulvius Flaccus led his troops from winter quarters and proceeded to lay waste the farther reaches of Celtiberia, where the natives had not surrendered. By this move he provoked rather than cowed the spirit of the barbarians; and they covertly mobilized their forces and blockaded the Manlian Gorge,128 through which they well knew the Roman army would pass.

Now when his colleague, Lucius Postumius Albinus, was leaving for Farther Spain, Gracchus had instructed him to inform Quintus Fulvius that he was to bring the army to Tarraco; there Gracchus wished to discharge the veterans, distribute the supplementary troops and generally reorganize the army. Flaccus was also notified of the day by which his successor was to arrive, and this was close at hand. The arrival of this news obliged Flaccus to abandon the project on which he had already embarked and lead the army swiftly from Celtiberia. The barbarians, unaware of his reason for doing so, supposed he had got wind of their uprising and covert mobilization of their troops, and had lost his nerve, and so they blockaded the gorge with even more determination. When the Roman column entered the gorge at break of day, the enemy suddenly rose up on both sides simultaneously and attacked the Romans.
