21. So that his soldiers would not lose their edge through inactivity and at the same time to avert suspicion that he had war with Rome in mind, Philip ordered the army to muster at Stobi in Paeonia, and then proceeded to lead it into Maedica.64 An urge had taken him to climb to the peak of Mt. Haemus65 as he had accepted the popular belief that the Pontus, the Adriatic Sea, the River Danube and the Alps could all be seen from there; and he thought this panorama laid out before his eyes would be of no little importance for his plans for war with Rome. He made inquiries about climbing Haemus of men who knew

the area and discovered that it was universally agreed that there was no path for an army but that there was a very difficult way up for a small group of lightly-armed men over trackless terrain. Philip had decided not to take his younger son with him and, wishing to soothe his feelings with a tête-à-tête, he first asked Demetrius whether, given the extreme difficulty of the proposed journey, he should persist with his enterprise or drop the idea. If he went ahead with the expedition, he said, he could not in such circumstances forget the example of Antigonus,66 who had once been overtaken by a violent storm when he had all his family with him in the same ship and was said to have instructed his children to remember for themselves, and also pass it on to their descendants, that nobody should venture to put himself at risk in perilous situations when accompanied by his entire family.67 Having Antigonus’ lesson in mind, said Philip, he would not put his two sons at risk together in the venture that was being proposed; and
