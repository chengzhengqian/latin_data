This move of Philip’s caused a great commotion throughout Macedonia. Few were those who in silence suppressed their resentment as they left their homes with their wives and children, and curses on the king could be heard in the columns of those departing as hatred overwhelmed their fear. Philip, infuriated by these reactions, began to suspect all men in all places and at all times. Eventually, he began declaring openly that he could have no real security unless he arrested the children of the men he had executed, kept them under guard and killed them off one by one at different times.

4. Such brutality, repulsive in itself, the tragedy befalling one household rendered more repulsive.15 Many years before, Philip had executed Herodicus,16 a leading Thessalian, and went on to put to death Herodicus’ sons-in-law as well. The daughters were left widowed, each having one young son. The names of these women were Theoxena and Archo. Though many sought her hand, Theoxena rejected the idea of marriage; Archo married a certain Poris, by far the most prominent of the Aenian people17 but after she had borne him several children she died in his house, leaving all of them still quite young. To have the upbringing of her sister’s children in her own hands, Theoxena married Poris and had the same regard for her own son and the sons of her sister as if she had herself given birth to all of them.
