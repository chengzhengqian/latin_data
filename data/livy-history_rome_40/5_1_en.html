Horrified at the suggestion of such an appalling act, Poris said he would take the children off to some loyal friends in Athens and would himself accompany them in their exile.

They left Thessalonica for Aenea to attend a traditional sacrifice that the citizens of the town celebrate each year with great ceremony in honor of their founder Aeneas.18 At Aenea they spent a day over the ritual feasts and then at about the third watch, when all were asleep, boarded a ship that had been made ready in advance by Poris, ostensibly to return to Thessalonica, though in fact their intention was to cross to Euboea. However, struggling vainly against a headwind, they were still close to land when dawn came upon them and the king’s men responsible for guarding the port sent out an armed clipper to take the ship in tow, with strict orders not to return without it.

As these men were now bearing down on them, Poris focused all his efforts on encouraging his oarsmen and sailors; and occasionally, raising his hands to heaven, he begged the gods to help. Meanwhile, the defiant lady, turning her thoughts back to the heinous act she had contemplated long before, mixed poison and brought forth weapons. Setting the cup before their eyes along with some unsheathed swords, she said: “Death is the only way to win our freedom. These are the paths to death; use whichever method you please to escape the king’s tyrannical behavior.
