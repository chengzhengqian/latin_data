BOOK XL

42. That same year Lucius Duronius, who had been praetor the previous year, had returned from Illyricum to Brundisium with ten ships.140 Leaving his fleet in the harbor, he had proceeded from there to Rome and, in giving an account of his actions in Illyricum, he squarely fixed the blame for all the piracy on the seas on Gentius, king of the Illyrians.141 From Gentius’ kingdom, said Duronius, came all the ships that had ravaged the coastline of the Adriatic;142 he had sent representatives to him on these matters, but they had not been given an opportunity to meet the king.

Ambassadors from Gentius had already come to Rome to say that when the Romans had come for an audience with him, the king happened to have been ill in the farthest reaches of his kingdom. Gentius, they said, asked the senate not to believe the trumped-up charges his enemies had brought against him. In his reply Duronius added that many Roman citizens, allies and holders of Latin rights had been maltreated in Gentius’ kingdom and that it was said that Roman citizens were being forcibly detained on Corcyra.143 The senate’s decision was that all these people should be brought to Rome, that the praetor Gaius Claudius should conduct an investigation into the matter, and that no response be given to King Gentius and his representatives before that.
