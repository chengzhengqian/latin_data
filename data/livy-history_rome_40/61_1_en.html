And so the victorious army was led to Tarraco, having burnished its earlier glory. The praetor Tiberius Sempronius, who had arrived two days earlier, came to meet Fulvius on his arrival and congratulated him on his outstanding service to the state. They were in perfect agreement on which soldiers to discharge and which to retain. Fulvius then embarked his demobilized men on ships and left for Rome, and Sempronius led the legions into Celtiberia.

41. The two consuls led their armies against the Ligurians in different regions. Postumius laid siege to Mt. Ballista and Mt. Letum with the first and third legions and, putting pressure on the enemy by blockading their narrow passes and thus cutting their supply lines, he crushed them by depriving them of all provisions. With the second and fourth legions, Fulvius launched an attack from the direction of Pisae on those of the Ligurian Apuani living on the banks of the River Macra.134 Accepting the surrender of about 7,000 men, he put them on ships and had them transported along the shore of the Tyrrhenian Sea to Neapolis.
