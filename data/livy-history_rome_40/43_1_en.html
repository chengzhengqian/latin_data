29. The colony of Graviscae87 was founded that year in Etruscan territory that had earlier been captured from the people of Tarquinii. Five acres of land was granted to each settler; and the founding triumvirs were Gaius Calpurnius Piso, Publius Claudius Pulcher and Gaius Terentius Histra. The year was exceptional for its drought and its crop failures. It is recorded that for six months no rain fell anywhere.

This same year, while some farmworkers were digging the soil at a greater than usual depth on the land of the clerk Lucius Petillius below the Janiculum,88 two stone coffins were unearthed. These were about eight feet long and four feet wide and had lids fastened down with lead. Each chest bore an inscription in Greek and in Latin, one stating that Numa Pompilius, son of Pompo and king of the Romans, was buried in it, and the other that the books of Numa Pompilius89 were contained in it. On the advice of his friends, the landowner opened the chests. The one with the inscription claiming that the king was buried in it was found empty, with no trace of a human body or anything else; the decomposition of so many years had removed everything.
