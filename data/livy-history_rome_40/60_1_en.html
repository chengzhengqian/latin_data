You will do this with more force in your charge if you drive the horses into them unbridled—it is recorded that Roman cavalry have often won great glory by such a tactic.”131

The men obeyed the command and, removing the bridles, twice ran back and fore through the ranks of the enemy, causing great loss of life and shivering all their spears. The wedge formation on which all their hopes had rested now broken, the Celtiberians panicked and, virtually abandoning the fight, looked around for a way to flee. Witnessing the notable coup brought off by the Roman cavalry, the allied cavalry were also inspired by their courage and they themselves, with no order from anyone, directed their horses against an enemy already in disarray. Then for sure the Celtiberians all scattered in flight and the Roman commander, looking upon the routed enemy, made a promise in a vow of a temple for Fortuna Equestris,132 and games for Jupiter Optimus Maximus.
