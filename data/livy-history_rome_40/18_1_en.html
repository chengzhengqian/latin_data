BOOK XL

“Father, all the means of assistance previously available

to defendants have been seized in advance by my accuser. By shedding crocodile tears in order to destroy another, he has led you to suspect my genuine tears. Ever since my return from Rome he has been plotting against me day and night, holding clandestine meetings with his supporters; and he has actually made me out to be not just a conspirator but a bandit caught in the act and a murderer. He frightens you by his claim to be in danger so that through you he can the more quickly destroy his guiltless brother. He says he has no place of refuge anywhere in the world, his purpose being to ensure that I have no shred of hope left with you. I am cut off, isolated, and helpless, and he puts on me the heavy opprobrium of having influence abroad (which is more a hindrance than a help). And then how like a prosecutor he was33 in putting his accusation relating to last night together with general criticism of my way of life! His aim was to make this event—and the true nature of it you will learn in a moment—look suspicious against the background of the rest of my life, and also to bolster the flimsy charges of my ambition, my wishes and my designs by means of this nighttime scenario34 that he has devised and scripted. He had another motive, too: to make it look as if his accusation was a spur-of-the-moment, unpremeditated reaction, arising (naturally!) from his fright and sudden trepidation last night. But, Perseus, if I was a traitor to my father and the realm and if I had entered into plots with the Romans and other enemies of my father, then you should not have waited for last night’s drama—you should have accused me of treason earlier.
