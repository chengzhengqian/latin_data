It had been agreed that Philip would give the Bastarnae a safe passage through Thrace and provisions for the journey. To enable him to do this he had cultivated the chieftains of those regions with gifts, assuring them that the Bastarnae would pass by peacefully in an orderly column. His plan had been to destroy the Dardanian people,206 and give the Bastarnae settlements in their territory. A twofold advantage would derive from this: the removal of the Dardanians, a race ever hostile to Macedonia and lying in wait for occasions when its kings were in difficulties; and the possibility that the Bastarnae, having left their wives and children in Dardania, could be sent to pillage Italy. Philip knew that a route to the Adriatic and Italy lay through the territory of the Scordisci,207 and that an army could not be taken across any other way. The Scordisci, he thought, would readily grant the Bastarnae a passage—there were no great linguistic or cultural differences between them—and would themselves join their venture when they saw that the aim of the journey was to plunder a nation of great riches. After that the king’s strategy was formulated to accommodate any eventuality. Should the Bastarnae be slaughtered by the Romans, he could still console himself with the fact that the Dardanians had been eliminated, that there would be spoils to take from what was left of the Bastarnae, and that he would have undisputed possession of Dardania. If they were successful with the operation, he would recover his lost possessions in Greece while the Romans’ attention was diverted to a war with the Bastarnae. Such had been Philip’s plans.
