Up to 23,000 of the enemy were killed that day and 4,700 were captured, along with more than 500 horses and 88 military standards. It was a great victory but not bloodless. From the two legions slightly more than 200 Roman soldiers lost their lives, along with 830 allies and holders of Latin rights and about 2,400 foreign auxiliaries. The praetor led his triumphant army back to camp and Acilius was ordered to remain in the camp that he had captured. The following day the spoils were gathered together from the enemy and the men who had shown outstanding valor in the battle were rewarded at an assembly.

The wounded were then transported into the town of Aebura and the legions were marched through Carpetania to Contrebia.96 When this town was besieged, it sent for assistance from the Celtiberians but they were slow to arrive, not from any hesitation on their part but because after leaving home they were held up by roads made impassable by incessant rain and by swollen rivers. Thus, losing hope of receiving aid from its friends, the town surrendered. Flaccus, too, compelled by the foul weather, brought his entire army into the city.

The Celtiberians, who had left home unaware of the surrender, eventually crossed the rivers as soon as the rains let up and reached Contrebia; but when they saw no camp outside the walls they assumed that either it had been taken to the other side of town or the enemy had retreated. They accordingly advanced on the town in a careless and disorganized fashion. The Romans charged out at them from two gates and attacked and drove back a disordered enemy.
