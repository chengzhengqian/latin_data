BOOK XL

It was at about this time that news arrived in a letter from the propraetor Aulus Terentius that Publius Sempronius had died in the farther province after a sickness lasting more than a year.10 The praetors were therefore instructed to hasten their departure for Spain.

After this, embassies from overseas were ushered into the senate, the first coming from the kings Eumenes and Pharnaces,11 and from the Rhodians, who deplored the disaster that had overtaken the people of Sinope.12 There arrived at about the same time envoys from Philip, from the Achaeans and from the Spartans. Responses were made to these after the senate first heard Marcius, who had been on a mission to examine the situation in Greece and Macedonia.13 The answer given to the kings of Asia and to the Rhodians was that the senate would send an embassy to examine the matters they raised.

3. The senate’s unease with regard to Philip had been increased by Marcius. He maintained that the king had complied with the senate’s wishes in a way that made it clear that he would continue doing so no longer than necessary, and it was no secret that he would restart the war—all his actions and statements pointed to it. First of all, he had removed practically the entire citizen population together with their families from the coastal cities to what is now called Emathia (formerly it was named Paeonia);14
