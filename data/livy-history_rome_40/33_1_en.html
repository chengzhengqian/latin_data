Demetrius was not unaware that he was being marginalized so that he would not attend the meeting when Philip, examining the geographical features, would be considering the question of the most direct routes to the Adriatic Sea and Italy and the strategy to be adopted for the war. But he was obliged not only to obey his father but actually to express approval, in order to avoid suspicion that he was obeying reluctantly. To assure Demetrius a safe journey to Macedonia, however, one of the king’s officers, Didas,68 who was governor of Paeonia, was ordered to attend him with a small escort. Perseus had also recruited this man, as he had the majority of his father’s friends, in the plot to destroy his brother—ever since doubt had begun to fade from everyone’s mind about who, given the king’s inclinations, would inherit the throne. For the moment his instructions to Didas were to use all manner of obsequious behavior to worm his way into as close a relationship with Demetrius as he could, so as to be able to elicit from him all his secrets and monitor his innermost thoughts. Thus it was that Demetrius went off with an escort that put him in greater danger than if he were traveling alone.
