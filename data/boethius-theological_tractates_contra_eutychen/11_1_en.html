IV

You must consider that all I have said so far has been for the purpose of marking the difference between nature and person, that is, οὐσία and ὑπόστασις. The exact name by which each should be called must be left to the decision of ecclesiastical usage. For the time being let that distinction between nature and person hold which I have affirmed, viz. that nature is the specific property of any substance, and person the individual substance of a rational nature. Nestorius affirmed that in Christ person was twofold, being led astray by thinking that person can be predicated of every nature. For on this assumption, understanding that there was in Christ a twofold nature, he declared that there was likewise a twofold person. And although the definition which we have already given is enough to prove Nestorius wrong in this, his error shall be clearly declared by the following argument. If the person of Christ is not single, and if it is clear that there are in him two natures, to wit, of man and of God (and no one will be so foolish as to fail to include either in the definition), it follows that there must apparently be two persons; for person, as has been said, is the individual substance of a rational nature.
