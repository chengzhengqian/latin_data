60Also the defendant should employ enumeration more rarely, because his business is to produce counter-arguments, the effectiveness of which as retorts will shine out and their sting have full effect in brevity. But care must be taken in enumeration to avoid the appearance of childishness involved in embarking on a parade of one’s powers of memory. This danger will be escaped by one who does not repeat all his very small points, but while briefly touching on them one by one brings into focus the actual values of the facts.

61XVIII. C. Jun. Well, you have spoken about bothIII. The question: (1) either a general thesis, theoretical or practical; the speaker himself and the speech, so now expound to me the topic that you put forward last of the three, that of the question.

C. Sen. Questions, as I said at the beginning, are of two kinds; one kind is limited by its referring to particular occasions and persons, and this I call a cause; and the other is unlimited, that is, marked by no persons or occasions, and this I designate a thesis.a In a manner of speaking however a discussionb is a division of a cause and a dispute, for what is limited contains an element that is unlimited, and all matters contained in the former

62have a reference nevertheless to the latter. Let us therefore first speak about the thesis. Of this there are two kindsc: one is a matter of learning—its object(a) is knowledge, for instance, whether the reports of the senses are true; the other is a matter of action— which is directed to doing something, for instance if it is asked what are the services by which friendship has to be cultivated.
