You call me back to the philosophers who do not often1 step into the battle-line, and one of whom, Dionysius of Heraclea,2 a person certainly of little resolution, after learning from Zeno to be brave was taught by pain to forget his lesson. For upon an attack of kidney trouble, even amid his shrieks, he kept on crying out that the opinions he had himself previously held about pain were false. And on being asked by Cleanthes,3 his fellow-pupil, what was the reason that had seduced him from his former opinion, he replied: “Because if, after I had given such devoted attention to philosophy, I yet proved unable to bear pain, that would be sufficient proof that pain was an evil. Now I have spent many years in studying philosophy and am unable to bear pain: pain is therefore an evil.”4 Then Cleanthes stamped with his foot upon the ground and, according to the story, recited a line from the Epigoni:5

Do you hear this, Amphiaraus, in your home beneath the earth?
