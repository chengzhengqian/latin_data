For it is at once better to submit to outrage than commit it,1 and to advance a little way to meet the actual approach of death now close at hand, as Catulus did,2 than like Marius by the murder of such a man to eclipse the fame of his six consulships and pollute the last period of his life.

XX. For thirty-eight years, after securing despotic control at the age of twenty-five, Dionysius3 was tyrant of Syracuse. How beautiful the city, how richly provided with resources the State which he kept under the crushing weight of slavery! And yet we are told on the authority of trustworthy writers that while this man was exceedingly temperate in his way of life and showed untiring energy in the conduct of affairs, he was yet unscrupulous by nature and unjust: and this means that in the eyes of all who have a clear insight into the truth he was necessarily supremely wretched. For he failed to secure the very objects of his covetous desires, even at the moment he thought that he could do all he wished.
