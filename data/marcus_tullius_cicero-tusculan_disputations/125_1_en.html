X. The same person therefore is susceptible of pity and envy. For the man who is pained by another’s misfortunes is also pained by another’s prosperity. For instance, Theophrastus in lamenting the death of his friend Callisthenes2 is vexed at the prosperity of Alexander; and so he says that Callisthenes fell in with a man of supreme power and unparalleled good fortune, but one who did not know how to turn prosperity to good account. And yet, as compassion is distress due to a neighbour’s misfortunes, so envy is distress due to a neighbour’s prosperity. Therefore the man who comes to feel compassion comes also to feel envy. The wise man, however, does not come to feel envy; therefore he does not come to feel compassion either. But if the wise man were accustomed to feel distress he would also be accustomed to feel compassion. Therefore distress keeps away from the wise man.

This is how the Stoics state the case, reasoning in a way that is unduly intricate. But the subject needs expansion and stating with considerably greater amplification. None the less we must above all make use of the opinions of thinkers who in the method they use and the opinion they adopt show a highly courageous and so to speak manly spirit.
