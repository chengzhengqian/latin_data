Disputations, IV.

Eagerly she’ll come to meet me, yearning for my welcome arms, She will give herself to me.

How fair the prospect his own words now show:

. . . my fortune Fortune’s self shall now outdo.

XXXII. Close attention is sufficient to give anyone complete insight into the degradation of such delight. And just as those who are transported with delight at the enjoyment of sexual pleasures are degraded, so those who covet them with feverish soul are criminal. In fact the whole passion ordinarily termed love (and heaven help me if I can think of any other term to apply to it)1 is of such exceeding triviality that I see nothing that I think comparable with it. Of love Caecilius2 expresses the opinion:

who him of Gods thinks not supreme A fool is or of life no knowledge hath; For Love has power whom he will to craze, Make wise or senseless, cast disease upon, But whom he will, make loved, desired and sought.

How glorious the reformation of life that poetry inspires! since it thinks love, the promoter of shame and inconstancy, fit for a place in the company of gods. I speak of comedy which would have no existence at all did we not approve of such shame. What does the leader of the Argonauts say in tragedy?3

You for love’s sake more than honour’s have preserved me safe from harm.
