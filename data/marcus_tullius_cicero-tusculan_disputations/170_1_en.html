Where this takes place in an equable and wise way the Stoics employ the term βούλησις for this sort of longing,1 we should employ the term wish. That, they think, is found in the wise man alone and they define it in this way: wish is a rational longing for anything. Where, however, wish is alien from reason and is too violently aroused, it is lust or unbridled desire, which is found in all fools. And also, where we are satisfied that we are in possession of some good, this comes about in two ways: for when the soul has this satisfaction rationally and in a tranquil and equable way, then the term joy2 is employed; when on the other hand the soul is in a transport of meaningless extravagance, then the satisfaction can be termed exuberant or excessive delight and this they define as irrational excitement of the soul. And since we naturally desire good in the same manner as we naturally turn away from evil, and such a turning away, when rational, would be called precaution,3 and is consequently found in the wise man only; but when dissociated from reason and associated with mean and abject pusillanimity, it would be named fear; therefore fear is precaution alien from reason.
