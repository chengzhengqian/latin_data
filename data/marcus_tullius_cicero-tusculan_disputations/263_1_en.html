There is a noble letter of Plato1 to the relatives of Dion which contains a passage written pretty nearly in these words: “On my arrival here I found no pleasure in the celebrated happy life, with all its fulness of Italian and Syracusan feasts; in having two rich meals2 a day and never passing the night alone, and all the other accompaniments of such a life in which no one will ever be rendered wise, far less indeed temperate. In what nature can the elements be so wonderfully mixed? “3 What charm then can there be in the life where there is no prudence, no temperance? This shows us the mistake of Sardanapalus, the very wealthy king of Syria, who had carved upon his tomb the lines:

“All I have eaten and wantoned and pleasures of love I have tasted, These I possess but have left all else of my riches behind me.”4

“What else,” says Aristotle,” could one inscribe on the grave of an ox, not on that of a king?” He says that in death he possesses the things which even in life he possessed only for the moment of enjoyment.
