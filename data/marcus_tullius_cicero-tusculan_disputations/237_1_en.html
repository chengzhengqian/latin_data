The man then who is temperate, steadfast, without fear, without distress, without any eagerness, without lust, is he not happy? But such is the character of the wise man always: therefore the wise man is always happy. Again, how can the good man fail to refer all his acts and feelings to the standard he holds to be praiseworthy? But he refers everything to the standard of living happily;1 therefore happy life is praiseworthy; and without virtue nothing is praiseworthy; happy life therefore is consummated by virtue.

XVII. And this conclusion is proved again in the following way: neither in a wretched life nor in such a life as is neither wretched nor happy is there anything commendable or worthy of glory; and yet in the lives of some men there is something commendable, illustrious and exemplary, as in the life of Epaminondas:

Shorn by the counsels I gave was the Lacedae- monian glory,2

and of Africanus:

West of the dawn where the sun rises over the marsh of Maeotis, No one to match my deeds is there on earth to be found.3
