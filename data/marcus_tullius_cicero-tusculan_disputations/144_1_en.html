That this is so is shown by lapse of time, the passage of which has such an alleviating effect, that, in spite of the continuance of the same evils, not only is the sense of distress rendered less poignant, but it is in a number of instances removed.1 Many Carthaginians were slaves at Rome, many Macedonians after the capture of King Perses.2 I have seen too in the Peloponnese in my youthful days some natives of Corinth who were slaves. All of them could have made the same lament as that in the Andromacha:

“All this did I see ….,”3

but by the time I saw them they had ceased, it may be, to chant dirges. Their features, speech, all the rest of their movements and postures would have led one to say they were freemen of Argos or Sicyon; and at Corinth the sudden sight of the ruins4 had more effect upon me than upon the actual inhabitants, for long contemplation had had the hardening effect of length of time upon their souls. I have read the book which Clitomachus5 sent by way of comfort to his captive fellow-citizens after the destruction of Carthage; it contains in its pages a lecture of Carneades which Clitomachus says he had entered in his notebook: the question that had been proposed for discussion was that the wise man, it seemed, would feel distress at the fall of his country, and the arguments used by Carneades in opposing this proposition are given at length.
