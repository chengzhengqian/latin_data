All I maintain is that, however anxious you might be to be a genuine prosecutor, it is simply impossible for you to be one. For I observe30 that a great many of the charges against Verres concern matters wherein you have been associated with him in such a way that you dare not refer to them as prosecutor.

X. It is a general complaint of the Sicilians that Verres, having requisitioned corn from the farmers for his private maintenance, exacted 12 sesterces a bushel from them in lieu of the corn, when the value of a bushel of wheat was 2 sesterces. A serious charge; a huge sum of money; a shameless theft; an intolerable wrong. I could not fail to secure his condemnation on this ground alone; but what will you do about it, Caecilius? Will you pass over a charge31 of this importance, or bring it up against him? If you bring it up, are you prepared to charge another man with the guilt of doing what you have done yourself at the same time and place? Will you dare to conduct your prosecution of another man in such a way as to leave you no defence against being condemned yourself? If on the other hand you pass this charge over, what can be your value as a prosecutor? Serious and well-grounded as the charge is, the personal risk to yourself will deter you not merely from any suggestion of its truth, but even from any allusion to its existence.
