5. “What then, speaker,” someone might object, “am I to do, after this opinion of yours, heavenly indeed and yet almost inhumane, if humans are driven far off from the immortal gods and so far banished to this underworld of earth 2that they are denied all communion with the gods above, and not one of the heavenly host comes to visit them as a shepherd, groom or cowherd visits their bleating, neighing or lowing charges so as to calm the violent, heal the sick, help the needy? 3No god, you say, intervenes in human affairs. To whom then shall I address my prayers? To whom shall I offer a vow? For whom shall I slaughter a victim? 4Whom shall I call on to help the downcast, support the good, confront the wicked at every stage of life? And finally, whom shall I summon to witness an oath, the most frequent need of all? 5Or shall I do as Virgil’s Ascanius did, and
