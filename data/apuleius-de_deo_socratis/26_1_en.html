24. Well then, you too should arm yourself to study philosophy, or at least do so soon, so that the praise you hear includes no externals, and someone wishing to extol your fame lauds you as highly as Accius lauded Ulysses in the opening of his tragedy Philoctetes:49

O man of fame, son of a small land, Mighty in famous name and splendid heart, Moving spirit of the Achaean fleet, Fearful scourge of the Dardanian clans, Son of Laertes.

2He mentions his father last: and yet you have already heard all that hero’s praises. None of them is claimed by Laertes, Anticleia or Acrisius: as you can see, the praise is the property of Ulysses alone. 3And that is just what Homer teaches us in the figure of this same Ulysses when he chose to make Wisdom his constant companion, by poetic license calling her Minerva. Hence with her as his companion he faced every terror and overcame every difficulty; 4for with her as his helper he entered the Cyclops’ cave and yet emerged; he saw the cattle of the Sun and did not eat; he descended to the underworld and yet emerged; again with Wisdom by his side, he sailed past Scylla and was not devoured; he was encircled by Charybdis and not caught; he drank Circe’s cup and was not transformed; he landed among the Lotus-Eaters and did not stay; he heard the Sirens and did not land.
