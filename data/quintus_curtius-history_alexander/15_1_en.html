The road barely allowed four armed men to walk abreast; a ridge of the mountain overhung a passage that was not merely narrow, but often broken by frequent streams which crossed it, trickling from 13the roots of the mountains. Nevertheless he had ordered his light-armed Thracians to go in advance and examine the mountain paths,a in order that a hidden foe might not burst forth upon them as they went up the pass. A band of bowmen also had taken their place on the ridge; they kept their bows bent, since they had been warned that they were not enter­ing 14upon a march, but a battle. In this manner the army came to the city of Tarsus, to which at that very time the Persians were setting fire, in order that 15the enemy might not invade a rich city. But Alexander had sent Parmenionb on with a light-armed troop to check the fire, and as soon as he knew that the barbarians had been put to flight by the arrival of his men, he entered the city which he had saved.
