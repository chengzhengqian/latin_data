Meanwhile Menidas, Alexander’s commander of cavalry, had come with a few squadrons to defend the baggage—whether on his own initiative or by the king’s order is uncertain—,but he could not sustain the attack of the Cadusians and Scythians; for with hardly any attempt at battle he fled back to the king, a witness to the loss of the baggage and not 13its rescuer. Already Alexander’s resentment had changed his plan of action, and he feared with some reason that anxiety to recover their property might turn his soldiers from fighting; therefore he sent Aretes, leader of the lancersb —they call them sari- 14sophori—against the Scythians. Meanwhile the chariots, which in the neighbourhood of the leading standards had thrown the army into confusion,c had charged upon the phalanx; the Macedonians with steady courage received them into the midst of their 15column. Their line was like a rampart; they had made a continuous row of spearsd together, and on both sides stabbed the flanks of the horses, as these rushed recklessly upon them. Then they began to encircle the chariots also and to hurl those who fought 16in them to the ground.
