On the following day,a before it was yet broad daylight, he was the first to see the cloths that showed that the top was taken. But the changing sky, where now a gleam of light shown through, which again was hidden,b compelled him to doubt whether his eyes did not deceive him. But as a clearer light appeared in the 22 heavens, his doubt was dispelled; and having summoned Cophes, through whom he had tested the feelings of the barbarians, he sent him to them, to warn them now at least to adopt a better purpose; but if they persisted through confidence in their situation, he ordered that those who had taken possession of the summit should be pointed out to them. 23 Cophes, being admitted, began to urge Ariamazes to surrender the rock, saying that he would gain the kingâ€™s favour if, while he was engaged in such great enterprises, he should not delay him in the siege of a single rock. He, speaking more proudly and arrogantly 24 than before, ordered Cophes to depart; but he took the barbarian by the hand and asked him to go with him outside the cave.
