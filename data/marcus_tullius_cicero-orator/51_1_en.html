Cicero

There now remains the actual type and χαρακτήρ 134 or “character” as it is called; its ideal form can be recognized from what has been said above.a There we touched on the ornaments of style both in the use of single words and in their combinations. These will be so plentiful that no word will fall from the orator’s lips that is not well chosen or impressive; there will be metaphors of all sorts in great abundance, because these figures by virtue of the comparison involved transport the mind and bring it back, and move it hither and thither; and this rapid stimulation of thought in itself produces pleasure. The other ornaments derived from combinations of words lend great brilliance to an oration. They are like those objects which in the embellishment of a stage or of a forum are called “ornaments,” not because they are the only ornament, but because they stand out from the others.
