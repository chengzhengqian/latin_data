XVI. But here is a wondrous theme for panegyric,43 not lauded in the poet’s verse or the historian’s record, but weighed in the scales of true discernment: he found a Roman knight, his friend of old, and his affectionate and devoted admirer, slipping downward not through licentiousness, not through discreditable waste and extravagance for the gratification of his passions, but through an endeavour to increase his patrimony; he held out to him a helping hand; he suffered him not to fall; he propped and sustained him by his purse, his fortune, and his protection; and to this day he continues so to sustain him; he does not allow his tottering friend to fall; the splendour of his name does not dazzle the penetrating vision of that great soul, nor the soaring height of his position and his renown obscure the prospect from the windows of his mind.
