Catachresis

These limitations of Latin make Catachresis all the more necessary. We rightly translate this as abusio (“misuse”); it lends the nearest name to things that have no name:

They build a horse50

and, in tragedy, “His father gives Aegialeos parental rites.”51 There are thousands of catachreses: acetabula (vinegar-bottles) may contain anything, “boxes”52 may be made of any wood, a “parricide” may be the murderer of his mother or his brother. The whole genus of Metaphor must be distinguished from this, because Catachresis is found where there was previously no word, Metaphor where there was a different word. The poets are in the habit of using closely related words catachrestically even for things which do have names of their own; this is rare in prose. Some say also that it is Catachresis when we say “courage” for rashness or “generosity” for extravagance.53 I
