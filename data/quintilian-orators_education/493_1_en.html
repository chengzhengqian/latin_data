Similitude is the most important element, but the comparison must be with something humbler or more trivial, as in the jokes made by our ancestors in calling Lentulus “Spinther”54 and Scipio “Serapio.”55 Similitude may be sought in animals as well as in men—when I was a boy, Junius Bassus, who had a biting wit, was called “the white donkey”; and Sarmentus <compared Messius Cicirrus to a wild horse—and also in inanimate objects>56 as Publius Blessius called Julius,57 who was dark, thin, and round-shouldered, “the iron buckle.” This is now a very common way of looking for a laugh. The Similitude is sometimes introduced openly, sometimes in the form of an analogy,58 as in the remark of Augustus to the soldier who presented a petition timidly: “Don’t do it as if you were giving an elephant a penny.”59
