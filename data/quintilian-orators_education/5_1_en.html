For as soon as the tongue began to offer a way of making a living, and the practice developed of making a bad use of the good gifts of eloquence, those who were counted able speakers abandoned moral concerns, and these, left to themselves, became, as it were, the prey of weaker minds. At this point, some, disdaining the effort of speaking well, returned to the business of forming character and establishing rules of life, and kept for themselves what would be, if the division were possible, the more important part; they laid claim, however, to a very presumptuous name, wishing to be regarded as the only “students of wisdom”5—a distinction which neither the greatest generals nor the most famous statesmen and administrators have ever dared claim for themselves, because they have always preferred to do right rather than to profess it. I am very ready to admit that many of the old philosophers taught honourable principles and lived in accordance with their teaching; but in our day, very great vices have been concealed under this name in many persons.
