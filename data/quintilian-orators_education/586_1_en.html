In these cases, the Syllogism relies also on the Letter of the law; for the prosecutor says that the provisions are adequate—“I demand that the unchaste priestess shall be thrown off the Rock; this is the law”; “the victim claims her choice”; “wool is on a sheep,” and so on; but, as the other side can reply “It is not stated that the condemned woman should be thrown over twice” or “that a victim of rape should exercise her choice at any time” or “that a tyrannicide should receive two rewards,” or again that “nothing is said about a ploughshare” or “about sheep,” it follows that an inference is being made from a certain fact to an uncertain one. (6) It is a more difficult business to infer from the Letter what is not stated: that is, “since A, therefore B.” “A man who has killed his father shall be sewn up in a sack.7 He has killed his mother.” “It is illegal to drag a man to court from his house.
