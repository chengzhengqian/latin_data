So who (nam quis), most confident of youths . . . ?27

as a way of beginning a sentence; or

So much more (tam magis) roaring, fierce with baleful flames, as much more (quam magis) bleeds the battle’s flood of gore.28

which is an inversion of

As much more (quam magis) as calamity bears down, so much more (tam magis) does its strength to do some mischief.29

The ancients are full of such things. At the beginning of his Eunuchus30 Terence writes: “So (igitur) what shall I do?” while elsewhere31 he has ain tandem, leno? (“You say so, do you (tandem), pimp?”). Catullus in his Epithalamium32 writes

As long as (dum) she remains unmarried, just so long (dum) is she dear to her own,

where the first dum means “as long as” and the second “just so long.”
