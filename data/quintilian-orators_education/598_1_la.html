The capacity “to make small things great and great things small” was indeed a defining characteristic of rhetoric from early times. We may follow ancient usage in calling this Amplification; but it means, of course, exaggeration, not just spending more words on the subject, or expressing it more fully. Background texts include Aristotle, Rhetoric 1. 1374b–1375a; “Longinus” 11–12.2; Anonymus Seguierianus 160–165; Ad Herennium 2.47–49. See Lausberg §§ 400–409. We must note that Quintilian here treats this whole topic under Elocution; but he points out that he has already had occasion to touch on it in the context of Invention (8.3.99). He accordingly begins with choice of words (8.4.1–3), but proceeds to climax-type devices (4.4–9), comparisons, inferences, and “accumulations”—all surely matters of thought, and closely related to the Figures which he will discuss in Book Nine. His aim in 8.4 seems to be to pull together various elements in a topic which he sees as central to the orator’s enterprise as a whole. He is certainly less precise in defining Amplification than, for example, “Longinus” 12.1.
