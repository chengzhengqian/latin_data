A Definition, then, is an accurate, lucid, and brief verbal expression of a fact. It normally comprises, as has been said,5 Genus, Species, Differentia, and Properties. For example, if you were to define a horse (I shall use a particularly familiar example), the Genus is “animal,” the Species “mortal,” the Differentia “nonrational” (man too is “mortal”!), the Property “neighing.”6

There are several reasons for introducing this into a speech. Sometimes there is no doubt about the name, but the question is what should fall under it. In other cases, the thing is plain enough but <there is no agreement about what name it should be called by. If> the name is agreed, and the doubt concerns the thing, sometimes (1) we have a Conjecture; for example, if the Question is “what is God?” Here, a man who denies that God is “a spirit diffused through all the parts of the world”7 would not be saying that it is mistaken to call the world divine, as Epicurus would, for he gave God human form and a place in the gaps
