(2) The prosecutor often also makes use of pity, by complaining of the misfortune of the victim whom he seeks to avenge, or of the desolation of his children and parents. He may also move the judges by a picture of the future, of everything which awaits those who have complained of violence and wrong if they do not secure justice: exile from their city, loss of property, the necessity of enduring whatever their enemy does to them. More often, however, it is the prosecutor’s business to make the judge reject any appeal to pity that the defendant may be going to make, and incite him to give a strong verdict. This move includes also anticipating what you think the opponent will say or do. It both strengthens the judges’ scruples about observing their oath and at the same time robs the coming reply of its appeal, because points which have been anticipated by the prosecution and later come to be repeated by the defence are now no longer new.
