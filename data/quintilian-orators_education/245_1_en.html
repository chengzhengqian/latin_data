Issues in Deliberative Speeches

Nor is it enough to confine Deliberative to the Issue of Quality, which includes questions of honour and expediency. For there is often scope here also for Conjecture, a Definition is sometimes relevant, and Legal Issues also occasionally arise, especially in private consultations, if there is some doubt as to whether something is legal. I shall discuss Conjecture at greater length shortly.4 Staying for the moment with Definition, we find it in Demosthenes—“Is Philip giving up Halonnesus or giving it back?”5—and in Cicero’s Philippics—“What is a ‘disturbance’?”6 Again, in the Question regarding the statue of Servius Sulpicius,7 namely whether a statue should be erected only if the ambassador has been struck down by the sword, is surely like a judicial Question. Thus Deliberative—also called the suasoria—debates the future, but also asks questions about the past. It has two functions: to persuade and to dissuade.
