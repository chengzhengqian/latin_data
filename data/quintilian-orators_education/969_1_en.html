Book 11.3

Here, the actor will introduce pauses for hesitation, inflections of voice, various hand-gestures, and different movements of the head. Oratory has a different flavour: it does not wish to be too highly spiced, because it is a real activity, not an imitation. There is therefore every reason to object to a Delivery that pulls faces, irritates by its gesticulations, or jumps from one tone of voice to another. It was a useful borrowing that our old writers took from the Greek, and Popilius Laenas from them, to call this sort of pleading mocosa.145

Cicero, as in all things, gives the best advice on this: I quoted above from the Orator, and there are similar remarks about Marcus Antonius in the Brutus.146 Nowadays, however, a somewhat more agitated style of Delivery is regarded as acceptable, and is indeed appropriate in some contexts; but it needs to be under control, lest, in our eagerness to pursue the elegance of the performer, we lose the authority of the good and grave man.147
