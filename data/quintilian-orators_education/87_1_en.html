Aristophanes also, in more than one work, shows that boys were brought up in music in the old times;21 and the old man in Menander’s Hypobolimaeus,22 who, in giving an account to the boy’s real father (who is claiming him back) of the expenses he has incurred on his education, says that he has paid large sums to “teachers of the lyre, and teachers of geometry.” Hence also the practice of taking a lyre round the company after dinner; when Themistocles admitted he could not play, he was (to use Cicero’s words) “regarded as rather un-educated.”23 It was the practice also to have lyres and pipes at banquets among the ancient Romans.
