Oldest of all—and properly called sententiae, though the same name serves for all types—are what are called in Greek gnōmai.2 (Both names come from the fact that these sayings are like proposals or decrees.)3 It is a universal pronouncement (such as might be praiseworthy even outside the context of a Cause), relating sometimes simply to things (“Nothing is so popular as goodness of heart”),4 sometimes to persons (“An emperor,” said Domitius Afer, “who wants to know everything has to forgive much”).5 Some have thought of a sententia as part of an Enthymeme, some as the major premiss or conclusion of an Epichireme.6 And so it is sometimes, but not always. A better classification is into (1) simple sententiae (like those just quoted); (2) sententiae with an added reason (“In any contest, the better resourced, even if he is the victim, is thought to be the aggressor, because of his greater power”);7 (3) double sententiae (“Obsequiousness wins friends, truth enmity”).8 There are even people who have made ten classes, but on principles on which even more could be produced; they give us sententiae based on Inter­rogation,
