Book 10.1

History, Oratory, Philosophy

History has been written by many with distinction, but no one questions that there are two far superior to the rest, whose very different excellences have won them almost equal praise. Thucydides is close-textured, concise, always pressing himself hard: Herodotus is pleasing, transparent, expansive. Thucydides is better at the tenser emotions, Herodotus at the more relaxed; Thucydides at set speeches, Herodotus at dialogue; Thucydides excels in force, Herodotus in giving pleasure.83 Next to these comes Theopompus, inferior as a historian to these two, but more like an orator, having indeed been one for a long time before he was diverted into history.84 Among the crowd of later historians, good though they were, Philistus deserves to be singled out; he imitated Thucydides, but was much feebler than his model, though somewhat more lucid. Ephorus, or so Isocrates thought, needed the spur.85 Clitarchus’ talents are respected, his veracity is impugned.86 Timagenes, born long after these, deserves respect for having revived the lapsed tradition of historical writing with fresh lustre.87 I have not forgotten Xenophon; he has his proper place among the philosophers.
