On other faults of Prooemia, the teachers have given truer advice. A Prooemium applicable to a number of cases is called a stock one;48 it is less attractive, but can sometimes be used to some advantage. Even the great orators have often not troubled to avoid it. One that your opponent can also use is called “common”; one that he can turn to his own advantage is “interchangeable”; one that has no connection with the Cause is “detached”; one that comes from another context, “transferred.” They also speak of “long” and “irregular” Prooemia. But most of these features are not only faults of the beginning but faults of a speech as a whole.

Prooemia not always necessary

So much for the Prooemium, when there is need for one. This however is not always so;49 sometimes it is actually superfluous, if the judge is sufficiently well prepared without it or if the subject does not require any preparation. Aristotle50 indeed says that a Prooemium is totally unnecessary with good judges. Sometimes, even if we wanted to have one, it is not allowed—when the judge is busy, the time short, or a greater authority orders us to go straight to the heart of the matter.
