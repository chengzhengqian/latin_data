Is there to be no difference then between the forensic genre and the declamatory? If the point of practice speaking is to make progress, none. I wish indeed that we could add to present practice the use of actual names and sometimes the invention of more complex cases which require longer pleadings; and also that we were less frightened of words that are in daily use, and thought it quite normal to insert some jokes. However well we have been trained in school in other respects, these things find us novices in court.

But if Declamation is really for show, then we ought indeed to bend over somewhat to give the audience pleasure. For even in speeches which, though undoubtedly concerned with real events, are designed to entertain the people (such as the Panegyrics which we read, and the epideictic genre as a whole), it is permissible to introduce more ornament, and not only confess but actually display, before an audience assembled for this purpose, the art which in forensic oratory must generally be concealed. Thus Declamation, inasmuch as it is the image of forensic and deliberative eloquence, must bear a resemblance to real life; but inasmuch as it has an epideictic element, it must assume a degree of elegance.5
