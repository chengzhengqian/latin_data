When the judgement concerns a penalty, the pleader must either defend, extenuate, or excuse the alleged act, or, as some think, beg for mercy.

Absolute Quality

Much the strongest defence is to say that the act alleged is itself honourable. A son is disinherited because he has served as a soldier, sought office, or married, against his father’s wishes. We defend what we did. The school of Hermagoras6 call this defence kat’ antilēpsin; <others call it kata dianoian,> referring the word to understanding the action;7 I find no literal Latin translation of this: the defence is called “Absolute.”8 The only question concerns the act: is it just or not? All justice rests either (1) on nature or (2) on convention. (1) “Nature” includes whatever is done because of the intrinsic value of the particular action. Under this head come piety, good faith, self-control, and the like. Some add “parity,” but this is not a point to consider without careful thought, because the use of force against force and the “eye for an eye” principle9 involve no injustice towards the aggressor, while parity of actions does not imply that the first action was just.
