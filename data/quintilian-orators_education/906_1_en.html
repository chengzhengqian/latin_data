Again, objectionable aspects of the subject are often toned down by using moderate words: you can say a brutal character is “too severe,” an unjust man is “misled by prejudice,” an obstinate man “sticks too much to his own standards.” Very often, however, you should try to be more reasonable than the other side: this is certainly the gentlest way.

In addition to all this, anything which goes too far is a breach of decorum: and this is why even what is naturally suitable enough to the situation loses its appeal if it is not also measured and restrained. Observance of this principle is more a matter of instinctive judgement than of teachable rules; how much is enough and how much the hearer will accept cannot be precisely calculated and weighed, as it were; as with food, people vary in the amount they can tolerate of various things.

I feel I should add a brief explanation of how it comes about that very different rhetorical virtues not only have their devotees but are often praised by the same persons. Cicero writes in one place76 that the best writing is what you think you can easily imitate but find that you cannot, and in another77 that his aim was not to speak in a way that everyone felt confident of achieving, but in one that no one did.
