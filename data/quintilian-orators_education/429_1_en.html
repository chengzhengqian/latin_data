Careless speakers are liable to yet more mistakes: amplifying a charge which is still to be proved; disputing whether something happened, when the question is who did it; attempting the impossible; dropping an argument as though it was complete, when it is hardly begun; speaking of the individual rather than the Cause; attributing the failings of persons to institutions (for example, accusing the Decemvirate and not Appius);30 controverting the obvious; saying things which can be taken in two ways; not considering the main question; not answering the charges. (This last, and this alone, may occasionally be acceptable, when a bad Cause has to be defended by palliatives introduced from elsewhere, as when Verres, accused of peculation, is said to have shown courage and energy in defending Sicily against the pirates.)31

Answering Objections

The same rules apply to Objections which are made against us, but there is a further point, inasmuch as in this connection many speakers fall into two quite distinct errors. Some, even in the courts, pass over these Objections as awkward and vexatious, content themselves for the most part with the Arguments they brought with them from home ready drafted, and speak without regard to their adversary. This is of course much commoner in the schools, where not only are Objections left out, but the themes themselves are commonly contrived in such a way that there is nothing to be said on the other side.
