Theory of Narrative

I turn next to the theory of Narrative. A Narrative is an exposition, designed to be persuasive, of an action done or deemed to be done;27 alternatively (as Apollodorus defines it)28 it is a speech instructing the hearer on what is in dispute. Most writers, especially the followers of Isocrates, require that it should be lucid, brief, and plausible.29 It does not matter if we say “perspicuous” instead of “lucid,” or “probable” or “credible” instead of “plausible.” I agree with this division, although Aristotle30 dissents from Isocrates in one respect, because he ridicules the rule of brevity, as though a Narrative necessarily had to be either long or short and one could never find a middle way; while the followers of Theodorus leave only the last criterion standing, on the ground that it is not necessarily expedient to be
