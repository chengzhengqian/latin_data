Introduction

This book has been carefully edited and commented on by R. G. Austin (1948). Quintilian claims it as the most difficult and original part of his work; its purpose is to exhibit the ideal of the consummate orator. It remains however somewhat puzzling and scrappy: on the structure, see C. J. Classen, Museum Helveticum 22 (1965) 181–190.

12.1 deals with the moral issues raised by the concept of “the good man skilled in speaking.” Only a virtuous man can be an orator in the real sense of the word (1–13). It is true that Demosthenes and Cicero lacked perfect virtue, but their actions and their deaths show the virtue in them (14–22). A bad man, however skilled a speaker, cannot be a real orator; our ideal demands a man of genuine authority and virtue (23–32). Yet, of course, the true orator will in some circumstances deceive or tell lies, but without losing his integrity (33–45).

Virtue is a matter both of nature and of teaching; so in 12.2 Quintilian relevantly takes up the need for the orator to study philosophy, at least in some depth (2.1–9). This applies to all three branches of philosophy: logic (10–14), ethics (15–20), physics (20–21). The great orators—Pericles, Demosthenes, Cicero—are examples to follow (22–23).
