Book 8.2

Words which signify more than they say may be thought to come under Lucidity, because they help one to understand. But I prefer to treat Emphasis23 under the ornaments of speech, because its effect is not just to make something understood, but to make an extra something understood as well.

Obscurity

Obscurity results from words no longer in common use: suppose, for example, a man were to hunt through the records of the pontifices, ancient treaties, and obsolete authors, deliberately looking for unintelligibility in the extracts he makes from them. Some seek a reputation for erudition from this; they want to be thought to be the only people who know certain things.

Words more familiar in certain districts or peculiar to certain professions are also misleading.
