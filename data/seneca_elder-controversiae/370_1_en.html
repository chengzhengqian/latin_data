“Now declaimers are dogged right into the courts2 by this fault of leaving out what is necessary and making for the attractive. Further, they make out their opponents to be as silly as they like; they give them replies as they will and when they will. Further, error never gets punished by any harm done—their folly costs them nothing. So a dullness that grew well in safe surroundings isn’t easy to throw off, though in court it spells danger. Declaimers, too, are kept going by frequent applause,1 and their memories are used to taking a rest at fixed intervals. When they get into court, and cheers cease to attend their every gesture, they fail or collapse.

“Moreover, in the schools there are no interruptions3 to put their memory out; no-one laughs, no-one purposely contradicts, every face is well-known.2 In the courts, apart from anything else, they are disturbed by the court itself.
