“Students are so coddled and pampered in the4 exercises of the declamation school that they cannot tolerate noise, silence, laughter, even the open air. But no exercise is any use unless it very closely resembles the activity for which it is a preparation. This is why it is often more demanding than the real thing: gladiators train on heavier weapons than those used in combat,1 their trainer keeps them longer in arms than will their opponents; wrestlers tire out two or three at a time in order to be able to stand up easily to one; runners, though their speed is to be tested over a tiny stretch of track, for training purposes cover many times a course they will have to run once only in the real contest. The labour of learning is purposely increased in order to lighten the labour of the decisive test. But the opposite is true5 of declamation in the schools; everything is softer and more casual. In the court they take the role they are given, in the school they choose it. There they have to coax the judge, here they give him orders. There they have to concentrate, and struggle to make their voices reach the judge’s ears amid the competing hubbub of the throng; here every face hangs on the face of the speaker.
