Asprenas said: “‘There is not much left of the thirty days.’ If I were to count the days since you were first successful in winning over, then they say the thirty days are up already.”1

Latro said something I have placed among the epigrams:2 “‘I shall die.’ Then tell the truth.”

Cestius’ declamation was based on the figure of begging his father as though the girl’s father had not consented. Then he made the transition on this epigram: “Surely I don’t have a worse case if I have been victorious in the opinion of the other judge?”

Romanius Hispo’s declamation also used this same figure, but his transition was smoother: “I know what the reply to me might be: It is easy to satisfy a judge from one’s own family; I shall see about you when you get back from the girl’s father.”3

In this controversia Triarius had said: “You will19 not know whether you are to win me over before the last day comes. Even then, I shall hang on as long as I can.” Then—amid thunderous applause from the schoolmen: “But do you, executioner, whoever you may be, when you raise the bare axe, before you strike look round at the father.”
