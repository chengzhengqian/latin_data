Controversiae 7

Bruttedius Brutus used an everyday word with emphasis: “He killed his rival,1 and wounded his mistress.”

Romanius Hispo said something of the same kind:2 “Show us, step-mother, show us where your lover pinched you.”

Sepullius Bassus said: “He killed the husband, grazed the mistress.”

Of those who said foolish things, “first before all”310 was your friend Musa, who, after describing the stepmother’s wound, added: “But, by heaven, my father was pierced just like the wall.”

Murredius: “He thinks it a support for his case that he let blood.”4

Licinius Nepos said: “That is no wound—it is the bite of a playful lover.”

Saenianus produced an epigram with that hallmark of stupidity: “He didn’t wound the step-mother—he splashed her with the blood of her husband”—though in fact in the theme she is said to have been wounded.

Vinicius, a man of extreme precision of mind, who11 could neither speak nor tolerate foolish things, used to make fun of another epigram of Saenianus’, and to compare it with one spoken in a speech of Votienus Montanus.
