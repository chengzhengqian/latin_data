If you regard it as a boon, why do you use it as a threat?—Is poison being made a charge without its having been administered?—Will you put any limits to the power of the tribunes, whom the Roman people, in order to ensure its own supreme power, wanted to be more powerful than itself?4—To possess poison5 is as great a crime as to kill one’s master.

The other side: Wishing to die, he chose for this service a good-for-nothing slave, a bold man who hated him.—He wasn’t interested in saving his master’s life (he saw he was wasting away under an incurable disease) but in prolonging his agonies.—A slave is not the judge of his master’s orders but their agent.—What is at stake is the law relating to wills, which have lost all their validity if slaves neglect the orders of the living, tribunes those of the dead.—Jailbird, do you refuse to die at the will of your master?—though your master has to die when you want him to.6

  • 2Livy 2.4.5 seq. For Brutus’ punishment of his children, see C. 10.3.8.

  • 4The tribunate grew up as a plebeian counterweight to the power of the aristocratic magistrates.
  • 5The Lex Cornelia punished anyone who “fecerit vendi- derit emerit habuerit dederit” poison (Cic. Cluent. 148). The point here is that the master was himself guilty of possessing poison, and the slave was justified in not aiding him.
