He was leaving me to his brother; was he to leave me to the tyrant?—“Even now your wounds show on your face.” Son, it goes against you that you killed the tyrant so swiftly.5

Mento. Do you ask who caused these wounds? The son at whose funeral I struck myself.—May I live and die a free man, may the hands of my son close my eyes—I, standing there between my sons, was more brave than either.

The other side

Arellius Fuscus Senior. He struck his father for6 as long as the tyrant demanded of his hireling.—How is this? Can’t you slay a tyrant unless you have exercised your hands on your father?—“My father is defending me.” All the better for me: you beat your counsel as well as your father.

Julius Bassus. Just because things are so topsyturvy that a father is speaking in favour of a parricide, are we to support his defence?—He defends him even
