The other side

. . . three times bravely. . . At least the disinherited4 are allowed to fight.

Albucius Silus. What is this sudden clamour? Isn’t it the general calling? I am coming.—I have laboured much in the front rank.3—I am ashamed: men who have been defeated three times go on soldiering.

Publius Asprenas. “How much longer,” he says, “will you endanger your life?” All is well, judges—my father thinks I deserve to survive.—A senator after his sixty-fifth year is not forced to come to the House—but he is not barred.4 Someone of praetorian rank may wear the toga praetexta5 on holidays or feast-days: does he have to? Whatever is given as reward or honour allows a choice: otherwise, if compulsion attaches to it, it stops being a reward.—It is you I obey, father, you who used to say we become immortal by the glory we win, you who used to kiss my wounds when I came back from battle.
