Latro brought up a straightforward colour for the16 youth. He had something to boast of, not to apologise for. “I could not tolerate that cruel sight. Do you think that I just forgot your wrong to me? I forgot everything; my consciousness dimmed, my feet were unable to support my body as they should, my eyes dulled in sudden darkness. Otherwise, if I had been in my right mind at that moment, should I have waited to be asked?”

Fuscus introduced, as he often did, the colour of religion: “Nature moved me, piety moved me, and the mutability of human fortune, so clearly exemplified. Fortune seemed to stand before my eyes and say: ‘Those who do not support their own go hungry.’”

Albucius used this colour: “My father came to me,17 and spoke to me in words that were not humble. He did not beg: he knew how one ought to behave towards a son—he gave me orders to feed him. He recited the law to me—a law that I have always supposed covers an uncle too.” Then he said: “I gave less than I ought to have given to a father—but as much as I could slip past him who forbade me.”
