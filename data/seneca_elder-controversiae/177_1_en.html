Latro said the pretence should be openly acknowledged. “I am beginning,” he said, “the defence of an aged man who is both honourable and sensible, if I can show to be a plan what might have been thought a vice.2 Why should he hint at his own honesty rather than declare it?”

Blandus used the following figure to declaim the part of the son: he made his reply as if on behalf of a son who had been disinherited.

Cestius said the role of the son should be given a7 more elaborate colour: he was doing something not altogether honest in refusing to allow to his father what his father had allowed to him. And so Cestius slanted his narration in such a way as to blame even the son’s own debauchery on to the father, saying there had been no rigorous discipline, no rules imposed by a well-conducted home to form a youth’s character and lead him away from the vices normal to his age. “In a way I was sent ahead into debauchery by my father. People were astonished when they saw his white hair soaked in perfumes, an old man constantly at orgies; in fact, he was thought unanimously to be not excessively extravagant but rather mad.
