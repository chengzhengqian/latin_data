Nor would he stand either for Triarius’ epigram—adapted by Haterius1—at the start of the epilogue: “At this point an accused ought to have been crying. Does the girl cry? I shall discover a way to make her. Someone, bring here the picture of her brother.” For if she is so much of a child that she can say: “Mother, what is poison?”, she cannot be so affectionate that her brother’s picture could move her to tears.

All spheres of study, and especially eloquence, whose rules are not fixed, are subject to the great trap that some people both realise their own faults and love them. Cestius realised he had been childish12 to say: “Mother, what is poison?” For he laughed at Murredius, who imitated this epigram in his epilogue. Starting to address the girl, he had said: “Make yourself look like one in danger of condemnation. Pour forth tears, put your hands on the judges’ knees. You are a defendant.” Then he had made the girl reply: “Father, what is a defendant?” Cestius said: “If he said this to mock me, he was a witty man—and I now realise that mine is a foolish epigram. However, there is much that I say not because I like it but because the audience will like it.”
