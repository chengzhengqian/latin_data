If you are angry, write rather: “If you kill him”; I killed a tyrant—what I ask is a tyrant’s end.—I am not afraid that judges will chain hands that pirates loosed.—Of all the evils2 which fortune has heaped up for me on land and sea, in public and in private, I have found nothing harsher than my father. The tyrant was afraid of my hands, but he did not cut them off; the injury done to my marriage did not remove any part of my body; the pirates—as though their life was due to my deed—took pity on me, and made no charge; I only had one inexorable enemy.—How happy for the commonwealth that, having rid myself of my enemy,2 I did not sail off before the tyrant came to power!—“I begot you,” he says, “and brought you up.” That was what you did for a tyrant and an adulterer.—If you cannot ransom these hands by yourself, appeal to the commonwealth.—The adulterer was buried with his hands, the tyrant flung to the dogs with his.—In great crimes the rights granted by nature perish; you are no more my father than they were my brothers.—Hear a strange utterance from a captive:
