The Arrangement with a Disinherited Brother

A brother gave a written undertaking to a son who had been disinherited that he would give him half the bequest if he did not protest at disinheritance. He kept quiet. The other son is disinherited by his father.

For the father: He is so far in debt that he cannot discharge it while his father is alive.—Do you want to know what your credit is like? Not even your brother believed you without a written undertaking.—I am delaying1 the hopes of one son, and the test of the other’s loyalty.—I still live—and already my estate has been divided.—Unless you2 come to my aid, even the one who kept quiet will defeat me.3—I am not trying to conceal4 the fact that today I am disinheriting two sons.—Bring out the undertaking, a parricides’ treaty, an agreement based on wicked hopes, an undertaking that is nefarious on the part of the giver, shameful to the recipient and dangerous to the father.

  • 1i.e. by staying alive.
  • 2The judges.
  • 3By receiving half the bequest (cf. “defeat my father” below).
  • 4Contrast the son’s secretiveness.
