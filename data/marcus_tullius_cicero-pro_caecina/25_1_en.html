XVII. Come now, Aebutius, you shall yourself48 pronounce judgement on the question of force. Answer me, if you please. Was Caecina in fact unwilling to enter on the estate or was he unable? In saying that you withstood my client and drove him back you admit that he had the will to enter on it. Can you, then, say that it was not force which hindered him, when he was debarred from entering by a gathering of men although he desired to enter and had come there with that intention? For if he was absolutely unable to do what he was extremely anxious to do, then some force must inevitably have prevented him; otherwise, pray tell me why, when he desired to enter, he did not do so.

Nay, but you cannot deny that force was used:49 the question is how, since he failed to enter, he was “driven out.” For if a man is to be driven out he must needs be removed and displaced. But how can he be, if he has never once been in the place out of which he claims to have been driven? Well, suppose he had actually been there and had fled in terror at the sight of armed men, would you say that he had been driven out? I think you would. Will you, then, who show such care and skill in settling disputes by the letter and not the spirit of the law, and who interpret laws in the light rather of their wording than of the general good—will you, I say, bring yourself to state that a man has been driven out without having been touched?
