Shall that which is termed vigour in the conduct of a soldier be adjudged as mildness under citizen law? Shall not that which dislodges hosts arrayed in arms be held to have dislodged a concourse of citizens in the garb of peace? Shall we consider a maimed body better evidence of force than a terror-stricken mind? Shall we go looking for wounds when the rout is an accepted fact? For it was one of your own witnesses who44 stated that he pointed out a way of escape to my client’s terror-stricken supporters. Shall it be held that no force was used on those who sought not merely to flee but to find a way by which to flee in safety? Why were they fleeing? Because they were afraid. Afraid of what? Obviously, of force. Can you then deny the cause when you admit the effect? You confess that they fled in terror; the reason for their flight you state to be what we all know it was—arms, a multitude, the furious onset of armed men. When this is an admitted fact, can it be denied that force was used?
