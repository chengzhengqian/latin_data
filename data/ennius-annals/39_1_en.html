*11 Gellius, Attic Nights

We also wondered if the ancients used superesse to mean “to remain and be lacking to complete a thing,” [15] for Sallust uses in that sense not superesse but superare [Iug. 70.2] . . . [16] but we find in this verse in the third book of Ennius’ Annals:

then he declares that one task remains for him

that is, “is left and remains undone,” but this is because it is divided and should be pronounced not as one part of speech [i.e., the verb superesse] but as two [i.e., adverb super + verb esse], which it is.
