t 2 Lucretius, On the Nature of Things

The nature of the soul is unknown, whether it is born or, on the contrary, makes its way into what is being born, and whether it perishes with us, dissolved by death, [115] or visits the darkness and vast caverns of Orcus, or by divine power makes its way into other creatures, as our Ennius sang, who was first to bring a wreath of undying foliage down from lovely Helicon to be celebrated among the Italic races of men. [120] Yet nevertheless, Ennius also explains and sets out in eternal verses that the Acherusian regions indeed exist, where neither our souls nor our bodies endure, but some kind of shades, wondrously pale; he recalls how the image of ever-flourishing Homer [125] arose for him from there and began to pour forth salty tears and unfold in speech the nature of things.
