ANNALS: BOOK IX

​

*9 Macrobius, Saturnalia

He [Virgil] also inserted words from Greek in his work, but he was not the first to dare this, for he was following the boldness of ancient authors: “lychni [lamps] hang from the gold-coffered ceiling” [Aen. 1.726], just like Ennius in Book Nine:

twice six lights of lychni

​

*10 Nonius

famul: famulus [“household slave”]. Ennius in Book 9:

Fortune suddenly casts the highest mortal from the highest power to be the lowest [?] slave1

​

*11 Priscian

Among them [the Greeks], if nouns ending in x have verbs with g, the genitive of the nouns is also declined with g . . . from which frux [fruit] too makes as its genitive frugis,1 since it derives from the Greek verb phrygo [to parch]. Ennius in Annals Book 16 [F 21] . . . ; again in Book 9 he puts frux, which is an adjective, in place of frugi homo [an honest man]:

but why do I mention these things? The honest man makes the word the deed.2

that is, “an honest man.”

  • 1The nominative frux is in fact rare. Cf. Varro, Ling. 9.76.
  • 2A proverbial expression like “no sooner said than done.” Cf. Ter. Haut. 760: dictum ac factum reddidi “I carried it out as soon as it was said.”
  • 1The general sense is clearer than the surviving text, for which various, not entirely satisfactory emendations have been proposed. Lucr. 3.1035 seems to echo the problematic line end proinde ac famul infimus esset “as if to be the lowest slave.”
