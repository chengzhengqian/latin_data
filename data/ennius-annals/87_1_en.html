*12 Priscian

Charisius says cambio, “I exchange” [Gk.], and its perfect campsi, which appear to be from Greek κάμπτω ἔκαμψα [I bend, I have bent], from which the oldest writers were accustomed to say campso, campsas [present and perfect]. Ennius in Book 10:

they doubled Leucata1

  • 1The southwest promontory of the island of Leucas (Lefkas), on the route into the Corinthian Gulf from the north and the west. L. Flamininus, the consul’s brother, would have passed it in 197 BC on his way to besiege Leucas, the capital of Acarnania (Liv. 33.17), but other contexts are imaginable. The Latin verb is a calque on the Greek nautical term for rounding a promontory.
