*9 Priscian

The oldest writers nevertheless as often lengthened as shortened the penultimate syllable of the above-mentioned word [tutudi]. Ennius in Book 14 . . . [F 8]. See, here he has shortened it. So too in Book 16:

the spear [sent?] with firm will by the right hand pierced his side1

​

*10 Macrobius, Saturnalia (on Virgil, “he fell forward on his wound; his armor clattered atop him”)1

Ennius in Book Sixteen:

he tumbled down, and his armor clattered atop him

  • 1“Clamor” was the subject and “the besiegers” the direct object of the missing verb.
  • 1Syntax and sense are uncertain. (The text may well be corrupt.) The translation follows Flores. Priscian seems to be mistaken in claiming a perf. form tūdit; Inc. Ann. F 66 should probably read contu<n>dit.
  • 1Servius ad loc. quotes Hom. Il. 4.504: δούπησεν δὲ πεσών, ἀράβησε δὲ τεύχε’ ἐπ’ αύτῷ “He fell forward with a thud, and his armor clattered about him.”
