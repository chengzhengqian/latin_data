Very ancient writers were also accustomed to terminate the genitive of first declension feminine nouns in -as, in the manner of Greek . . . Ennius in Annals 17:

the leader himself of the route1

*4 Macrobius, Saturnalia (on Virgil, “the hoof shakes the crumbling ground with its four-footed beat”)

Ennius in Book Six [F 25] . . . , so too in Book Eight [F 7] . . . , so too in Book Seventeen:

the cavalry passes, and the hollow hoof shakes the ground with its beat

*5 Macrobius, Saturnalia (on Virgil, “just as in a great cyclone winds from different quarters clash, the West Wind, the South Wind, and the East Wind that delights in the horses of Dawn”)

Ennius in Book Seventeen:

they clash like the winds, when the South Wind’s gust, bringing rain, and the North Wind with its own counterblast compete to raise swells on the mighty main
