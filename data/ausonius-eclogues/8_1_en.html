In the same way the pound is a solid whole, for it consists of twelve equal parts, and in these equal parts, small though they are, one virtue always abides. For if aught is subtracted from their sum, the total is impaired, the parts are thrown out of place and fall because the frame is ruined. As, if we were to wrench out from an arch the keystone upon which all the voussoirs1 bear, the rest will follow suit and come to the ground, their general downfall caused by the topmost stone; even so is it with the pound. If one ounce is wanting, it will no longer be a pound, but being short in weight will be called deunx (eleven-twelfths). The dextans (five-sixths), too, does not retain that name if a sextans (one-sixth) be taken from it, and the dodrans (three-quarters) will be left without the author of its being if we subtract the quadrans (one-fourth). So, too, the bessis (two-thirds) cannot endure once the triens (one-third) is torn from it. Take away one scruple,2 and you will have no quincunx (five-twelfths) left you. And how can the semis (one-half) be a half if the fractions of the pound thus waste away? And of what pound will the septunx (seven-twelfths) stand for seven parts?
