XXXV. Of kitchen-garden plants the quickest toOther kitchen-garden plants grown from seed. grow are basil, blite, navew and rocket; these break out of the ground twoa days after they are sown. Dill comes up in 3 days, lettuce 4, radish 9, cucumber 5, gourd even 6—cucumber is earlier—, cressb and mustard 4, summer beet 5, winter beet 9, orage 7, onions 18 or 19, long onion 9 or 11; coriander is more obstinate, and indeed cunilac and wild marjoram do not come up before 30 days, but the most difficult of all is parsley, for it comes up in 39 days at the quickest, and in the majority of cases in 49 days. Something also depends on the age of the seed, as fresh seed comes up more quickly in the case of leek, long onion, cucumber and gourd, but parsley, beet, cress, cunila, wild marjoram and coriander grow more quickly from old seed. There is a curious thing about beet seed that the whole of it does not germinate in the same year but some only in the year following, and some even two years later; and consequently a quantity of seed only produces a moderate crop. Some plants only produce seed in the same year as they are planted, but some more often, for instance parsley, leek and long onion, as these when once sown retain their fertility and come up several years running.

  • aIn Latin arithmetic 3 is called the third number after 1 (tertio die = the day after to-morrow), and this applies to all the numbers here.
  • bNasturtium is cress, not our ‘nasturtium’.
