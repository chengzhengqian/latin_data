This is the method used in cultivating old trees as well, as in very warm localities growers heap earth over the roots in summer and cover them up, to prevent the heat of the sun from parching them. In other places they turn up the earth round them and give access to the air, but also in winter pile up earth to protect them from frost; whereas growers in hot climates open up the roots in winter and try to obtain moisture for the thirsty trees. XXXII. It remains to give an account of thoseTrees grown for supports and for timber: osier and white willow, white popular, reed, chestnut and others. which are grown as supports for other trees, particularly for vines, and which are felled for timber. Among these the first place is taken by willows, which are planted in a damp place, but in a hole dug two and a half feet deep, a truncheon or rod 18 inches long being used, the stouter the more serviceable. They should be set six feet apart. When three years old they are lopped off two feet from the ground to make them spread out wide and to enable them to be cut back without using ladders; for the willow is the more productive the nearer it is to the ground. It is advised that these trees also should be dug round every year, in April. This is the mode of cultivating the osier willow.
