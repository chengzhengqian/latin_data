Book XXI

The flower never opens except when the wind is blowing, a fact to which it owes its name.a The wild anemone is the larger, and its leaves are broader,b the flower being scarlet. Many have been misled into identifying the wild anemone with the argemone, others again with the poppy that I have called rhoeas.c But there is a great difference between them, because these two blossom after the anemone, which does not yield a juice like theirs, has not their calyx,d and there is no likeness except the head like asparagus. Anemones are good for headache and inflammations, for uterine complaints and for lacteal troubles.e They also promote menstruation when taken with barley water or used on a wool pessary. The root chewed brings away phlegm, is healing to the teeth,f and when boiled down to fluxes of the eyes and to scars. The Magi have attributed to the anemones a kind of mystic potency, recommending that the plant which is first seen should be taken up in that year with the utterance that it is being gathered as a remedy for tertian and quartan agues; after this the blossoms must be wrapped up ing a red rag and kept in the shade, and so be used, should occasion arise, as an amulet. If the crushed root of the anemone bearing a scarlet flower be applied to the skin of any living creature, it produces a sore by reason of its astringent qualities, and for this reason it is employed for cleansing ulcerous sores.

  • bDioscorides has (II. 176 Wellmann) τοῖς φύλλοις πλατυτέρα καὶ σκληροτέρα, so that Mayhoff’s insertion of durioribusque (‘and harder’) may be correct.
  • cXIX. 168.
  • dPerhaps, corolla. However, anemones have no petals, only coloured sepals.
  • eDioscorides has: ἐσθιόμενα γάλα κατασπᾷ, ἐν προσθέτῳ δὲ ἔμμηνα ἄγει (II. 176). Mayhoff’s emendation and punctuation are therefore attractive, especially as lacte accusative would naturally be changed to lacti by an ignorant scribe, and with the received punctuation quoque comes in with a jerk. The sense however is much the same either way.
  • fPossibly, “cures toothache.”
  • gOr, “bound together with,” unless we read, as Mayhoff suggests, ligari.
