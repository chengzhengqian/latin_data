The ripe fruit is dried in the shade. The fruit juice is thickened in the sun, and so is that of the root, which is crushed or boiled down to one third in darkb wine. The leaves are kept in brine, more effectively those of the white kind. The juice of leaves that have been touched by dew are deadly.c Even when kept in brine they retain harmful properties. The mere smell brings heaviness of the head and—although in certain countries the fruit is eaten—those who in ignorance smell too much are struck dumb, while too copious a draught even brings death. When the mandrake is used as a sleeping draught the quantity administered should be proportioned to the strength of the patientd a moderate dose being one cyathus. It is also taken in drink for snake bite, and before surgical operations and punctures to produce anaesthesia. For this purpose some find it enough to put themselves to sleep by the smell.e A dose of two oboli of mandrake is also taken in honey wine instead of hellebore—but hellebore is more efficaciousf—as an emetic and to purge away black bile.
