The Greeks also give the name of ‘bearded stars’ to those from whose lower part spreads a mane resembling a long beard. ‘Javelin-star’ quiver like a dart; these are a very terrible portent. To this class belongs the comet about which Titus Imperatora Caesar in his 5th consulship wrote an account in his famous poem, that being its latest appearance down to the present day. The same stars when shorter and sloping to a point have been called ‘Daggers’; these are the palest of all in colour, and have a gleam like the flash of a sword, and no rays, which even the Quoit-star, which resembles its name in appearance but is in colour like amber, emits in scattered form from its edge. The ‘Tub-star’ presents the shape of a cask, with a smoky light all round it. The ‘Horned star’ has the shape of a horn, like the one that appeared when Greece fought the decisive battle of Salamis. The ‘Torch-star’ resembles glowing torches, the ‘Horse-star’ horses’ manes in very rapid motion and revolving in a circle. There also occurs a shining cometb whose silvery tresses glow so brightly that it is scarcely possible to look at it, and which displays within it a shape in the likeness of a man‘s countenance. There also occur ‘Goat comets,’ enringed with a sort of cloud resembling tufts of hair.
