X. That sea fish were commonly eaten immediately after the foundation of Rome is told us by Cassius Hemina,b whose very words on the subject I will quote here. “Numa ordained that scaleless fish should not be provided at sacrificial meals, being induced by reasons of economy, so that provision could be more easily made for public and private banquets and for feasts of the gods, to prevent caterers on those sacred occasions from being extravagant and buying up the market.”

XI. Coral is as valuable among the Indians as Indian pearls, about which I have spokenc in their proper place, are among the Romans, for cost varies with the demand of any particular people. Coral is also found in the Red Sea, but this is of a darker colour; also in the Persian Gulf—this is called lace—the most valued is in the Gallic Gulf around the Stoechades Islands, in the Sicilian Gulf around the Aeolian Islands, and around Drepana. Coral also grows at Graviscae and before Naples in Campania; but that at Erythrae, which is very red indeed,d is soft and therefore thought worthless.
