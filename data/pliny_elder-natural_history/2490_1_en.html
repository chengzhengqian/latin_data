XXX.Carchedonia. It is said that the same power is exerted by the Carthaginian stone, although it is far less valuable than those previously mentioned. It is formed in the mountain country of the Nasamones by rains of divine origin, as the inhabitants like to think. The stones are found when they reflect the moonlight, particularly at full moon, and in former times were exported to Carthage.a Archelaus records that brittle stones, full of veins and resembling a dying ember, are found in Egypt near Thebes.b I find that drinking vessels used commonly to be made from this stone and from ‘lychnis.’c All these varieties, however, obstinately resist engraving and, when used as signets, retain a portion of the wax.
