Nonetheless Euathlus, as the star pupil of so great a casuist, turned that double-edged argument around. 27“Well, if that’s so,” said he, “in neither case do I owe what you ask. For either I win and the verdict acquits me, or I lose and the contract absolves me, since according to that I owe no fee if I lose this first case in court. So either way the contract acquits me if I lose, and the verdict does if I win.” 28Don’t you think that these arguments between two sophists are like tumbleweeds rolled together by the wind? They cling to each other, both of them equally prickly, similarly piercing, mutually wounding? 29And so Protagoras’ bargain, though so sharp and thorny, should be left to cheats and misers: better by far, undoubtedly, is that other fee that Thales is said to have proposed.
