4.* I have yielded to certain people’s wish—how they wanted me to give an extempore speech!2Yet, so help me, I greatly fear that what befell the crow in Aesop’s fable may befall me, and that aiming at this novel sort of praise may oblige me to lose the little praise I have earned up to now. But since you ask about the fable, I am not ashamed to ramble a little.75

3A crow and a vixen both at the same time noticed a single scrap of food, and rushed to seize it with equal greed but unequal speed, the vixen running and the crow flying. And so the bird outraced the beast, and with a following wind and with both wings outspread it swooped on ahead and got in first. So, pleased with both its booty and its victory, it flew high up and perched safely at the very top of a certain oak nearby. 4Even that far, however, the vixen could not send a stone but could send a trick. For she came beneath that same tree, stood there, and when she saw the robber up above gloating over his prize, she began craftily to praise him. 5“What a fool I am, in a pointless competition with Apollo’s own bird! For he has always had a body so trim as to be neither very small nor overly large, but just the right size to be practical and beautiful. 6He has soft feathers, a chiseled profile, a hard beak, and he is swift in flight, keen of sight, and has talons that hold tight. 7And as for his color, words fail me!
