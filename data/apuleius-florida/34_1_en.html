FLORIDA

9Accordingly, please imagine, if what I produce is worthy of the council, that you are hearing me in the council house itself, if it is scholarly, that I am reading it in the library. 10I only wish, considering the size of the audience, that I had a copious speech at my command and not a lame one, here above all where I would wish to be most eloquent. 11But it is certainly a true saying: heaven gives no man success without some difficulty mixed in,60 so that even in the greatest joy there lurks ever so slight a drawback, a sort of combination of honey and vinegar: the richer the plot, the more the rot.

12That has happened to me on other occasions, and on this one too. For the more assurances of your approval I seem to have, the more my great respect for you makes me hesitant to speak; 13and though I have very often been very ready to lecture before strangers, yet now before my fellow citizens I waver, and (strange to say) I am deterred precisely by the inducements, restrained by the promptings, and checked by the incentives. 14And yet I surely have many grounds for encouragement in your city, since I am not alien to you by origin, unknown to you from boyhood, taught by foreign teachers, obscure as a philosopher, unfamiliar as a speaker, unread or unappreciated as a writer. 15Witness the fact that my native city is part of the Council of Africa, to which you too belong,61 my boyhood was spent among you, you were my teachers, my philosophical studies, though matured in Attic Athens, nevertheless
