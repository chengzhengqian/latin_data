The people of Ambracia presented the consul with a 150-pound crown of gold.34 All their bronze and marble statues and all their pictures were removed and taken away—Ambracia was more richly endowed with such art works than other cities in the region because it had been the site of Pyrrhus’ palace—but nothing else was touched or harmed.

10. The consul now left Ambracia for the interior of Aetolia, and pitched camp at Amphilochian Argos (this is twenty two miles from Ambracia).35 The Aetolian envoys arrived there after some time, the consul meanwhile feeling surprise at their delay. When told that the Aetolian council had approved the peace treaty, he instructed the envoys to proceed to the senate in Rome and also permitted both the Rhodians and the Athenians to go there to plead on the Aetolians’ behalf. He had his brother Gaius Valerius accompany them and he himself crossed to Cephallania.

The envoys found the ears and minds of the important people at Rome already monopolized by charges made by Philip, who had been protesting through embassies and letters that the Dolopians, Amphilochians and Athamania had been filched from him and that his garrisons and, to cap it all, even his son Perseus had been driven from Amphilochia. Philip had thus made the senate reluctant to hear the pleas of the Aetolians. Nevertheless the Rhodians and the Athenians were heard in silence.
