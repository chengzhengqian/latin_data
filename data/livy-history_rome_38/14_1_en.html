BOOK XXXVIII

When the consul was informed by these people of what had befallen the Aetolian delegates, he had them brought from Thyrreum and on their arrival the peace negotiations began.

Amynander worked hard to get the people of Ambracia to surrender, that being his principal concern. After approaching the town wall for discussions with the leading citizens and achieving little success, he finally, with the consul’s permission, went into the city and convinced the citizens by argument as well as by entreaty to put themselves in the Romans’ hands. As for the Aetolians, they too received singular assistance from Gaius Valerius Laevinus, half brother of the consul (both being born of the same mother)31 and son of the Laevinus who had first struck a treaty of friendship with that people.32 After first making a pact to send out Aetolian auxiliaries in safety, the Ambraciots opened their gates. Then <. . .>33 they were to pay 500 Euboean talents, 200 immediately, and 300 in equal installments over six years; they were to surrender prisoners of war and deserters to the Romans; they were to claim jurisdiction over no city which, since the date of Titus Quinctius’ crossing to Greece, had been either taken by force by the Romans or had voluntarily entered into friendship with them; and the island of Cephallania was to be exempted from the provisions of the treaty.
