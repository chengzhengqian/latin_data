On the other hand, Culleo is also said to have been on such bad terms with the family that, because of his well-known animosity, he was the one especially chosen by the anti-Scipio faction to conduct the inquiry. At all events, it was before this praetor—too favorably or too unfavorably disposed to him—that Lucius Scipio was immediately arraigned. At the same time the names of Scipio’s legates, Aulus and Lucius Hostilius Cato,242 were brought forward and accepted for indictment, as was that of his quaestor Gaius Furius Aculeo; and so that it would seem that everything was tainted with complicity in the corruption even two secretaries and an attendant were indicted. Lucius Hostilius, the secretaries and the attendant were acquitted before Scipio’s case came to court, but Scipio, his legate Aulus Hostilius, and Gaius Furius were found guilty. The verdict was that, in order for Antiochus to be granted a more favorable peace treaty, Scipio had received 6,000 lbs of gold and 480 lbs of silver more than he deposited in the treasury, while Aulus Hostilius had taken 80 lbs of gold and 403 of silver, and Furius the quaestor 130 lbs of gold and 200 of silver.

Such are the amounts of gold and silver that I found in the work of Antias. In Lucius Scipio’s case I should prefer to assume a scribal error rather than misrepresentation on the author’s part with respect to the amounts of gold and silver.
