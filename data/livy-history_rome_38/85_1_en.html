The two greatest cities in the world were virtually at the same time found guilty of ingratitude toward their most prominent citizens, they said, but Rome’s ingratitude was greater in that whereas it was a defeated Carthage that had driven a defeated Hannibal into exile216 it was a victorious Rome that was driving out a victorious Africanus. Others opined that no one citizen should be so preeminent as to be above legal investigation, that nothing guaranteed equality of freedom as much as that all the most powerful people should defend themselves in court. Without accountability, can anyone be entrusted with anything at all, much less governance of the state? Force could be justly used on anyone unable to accept the impartiality of justice, they argued.

Such points were raised in conversation until the day of the trial arrived. Nobody before that time, not even Scipio himself during his consulship or censorship, had been escorted to the Forum by a greater crowd of people of all stations than the defendant on that day. Instructed to plead his case, he made no mention of the charges but launched into a discourse on his own career so stirring that it was generally agreed that no one had ever received a more eloquent or truthful encomium. For these achievements were spoken of by him in the same spirit and with the same brilliance with which he accomplished them, and did not offend the ears of his listeners because they were brought up when he was at risk in court and not as self-advertisement.
