Their wives and children had been dragged off into slavery, their property had been taken from them and—what distressed them more than anything—their temples had, throughout the entire city, been stripped of their ornaments. The statues of the gods—no, the gods themselves—had been torn from their dwellings and carried off, and all that the Ambraciots had been left to worship, and offer prayers and supplications to, was bare walls and doorposts.192 While the envoys made these complaints the consul followed a prearranged plan, maliciously questioning them in an accusing manner and apparently enticing from them more than they would have said of their own accord.

The senators were impressed but the other consul, Gaius Flaminius, came to the defense of Marcus Fulvius. The Ambraciots had followed an old and commonplace procedure, he said—these were the same charges that had been brought against Marcus Marcellus by the Syracusans and against Quintus Fulvius by the Campanians.193 So why should the senators not entertain such a line of attack against Titus Quinctius by King Philip, he asked, or against Manius Acilius and Lucius Scipio by Antiochus? Or against Gnaeus Manlius by the Gauls, and against Marcus Fulvius himself by the Aetolians and the peoples of Cephallania?

“Ambracia was attacked and captured,” Flaminius continued. “Statues and artworks were taken from it, and other things were done that usually occur when cities are captured.
