The Gauls then reunited and lent their support to Nicomedes in his war against Ziboetes,75 who controlled part of Bithynia. Thanks largely to their assistance, Ziboetes was defeated and all Bithynia came under the sway of Nicomedes.

Leaving Bithynia, the Gauls next came into Asia. No more than 10,000 of their total force of 20,000 were under arms; nevertheless, they instilled such terror in all the peoples living west of the Taurus that, whether or not they attacked them, these totally submitted to their authority and the most distant and nearest of them alike. Eventually, since they comprised three tribes—the Tolostobogii, the Trocmi and the Tectosages76—the Gauls made a tripartite division of Asia as taxation districts for each of their constituent tribes. To the Trocmi was assigned the shore of the Hellespont; the Tolostobogii were allotted Aeolis and Ionia; and the Tectosages the interior of Asia. They wrested payment from all of Asia west of the Taurus range and they themselves chose to settle on the banks of the River Halys.77 Such was the terror inspired by their name (especially after their numbers increased through their great fertility) that even the kings of Syria eventually did not refuse to pay tribute. The first of the inhabitants of Asia to refuse payment was Attalus, father of King Eumenes.
