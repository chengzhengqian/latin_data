Romans plundered a town that was deserted and full of all manner of provisions. From there they advanced to the headwaters of the River Lysis, and the next day to the River Cobulatus.60

The people of Termessus were at that time blockading the citadel of the Isiondenses,61 having captured the city. Since they had no other hope of assistance, the beleaguered townspeople sent envoys to the consul begging for support. They were imprisoned in their citadel with their wives and children, they said, and with every day that passed they were awaiting death either by the sword or starvation. The consul was thus offered the pretext he wanted for making a detour into Pamphylia.

His coming raised the siege of the Isiondenses, and he granted peace to Termessus for a price of 50 talents of silver (he made the same arrangement with the Aspendians62 and the other peoples of Pamphylia). On his return journey from Pamphylia he encamped at the River Taurus on the first day and at the place they call Xyline Come the next. Setting out from Xyline Come he reached the city of Cormasa by continuous marches. The next town was Darsa;63 Manlius found it deserted by its panic-stricken inhabitants but replete with all manner of provisions. As he advanced along the marshes, envoys from Lysinoe64 came to him offering the surrender of their town. After that the army came into the territory of Sagalassus, which is fertile and rich in crops of all kinds. It is inhabited by Pisidians, by far the best soldiers in the region. This fact, together with the fertility of their land, the size of their population and their position as one of the few fortified cities in the area, gave them confidence.
