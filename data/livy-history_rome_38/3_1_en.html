Leaving most of his force there—they would not have had the strength for such strenuous marches—he came with 2,000 men to Athenaeum, the only town to have been successfully held by a garrison of his. Then, probing the surrounding areas and easily recognizing that he was facing resistance everywhere else, he fell back on Gomphi and marched back into Athamania with all his troops gathered together. He then sent Xenon ahead with 1,000 infantry and ordered him to seize Aethiopa,8 which was strategically placed, overlooking Argithea. When Philip saw that the position was occupied by his men, he himself encamped near the temple of Jupiter Acraeus.9 Detained here for a day by a fierce storm, he proceeded with his march toward Argithea the following day. As the Macedonians advanced, however, the Athamanians suddenly appeared, making a dash for the hills that dominated the road. The leading companies came to a halt on sighting them, and there was panic and trepidation throughout the column as each man speculated on what might transpire if the column were taken into the valleys lying below the cliffs. The king wanted to get through the ravine quickly, if his men would follow him, but their discomposure forced him to recall the forward units and beat a retreat along the path by which he had come.

The Athamanians proceeded to follow him, initially at a distance and biding their time; but when the Aetolians joined them they left them to put pressure on the rear while they themselves closed in on the flanks.
