In seeking a treaty, they should follow the lead of Antiochus, whom they had dragged into the war—Antiochus had relinquished not just a handful of cities whose independence was disputed but a rich empire comprising all of Asia west of the Taurus range. He would not listen to the Aetolians’ plea for peace unless they were disarmed, he said. They first had to hand over their weapons and all their horses,29 and then deliver to the Roman people a thousand talents, half to be paid immediately, if they wanted to have peace. Moreover, he concluded, he would add to the treaty a further clause that they were to have the same friends and enemies as the Roman people.

9. Because the conditions were harsh, and also because the envoys knew the wild and capricious disposition of their people, they made no reply but returned home so that, with no commitment made, they could ever more urgently press their praetor and leading citizens for an answer on what should be done. They were met with shouts and jeers, asked how long they were going to drag out the business, and told to bring back a peace on any terms. As they were returning to Ambracia, however, they were caught in an ambush set near their road by the Acarnanians, with whom they were at war, and taken to Thyrreum30 for imprisonment. This caused a delay in the peace process and at the time deputations from the Athenians and Rhodians, who had come to plead for them, were now with the consul. The king of the Athamanians, Amynander, had also come to the Roman camp with a safe conduct, more concerned for the city of Ambracia, where he had spent the greater part of his exile, than for the Aetolians.
