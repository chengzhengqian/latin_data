On the flanks the Gauls stood their ground longer because the Romans were later reaching them, but then they failed to withstand even the first volley of projectiles. Unable to drag the men who had entered the camp away from their pillaging, the consul sent those who had been on the wings in immediate pursuit of the enemy. They followed the Gauls for some distance but killed no more than 8,000 men in the rout—there was no battle as such—and the rest crossed the River Halys.

Most of the Romans remained in the enemy camp that night; the rest the consul took back to his own. The following day Manlius made an inventory of the captives and spoils, which were as great as a rapacious tribe could amass after many years of occupying all the lands west of the Taurus range with armed force. The Gauls gathered together in one spot after scattering in random flight, most of them wounded or weaponless and stripped of all their possessions, and they sent spokesmen to the consul to sue for peace. Manlius ordered them to come to Ephesus. He himself was in a hurry to leave the region, which was chilled by the proximity of the Taurus mountains—it was already midautumn—and he led his victorious army back to winter quarters on the coast.
