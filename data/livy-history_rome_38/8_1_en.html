He prearranged a certain night, and even a point during the night, when Nicodamus’ men were to launch an attack from the city on the enemy siege works facing the Pyrrheum and when he himself was to cause alarm in the Roman camp. He felt that with the commotion in the two spots and with the darkness heightening the panic a sensational coup could be brought off.

Nicodamus did manage to slip by some of the sentry posts at the dead of night and with a determined charge force his way through others; he then surmounted the rampart and made his way into the city. In so doing he inspired the beleaguered population with considerable spirit for any venture and boosted their hopes; and as soon as the prearranged night arrived he launched his sudden attack on the siege engines according to plan. The operation was more impressive in theory than in its outcome because there was no pressure applied from outside. Either the Aetolian praetor had been frightened off or it seemed a better idea to bring succor to the newly recovered Amphilochians, on whom Philip’s son Perseus,21 who had been sent to retake Dolopia and the Amphilochians, was now mounting an all-out attack.
