At this point the Gauls applied pressure and cut down the scattered Romans; and most would have lost their lives but for the arrival on the scene of the escort of the foragers, the 600 horsemen. On hearing the distraught shouting of their comrades in the distance, they had made ready their weapons and mounts and, fresh in the field, they took in hand the battle that had turned against the Romans. Fortunes were thus immediately reversed and the panic was transferred from the conquered to the conquerors. The Gauls were scattered at the first assault; the foragers came running to the spot from the fields; and the Gauls were faced by their enemy on every side. The result was that even flight was not easy or safe since the Romans were pursuing exhausted men with horses that were fresh. Few Gauls therefore made good their escape; no prisoner was taken; and the large majority paid with their lives for violating the safe conduct guaranteed to a parley. Their hearts fired with anger, the Romans came to the enemy in full force the next day.
