A huge quantity of projectiles was then hurled onto the large numbers driven back into the camp and the cry that went up, mingled with the lamentations of women and children, indicated that many were being wounded. The advance troops of the legions hurled their javelins at those who had blocked the gates by installing themselves there. While these men received no wounds from them, several became entangled and caught up with each other when their shields were pierced right through;108 and they withstood the Roman assault no longer.

23. The gates now lay open, but before the victors could burst in the Gauls fled in all directions from their camp. They rushed blindly ahead, roads or no roads. No precipices, no cliffs could stand in their way—their only fear was their enemy. As a result many or . . . maimed,109 fell headlong to their deaths from enormous heights.

The camp taken, the consul kept his men from pillaging and looting and ordered them to take the initiative in following the enemy, putting pressure on them and adding panic to their demoralization. The second column under Lucius Manlius also arrived on the scene, and he did not allow those men to enter the camp, either. He sent them off immediately to pursue the enemy and shortly afterward he himself followed, leaving the guarding of the captives to his military tribunes—he thought that the war would be finished if the greatest possible numbers were killed or captured while in such a state of panic.
