While it would have been enough for their descendants to uphold these men’s glorious reputation, said Nasica, Publius Africanus had instead so far surpassed the praiseworthy record of his father as to make people believe that his bloodline was not human but that he was of divine lineage.256 As for Lucius Scipio, who was now in question, even if one passed over his achievements in Spain and Africa as his brother’s legate, he had been considered257 by the senate worthy of being assigned the province of Asia, without sortition, and direction of the war

with King Antiochus; and his brother, after two consulships, the censorship, and a triumph, thought enough of him to go to Asia as his legate.258 There, so that the grandeur and eminence of the legate should not overshadow the merits of the consul, it happened that on the day that Lucius Scipio vanquished Antiochus in pitched battle at Magnesia, Publius Scipio lay sick at Elaea,259 a number of days’ journey away. And that army of Antiochus was no smaller than the army of Hannibal that the Romans had faced in Africa—the same Hannibal who had been commander in chief in the Punic War was in fact only one of the king’s many officers. The war, too, had been fought in such a way that nobody could find fault even with fortune. It was in the peace treaty that grounds for criticism were sought—this, it is said, was put on sale.
