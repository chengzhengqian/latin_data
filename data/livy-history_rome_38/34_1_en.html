Manlius himself went up with the largest division of the troops along the path where the mountain afforded the easiest access. He instructed his brother, Lucius Manlius,100 to climb the slope of the winter rising as far as the terrain would allow and as far as he could in safety; faced with ground that was dangerous and precipitous, he was not to combat the difficulty of the land or battle against insuperable obstacles, but cut obliquely across the mountain toward him and join up with his column. Gaius Helvius101 he ordered to move gradually around the lower slopes of the mountain with the third part of the force, and then bring his column uphill on the sun’s summer-setting side. Manlius also divided Attalus’ auxiliaries into three sections of equal size and ordered the young man himself to accompany him. He left his cavalry and elephants102 on the plain close to the hills, and he commanded his officers to watch carefully the developments in every location and ensure that they could bring support wherever the situation required it.

21. Convinced that their position was inaccessible on both flanks, the Gauls wanted to block access from the area to the south with an armed force. They therefore sent some 4,000 men under arms to seize a hillock overlooking the route, less than a mile from their camp, thinking that with this strongpoint they would obstruct their enemy’s advance. When the Romans saw that, they prepared themselves for battle. The skirmishers went forward a short distance before the standards and with them went Cretan archers and slingers, which had been provided by Attalus, as well as the Trallians103 and the Thracians.
