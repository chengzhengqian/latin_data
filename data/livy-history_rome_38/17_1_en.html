(No change was made to the agreement reached with the consul regarding the sum of money the Aetolians were to pay and its installments, but it was decided that they should be allowed to pay in gold instead of silver if they preferred, provided that one gold piece be worth ten pieces of silver.)

“The Aetolians are to renounce any intention of reclaiming cities, lands or persons that were formerly under their jurisdiction but which came into the control of the Roman people either by military conquest or their own volition in or after the consulship of Lucius Quinctius and Gnaeus Domitius.39 Oeniadae,40 both city and its agricultural lands, are to belong to the Acarnanians.” Such were the terms of the treaty struck with the Aetolians.

12. Not just that same summer but almost during the very days that these actions were taken in Aetolia by the consul Marcus Fulvius, the other consul, Gnaeus Manlius,41 fought a war in Galatia, an account of which I shall now begin.

At the beginning of spring the consul arrived at Ephesus.
