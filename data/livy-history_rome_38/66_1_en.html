BOOK XXXVIII

On the day he left Lysimachia Manlius reached the river they call “The Melas,” and on the day after that he reached Cypsela.172 After Cypsela he was faced with a narrow, broken road for about ten miles through a wooded area, and because of the difficulty of this route he split the army in two. He ordered one part to go ahead and the other to bring up the rear after a large interval, and he set between them the baggage (this comprised carts loaded with public money and other valuable spoils).

As he was proceeding in that formation through a pass, a band of Thracians, numbering not more than 10,000 and drawn from four tribes (the Asti, Caeni, Maduateni and Coreli173), blocked the road right at the neck of the defile. The general opinion was that this did not happen without the complicity of King Philip of Macedon, that Philip knew that the only route by which the Romans would return was through Thrace, and that he also knew how much money they were transporting.

The commander was at the head of the column, and he was worried about the difficulty of the terrain. The Thracians made no move until the armed troops went by; but when they saw that the vanguard had passed the neck of the defile and that the troops at the rear were still not close, they pounced on the baggage train and its bundles. They killed the guards and some pillaged the contents of the wagons while others dragged away pack animals together with their loads. When shouting from this action first reached those following behind, who had now entered the pass, and then also carried to the front of the column, there was a rush from each end to the center of the defile and disorderly fighting broke out simultaneously at several points.
