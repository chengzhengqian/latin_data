While they were encamped there, the envoys came from Eposognatus with the news that his journey to the Gallic chieftains had achieved no success. The Gauls, they said, were moving in large numbers from their villages and farms in the plains, taking their wives and children with them and driving before them and carrying everything they could remove or drive; they were heading for Mt. Olympus96 in order to defend themselves there with their arms and the natural features of the place.

19. Envoys from Oroanda subsequently brought more reliable information: the Tolostobogian people had seized Mt. Olympus; the Tectosagi, taking a different path, had headed for another mountain, which is called Magaba;97 and the Trocmi had left their wives and children with the Tectosagi and decided to bring help to the Tolostobogii with an armed column.

At that time the chieftains of the three peoples were Ortiago, Combolomarus and Gaulotus.98 They had adopted this plan of campaign because they believed that by occupying the highest mountains in the area, and transporting there all kinds of supplies in quantities sufficient for an indefinite period, they would wear down and exhaust their enemy. The Romans would never venture to climb such precipitously steep terrain, they thought; and if they did try they could be held at bay or hurled down even by a small group of men, and would not tolerate cold and deprivation sitting inactive at the foot of these icy mountains.
