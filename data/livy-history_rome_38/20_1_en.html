The Maeander rises at the top of the citadel of Celaenae, flows down through the city center, passes first through Caria and then Ionia and debouches into a gulf of the sea between Priene and Miletus. At Antioch Seleucus, the son of Antiochus, came to the consul’s encampment to deliver grain to the army in accordance with the treaty made with Scipio. A slight argument arose over Attalus’ auxiliaries as Seleucus maintained that Antiochus had agreed to supply grain only to the Roman soldiery. That too was brought to an end by the firm resolve of the consul, who sent a tribune and ordered the Roman soldiers not to accept their supply until Attalus’ auxiliaries had received theirs.

The army then advanced to a place they call Gordiutichos. From there they reached Tabae after a three days march. This city lies on the borders of Pisidia, in the region facing the Pamphylian sea.50 The military strength of the region was as yet untouched and it possessed pugnacious warriors. On this occasion, too, cavalry charged out against the Roman column and caused considerable disarray with the initial assault. Later, when it became clear that they were no match for the Romans either in numbers or fighting ability, they were pushed back into the city and they proceeded to beg pardon for their error and were ready to surrender the city.
