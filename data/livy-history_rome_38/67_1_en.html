In the Romans’ case, it was the difficulty of the terrain that told against them because the barbarians fell upon them by paths that they knew and sometimes lay hidden in the deep recesses. The loads and wagons themselves, adversely positioned for one side or the other as chance would have it, also obstructed the combatants. In one sector a plunderer fell, in another a man defending that plunder. The fortunes of the battle varied according to whether the lie of the land was favorable or unfavorable for this side or that, according to the spirit of the fighters, and according to the numbers involved—for some had met numbers superior, and others numbers inferior, to their own. Casualties were heavy on both sides. Night was already coming on when the Thracians left the battle, not running away from wounds or death but because they had sufficient booty.174

41. The leading companies of the Roman column pitched camp in an open area outside the pass near the temple of Bendis;175 the rest remained in the middle of the pass to guard the baggage, surrounding themselves with a double palisade. The following day they reconnoitered the pass before striking camp and then joined the leading contingent.

In that battle, since it was fought at various points virtually all along the defile, some of the baggage and some camp followers were lost as well as a number of fighting men, but the most serious setback was the death of Quintus Minucius Thermus,176 a man of great courage and energy.
