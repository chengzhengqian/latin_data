As at Mt. Olympus, the consul put his light-armed troops in the front line and saw to it that they had there just as great a supply of weapons of all kinds available. When the Romans made their approach, all was the same on both sides as in the previous battle—apart from morale. In the victors this was buoyed by their success; in the enemy it was broken because, while not defeated themselves, they took the disaster suffered by men of their own race as being also one for them. And so, after a similar beginning, the engagement had the same result. What looked like a cloud of light missiles covered the battle line of the Gauls. No one dared to run forward from the ranks for fear of exposing his body to weapons coming from all sides and the closer together they stood the more wounds they would receive, being like fixed targets for their enemies’ projectiles. The consul thought that if he merely showed the legionary standards to his enemies, who were already disorganized themselves, they would all immediately turn to flight; and so, taking the skirmishers and the rest of the auxiliaries back into the ranks, he moved his line forward.
