BOOK XXXVIII

After taking over command of the troops from Lucius Scipio and conducting a purification of the army,42 he delivered an address to the men in which he commended them for their courage in ending the war against Antiochus with a single battle and encouraged them to undertake a fresh campaign, this time against the Gauls. These, he said, had assisted Antiochus with auxiliary forces and, in addition, they had such a violent nature that Antiochus’ removal to a point beyond the Taurus range would prove to have been pointless if the power of the Gauls were not broken. He finished with a few remarks on himself, which were neither untrue nor overblown. The men heard the consul’s words with elation, frequently applauding them—they believed the Gauls to have been no more than a division of Antiochus’ strength, and that with the king defeated the Gallic troops would be no effective force on their own.

The consul believed Eumenes’ absence (he was then in Rome) to be inconvenient: he knew the terrain and the people, and crushing the power of the Gauls was in his interest.43 Manlius therefore summoned Eumenes’ brother Attalus44 from Pergamum, and encouraged him to join him in the war; and when Attalus promised his own and his people’s support Manlius sent him home to make preparations. A few days later, after the consul had set off from Ephesus, Attalus met him at Magnesia with 1,000 infantry and 500 cavalry. He had instructed his brother Athenaeus to follow behind with the rest of his forces and had left the protection of Pergamum with men he believed loyal to his brother and the throne.
