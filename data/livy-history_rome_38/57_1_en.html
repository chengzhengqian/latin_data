In the praetorian sortition that followed Marcus Claudius was allotted the urban jurisdiction and Publius Claudius the foreign jurisdiction; Quintus Marcius drew Sicily and Gaius Stertinius Sardinia; and Lucius Manlius drew Hither Spain and Gaius Atinius Farther Spain.

36. The following decisions were reached about the armies: The legions formerly under the command of Gaius Laelius were to be transferred from Gaul to the propraetor Marcus Tuccius in Bruttium. The army that was in Sicily was to be disbanded and the fleet that was there147 the propraetor Marcus Sepronius was to bring back to Rome. The Spanish provinces were both allocated the single legion that was then stationed in them, and the two praetors were each given authority to requisition from the allies and take with them a supplementary force of 3,000 infantry and 200 cavalry. Before the new magistrates set off for their provinces, a three-day period of supplication,148 ordained in the name of the college of decemvirs because darkness had fallen between roughly the third and fourth hours of daylight, was held at all the crossroads.149 A nine-day sacrifice was also proclaimed after a shower of stones fell on the Aventine.150

  • 146“allies and those with Latin rights” (sociis Latini nominis [lit., “allies of Latin name”] here, but with variations throughout Livy) puts together two groups with different treaties with Rome: the Latins had closer ties, including marriage and commercial rights, while the allies’ treaties were generally less favorable. Both were to provide troops for Rome in time of war, and Livy does not distinguish between them in military contexts. On Roman armies and fleets, see Introduction to vol. IX (LCL 295), lviii–lxv.
  • 147To guard against a possible invasion (cf. 36.2.11), which was no longer a threat.
  • 148The supplication (supplicatio) was a period of collective prayer decreed by the senate in times of crisis or calamity. It could also be decreed for thanksgiving to the gods, especially for a victory (cf. Oakley 2.735). It seems to have eventually become assimilated to the lectisternium, a banquet for the gods, figures of whom were set on cushioned couches for the feast. Cf. OCD s.v. supplication, Roman.
