Here the Trausi, also a Thracian people, gathered with similar hopes of plunder; but since the treeless valleys rendered them visible in the distance as they were blocking the pass, there was less panic and consternation among the Romans because, despite the unfavorable terrain, it was still a regular engagement that had to be fought, with lines in the open and troops deployed. The Romans approached in close formation and attacked with a shout, at first dislodging and then driving back the enemy. Now began flight and slaughter because the narrow terrain, which they themselves had chosen, obstructed the Thracians. The victorious Romans pitched camp near a village of the Maroneans (they call it Sale).179

The following day, after a march along an open road, the Priatic plain180 welcomed them, and they stayed on it for three days provisioning themselves with grain (some coming from the Maroneans’ own fields, and some from the Romans’ ships, which followed them loaded with all manner of supplies). From the camp it was a day’s march to Apollonia; and from there they came through the land of Abdera to Neapolis.181 This part of the route, through the Greek colonies, was completely peaceful; but after that the rest, through central Thrace, though without event, was unnerving day and night until they reached Macedonia.
