The Spartans were aggrieved, and in order to have free access to the sea at some point for sending embassies to Rome or elsewhere, and also to have a market and haven for foreign merchandise to meet urgent needs, they took a coastal village called Las126 with a surprise assault at night. The villagers and the exiles living there were initially daunted by the unexpected blow but then, just before dawn, they came together and drove out the Spartans in a minor skirmish. Even so panic spread all along the coast and all the fortresses, villages and exiles having homes there sent a joint embassy to the Achaeans.

31. The praetor Philopoemen had right from the start espoused the cause of the exiles and had always recommended to the Achaeans the curtailment of Spartan power and influence. Now he granted an audience to the complainants and on his proposal the following decree was passed: that inasmuch as Titus Quinctius and the Romans had transferred the strongholds and villages of the Laconian seaboard to the protection and custody of the Achaeans, and as the village of Las had been attacked, and blood had there been shed although the Spartans were bound by treaty not to interfere with these places, the treaty would be deemed broken unless the authors of that deed and their accomplices were delivered to the Achaeans.
