BOOK XXXVIII

When he had also received from Antiochus the elephants that were among the various items he was supposed to receive from the king, Gnaeus Manlius made a gift of all of them to Eumenes. He then looked into the concerns of the city-states, many of which were in turmoil because of the changed conditions. Thanks to the support of Eumenes, to whom he had at about this time betrothed his daughter, King Ariarathes was excused payment of half the money demanded of him and was accepted into friendship with Rome. After examining the cases they presented,

the ten commissioners made different dispositions for the various states.170 They granted tax exemption to those who had been paying tribute to King Antiochus and had sided with Roman people, but they ordered all who had been on Antiochus’ side, or had paid tribute to King Attalus, to pay taxes to Eumenes. In addition, they made special grants of tax exemption to the Colophonians living in Notium, to the Cymaeans and to the Mylassenians. To the people of Clazomenae they presented the island of Drymussa as a gift in addition to exemption from taxes; to the Milesians they restored what they call the “sacred territory;” and to the people of Ilium they also made a gift of Rhoeteum and Gergitha, not so much because of any recent services on their part as in remembrance of the Romans’ ancestry. The same consideration lay behind the liberation of Dardanus. They also made gifts of land to the Chians, the Smyrneans and the Erythraeans in recognition of their exemplary loyalty in the war, and they accorded them every mark of honor. The Phocaeans had the territory that they possessed before the war restored to them and were also granted the right to live under their ancient laws.
