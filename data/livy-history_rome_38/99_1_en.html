59. But heavens above, Nasica continued, in Scipio’s case the allegation was that the peace terms themselves looked suspiciously favorable to Antiochus. His kingdom was left untouched, it was said: he had, in defeat, everything that he had possessed before the war; and though he had had a large amount of gold and silver none of it had been made public property—all had gone into private hands. In fact, though, at Lucius Scipio’s triumph, had not as much gold and silver as in ten other triumphs put together been carried before the eyes of the whole population?

“What am I to say about the bounds of Antiochus’ kingdom?” Nasica continued. Antiochus had held the whole of Asia and the parts of Europe next to it. How much of the world this covers, extending as it does from the Taurus range all the way into the Aegean Sea, and how many peoples and not just towns it encompasses—of this everybody is aware. This area, more than thirty days’ journey in length and ten days’ in width between the two seas, and extending as far as the heights of the Taurus range—this was taken from Antiochus, who was driven to the farthest corner of the world. What more could have been taken from him if the peace settlement had cost him nothing?260 The conquered Philip had been left Macedonia, and Nabis had been left Sparta, but no indictment was sought against Quinctius.261 No, because he had not had Africanus as a brother, a man whose brilliant reputation should have helped Lucius Scipio, whereas the jealousy felt for the man had harmed him.
