Nicander’s first plan had been to launch a night attack on the Epirote camp with the rest of the force, it not being easy for help to be brought by the Romans because the river lay between them. Later he felt the operation was risky—the Romans might get wind of it and his withdrawal be jeopardized—and so, put off this particular plan, he turned instead to making raids on Acarnania.

5. After completing the structures by which the city was to be surrounded as well as the siege engines that he was intending to move up to the walls, the consul attacked the city’s defenses at five points simultaneously. Access from the plain being easier, he moved three of his engines, equidistant from each other, toward what they call the Pyrrheum;20 another he advanced on the area of the temple of Aesculapius; and one more on the citadel. He proceeded to pound the walls with the rams and to shear off the turrets with poles armed with hooks. At the sight of this and with the blows being delivered to the walls with a terrifying crash, the townspeople were initially filled with panic and alarm; then, when to their surprise they saw the walls still standing, they pulled themselves together again and began using swing beams to drop weights of lead or stone, or thick logs, on the battering rams. Hurling grappling irons, they also pulled the hooks to the inside of the wall and broke off the poles. In addition, by mounting counterattacks on the sentries at the siege engines at night and on the advance posts during the day, they now themselves began striking terror into their enemy.
