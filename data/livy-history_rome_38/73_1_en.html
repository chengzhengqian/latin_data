44. Aemilius kept attacking the cunning malevolence of his foe, insinuating that it was universally recognized and claiming that Fulvius would stall and draw out the time so as not to return to Rome while his enemy was consul, and so two days were spent on the struggle between the consuls; and it seemed that no decision could be taken while Flaminius was present. However, the opportunity was seized when Flaminius happened to be absent through illness, and a senatorial decree was passed on a motion from Aemilius that the Ambraciots should have all their property restored. They were also to be independent and live under their own laws and gather whatever tariffs they wished on goods carried by land and sea provided that the Romans, their allies and holders of Latin rights were exempted. As for the statues and other works of art that the Ambraciots complained had been removed from their holy shrines, the senate decided that the matter should be referred to the college of pontiffs on Marcus Fulvius’ return to Rome and that the college’s decisions should be put into effect.
