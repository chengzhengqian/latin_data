Scipio protested that it was because of the death of his brother Publius Africanus—a man of superlative bravery and distinction—that the proposal had arisen. It was not enough that there had been no eulogy for Africanus at the rostra after his death—he had to face charges as well. Even the Carthaginians had been content with Hannibal being sent into exile,239 he said, but the Roman people could not be satisfied even with the Publius Scipio’s death—no, the man’s reputation was to be torn to shreds while he lay in the grave and his brother, too, was to be sacrificed, a further victim of envy. Marcus Cato spoke in favor of the bill—his speech “On the Money of King Antiochus” still survives240—and with his influence deterred the Mummii (the tribunes) from opposing it. Accordingly, when these withdrew their veto, all the tribes voted in favor of the bill.
