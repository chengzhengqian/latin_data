At first they rushed to arms; but when they heard of the Amphilochians joining the Aetolians, of Philip’s flight from Athamania and the massacre of his garrison, they too abandoned Philip for the Aetolians. With these tribes lying around them, the Aetolians now believed themselves protected from the Macedonians on all sides, but then came word of Antiochus’ defeat by the Romans in Asia. Shortly afterward, too, their deputation returned from Rome bringing no prospect of a peace treaty, and bearing the news that the consul Fulvius had already made the crossing with an army. Terrified by this report, the Aetolians first summoned delegations from Rhodes and Athens so that through the influence of these states their recently rejected entreaties might gain easier acceptance in the senate. Then they sent the most eminent of their people to Rome for one last hopeful attempt—though they had given no thought to avoiding war before the enemy was almost in sight.

By now Marcus Fulvius15 had taken the army over to Apollonia and was discussing with the Epirote leaders where to start the campaign. The Epirotes were in favor attacking Ambracia,16 which had at this time allied itself with the Aetolians. Should the Aetolians come to defend the city, they said, there were open plains round about on which to do battle; otherwise, if they declined battle, a siege would not prove difficult.
