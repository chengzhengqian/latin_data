17. It was with such an enemy that the Romans had to fight, one so frightening to all in the region. The consul therefore addressed the men at an assembly, speaking much as follows:

“I am not unaware, men, that of all the peoples that live in Asia the Gauls have the preeminent reputation in warfare. They are a fierce tribe that has wandered virtually throughout the world doing battle—and they have chosen to settle among the most docile race of people. They have tall frames and long, red-dyed hair, huge shields and long swords. In addition there are their chants as they go into battle, their yells and war dances, and the frightening clash of arms as they strike their shields following some ancestral custom—all deliberately contrived to instill terror. But let Greeks, Phrygians and Carians be frightened by such things, of which they have no knowledge or experience. The wild antics of the Gauls are familiar to Romans, and their empty bluster is also known to us. Once in the past, when the two first met in battle at the Allia,79 our ancestors fled before them; but in the two hundred years since that time the Romans have been cutting them down or putting them to flight, stampeding them like cattle, and more triumphs have been celebrated over the Gauls, it is almost true to say, than over the rest of the world put together. And this is a fact we have learned from experience: if you withstand their initial onslaught, which they let loose with burning fervor and blind rage, their limbs become flaccid with sweat and fatigue and their weapons slip from their hands.
