They were particularly agitated by the fear that, once they accepted the yoke by complying with the first commands, Philopoemen would carry out what he had long been planning and deliver Sparta to the exiles. So, in a paroxysm of rage, they murdered thirty men of the party that had been connected with the policies of Philopoemen and the exiles and decreed that the pact with the Achaeans should be revoked.127 They further decreed that envoys be sent immediately to Cephallania to surrender Sparta to the consul Marcus Fulvius and the Romans and to beg the consul to come to the Peloponnese to take the city of Sparta under the protection and authority of the Roman people.

32. When the envoys brought this news back to the Achaeans, war was declared on Sparta with the unanimous agreement of the states present at the council meeting. Winter prevented immediate commencement of hostilities but the territory of the Spartans was subjected to minor incursions, more like banditry than warfare, and not just overland but with naval attacks from the sea as well. This disorder brought the consul to the Peloponnese; and at his command a meeting of the council was called at Elis and the Spartans were invited to debate the matter. There it was not just a heated debate but a shouting match. Although the consul had been showing favor to both sides and making diplomatically evasive remarks, he brought closure to the matter with a single command, that they avoid military action until they had sent envoys to the senate in Rome.
