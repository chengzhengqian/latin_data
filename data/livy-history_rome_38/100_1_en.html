BOOK XXXVIII

According to the judgment, added Nasica, gold and silver had been taken into Lucius Scipio’s home in such quantities as could not be raised by the sale of all his property. So where was the royal gold, where all this windfall that Scipio had received? A pile of newly acquired wealth should have come to light in a house that expenses had not drained. And of course Lucius Scipio’s enemies were now going to take from his body and his back, by hounding and insulting him, what could not be raised from his property. Their aim was to have a man of great distinction locked in a prison among thieves of the night and footpads, to have him breathe his last in dark confinement, and then thrown out naked before the prison.262 That would be as scandalous for the city of Rome as for the Cornelian family, he concluded.

60. In response the praetor Terentius read out the bill of the Petillii, the decree of the senate and the verdict passed on Lucius Scipio. Unless the fine imposed by the sentence were paid into the treasury, said Terentius, he had no option but to order the convicted man arrested and hauled off to prison. A little later, after the tribunes had withdrawn to confer, Gaius Fannius declared that he and all his colleagues, apart from Gracchus, were agreed that the tribunes should not veto the praetor’s exercise of his authority. Tiberius Gracchus made a declaration that he did not veto the praetor’s collection of the fine prescribed by the sentence from the property of Lucius Scipio. But, he added, Lucius Scipio was a man who had vanquished the richest king in the world and advanced the rule of the Roman people to the farthest limits of the earth; he had put King Eumenes, the Rhodians, and many other cities of Asia under obligation through services rendered them
