When an appeal was made to them by Lucius Scipio, the tribunes of the plebs decreed as follows: if illness were adduced as the reason for the defendant’s failure to appear, their wish was that this reason be accepted and the trial date be held over by their colleagues.

One of the plebeian tribunes at this time was Tiberius Sempronius Gracchus and there was bad blood between him and Publius Scipio. When Gracchus refused to have his name added to his colleagues’ decree, everyone expected an even more severe judgment from him. Instead he made the following declaration. Lucius Scipio’s plea of sickness on his brother’s behalf he found satisfactory, he said, and he would not permit any accusation of Publius Scipio to proceed before the man returned to Rome; and even then, if Scipio appealed to him, he would help him avoid a court appearance. Through his achievements and the honors granted him by the Roman people, Publius Scipio had, by the agreement of gods and men, achieved such eminence that for him to stand as a defendant beneath the rostra and give ear to the abusive comments of young men was a greater disgrace for the people of Rome than for the man himself.
