BOOK XXXVIII

Spartan exiles joined their case and their deputation with those of the Achaeans. The leaders of the Achaean delegation were Diophanes and Lycortas,128 both of Megalopolis, who were opponents in public policy and who on this occasion, too, delivered speeches that had very few points of agreement. Diophanes advocated leaving the adjudication of everything to the senate: it was they who would best settle the disputes between the Achaeans and the Spartans, he said. Lycortas, following Philopoemen’s instructions, insisted that the Achaeans should have the right under the treaty and their own laws to put their decrees into effect and that the Romans should grant them unrestricted exercise of the freedom that they had themselves conferred on them. At that time the Achaean people had great influence with the Romans, but even so it was decided was that there should be no change in regard to the Spartans. However, so cryptic was the Roman answer that the Achaeans took it as permission to act in the case of Sparta, while the Spartans’ interpretation was that the Achaeans had not been given free rein in everything. The Achaeans were extreme and high-handed in exercising this power.

Philopoemen’s office was renewed.129 At the beginning of spring he mobilized an army, pitched camp in Spartan territory, and then sent envoys to demand the surrender of those responsible for the sedition, with a promise that their city-state would have peace if it complied and the guilty parties would receive no punishment without a hearing.
