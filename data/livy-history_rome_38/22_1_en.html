BOOK XXXVIII

With the consul’s permission the tyrant came into the camp the following day. His dress and retinue barely met the standard of a moderately wealthy man and his speech was submissive and humble as he played down his personal fortune and deplored the poverty of the cities under his control. In fact, however, in addition to Cibyra, Sylleum and the city called “ad Limnen”57 were also subject to him. From these he promised that he would—but almost as if doubting that he could—raise 25 talents, though this would be stripping his own and his subjects’ resources.

“No,” said the consul, “this charade cannot be tolerated any longer. It is not enough that you felt no embarrassment at staying away while using your envoys to toy with us—now, too, in our presence, you persist in the same impudence. Will 25 talents drain your kingdom? If you do not pay up 500 talents in three days you can expect to see plundering in your fields and a blockade on your city.” Though alarmed by this threat, Moagates persisted with his stubborn pretense of poverty. Gradually, by niggardly additions and over quibbles that he alternated with entreaties and crocodile tears, he was brought up to 100 talents, and 10,000 medimni58 of grain were also added. The total amount was collected within six days.

15. From Cibyra the army was led through the territory of the Sindensians, and pitched camp after crossing the River Caularis. The following day the column was led along the banks of the Marsh of Cabalitis, and halted at Madamprus.
