Manlius wanted to cross the Taurus range and was only with difficulty restrained by the entreaties of all the commissioners from his aim of putting to the test the prediction of disaster made in the Sibyline verses for those who passed those fateful bounds.198 He brought up his army even so and encamped almost on the very summit, where the waters divide. As he found no pretext for opening hostilities here, since the king’s people kept the peace, he had brought the army around against the Galatians, a tribe on which war was inflicted without senatorial authorization or a directive from the people.199 Who, they asked, had ever presumed to take such action on his own authority? The most recent wars had been with Antiochus, Philip, and Hannibal and the Carthaginians, they said. In every case there had been a resolution of the senate and a directive from the people; restitution had been formally demanded through emissaries; and finally men had been sent to declare war.200

“Which of these protocols was followed in your case, Gnaeus Manlius,” they continued, “to lead us to believe that that war of yours was one officially fought by the people of Rome and not your own private buccaneering? You were satisfied with your procedure, you say, so then you took your army directly against those people you had targeted as your enemy!
