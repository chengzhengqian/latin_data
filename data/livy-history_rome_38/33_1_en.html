20 Anticipating that the battle would entail long-range attacks on the enemy positions rather than hand-to-hand fighting, the consul had prepared a huge quantity of javelins, skirmishing spears and arrows, as well as lead balls and smallish stones that could be shot with a sling. Armed with this array of missiles he led his men toward Mt. Olympus and pitched camp about five miles short of it. The next day he advanced with Attalus and 400 cavalry to examine the natural features of the mountain and the position of the Gallic camp, but enemy cavalry in numbers twice his own poured from the camp and chased him off. In addition, a few of the fleeing Romans were killed and more wounded.

On the third day Manlius set off with all his troops to examine the position and because none of the enemy ventured beyond their defenses he rode safely around the mountain. He noticed that, to the south, the hills had a good covering of soil and a gentle gradient up to a certain point and that, to the north, there were sheer and almost perpendicular cliffs. He further observed that there were only three viable means of access, almost all others being impossible: one up the center of the mountain, where the surface had a layer of soil, and two difficult approaches, where the sun rises in winter and where it sets in summer.99 After taking note of these, Manlius encamped that day right at the foot of the mountain.
