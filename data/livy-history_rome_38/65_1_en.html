To King Eumenes the commissioners assigned, in Europe, the Chersonese and Lysimachia, and the strongholds, villages and lands that had been within the territory held by Antiochus; and in Asia they restored to him both Phrygias—the one called Phrygia-on-the-Hellespont and the other called Greater Phrygia—as well as Mysia (which King Prusias had filched from him), Lycaonia, Milyas and Lydia, and the cities of Tralles, Ephesus and Telmessus, which were specifically mentioned by name. As there was a dispute between Eumenes and the representatives of Antiochus over Pamphylia (because part of it lies west of the Taurus and part east of it) the whole matter was referred to the senate.

40. After issuing these terms and decrees, Manlius set off for the Hellespont with the ten commissioners and the entire army. There he called to him the chieftains of the Gauls and specified the terms under which they were to keep the peace with Eumenes. He informed them that they were to discontinue their practice of armed migrations and to remain within the boundaries of their lands. He next brought together ships from all along the coast, and also had Eumenes’ fleet brought there from Elaea by the king’s brother, Athenaeus,171 and with them transported all his troops over to Europe. Then, at the head of a column weighed down with plunder of all kinds, he proceeded by short stages through the Chersonese and established a base at Lysimachia in order to enter Thrace (the journey through which the men generally contemplated with dread) with his pack animals in as fresh and vigorous a condition as possible.
