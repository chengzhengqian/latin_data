Unassigned Fragments

28–9

Varro: In the circus, the place whence the horses are sent off at the start is now spoken of as ‘carceres’ (the barriers), while Naevius calls it ‘oppidum.’ . . . The poet wrote ‘oppidum’ because the barriers at one time had pinnacles and towers so as to look like a wall:—

When the dictator a takes his seat in the chariot, he is driven as far as the town.

Eating and drinking:

30 a–c

Paulus: Says Naevius—

The cook ate Neptune, Ceres, Venus too That had known Vulcan, b Liber too he swallowed, All at one go.

By Ceres he means bread, by Neptune fish, by Venus greens.

31

Paulus: ‘Bilbit’ is a verb formed because of its likeness to the sound which is made in a jar. Says Naevius—

The bottle guggles. 32

Nonius: ‘Guttur’ is of the neuter gender . . . masculine . . . in Naevius—

He gulped it in right to the back of his gullet.
  • aPossibly T. Manlius Torquatus, who was made dictator comitiorum ludorumque faciendorum, causa in 208 b.c.; or Q. Ogulnius Gallus, who was made dictator Latinarum feriarum causa in 257, in which case the fr. may belong to The Punic War; but the metre does not appear to be Saturnian. Cf. Cichor., R. St., 55–6. Possibly from a fabula togata. The word oppidum really means ob pedvm (πέδον), ‘on the ground.’
  • bHephaestus, husband of Aphrodite (Venus).
