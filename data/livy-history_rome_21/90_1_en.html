About twelve thousand were killed; nearly all the others, stripped of their weapons, dispersed far and wide throughout the countryside and fled to their homes. As for the Ausetani, now under siege, all that saved them was the winter weather that was plaguing their assailants. The blockade lasted thirty days, during which the snow hardly ever lay less than four feet deep; and its cover on the Roman mantlets and siege sheds was such that it alone was sufficient protection against the firebrands occasionally hurled by the enemy. When their leader Amusicus sought refuge with Hasdrubal, the Ausetani finally surrendered, agreeing to a payment of twenty talents of silver. The Romans then returned to their winter quarters at Tarraco.

62. In Rome or the city’s environs many prodigies occurred that winter or, rather, many prodigies were reported and too readily accepted, the usual experience once people’s minds have turned to superstition. They included the following:236 A freeborn six-month-old child had shouted “Triumph” in the vegetable market,237 and in the cattle market238 an ox had of its own accord climbed to the third story of a building and then thrown itself off when frightened by the alarmed reaction of the tenants. Glowing ship-like figures had appeared in the sky, and the temple of Hope in the vegetable market239 had been struck by lightening.
