Hannibal dug in not far away, but his elation with his cavalry’s victory was no greater than his concern about his shortage of supplies, which was every day becoming more acute as he passed through enemy territory where no provision had been made for replenishment. He therefore sent men to the village of Clastidium,190 where the Romans had stockpiled a large quantity of wheat. There, while the Carthaginians were preparing an assault, the prospect of a sellout emerged; and the garrison commander, Dasius of Brundisium, was bribed with a price that was certainly not high (four hundred gold pieces) and Clastidium was handed over to Hannibal. That was the granary of the Carthaginians while they remained at the Trebia. So that Hannibal could earn a reputation for clemency at the start of the campaign, no harsh treatment was inflicted on the prisoners from the surrendered garrison.

49. While the war on land had now halted at the Trebia, land and sea operations had meanwhile been conducted by the consul Sempronius—and some even before his arrival—around Sicily and the islands off the Italian coast.

Twenty quinqueremes with a thousand marines on board had been dispatched by the Carthaginians to conduct raids on the coastal areas of Italy; nine headed for the Lipara islands191 and eight for the island of Vulcan,192 while three were swept off course into the straits by rough seas. When these three were sighted from Messana, twelve ships were sent out to them by King Hiero of Syracuse,193 who happened to be in Messana at the time awaiting the arrival of the Roman consul.
