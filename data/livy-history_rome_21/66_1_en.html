46. There was nothing like as much enthusiasm among the Romans who, on top of everything else, were also terrified by recent portents. A wolf had entered the camp, savaged those that it encountered and itself escaped unharmed;179 and a swarm of bees had settled on a tree overhanging the general’s headquarters.180 After rites to avert the omens, Scipio set off with his cavalry and light-armed javelin throwers to get a close look at the enemy camp and the size and nature of his troops, and he came upon Hannibal, who had also himself gone forth with his cavalry to reconnoiter the surrounding area. At first, neither party could see the other; then the dust from so many men and horses on the move rose thicker, indicating the approach of the enemy. Both columns came to a halt and proceeded to prepare for battle.

Scipio positioned his javelin throwers and Gallic cavalry in front, with the Roman and the cream of the allied cavalry in reserve. Hannibal brought his bridled cavalry into the center and strengthened the wings with his Numidians. The battle cry had scarcely gone up when Scipio’s javelin throwers ran back through the supporting troops to the second line. The cavalry battle that followed remained even for some time; but then the infantry joined the fighting and this startled the horses.
