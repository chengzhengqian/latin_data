BOOK XXI

“Do I appear to you to have come upon him accidentally when I was trying to avoid engagement or rather to be dogging his footsteps, trying to provoke him and draw him into a decisive battle? I want to find out if the earth has suddenly produced Carthaginians of a different sort over these past twenty years,168 or if these are the same kind who fought at the Aegates Islands and whom you released from Eryx only after setting a price of eighteen denarii apiece on their heads. I want to see if this Hannibal really is, as he himself claims, on a par with Hercules on his travels,169 or, rather, has been left by his father as a mere tribute- and tax-payer, indeed a slave, of the Roman people. If the crime of Saguntum were not troubling the man’s mind, he would be giving some thought, if not to his defeated country, then at least to his home, his father and the treaties written down in Hamilcar’s hand.170 It was Hamilcar who withdrew the Carthaginian garrison from Eryx on our consul’s order, who accepted with rage and sorrow the harsh conditions imposed on the defeated Carthaginians, and who, on withdrawing from Sicily, agreed to pay tribute to the Roman people.

“And so, men, I should like you to fight not just with the spirit with which you usually face other enemies but with some resentment and anger, as though you are looking at your own slaves who are suddenly shouldering arms against you. When we blockaded them at Eryx, we could have starved them to death, the worst punishment to be inflicted on human beings.
