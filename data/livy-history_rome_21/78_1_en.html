BOOK XXI

54. Between the two armies was a stream, high banked on both sides and overgrown all around with marsh reeds and the shrubs and briers usually found covering wastelands. After riding around the area, and from careful inspection seeing for himself that it provided sufficient cover for concealing even cavalry, Hannibal said to his brother Mago: “This will be the spot for you to occupy. Choose a hundred men from all your infantry and a hundred from your cavalry, and come to me with them at first watch.206 Now it is time for food and rest.”

With that the meeting was adjourned. A little later Mago appeared with the men he had selected. “I see here the toughest of my men,” said Hannibal. “But to have the strength of numbers to match your courage, you must each chose nine like yourselves from your respective troops and maniples. Mago will show you the spot for your ambush—you face an enemy blind to such arts of war.”

Mago was then sent off with his thousand horsemen and thousand infantry,207 and at dawn Hannibal ordered his Numidian cavalry to cross the River Trebia, ride up to the gates of the enemy, and entice him out to battle by hurling spears at the sentry posts. Then, after provoking a confrontation, they were to draw him over the river by gradually ceding ground.

Such were the orders for the Numidians. The other infantry and cavalry officers were to tell all their men to take their meal and then await the signal, armed and with horses saddled.

  • 206It was Roman practice to divide the night into four watches, and, as often, Livy here applies Roman terminology to non-Romans.
  • 207There is a problem with the text here (see textual note), and Briscoe prefers to obelize.
