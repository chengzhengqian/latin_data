BOOK XXI

Sempronius, at the turbulent attack of the Numidians, first led out all his cavalry, supremely confident as he was in that branch of his forces, then six thousand infantry, and finally all his troops—he was eager to fight and had already decided what he was going to do. It happened to be the winter solstice and there was snowy weather in the country between the Alps and the Apennines, an area also subject to intense cold from nearby rivers and marshlands. In addition, men and horses had been hurriedly led out without first taking nourishment or any measures against the cold. They were chilled to the marrow, and the closer they approached the breeze coming from the river, the more intense became the cold blowing at them. But when they plunged into the water in pursuit of the fleeing Numidians—and, swollen by overnight rain, it came up to their chests—they all then felt their limbs go so numbingly cold, especially as they emerged, that they scarcely had the strength to hold their weapons; and in addition their strength was sapped by exhaustion and also, as the day progressed, by hunger.

55. Hannibal’s infantrymen had meanwhile lit fires before their tents; oil had been distributed among the companies for softening up the limbs; and they had taken a leisurely meal. When news arrived that the enemy had crossed the river, they took up their weapons, in good form mentally and physically, and went forward to the battle.

Hannibal deployed his Balearic208 and other light-armed troops, some eight thousand men, before the standards, and behind them he then placed his heavier infantry, which represented the real strength and kernel of his force. On the wings he set ten thousand cavalry and he placed his elephants, separated into two groups, beside the two wings.
