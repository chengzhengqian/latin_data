About two thousand infantrymen and two hundred cavalry killed the sentries at the gates and deserted to Hannibal.186 The Carthaginian welcomed them with kind words and, arousing in them expectations of rich rewards,187 sent them off to their various communities to enlist the support of their compatriots.

Scipio thought that the coup portended wholesale insurrection by the Gauls who, becoming infected by this villainous behavior, would now rush to arms in a fit of madness; and so, though still in pain from his wound, he nevertheless slipped quietly away with his army toward the River Trebia at the fourth watch of the following night188 and moved his camp to a higher elevation where the hills would better obstruct cavalry. His attempted concealment was less successful than at the Ticinus; and Hannibal first sent the Numidians in pursuit and then the entire cavalry, and he would certainly have created havoc at the rear of the Roman column had not the Numidians, in their greed for plunder, veered off into the now-unmanned Roman camp. There, while they wasted time rifling through every corner of the camp without finding any prize worth the delay, their enemy slipped through their fingers. The Numidians sighted the Romans only when they had already crossed the Trebia189 and were measuring off their camp, but they did kill a few stragglers whom they cut off on their side of the river.
