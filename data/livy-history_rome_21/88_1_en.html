Command of the area north of the Ebro lay with Hanno231 (Hannibal had left him behind to safeguard the region). Believing he should face the Romans before everything was taken away from him, he now encamped in sight of the enemy and led his men out for battle. The Roman commander also thought there should be no delaying an engagement: he knew that he had to fight Hanno and Hasdrubal, and he preferred to take them on one at a time rather than both together. The engagement proved no great struggle. Six thousand of the enemy were cut down and two thousand captured together with the camp garrison—for the camp was stormed and the commander himself taken prisoner, along with several chieftains. Cissis,232 a town close to the camp, was also taken by storm. But the booty from the town consisted of items of little value: some primitive furniture and some worthless slaves. The camp, however, enriched the soldiers; it was the base not only of the army that had been defeated but also of the one campaigning in Italy with Hannibal, almost all its valuables having been left south of the Pyrenees so that the soldiers would not have heavy baggage to carry.
