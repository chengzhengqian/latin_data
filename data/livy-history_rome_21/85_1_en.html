BOOK XXI

ans.220 As he crossed the Apennines, he was overtaken by a storm so severe as to be almost worse than the foul weather of the Alps. A mixture of rain and wind blew straight into the men’s faces; and at first they halted because they either had to drop their weapons or, if they struggled on against it, would be spun round by the blast and bowled over. Then, when it began to stifle them and not allow them to catch their breath, they sat for a while with their backs to the wind. There came next a huge crash from the heavens, and flashes of lightening amid frightening peals of thunder. Deafened and blinded, they were all paralyzed with fear. Finally, there was a torrential downpour and, when the force of the wind rose all the more, pitching camp in the very spot where they had been caught seemed unavoidable.

That, however, proved to be the start of fresh problems. The men could not unfold or set up anything,221 and what was set up would not stay up since the wind tore everything apart and swept it away. Soon the water whipped up by the wind222 froze above the frigid mountaintops, and so much sleet did this bring down that the men dropped everything and fell to the ground, buried under their shelters rather than protected by them. Temperatures so low followed that anyone trying to get up or extricate himself from that sad mess of men and pack animals was long unable do so because, their muscles being numb with cold, they could scarcely bend their joints. Then, when, finally shaking themselves, they began to move about and pull themselves together and a few fires started to appear, they all helplessly looked for assistance from each other.
