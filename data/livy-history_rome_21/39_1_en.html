The Gauls attempted resistance to both but were driven back, and they then forced a passage wherever a way seemed most open and scattered and fled in panic hither and thither to their villages. Hannibal ferried over the rest of his troops at a leisurely pace, unconcerned now about attacks from the Gauls, and pitched camp.

I believe there were various ideas for bringing over the elephants; at least there are varying accounts of how it was done. Some claim that when the elephants had been herded together on the bank, the most fractious one among them was goaded to anger by its driver, who then ran off into the water; and when the beast followed him as he swam off it drew the rest of the herd along. Although every animal feared the deep water as it left the shallows, the very force of the current carried it to the other bank. The more reliable version, however, is that they were brought over on rafts.114 This would have appeared the safer plan before the event and, in the event, it also appears the more plausible one. They pushed a single raft, two hundred feet long and fifty feet wide, into the river from the shore and secured it with a number of strong ropes attached to the bank upstream so it would not drift down with the current. On this they laid a carpet of soil to form a sort of bridge so that the animals would confidently walk on it as on dry land. A second raft of the same width and a hundred feet in length, and suitable for crossing the river, was fastened to it. Then the elephants were driven over the immobile raft, females first, as though it were a road.
