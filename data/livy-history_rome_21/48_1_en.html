Frightful as the scene was, Hannibal none the less halted for a short while and held back his men for fear of only worsening the confusion and panic. Then, when he saw that a break in the column was occurring and that if the baggage were lost he faced the risk of having wasted his time in bringing the troops through safely, he swooped down from his higher position and with his assault scattered the enemy, but also increased the commotion among his own men. That commotion, however, was calmed in an instant when the mountain men fled and the paths were left clear, and soon all were brought through not simply unmolested but almost in silence.

After this Hannibal took the stronghold, which served as the regional capital, as well the villages around it, and with the food and livestock that was captured there he fed his army for three days. And because he now had little trouble with the mountain men, who were cowed by that initial defeat, or with the terrain, he made considerable progress during the next three days.
