Thus, the more fiercely the two fought, and the more densely packed they were, the greater the number of wounds inflicted since no spear fell ineffectually amid arms and bodies.38

The Saguntines had a weapon for throwing called a phalarica, which had a shaft of fir wood that was entirely cylindrical except toward the tip where its iron head protruded. This part, cube-shaped as on a javelin, they would wrap with tow and smear with pitch.39 It had an iron head three feet long, and so able to pass through a man’s body as well as his shield. But even if it stuck in the shield and did not penetrate the body it still caused immense alarm since the weapon was discharged with its center set alight and the flames, further magnified by the forward motion, then made the soldier drop his shield and left him exposed to oncoming weapons.

9. The battle long hung in the balance and the Saguntines’ confidence had soared because their resistance was more effective than they could have hoped, while the Carthaginians’ failure to secure victory had left them feeling beaten. Then the townspeople suddenly raised a shout, chased the enemy back to where the wall had collapsed, dislodged them from there in confusion and panic, and finally drove them back to their camp in disordered flight.
