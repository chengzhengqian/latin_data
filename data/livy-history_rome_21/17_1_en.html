On the two sides the fortifying and fighting went on with maximum intensity; but the Saguntines, focusing on defending the interior sections, were every day making their city smaller. At the same time there was a growing shortage of everything because of the protracted siege, and prospects of assistance from without were becoming dimmer since the Romans (their only hope) were so far away and everything around was in enemy hands. The townspeople’s flagging spirits were, however, briefly raised by Hannibal’s suddenly marching off against the Oretani and Carpetani.49 The two peoples, galled by the severity of the troop levies among them, had seized his recruiting officers and raised fears of insurrection, but they were taken by surprise by Hannibal’s speed and abandoned the fight they had started.

Nor was the blockade of Saguntum any less vigorous under Maharbal50 son of Himilco (Hannibal had left him in command), who operated with such energy that neither his fellow citizens nor the enemy felt the commander’s absence. He fought a number of successful engagements and also shattered a section of the wall with three battering rams, presenting Hannibal on his return with a view of the whole area strewn with fresh rubble. The Carthaginian troops were therefore immediately led against the citadel itself, a furious engagement ensued with heavy losses on both sides, and part of the citadel was captured.
