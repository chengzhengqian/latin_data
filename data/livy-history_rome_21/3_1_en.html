Hasdrubal’s administration was characterized by diplomacy rather than aggression: he extended the authority of Carthage more by fostering relations with chieftains and enlisting the support of new tribes through befriending their leaders than by employing armed force. However, peace afforded him no more security: a barbarian, incensed over Hasdrubal’s execution of his master, assassinated him in the open. When he was apprehended by bystanders, the man’s expression remained that of one who had made good his escape, and even under torture his facial expression was such that he actually appeared to be smiling, his joy surmounting his suffering.

Because Hasdrubal had possessed an amazing talent for winning over tribes and bringing them under his governance, the Roman people had renewed their treaty with him on condition that the limit of the authority of each would be the River Ebro and that the Saguntines, lying between the domains of the two peoples, would have their independence preserved.11
