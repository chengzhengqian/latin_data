BOOK XXI

4. Sent to Spain, Hannibal attracted the attention of the entire army immediately upon his arrival. The older soldiers thought a young Hamilcar had been brought back to them; they saw that same dynamism in his expression, the same forcefulness in his eyes, the same facial expression and features. Then he soon brought it to pass that his father counted for little in winning him support. Never was the one character so amenable to the two extremes of obedience and command. As a result one would have found it hard to tell whether he was better liked by the commander or by the army. There was no one whom Hasdrubal preferred to put in charge when a gallant or enterprising feat was called for, and there was no other officer under whom the rank and file had more confidence and courage.

Hannibal15 was possessed of enormous daring in facing dangers, and enormous resourcefulness in the midst of the dangers themselves. By no hardship could he be physically exhausted or mentally cowed. He was able to withstand heat and cold alike; his eating and drinking were determined by the requirements of nature, not pleasure. His times for being awake and asleep were not set by day or night. To sleep was given only the time left after discharging his duties and it was not brought on by a soft bed or silence—many often observed him covered with a soldier’s cloak, lying on the ground amid the sentry posts and pickets. In clothing he did not stand out from his comrades in any way, but his weapons and horses drew attention. Of cavalrymen and infantrymen he was by far the best; the first to enter battle, he was the last to leave after battle was joined.
