Not far from Tarraco he encountered their marines and crews wandering aimlessly through the countryside (because success almost inevitably leads to carelessness) and sending out his cavalry in every direction against them he drove them back to their ships, producing great carnage and greater flight. Not daring to linger in the area any longer for fear of being overtaken by Scipio, he then fell back across the Ebro. At the news of his new enemy, Scipio lost no time in bringing his column there, but then, after punishing a few of the ships’ officers and leaving a small garrison at Tarraco, he returned to Emporiae with his fleet.

Scarcely had he left when Hasdrubal reappeared, pushed the Ilergetes,233 a tribe that had given hostages to Scipio, into insurrection and then, together with the soldiers from that very tribe, laid waste the farmlands of allies that had remained loyal to Rome. When Scipio was brought out from his winter quarters by this, Hasdrubal once more withdrew from all the territory north of the Ebro. Scipio launched a campaign against the Ilergetes, now abandoned by the man responsible for their uprising, drove them all into the city of Atanagrum,234 which was the tribal capital of that people, and then blockaded it. In a matter of days he took the Ilergetes back under his authority and control, exacting from them a greater number of hostages than he had on the earlier occasion and imposing a fine on them as well.
