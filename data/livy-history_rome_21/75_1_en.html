BOOK XXI

The land that lies between the Trebia and the Po was then inhabited by Gauls who, as the two superpowers came into conflict, vacillated in their support, quite clearly with an eye to gaining the victor’s favor. The Romans were happy enough with this, provided they took no action, but the Carthaginian leader was very unhappy—he had come to liberate the Gauls, he kept saying, and at their request. In his anger over this, and at the same time to provide his men with spoils, he commanded two thousand infantry and a thousand cavalry (Numidians mostly, but with some Gauls also among them) to systematically pillage Gallic farmlands right up to the banks of the Po. The Gauls, who to that point had been ambivalent, were now, with no help at hand, driven to turn from the perpetrators of the wrong done to them to those they hoped would avenge it. They sent a deputation to the consuls and begged for Roman aid for a land suffering damage through its inhabitants being all too loyal to the Romans.

Cornelius liked neither the reason for taking action nor the timing: he was wary of that people first because of their many acts of treachery and then also—even if the memory of other things had been blurred by the passage of time—because of the recent duplicity of the Boii.202 Sempronius, on the other hand, was of the opinion that the strongest bond for maintaining allies’ loyalty was forged by defending the first who needed help. Then, while his colleague hesitated, he sent his cavalry across the Trebia together with some thousand javelin-throwing infantry to defend the Gallic farmlands.
