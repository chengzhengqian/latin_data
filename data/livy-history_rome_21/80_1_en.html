BOOK XXI

When the Roman cavalry, giving chase in disorder, were unexpectedly confronted by the Numidians, who suddenly made a stand, the consul called them back by signaling the retreat and stationed them on the flanks of his infantry. There were eighteen thousand Romans and twenty thousand Latin allies, plus some auxiliary forces from the Cenomani (the one Gallic tribe that had remained loyal).209 Such were the troops with which they met in battle.

The action was initiated by the Balearic troops, but when the Roman legions confronted them with their superior strength, the light-armed forces were swiftly withdrawn to the wings, which had the immediate effect of putting the Roman cavalry under pressure. Even as it was, the cavalry could on its own barely withstand the Carthaginian cavalry—four thousand men were facing ten thousand, and they were exhausted and facing mostly fresh troops—and now they were also buried under a virtual cloud of javelins hurled by the Balearic forces. Furthermore, the elephants towering at the extremities of the wings caused widespread flight, especially among the horses, which were terrified not just by the sight of them but also by their unfamiliar smell.210

In the case of the infantry the engagement was more even, in terms of courage rather than strength: the Carthaginians had come into battle with strength unimpaired, having shortly before taken food and rest; the Romans, by contrast, were hungry and tired, their limbs stiff and numb with cold. Even so, their courage would have enabled them to make a stand—had their fight been against the infantry alone.
