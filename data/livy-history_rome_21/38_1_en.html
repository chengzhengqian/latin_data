Setting off the next day, they indicated with a smoke signal from an elevated point that they had made the crossing and were not far off; and when he received the signal Hannibal, not to miss his opportunity, gave the order to cross. The infantry already had boats prepared and equipped; the cavalry, mostly because of their horses, †swimming†.112 A line of the larger vessels, crossing upstream to absorb the force of the current, provided smooth water for the boats traversing below them. Most of the horses were swimming and being pulled along by their reins from the boats’ sterns, apart from those that they had set on board saddled and bridled to be ready for the cavalry to use as soon as they set foot on the bank.

28. The Gauls rushed to confront them on the bank with discordant yells and their customary war chant, shaking their shields above their heads and brandishing spears in their right hands.113 But the size of the flotilla facing them, together with the loud roar of the river, began to unnerve them, as did the confused shouting of the sailors and soldiers, both those trying to cope with the river current and those who were from the far bank cheering on their comrades making the crossing. They were startled enough by the commotion before them, but then a more terrifying uproar arose to their rear, where their camp had been taken by Hanno.
