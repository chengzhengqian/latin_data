Publius Cornelius, after raising a new legion to replace the one that had been sent with the praetor, also set out from the city with sixty warships. Skirting Etruria and Liguria, and then the mountains of the Salluvii,107 he reached Massilia and encamped at the closest mouth of the Rhone (the river divides and runs to the sea by several different channels), finding it hard to believe that Hannibal had crossed the Pyrenees. When he realized that he was now planning also to cross the Rhone, he was unsure where to meet him, his men being still insufficiently recovered from the rigors of the sea voyage; and in the meantime he sent out three hundred handpicked horsemen with Massiliot guides and Gallic auxiliaries to reconnoiter the area thoroughly and observe the enemy at a safe remove.

Hannibal had by now reached the lands of the Volcae—a powerful tribe—having established peaceful relations with all the others by intimidation or bribery. The Volcae live on both banks of the Rhone;108 however, lacking confidence in their ability to keep the Carthaginian away from the west bank, they had ferried almost all their people across the Rhone so as to have the river as a barrier, and they were proceeding to secure the eastern bank with their armed forces.
