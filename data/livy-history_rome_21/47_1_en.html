33. At first light camp was struck and the rest of the army began to advance. By now the mountain men, having been given the signal, were leaving their strongholds and converging on their customary post when, above their heads, they suddenly caught sight of some of their enemy occupying the vantage point that had been theirs, and others coming along the road. Perceiving and understanding the two things together left them momentarily pinned motionless to the spot. Then, observing the disarray in the pass, with the column thrown into disorder by its own difficulties, and in particular the horses panic-stricken, they thought that whatever fright they themselves could add would be enough to finish off their enemy; and after †overturning the rocks closest to them†137 they came charging down together, familiar as they were with the impassable and trackless terrain. The Carthaginians were then truly under pressure, both from the enemy and the difficulty of the ground, and as they each struggled to be the first out of harm’s way there was more fighting among themselves than with the enemy. It was the horses that posed the greatest danger for the column. Startled by the confused shouting, which the woods and echoing ravines amplified, they reared in panic, and those that chanced to be struck or wounded became so frantic that they inflicted severe damage simultaneously on the men and on all kinds of baggage.
