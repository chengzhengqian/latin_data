They think they have the right to decide with whom we may be at war and with whom at peace. They confine and restrict us, using mountains and rivers as boundaries that we are not to cross, and they do not themselves observe those boundaries that they have defined.

“‘Do not cross the Ebro!’ they say. ‘Have no contact with the people of Saguntum!’ ‘Do not take a step anywhere!’ Is it not enough that you have taken my oldest provinces, Sicily and Sardinia? Are you taking the Spanish provinces, too? And if I cede these as well, will you cross to Africa? Will cross? I say ‘have crossed.’ They have sent out this year’s two consuls, one to Africa and the other to Spain. We are left nothing anywhere except what we shall have defended with our weapons. The people who can be timid and cowardly are those who have a refuge behind them, those with lands and farms to receive them as they withdraw along safe and peaceful routes.176 But you, you must be men of courage. You must set aside as absolutely hopeless any result between victory and death and either win or, if your fortunes waver, meet your end in battle rather than flight. If this is firmly fixed in the minds of all, then—I shall say it again—you have won. Man has been given by the immortal gods no sharper incentive to conquer than indifference to death.”
