BOOK XXI

Alco and the Spaniard Alorcus. Alco, thinking he could achieve something by entreaty, crossed over to Hannibal at night without the knowledge of the Saguntine people; but as tears had no effect and the conditions offered were severe, coming as they did from an exasperated victor, he became a deserter rather than spokesman and remained with the enemy, declaring that anyone who negotiated peace on such terms faced certain death. The Carthaginian demands were that they make restitution to the Turdetani, surrender all their gold and silver, and leave the city with a single article of clothing to settle wherever the Carthaginian decreed. Alco said that the Saguntines would not accept such peace terms, but Alorcus, asserting that when everything else is crushed the spirit is also crushed, engaged to broker a peace accord on the terms (he was then a soldier of Hannibal’s but was officially regarded as a friend and foreign representative51 of the people of Saguntum).

Alorcus openly surrendered his weapon to the sentries, passed the defense works and was at his own request escorted to the Saguntine general. A horde of people of every class immediately converged on the spot, but the crowd was pushed aside and Alorcus was granted an audience with the senate. His address went as follows:

13. “Your fellow citizen Alco came to Hannibal to sue for peace. Had he also brought Hannibal’s peace terms back to you, this mission of mine would have been unnecessary, a mission on which I have come to you neither as Hannibal’s representative nor as a deserter.
