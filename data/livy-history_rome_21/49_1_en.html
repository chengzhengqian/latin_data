Hannibal was polite in his response, thinking he should neither put blind faith in them nor spurn the offer, in case rejection made them openly hostile. He accepted the hostages they were giving him and availed himself of the provisions, which the spokesmen had personally brought down to the road; but he followed their guides with his column carefully ordered, by no means assuming he was in friendly country.

The vanguard comprised elephants and cavalry; Hannibal himself brought up the rear with the main body of the infantry, anxiously looking about in all directions. When they reached a narrowing of the road, overlooked on one side by a mountain ridge, the barbarians rose up from their hiding places everywhere. They attacked the Carthaginians front and rear, engaged them hand to hand and from a distance, and rolled huge rocks down on the column. An enormous body of men proceeded to bear down on the rear. The infantry wheeled round to face them, leaving no doubt that but for the reinforcement provided to the end of the column there would have been a calamitous defeat in that defile. Even as it was they faced extreme peril and were almost destroyed. For while Hannibal hesitated to send his division139 down into the defile—that would have meant leaving the infantry without the support in the rear that he was himself providing for the cavalry—the mountain men attacked from the side, cut the column in half, and occupied the road.
