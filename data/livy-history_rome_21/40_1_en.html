The elephants showed no real agitation while they were driven over what appeared to be a bridge attached to land; the first signs of disquiet came only when the raft was disconnected from the other115 and the animals were swept into deep water. They then began jostling each other as those on the outside edged back from the water, and they caused some panic until fear itself calmed them down when they looked around at the water about them. Some, in a frenzy, even fell into the river. Their riders were thrown off but the animals, kept steady by their own weight, made it ashore by feeling cautiously for the shallower water with their feet.116

29. While the elephants were being ferried over Hannibal had meanwhile sent five hundred Numidian riders to the Roman camp to investigate the position and strength of his enemy’s forces and what their plans were. This cavalry squadron was met by the three hundred Roman horsemen who, as stated earlier,117 had been sent out from the mouth of the Rhone. The ensuing battle was more ferocious than might be expected from the number of combatants involved. For apart from many being wounded on both sides fatalities were also about equal; and it was the panic-stricken flight of the Numidians that ceded victory to a Roman side that was by then quite exhausted.
