Romans. ‘Well, then, are we to surrender Hannibal?’ someone will say. I am aware that my influence in his case is slight because of my differences with his father. But just as I was pleased with Hamilcar’s death because, if he were alive, we would already be at war with the Romans, so I hate and abhor this young man as the fury that is firing this conflict. Nor is it simply a matter of his having to be surrendered to atone for the broken treaty; even if no one is demanding his surrender, he should be hauled off to the remotest limits of sea and land—banished to some place from which his name or news of him could not reach us and where he could not upset the tranquility our nation now enjoys. This is my proposal: ambassadors should immediately be sent to Rome to make amends to the senate, and others should be sent to tell Hannibal to withdraw his army from Saguntum and to surrender Hannibal himself to the Romans in accordance with the treaty. I further propose that a third delegation be sent to compensate the people of Saguntum.”

11. When Hanno had wound up his speech, there was no need for anyone at all to debate him—so far was the senate in support of Hannibal, almost to a man. Hanno, they claimed, had been even more vitriolic in his remarks than Valerius Flaccus, the Roman envoy.46 The reply then given to the Roman ambassadors was that the war had been started by the Saguntines, not Hannibal, and that the Roman people were being unjust if they held Saguntum in higher regard than their time-honored alliance with the Carthaginians.
