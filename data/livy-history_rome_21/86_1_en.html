59. Descending from the Apennines Hannibal moved camp back toward Placentia and halted after advancing about ten miles. The next day he led against his enemy twelve thousand infantry and five thousand cavalry; and the consul Sempronius—he had by now returned from Rome—did not refuse battle.

That day there was a distance of three miles between the two camps; on the next the sides engaged with great spirit and fluctuating results. In the first clash, the Romans were so clearly superior that they not only triumphed in the field but after driving back the enemy chased him to his camp, and soon after also attacked the camp. Hannibal stationed a few defenders on the rampart and in the gateways; the rest he withdrew en masse into the center of the camp, ordering them to be on the alert for the signal to counterattack.

It was already about the ninth hour of the day,224 when the Roman commander, after wearing out his men to no purpose and having no prospect of taking the camp, signaled the recall. When Hannibal heard it and saw that the fighting had slackened and the Romans had withdrawn from his camp, he suddenly sent out his cavalry from right and left against his enemy, and he himself burst from the center of the camp with his strongest infantry. Seldom would there have been a battle more savage and more remarkable for losses on both sides had daylight allowed it to be further prolonged; but night broke off an engagement that had flared up with enormous ferocity.
