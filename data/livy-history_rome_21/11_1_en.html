8. It was then a siege rather than direct assault for a few days to give the general’s wound time to heal, during which time there was a lull in the fighting but no letup in the construction of siege works and fortifications. And so hostilities resumed with greater determination than before, and the siege sheds began to be advanced and rams brought up in more locations despite a number of places scarcely accommodating such operations. The Carthaginians had abundant manpower—they are reasonably estimated to have had some one hundred and fifty thousand under arms37—while the townspeople, who thus began to be drawn off in different directions to defend and patrol all points, had insufficient resources for the fight.

And so the walls were now being pounded by the rams and many sections had been damaged. One section where there was a continual line of breaches had laid bare the city; three towers in a row and the wall running between them collapsed with a deafening roar. With that collapse the Carthaginians had believed the town to be captured, but there the two sides rushed into the fray just as if the wall had been offering both of them protection. It was nothing like the scrappy fighting that usually arises during assaults on cities, when one side grasps an opportunity; rather, two regular fighting lines had taken up a stand in what was virtually an open field between the collapsed wall and the city buildings a short distance away.
