Because they could see they would not be engaging men caught off guard, the Carthaginians kept their distance from the harbor until daylight, using the time to lower their sails and ready the fleet for action. When dawn came they backed water into the open sea to allow room for the engagement and ensure that their enemy’s ships had a clear passage from the harbor. The Romans did not refuse battle, either; and they drew confidence from the memory of their past successes in those very waters196 and from the numbers and courage of their men.

50. When they got out to sea, the Romans wanted to join battle and fight at close quarters. The Carthaginians, by contrast, wanted to maneuver, to use tactics rather than brute force, and to make it a battle of ships rather than of men and weapons. As their fleet was well manned with crews but short of marines, any time a ship was grappled they had nothing like the same number of soldiers fighting from it. When this was noticed, the Romans’ numerical advantage raised their morale and the Carthaginians’ deficiency lowered theirs. Seven Carthaginian vessels were immediately surrounded and the rest took flight. There were seventeen hundred marines and sailors in the captured ships, including three Carthaginian noblemen. The Roman fleet returned to port intact; one ship alone was holed, but that, too, made it home itself and returned to port.
