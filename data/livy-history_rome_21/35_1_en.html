At that time woods surrounded the road, the area being mostly uncultivated. There, having set off without reconnoitering, Manlius fell into an ambush and after heavy losses only with difficulty emerged into open country. There he established a fortified camp and, because the Gauls lacked the confidence to attack it, the morale of Manlius’ men rose again, though it was well enough known that about five hundred had fallen. The march then recommenced, and while the column was being led through open terrain there was no sign of the enemy. When it entered woodlands again, the Gauls attacked the rearmost and, in the great confusion and general panic that followed, killed seven hundred men and carried off six standards. An end to the intimidating pressure of the Gauls and the Romans’ fear came only when they emerged from the trackless, impenetrable woods. In the open country after that the Romans easily defended their column as they advanced to Tannetum, a village close to the Po.104 There they protected themselves against the daily increasing numbers of the enemy with a temporary fortification, by bringing provisions in by river, and also with help from the Brixian Gauls.105
