The refugees from Hermandica then joined forces with exiles of the Olcades, the tribe that had been crushed the previous summer. They roused the Carpetani24 to action, launched an attack on Hannibal not far from the River Tagus25 as he was returning from the Vaccaei, and threw his column, which was heavily burdened with plunder, into confusion.

Hannibal refused to engage, and after pitching camp on the bank he forded the river as soon as all was calm and silent on the enemy’s side. He then extended his entrenchment in such a way that the enemy would have room to cross, for he determined to attack them as they made their way over.26 He ordered the cavalry to attack as soon as they saw that they had entered the water and their column was preoccupied with the crossing, and he deployed his elephants (forty in number) on the bank. With supplementary detachments from the Olcades and Vaccaei, the Carpetani were one hundred thousand strong, an unbeatable army were it a battle fought on a level field. They were ferocious by nature and confident in their superior numbers; and as they thought the enemy had fallen back from fear they assumed that all that stood in the way of victory was that a river lay between them.
