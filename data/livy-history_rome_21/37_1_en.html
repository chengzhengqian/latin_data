Thus a huge number of ships and boats, rough hewn for local use, was assembled; and the Gauls then took the lead in hollowing out new vessels from individual trees. After that even the soldiers themselves, encouraged by the plentiful supply of wood and the ease of the work, also began hurriedly constructing unsightly tubs for transporting them and their equipment over the water, their only concern being that these could float on water and carry a load.

27. All was now ready for the crossing109 but they were daunted by the enemy cavalry and infantry occupying all of far bank. To dislodge them, Hannibal ordered Hanno son of Bomilcar to take a division of the troops, Spaniards for the most part, and at the first watch of the night110 to make a one-day’s journey upstream. As soon as possible, Hanno was to cross the river as unobtrusively as he could and at the appropriate moment lead the column around to attack the enemy from the rear. The Gauls assigned to him as guides for this informed Hanno that the river flowed around a small island about twenty-five miles upstream111 and, being broader where it divided, it was also less deep and so made a crossing possible.

At that spot wood was swiftly cut and rafts constructed on which horses, men and other cargo could be ferried over. The Spaniards had no difficulty in stuffing their clothes into skins, placing their shields over them, and swimming across the river lying flat on them.
