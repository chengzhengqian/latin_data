Although the Gauls had been repeatedly told that the war’s objective was Italy, there was a rumor that the Spaniards beyond the Pyrenees had been reduced by force and that powerful garrisons had been imposed on them: and from fear of being subjugated themselves a number of tribes took up arms in panic and converged on Ruscino.96 When news of this was brought to Hannibal, he was more fearful of delay than a fight and sent spokesmen to their chieftains to say that he wished to speak to them in person; either they could come closer to Iliberri or he could advance to Ruscino so that, if they were in close proximity, a meeting could be more easily arranged. For, he said, he would gladly welcome them into his camp and would not himself hesitate to go to them; he had come to Gaul as a friend not an enemy, and would not draw his sword—the Gauls permitting—before reaching Italy. Such was Hannibal’s communication through his messengers; but when the Gallic chieftains actually showed no reluctance in moving camp immediately to Iliberri and coming to the Carthaginian, they fell prey to his gifts and let his army pass quite peacefully through their territory, past the town of Ruscino.
