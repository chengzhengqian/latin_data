Rain mixed with snow, and cold of unbearable intensity, took the lives of many men and pack animals, and of almost all the elephants.212

The River Trebia marked the end of the Carthaginians’ pursuit of their enemy, and they returned to camp so numb with cold as to barely feel the joy of victory. Thus when, the following night, the garrison of the Roman camp and all <the soldiers> remaining after the defeat <. . .>213 were crossing the Trebia on rafts, they either heard nothing in the deafening rain or, unable to stir from exhaustion and their wounds, simply pretended to hear nothing. And while the Carthaginians remained inactive the army was led in silence to Placentia by the consul Scipio and, after crossing the Po, on to Cremona,214 so the one colony should not be burdened with wintering two armies.

57. Such was the panic brought to Rome by this disaster215 that people believed that the enemy would now march on the city of Rome and that they had no hope and no means of staving off an attack on their gates and walls. One consul had been defeated at the Ticinus, they said, and the other recalled from Sicily.
