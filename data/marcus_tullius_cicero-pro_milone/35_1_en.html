He entrusted himself not merely to the people, but also to the Senate; not merely to the Senate, but to the public guards and armies; and not merely to these, but to the discretion of the manb to whom the Senate had entrusted the whole commonwealth, the whole man-power of Italy, and all the arms of the Roman people; and you may be quite sure that my client would never have put himself at his disposal had he not had confidence in his own cause, especially seeing that the man in question was one who heard everything, apprehended great dangers, suspected much and believed not a little. Great is the power of conscience, gentlemen, great for bliss or for bane; it makes the innocent fearless, while it haunts the sinner with the ever-present vision of retribution.

Nor indeed is it without definite reason that Milo’s62 cause has ever been favourably viewed by the Senate; for, as highly intelligent men, they saw the reasonableness of his conduct, the imperturbability of his spirit, and the consistency of his defence. Or have you forgotten, gentlemen, when the news of Clodius’s death had but just reached us, the remarks and surmises not only of Milo’s enemies but even of several persons who were imperfectly informed?
