Agricola

Agricola pushed headlong, both by his own qualities and by the faults of others, to that very prominence which he had tried to avoid.

42. The year was now at hand for him to draw lots between the governorship of Africa and Asia;1 but Civica had just been executed, and Agricola’s discretion was as ready as the Emperor’s precedents. He was approached by certain confidants of the Imperial mind, who were to ask of their own motion whether he would take a province. Their first step showed some finesse. They extolled peace and quiet: a little while and they were offering their own services to support his excuse: finally, forgoing further mystery, they dragged him to Domitian with mingled advice and warning. The Emperor with ready hypocrisy assumed an official air, listened to the petition “to be excused,” granted it, and permitted himself to be thanked for it: the sinister favour brought him no blushes. As for the salary, however, usually offered to a proconsul, and in some cases conceded by the Emperor’s personal intervention, he did not give it to Agricola: either he was offended that it was not asked for, or he was self-conscious, and did not wish it to appear that he had purchased the decision, which was really due to his own prohibition.

It is a principle of human nature to hate those whom you have injured: nevertheless Domitian though by nature of a violent temper and unrelenting in proportion to his secretiveness, was pacified by the moderation and discretion of Agricola, in whom was no truculence, no fatuous parade of independence, to invite renown and ruin.
