At Reate there was a rain of stones. At Cumae the Apollo on the citadel shed tears for three days and three nights. In the city of Rome two temple-attendants announced portents; one, that a crested snake had been seen by many people in the temple of Fortune; the other announced two different portents in the temple of Fortune the First-Born which is on the hill,1 that a palm had sprung up in the courtyard and that during the day blood had rained down. Two portents were not treated as public matters, the one because it took place in a privately-owned spot—Titus Marcius Figulus reported that a palm had sprung up in his catch-basin2—the other because it occurred in a non-Roman place3—at Fregellae, in the house of Lucius Atreus, a spear which he had bought for his son’s service in the army was said to have blazed during the day for more than two hours in such a way that the fire consumed none of it.
