His crowning example of disgrace is the emperor Nero (211–30). After a few more negative examples, the poem draws to a close with a catalogue of people of humble origins who behaved more courageously and patriotically than the aristocrats (231–68). Finally, Juvenal produces the clinching argument in a wonderful reductio ad absurdum: there is no point in relying on pedigrees because everyone is ultimately descended from the herdsmen and the criminals in Romulus’ asylum (269–75). This new Juvenal is clearly a nihilist with an acute sense of humour.
