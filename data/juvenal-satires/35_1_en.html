I’m impressed by the genius’ plan, if by such a splendid gift he stole first place in the will of a childless old man or (an even better ploy) sent it to a grand mistress who rides in her closed cavern with its wide windows. Don’t expect anything like that. He bought it for himself! We witness many things that poor mean Apicius 6 didn’t do. Did you, Crispinus—who in the old days were kitted out for work in your native papyrus clothes—did you pay this much, this much for scales? The fisherman could perhaps have been bought for less than his fish. That’s the price of land in the provinces—and Apulia offers better value still. What kind of banquets are we to think the emperor himself guzzled at that time, when so many thousand sesterces—a tiny fraction, a side dish from an ordinary dinner—were belched up by the clown-in-purple of the Great Palace? 7 He’s now Leader of the Cavalry, 8 but he used to sell sprats (his compatriots) from a damaged cargo in a loud voice.

Commence, Calliope. 9 You may sit down. This is no poetry recitation. Our theme is the truth. Tell your story, young girls of Pieria 10 —and may it do me good to call you “young girls.”
