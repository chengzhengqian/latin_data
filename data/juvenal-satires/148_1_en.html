Who else is there, anywhere in the world, who would have the nerve to prefer his life to his money, his survival to his property?

Most of the useful articles have gone overboard, but not even these losses bring any relief. Then under pressure of adversity he resorted to lowering the mast with steel. And so he gets himself out of his corner: that’s the ultimate in danger, when the remedy we supply makes the ship smaller. Off you go then, entrust your life to the winds, relying on a sawn plank, four fingers of pinewood away from death, or seven, if it’s extra thick. Just remember in future that along with your nets of bread and round-bellied flagons you’ll need axes—for use in a storm. But once the sea lay flat, once the passenger’s conditions improved and his destiny turned out more powerful than wind and ocean, once the Fates were working better stints with generous hands and cheerfully spinning threads of white wool, and once there sprang up a wind not much stronger than a slight breeze, the pitiable ship ran on with its skill impoverished, using clothes spread out along with its own sail, the only one left.
