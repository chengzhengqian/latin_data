Satire 16

Gallius, who can count the rewards of a successful military life? After all, if you join a top company <there’s nothing more desirable that the gods can bestow on you. So> I’d like to enter the camp gate as a nervous recruit, with the stars smiling. A moment of generous fate 1 is more powerful, after all, than a letter of recommendation to Mars from Venus or from his mother, who loves the sands of Samos. 2

First, let’s deal with the advantages shared by all soldiers. Not the least of these is that no civilian will have the nerve to beat you up. Instead, if he gets beaten up himself, he’ll pretend he wasn’t, and he won’t be eager to show the praetor his teeth that have been knocked out, or the black lump on his face with the swollen bruises, or the eye he still has, though the doctor isn’t making any promises. If he seeks redress for this, he gets a hobnailed boot 3 for a judge, with huge calf-muscles sitting at the big bench. The ancient military law and the rule of Camillus have been maintained, forbidding soldiers from attending court outside

  • 1I.e. the hour of a person’s birth.
  • 2Juno, who had a cult centre on the island of Samos. When young men joined the army, they had letters of recommendation from powerful connections.
  • 3I.e. a centurion. The Bardaei were Illyrians who provided Marius’ bodyguard and gave their name to the military boot.
