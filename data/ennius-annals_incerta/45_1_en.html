ANNALS: UNPLACED FRAGMENTS

​

*140 Servius, Commentary on Virgil

“and the cool Anio”: The Anio is a river not far from the city, but here he pursued euphony, for Ennius said:

the Anio

following the rule.1

​

*141 Paul the Deacon, Epitome of Festus

ambactus in Ennius is a word for a slave in the Gallic language. The prepositional prefix am in that language means “around”; from which comes the above ambactus, i.e., one driven around.

ambactus

​

*142 Isidore, Origins

teterrimus for something extremely fierce, for the ancients said tetrum for ferus [fierce], as Ennius:

ferocious elephants

​

*143 Porphyrio, Commentary on Horace

“do you see how thick with snow stands . . .”: stet [stands] indicates “is full,” as Ennius:

the plains stand thick with dust

and Virgil: “now they see the sky appear thick with dust” [Aen. 12.407–8].

  • 1At issue is the declension of the name, whether Anio, Anionis (Ennius) or Anien, Anienis (Virgil).
