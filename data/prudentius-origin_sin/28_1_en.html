Choose the way of life; thy goodness must promote thee to eternity, thy sin, again, for ever condemn thee; liberty is in thy hands to assure either fate.” Thus allowed by God’s goodness to go his own way, and amply enriched with this great gift, he transgresses the right that is set before him and knowingly, of his own will, chooses the things of death, believing that to be more profitable to him which the cunning serpent has persuaded him to do against the will of God. The serpent did indeed persuade by urging, not drive with sharp behest; the woman’s answer to God’s reproach, when she stood accused of the sin, was that she was won over by crafty persuasions and then urged the man; and the man also readily agreed. Might he not have rejected her urging, in the freedom of an upright soul? He might, for God too had before urged him to follow after the better of his own will; but he, rejecting the counsel, trusted more in his cruel enemy. Now he stands between the Lord of life and the teacher of death. On the one hand God calls him, on the other the devil, the while he wavers and goes from side to side.
