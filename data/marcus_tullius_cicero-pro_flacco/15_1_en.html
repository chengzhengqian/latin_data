It is indeed surprising that men to whom 19 our axesb are a hated symbol, our name is gall, our pasture-tax, tithes and customs dues are death, gladly seize any opportunity for harming us that is offered! Remember, then, that when you hear Greek resolutions, you are not hearing evidence; you are hearing the wild decisions of a mob, the voice of every nonentity, the din of ignoramuses, an inflamed meeting of the most unstable of nations. Scrutinize thoroughly the nature and motivation of the charges; you will find nothing there but the hope of one group and terrorization and threats against the other.

Suppose that a city has nothing in the treasury, 20 nothing in taxes; then there are two ways of raising money—by raising a loan or by a poll-tax; but no accounts of a creditor are produced and no imposition of a tax is offered in evidence. I invite you, however, to learn from the letter of Gnaeus Pompeius to Hypsaeusc and his reply to Pompeius how readily they submit false accounts and enter in the books whatever suits them.
