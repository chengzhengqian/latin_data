Some things there are that no order can compel, yet happening by chance they often help. Let Phaedra lose her wealth: Neptune, you will spare your grandson, nor will his grandsire’s bull alarm the steeds. Hadst thou made the Cnosian poor, she had loved wisely: wanton love is fed on riches. Why has no man taken Hecale, no woman Irus?1 surely, because she was poor, and he a beggar. Poverty has no means to feed its passion; yet it is not worth while to wish to be poor for that. But let it be worth while to abstain from theatres, until love ebb quite away from your empty heart. Zithers and flutes and lyres enervate the mind, and voices, and arms that move to their own rhythm.2 There constantly in the dance are lovers played: the actor’s art teaches the allure of what you must avoid. Unwillingly I speak: touch not the poets of love; treacherously I remove my own gifts from you. Avoid Callimachus: he is no enemy to love; thou, too, O Coan,3 with Callimachus dost harm. Me certainly did Sappho make more welcome to my mistress, nor did the Muse of Teos4 teach me strictness.
