ANNALS: DOUBTFUL FRAGMENTS

6 Servius, Commentary on Virgil

“you must bend your oar on the Sicilian sea”: and indeed, they think “bend” is a word invented from scratch, but it is read in the Annals:

and rubbed with oil, made flexible and prepared for arms

from the verb “to be bent.”

7 Marius Victorinus

the hexameter is a dactylic verse. . . . One kind has twelve syllables in the verse, and indeed, without any variation it contains all spondees and is called a spondiazon, as is

the envoys from Minturnae are given audience1

which kind of versification is so entirely harsh that Albinus,2 in the book he wrote on meters, not unattractively declared it so.

  • 1The verb introduco is the technical term for appearing before the Roman Senate. The town of Minturnae on the Appian Way at the border of Latium and Campania sent a delegation to Rome in 207 BC to claim exemption from military service (Liv. 27.38.3–5), one possible context for what may well be an authentic line of Ennius.
  • 2The Albinus in question is uncertain. See Kaster 1988, 382–83. Five such twelve-syllable lines are found among the securely attested fragments: Inc. Ann. F 31, 117, 157, 286, and 621.
