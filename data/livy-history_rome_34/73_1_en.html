Some thought that the distinguished order had now been accorded a privilege long overdue; others surmised that anything adding to the prestige of the senators detracted from the standing of the common people, and that any such divisions separating the classes must entail a weakening of the harmony and balance of freedoms within the community. This was a strange and arrogant whim, they said, neither wished for nor set in place by the senate of any people in the past. In the end, they say, even Africanus himself regretted having been the consul to propose the idea.157 So true it is that no change in time-honored practice wins approval; people prefer to stick with the old ways unless practice clearly finds them wanting.
