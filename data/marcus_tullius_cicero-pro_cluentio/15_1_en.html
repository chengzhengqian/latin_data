X. I realize, gentlemen, that your human hearts29 are wrung by this, my brief recital of his foul crimes. What then do you suppose their feelings were who had not only to listen to such a story, but to pass judgement on it? You are hearing the story of a man whom you are not set to judge, whom your eyes do not behold,who is beyond the reach of your hatred; who has paid his debt to nature and to the law, and has been punished, by the law with exile, by nature with death. You are hearing that story not from the lips of his enemy or on the evidence of witnesses; you are hearing my short and compendious version of events which might be narrated at length. But his judges were hearing the story of a man on whom they were bound by oath to give their verdict, a man whose wicked and guilt-stained countenance they beheld as he stood before them, a man whose effrontery commanded universal hatred and the universal opinion that he was worthy of the severest penalties: they were hearing it as told by his accusers, as supported by the testimony of many witnesses; they were hearing the grave and lengthy recital of each several point by the eloquence of P. Cannutius. Is there anyone who, with such facts30 before him, could possibly imagine Oppianicus to be the innocent victim of judicial corruption?
