The case came into court and was44 taken up to Rome, and there was a great disputation every day between Habitus and Oppianicus, so keen was each to make good his cause. It was Oppianicus’s nature to be ungovernable and violent, and his madness was further inflamed by the hatred and enmity of Habitus’s mother against her son. They, then, thought it indispensable to their interests to detach my client from the case of the Martiales. But behind this there was another and a more cogent reason, appealing strongly to Oppianicus’s great love of money; for up to the time of Oppianicus’s trial,45 Habitus had never made a will, being unable to bring himself either to leave anything to such a mother as his, or entirely to pass over a parent’s name in his will. When Oppianicus knew this (for there was no secrecy about it), he realized that on Habitus’s death all his property would pass to his mother, who could afterwards be put to death with greater advantage to himself, through the addition to her fortune, and with less risk, through the loss of her son. With these motives then to urge him on, hear how he endeavoured to get rid of Habitus by poison.
