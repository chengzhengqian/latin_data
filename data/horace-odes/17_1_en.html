In the end, alas, you will still get your adulterous hair dirty in the dust. Look round! There is Laertes’ son, who spells ruin for your people; there is Nestor of Pylos. You are hard pressed by dauntless warriors: Teucer of Salamis and Sthenelus, who is skilled in hand-to-hand fighting and no mean driver if he is called on to command a team of horses. You will also come to know Meriones. And see, the ferocious son of Tydeus, a better man than his father, is mad to track you down. Like a deer, which, on sighting a wolf on the far side of the valley, forgets about the grass, you will run timidly away from him, gasping for breath; this was something you did not promise to your woman! The anger of Achilles and his forces will postpone the day of doom for Ilium and the Phrygian mothers;31 yet after a destined number of winters the fire of Achaea will burn down the houses of Troy.”

16The poet’s recantation
