Odes II

ivy rod!43 I am allowed to sing of tireless Bacchants, fountains of wine, and rich rivers of milk, and to tell again the tale of honey oozing from hollow tree trunks.44 Iam allowed to sing of how your consort45 was beatified and her crown set among the stars, how the palace of Pentheus was blasted apart and collapsed in devastation, and how the Thracian Lycurgus was destroyed.

You bend rivers to your will, and the savage sea; on lonely mountain tops, soaked with wine, you bind the Bistonian46 women’s hair with a harmless knot of vipers. You, when the impious band of Giants climbed up the slope to reach your father’s kingdom,47 hurled back Rhoetus, causing panic with a lion’s claws and fangs.48 Although men said you were more suited to dances, fun, and games, and were not really fit for fighting, yet you proved a central figure in war as in peace. When Cerberus caught sight of you, arrayed in all your beauty with golden horns, he did you no harm, but meekly brushed his tail against you; and as you went away he licked your feet and legs with the tongues of his three muzzles.

  • 43The thyrsus, or wand, of Bacchus, whose touch induced frenzy.
  • 44Such phenomena are mentioned in the Bacchae of Euripides.
  • 45Ariadne.
  • 46Thracian.
  • 47i.e. Jupiter’s.
  • 48Bacchus could assume various shapes.
