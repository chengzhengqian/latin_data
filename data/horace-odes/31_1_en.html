Odes I

32An invocation to the lyre

I pray you, if I have ever in an idle hour played with you beneath the shade something that I hope may live for this year and longer, come, my Greek lyre, sing a Latin song. You were first tuned by a citizen of Lesbos,59 who was a valiant warrior, and yet, between attacks, or if he had tied up his storm-tossed ship on the still-wet sand, would sing of Bacchus and the Muses and Venus and the little boy who clings to her side, and the gorgeous Lycus with his jet-black eyes and jet-black hair.60 O glory of Phoebus, shell that is welcome at the feasts of highest Jove,61 o sweet balm and medicine of toil, accept my salutation as I duly call upon you.
