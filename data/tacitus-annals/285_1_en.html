In the same letter, the Caesar, to the intense pleasure of the senate, struck at the former praetor Sextius Paconianus—fearless, mischievous, a searcher into all men’s secrets, and the chosen helper of Sejanus in the laying of his plot against Gaius Caesar. On the announcement followed an explosion of long-cherished hatreds, and the last penalty was all but decreed, when he offered to turn informer.

IV. However, when he began upon Latinius Latiaris, accuser and accused—impartially detested as they were—furnished the most grateful of spectacles.—Latiaris, as I have recorded,1 had formerly been the chief agent in entrapping Titius Sabinus; and he was now the first to make atonement.

In the midst of all this, Haterius Agrippa attacked the consuls of the year before:—“Why,” he demanded, “after preferring their charges and countercharges,2 were they silent now?
