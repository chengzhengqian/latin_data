The pretext, however, was a Suebian1 request for help against the Cherusci: for, now that the Romans had withdrawn and the foreign menace was removed, the tribes—obedient to the national custom, and embittered in this case by their rivalry in prestige—had turned their weapons against each other. The power of the clans and the prowess of their leaders were upon a level; but while his kingly title rendered Maroboduus unpopular with his countrymen, Arminius aroused enthusiasm as the champion of liberty.

XLV. The result was that not only the veteran soldiery of Arminius—the Cherusci and their confederates—took up the campaign, but even from the dominions of Maroboduus two Suebian tribes, the Semnones and Langobardi,2 revolted to his cause. This accession assured him the preponderance, had not Inguiomerus3 with a band of his retainers deserted to the enemy, for the sole reason that as an old man and an uncle he scorned to obey the youthful son of his brother. Hope ran high on both sides as the lines of battle drew up, no longer to the old German accompaniment of charges either desultory or executed by scattered parties: for their long campaigns against ourselves had accustomed them to follow their standards, to secure their main body by reserves, and to give attention to their generals’ orders.
