During the presence of Claudius at the Circensian Games, when a cavalcade of boys from the great families opened the mimic battle of Troy,3 among them being the emperor’s son, Britannicus, and Lucius Domitius,4—soon to be adopted as heir to the throne and to the designation of Nero,—the livelier applause given by the populace to Domitius was accepted as prophetic. Also there was a common tale that serpents had watched over his infancy like warders: a fable retouched to resemble foreign miracles, since Nero—certainly not given to self-depreciation—used to say that only a single snake had been noticed in his bedroom.
