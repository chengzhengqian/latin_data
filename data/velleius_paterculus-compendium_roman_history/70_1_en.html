Later,a but when still quite a young man, he was captured by pirates and so conducted himself during the entire period of his detention as to inspire in them to an equal degree both fear and respect. Neither by day nor by night did he remove his shoes or loosen his girdle—for why should a detail of the greatest significance be omitted merely because it cannot be adorned in imposing language?—lest the slightest change in his usual garb might cause him to be suspected by his captors, who guarded him only with their eyes.

XLII. It would take too long to tell of his many bold plans for the punishment of the pirates, or how obstinately the timid governor of Asia refused to second them. The following story, however, may be told as a presage of his future greatness. On the night following the day on which his ransom was paid by the cities of Asia—he had, however, compelled the pirates before payment to give hostages to these cities—although he was but a private citizen without authority, and his fleet had been collected on the spur of the moment, he directed his course to the rendezvous of the pirates, put to flight part of their fleet, sank part, and captured several ships and many men.
