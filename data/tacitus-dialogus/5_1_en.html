The criticisms of declamation are found elsewhere1—most amusingly at the start of what is left of Petronius’ Satyricon; and there is no suggestion in the Dialogus that they are invalid. But, as will appear from Maternus’ final speech, they do not go to the roots.

The transition to this final speech is lost to us in a lacuna at the end of 35. The size of the gap has been variously computed from the conflicting evidence of the manuscripts, but strong arguments have been put forward recently that it was not a very long one.2 Whatever its length, it perhaps contained some contribution from Julius Secundus, who has so far taken no worthy part. Something of the sort seems to be foreshadowed by Maternus’ remarks in 16.3.

In any case, unless there is some further lacuna, the rest of the Dialogus as we have it down to the closing pleasantries of 42 is put in the mouth of Maternus. Here is a historian’s view, that goes beyond educational symptoms to root causes. The age is not adapted to great oratory.
