At any rate, it is clear from the Institutio that Quintilian thought the position not irredeemable: what was to redeem it was a renewed application to the details of academic rhetoric and a recognition of the importance of the wide education recommended by Cicero in the De Oratore.

The actual dialogue begins from Maternus’ tragedy, the Cato. Secundus is concerned that it has offended the great by its republican sentiments. Aper is concerned that Maternus has abandoned the law and given himself to poetry. He lauds the prestige brought by oratory, and comments on the importance it has conferred on such men as Eprius Marcellus and Vibius Crispus, now high in Vespasian’s counsels-Poetry by comparison brings merely fleeting rewards (5–10). Maternus, “inspired” where Aper had been “vehement,” denies the whole of Aper’s case. The eloquence of today is bloody and brutal; poetry brings true pleasure and removes the mind from everyday cares to the sacred fountains of the Muses (11–13).

It is important to realise that Maternus is no shrinking aesthete, terrified by the crude realities of the forum. He had stood up to a favourite of Nero (11.2), and he was not afraid to set Rome by the ears with his tragedies. But he is an honest man, disgusted by the uses to which oratory has been prostituted.
