13. “Nor should I hesitate to contrast the poet’s lot in life and his delightful literary companionships with the unrest and anxiety that mark the orator’s career. Even though in his case a consulship be the crown of all the contests and lawsuits he so dearly loves: for my part I would rather have the seclusion in which Virgil lived, tranquil and serene, without forfeiting either the favour of the sainted Augustus, or popularity with the citizens of Rome. This is vouched for by the letters of Augustus, and by the behaviour of the citizens themselves; for on hearing a quotation from Virgil in the course of a theatrical performance, they rose to their feet as one man, and did homage to the poet, who happened to be present at the play, just as they would have done to the Emperor himself. And in our own day too Pomponius Secundus6 ranks just as high as Domitius Afer,7 alike in personal standing and in enduring reputation.

  • 1Attic orators, contrasted with the Attic tragedians just mentioned.
  • 2C. Asinius Pollio (consul 40 b.c.), orator, tragedian, and historian: friend of Horace and Virgil.
  • 3M. Valerius Messalla Corvinus (consul 31 b.c.), a Republican who later served with distinction under Augustus. Patron of Tibullus, and an orator who was a stickler for grammatical niceties.
  • 4Unfortunately lost.
  • 5L. Varius Rufus, literary executor of Virgil and prominent Augustan epic and tragic poet.
  • 6P. Pomponius Secundus (consul suffectus 44), the “consular poet” whose life was written by the elder Pliny: tragedian and scholar.
  • 7Cn. Domitius Afer (consul suffectus 39), orator and wit, highly praised by his friend Quintilian (10.1.118), but less admired by Tacitus, who criticises his activities as a delator (Ann. 4.66).
