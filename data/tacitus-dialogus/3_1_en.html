Syme argues for 101–2, to coincide with the consulship of the dedicatee, Fabius Justus; others have gone still later (one might think of 103–4, the sixth year of another felix principatus—that of Trajan). The advantages of late dating are manifest. The critical remarks on Vibius Crispus and Eprius Marcellus come better under an emperor who had banished delatores than under a dynasty, the Flavian, that had relied on their support.1 And the parallel of the De Oratore, where Cicero, writing in 55 b.c., describes a discussion set in 91 b.c., hints that Tacitus should be talking of the far past and employing interlocutors now dead.

The introductory paragraph raises at once the main topic of the dialogue: why has oratory so decayed? The question was no new one.2 Cicero had, on a priori grounds, forecast that the decay would take place (Tusc. 2.5). And complaints are raised as early as the reign of Tiberius, when the elder Seneca speculated whether the decadence was due to luxury (a common scapegoat), materialism, or merely the grudging fate that dictates the fall of what has gone high (Contr. 1, praef. 7). Quintilian took the matter seriously enough to devote a treatise to it; his On the Causes of the Corruption of Oratory is now lost, but it is probable from allusions to it in the slightly later Institutio (about 95) that it compared the downward path of Roman oratory with the decadence
