Epigrams on Various Matters

XCII.—To a Lawyer who had a faithless Wife

A lawyer who had a faithless wife approved of the Papian statute1 but disapproved of the Julian.2 Do ye ask why this difference? Effeminate himself, fearing the Scantinian,3 he feared not the Titian Law.4

XCIII.—To a Man who used to remove the Hair from his Groin

For you to smooth your groin with a depilatory is understandable: smooth lovers stimulate satin-skinned prostitutes. But why you pluck hairs from your backside after a bath and polish your anus with pumice remains obscure, unless it be that pathicism leads to a double depravity, you being a woman in the rear as well as a man in front.

XCIV.—To Zoïlus who had married a lewd Woman

Effeminate thyself, Zoïlus, thou hast wedded an unchaste wife: how great a profit will ye twain earn at home, when thy debaucher pays thy wife, and her lover thee, the fees of shame! But lust, which now seems to you profitable, will soon, as age creeps on, cause loss: lovers will begin to sell you their services for pay, whom prostituted youth now makes your customers.
