She named it and added: ‘Yes, kind stranger, not a farthing less.’ It vexed him that she had detected his foreign birth, although he had lived long at Athens and was accounted the most perfect speaker of his time.a In like manner I take it there is in our urban speakers a characteristic accent analogous to that peculiar to Athens.

“But let us come back home, that is let us return to our urban orators. While then Crassus and 173 Antonius were the leaders, Lucius Philippus held the place next to them, but next after a long interval. Thus, though no one who could claim to surpass him stood between him and them, yet I would not place him second or third in rank. In a chariot race I should not name as second or third one who has barely crossed the starting line when the leader has already been awarded the prize, nor in orators, one who is so far from the leader that he scarcely seems to be in the same race. But yet there were those qualities in Philippus, which, looked at without comparison with those two, one could call very considerable. He was notably free and outspoken in his language, with a large fund of humour; resourceful in invention, and in exposition unconstrained and facile; for his time conspicuously versed in Greek learning; clever in the give and take of debate with a certain pungent and personal wit.
