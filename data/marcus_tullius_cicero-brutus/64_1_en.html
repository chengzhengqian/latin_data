Upon him the poet Lucius Afranius strove to pattern himself, a man of cunning wit and, as you know, in his plays really eloquent. There 168 was also Quintus Rubrius Varro (whom the senate pronounced a public enemy along with Gaius Marius), a sharp and aggressive prosecutor, and in that type of activity an acceptable speaker. A man thoroughly trained in Greek letters was my relative Marcus Gratidius, a born orator, the intimate friend of Marcus Antonius, whose prefect he was in Cilicia, where he lost his life. He will be recalled as the one who brought accusation against Gaius Fimbria, and as the father of Marcus Marius Gratidianus.

Very well known too is his speech delivered at Rome against Caepio, to which reply was made through the mouth of Caepio by Aelius, a writer of speeches for many, though no orator himself.
