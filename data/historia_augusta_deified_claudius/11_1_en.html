The Deified Claudius XI.

XI. While these things were being done by the Deified Claudius, the Palmyrenes, under the generals Saba and Timagenes, made war against the Egyptians,1 who defeated them with true Egyptian pertinacity and unwearied continuance in fighting. Probatus, nevertheless, the leader of the Egyptians, was killed by a trick of Timagenes’. All the Egyptians, however, submitted to the Roman emperor, swearing allegiance to Claudius although he was absent.

In the consulship of Antiochianus and Orfitus the270 favour of heaven furthered Claudius’ success. For a great multitude, the survivors of the barbarian tribes, who had gathered in Haemimontum,2 were so stricken with famine and pestilence that Claudius now scorned to conquer them further. And so at length that most cruel of wars was brought to an end, and the Roman nation was freed from its terrors.3
