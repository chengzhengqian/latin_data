58. If the nominative plural is by any chance a corrupted form, which rarely occurs, we shall correct this before we make it our starting-point; it is proper to take from the oblique cases, either singular or plural, some forms which are not ambiguous, from which can be seen the make-up which the other forms ought to have.

59. For sometimes the one is seen from the other and at other times the other is seen from the one, as Chrysippus writes,a as the father’s qualities may be seen from the son, and the son’s from the father, and in arches the right-hand side stands on account of the left-hand side, no less than the left on account of the right. Therefore the oblique forms can sometimes be regained from the nominatives, and sometimes the nominatives from the oblique forms; sometimes the plural from the singular forms, and sometimes the singular forms from the plural.

60. The principle that we should most of all follow, is that in this the foundation be nature, because in naturea there is the easier relationship in inflections.
