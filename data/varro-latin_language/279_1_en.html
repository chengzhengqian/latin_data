35. . . . When we meet a new word,a we ask about its case-forms, as to how we shall inflect them; and yet if some poet has made up some word and has himself formed from it some case-form in an incorrect way, we blame him rather than follow his example. Therefore Ratio or Relation, of which I am speaking, is present in bothb: in the words which are imposed upon things,c and in those which are formed by inflectiond; and then also there is that third kind of Relation, which combines the characteristics of the two.e
