On the Latin Language, VII

Claudiusc writes that women who make joint entreaties are clearly shown to be axitiosae ‘united, unionist.’ Axitiosaed is from agere ‘to act’: as factiosae ‘partisan women ‘are named from facere ‘doing’ something in unison, so axitiosae are named from agere ‘acting’ together, as though actiosae.

67. In the Cesistioa:

For the gods the thigh-meats or the lewd parts from the loins.

Stribula ‘thigh-meats,’ as Opillusb writes, are the fleshy parts of cattle around the hips; the wordc is Greek, derived from the fact that in this place there is a socket-joint.d

68. In The Story of the Prison Ropesa:

At once I with my rasp did scrape the old fellow clean.

Scobina ‘rasp,’ from scobis ‘sawdust’; for a file belongs to a carpenter’s equipment.

69. In The Little Man from Carthagea:

You’d outdo the stag in running or the stilt-walker in stride.

Grallator ‘stilt-walker’ is said from his great gradus ‘stride.’
