17. There is a third division, the words which are by their nature inflected. These are divided into four subdivisions: one which has cases but not tenses,a like docilis ‘docile’ and facilis ‘easy’; a second, which has tenses but not cases,b like docet ‘teaches,’ facit ‘makes’; a third which has both,c like docens ‘teaching,’ faciens ‘making’; a fourth which has neither,d like docte ‘learnedly’ and facete ‘wittily.’ The individual parts of this division are each unlike the three remaining parts. Therefore, unless the words are compared with one another in their own subdivision, even if they do agree the one word will not be so like the other that it ought to make the same inflectional scheme.
