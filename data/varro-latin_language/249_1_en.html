On the Latin Language

78. For as some statues lack the head or some other part without destroying the Regularities in their other limbs, so in words certain losses of cases can take place, with as little result. Besides, what is lacking can be remadea and put back into its place, where nature and usage permit; which we sometimes find done by the poets, as in this verse of Naevius, in the Clastidiumb:

With life unburied, glad, to fatherland restored.c

XLVII. 79. Likewise they find fault with the nominatives strues ‘heap,’ Hercules, homo ‘man’; for if Regularity actually existed, they say, these forms should have been strus, Hercul, homen.a These nouns do not show that Regularity is non-existent, but that the oblique cases do not have a head or starting-point according to their type of Regularity.b Is it not a fact that, if you should put a head of Philip on a statue of Alexander and the limbs should be proportionately symmetrical, then the head which does correspond to the statue of Alexander’s limbsc would likewise be symmetrical? And it is not a fact that if one should in practice sew together a tunic in such a way that one breadth of the cloth has narrow border-stripes and the other has broad stripes, each part lacks regular conformity within its own class.d
