On the Latin Language

52. But if any one should wish to say that in this also there are Regularities in the things, he can maintain it. For as they themselves saya that some nouns, because they have five forms, have five cases, and others have four, and others fewer in like manner, they will be able to say that the letters and syllables which have one case-form apiece in sound, have several in connexion with the thingsb; as they will compare only with each other those which have four case-forms for the words, and likewise those which have three apiece, so let them compare with each other those which have only one form each, saying that nominative E, dative E is like nominative A, dative A.

XXXVII. 53. As to the fact that they saya that there are certain words which have declensional forms, like caput ‘head,’ genitive capitis, and nihil ‘nothing,’ genitive nihili, a match for which cannot be found, and therefore the Regularities do not exist, answer must be made that unquestionably any word which is the only one of its kind is outside the systems of Regularity; there must be at least two words for a likeness to be existent therein. Therefore, in this case,b they eliminate the possible existence of the Regularities.

54. But the word nihilum ‘nothing’ is found in the nominative in the followinga:

The body she’s given Earth doth herself take back, and of loss not a whit does she suffer,

  • aCf. viii. 63.
  • bThat is, words indeclinable in form have only one case-form, but still have all the case-uses.
  • aThere is no corresponding passage in Book VIII.
  • bThat is, when they select a unique word as basis for argument.
  • aEnnius, Ann. 14 Vahlen2; R.O.L. i. 6–7 Warmington; cf. v. 60 and 111. The neuter accusative, having the same form as the nominative, is used as a proof of the nominative form.
