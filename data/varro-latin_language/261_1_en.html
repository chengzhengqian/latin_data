106. In Plautus, when he saysa:

The fish, I really think, that bathe through all their life, Are in the bath less time than this Phronesium,

lavari ‘are in the bath,’ with final I instead of E, does not attach to lavant ‘bathe’: Regularity refers lavari to lavantur, and whether the error belongs to Plautus or to the copyist, it is not Regularity, but the writer that is to be blamed. At any rate, lavat and lavatur are used with a difference of meaning in certain matters, because a nurse lavat ‘bathes’ a child, the child lavatur ‘is bathed’ by the nurse, and in the bathing establishments we both lavamus ‘bathe’ and lavamur ‘are bathed.’

107. But since usage approves both, in the case of the whole body one uses rather lavamur ‘we bathe ourselves,’ and in the case of portions of the body lavamus ‘we wash,’ in that we say lavo ‘I wash’ my hands, my feet, and so on.a Therefore with reference to the bathing establishments they are wrong in saying lavi ‘I have bathed,’ but right in saying lavi ‘I have washed’ my hands. But since in the bathing establishments lavor ‘I bathe’ and lautus sum ‘I have bathed,’ it follows that on the other hand from soleo ‘I am wont,’ which is in the active, one ought to say solui ‘I have been wont,’ as Catob and Enniusc write, and that solitus sum, as the people in general say, ought not to be used. But as I have said above,d Regularity exists none the less for these few inconsistencies which occur in speech.
