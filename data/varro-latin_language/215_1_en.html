77. Besides, if they were spoken in like manner, then since macer ‘lean’ and tener ‘tender’a are alike, and their superlatives macerrimus and tenerrimus, there would be no difference in their comparatives macrior and tenerior, nor would some such words have three syllables and others four. And if likeness ruled in such words, then just as we say candidissimus ‘most shining’ and fem, candidissima, pauperrumus ‘poorest’ and fem, pauperrima, so we should say candidus ‘shining’ and fem. Candida, pauper ‘poor’ and fem, paupera.b And as we say docius ‘learned’ and fem, docta, doetissimus ‘most learned’ and fem. doctissima, so we should say frugalissumus ‘most thrifty ‘and fem, frugalissima, frugalus ‘thrifty’ and fem. frugala.c

78. And if words were in regular relation to each other, as with one worda we call a man and a woman sapiens ‘wise’ and diligens ‘diligent,’ sapientior ‘wiser’ and diligentior ‘more diligent,’ so we should speak in the same way when we had come to the superlative—a thing which we now do quite otherwiseb: for we call a man sapientissimus and diligentissimus, a woman sapientissima and diligentissima.
