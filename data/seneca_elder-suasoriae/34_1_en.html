Suasoriae

A pupil of Fuscus’, whose shame I will respect,1 when5 declaiming our suasoria on Alexander the Great before his master, thought that this same line could come in equally prettily here, and said:

“Naturally that is a task for the gods, that is a care That troubles them in their calm.”

Fuscus said to him: “If you had said that in the hearing of Alexander you’d have been made aware there’s another verse in Virgil:

‘he buried the sword to its hilt.’”2

Now because you keep bothering me about Fuscus and why no-one was thought a more elegant speaker, I shall keep piling Fuscan delevopments on you. He was very ready to speak suasoriae, more frequently in Greek than Latin.

It was in this suasoria that Hybreas said: “What a shield Babylon has found in an augur!”3
