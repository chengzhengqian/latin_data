Table VIII

5

Festus: ‘Rupsit’ shall have broken or maimed in the Twelve Tables means ‘shall have caused loss.’a

6

Harm done by an animal:

Ulpian: If a four-footed animal shall be said to have caused ‘pauperies,’b loss, legal action for the same is derived from the Law of the Twelve Tables.c This Law sanctioned either the surrender of the thing d which damaged, that is the animal which committed the damage, or else the offer of assessment for the damage.

7

Ulpian: If fruit should fall from your tree onto my estate, and I cause feeding off such fruit by letting cattle onto it, . . . no legal action can be taken by the Law of the Twelve Tables either under the enactment on the pasturing of cattle, for it is not on your land that it is being pastured, or under the enactment on loss caused by an animal.e

8a-b

Magical enchantment of another’s crops:

Pliny: Do not the following words belong even to our laws in the Twelve Tables?

8a Person who has enchanted crops away . .f

  • aNot, apparently, quoted from law 2 given above; but the reading and meaning are uncertain. It seems best to connect the fr. with damage to property. Havet, in Bibl. de l’ Éc. des H. Études, 274 (1914), p. 11, in suggesting here rupsit as〈sem dolo mato〉, would apply the words to a bankrupt, who ‘breaks the unit’ (as) or sum-total of his debts by paying part only. This may be right, but we must not attribute to the Twelve Tables any law of bankruptcy like Caesar’s or the modern law.
  • ba special term for damage done by an animal.
  • cNote that the Tables contained various enactments for particular sorts of damage done to immovable property. If there was any general enactment, it thus probably applied to movable property only.
  • dThe Tables perhaps here used the expression noxam dedere ‘to surrender the guilty thing’; though noxa and noxia were both used for ‘hurt, harm,’ noxa was used also for ‘harm-doer,’ ‘offender,’ for instance, when the delinquent was a slave or a child; see below, pp. 508–9.
  • eFor this, see preceding fr.
  • fnot apparently into one’s own fields, but to destroy them where they stood. Fruges here may mean hanging fruits.
