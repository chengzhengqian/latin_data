Table VIII

24

Accidental homicide by missile:

Cicero: There is a law in the Twelve Tables—

24If missile has sped from hand, and holder has not aimed ita . . .

Cicero: To aim or throw a missile is an act of the will; to strike a person whom you did not wish to, an act of chance. Hence comes that well-known substitution of a ramb in your actions at law, ‘if missile has sped from hand, and holder has not aimed it.’

25

Poisoning:

Gaius: Whoever uses the term ‘venenum,’ drug, should add some expression to show whether it be noxious or beneficial; for ‘medicamenta,’ medicines, also are included in ‘venena.’

26

Nocturnal meetings not permitted:

Porcius Latro: We learn in the Twelve Tables that provision was made that no person shall hold meetings by night in the city.

27

Associations permitted:

Gaius: ‘Associates’ are persons who belong to the same ‘collegium,’ guild, for which the Greeks use the term ἑταιρία. These are granted by a law (of the Twelve Tables) the right to pass any binding rule they like for themselves, provided that they cause no violation of public law. But this law appears to have been taken over from a law of Solon.

  • aNote that we do not know what the old penalty for homicide was; from Pliny, XVIII, 12 we know that it was less severe than the penalty for cutting another’s corn by night (see above, pp. 480–1).
  • bOriginally a peace-offering in order to stop blood-revenge, the offering of a ram was intended, in the Tables, to stop prosecution for murder, which kinsmen of a murdered man were bound to institute. The ram was probably given to the agnates of the man killed.
