Table XII

Gaius: 2b Arising from delicts committed by children and slaves of a household establishment . . . actions for damages were appointed whereby the father or master could be allowed either to undergo ‘assessment of suit,’a or hand over the delinquent to punishment. . . . These actions were instituted . . . partly by legal enactments, partly by the praetor’s edictb; by legal enactments as for instance by that for theft in the Law of the Twelve Tables.

3

False claims:

Festus: ‘Vindiciae,’ legal claim ... in the singular . . . in the Twelve Tables—

3If person has taken thing by false claim,c if he should wish . . . officiald must grant three arbitrators; by their arbitration . . . defen- dant must compound, for loss caused, by paying double damages from enjoyment of article.e

4

Dedication of articles which are in dispute; Gaius: We are prohibited (sc. by the Law of the Twelve Tables) from dedicating for consecrated use anything

  • alitis aestimalio was assessment of damages, in this case based on what a free or independent person would have to pay if he was found guilty of the same delict. In the other alternative the Tables perhaps used the expression noxam dedere ‘to surrender the offending person’; for this see note on Table VIII, 6, p. 479.
  • bFrom the accumulation of praetors’ edicts arose ius honorarium or magisterial law which supplemented such law as had been made by statute and interpretation.
  • cThat is, apparently, if a person, with or without fraudulent intent, had held and claimed as his a thing which a judicial court now decided belonged to another party. But the fr. is uncertain.
  • dThe missing word ending in tor seems to be an official; praetor would not apply to the time of the Tables, unless the consuls were, in the Tables, denoted by the old name praitor (see above, pp. 436, 480, in several sources for enactments of the Tables). Perhaps quaestor (pp. 495–7; but he would deal with worse misdeeds) or recuperator. Boards of recuperatores or ‘recoverers’ were originally appointed to deal with cases between Romans and foreigners, but later on they could be appointed to deal with disputes (especially about possessio) between citizens.
  • eRetention of the article was deemed to have brought defendant some profit; he must pay double this profit.
