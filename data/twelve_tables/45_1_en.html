Unplaced Fragments

4

Donatus, on ‘dolo malo’ (by wilful fraud) in Terence: His addition of the word ‘malo’ . . . is an archaism, for this was an expression used by the ancients in the Twelve Tables.

5

Cicero: That appeal from any judgment or sentence was allowed is shown by the Twelve Tables in many laws.

6

Cicero: No bond, by the wish of our ancestors, was to be tighter in binding good faith than a sworn oath. This is shown by the Laws of the Twelve Tables.

[6a]

[Augustinus: Eight kinds of penalty, writes Tullius, are to be found in the laws,a namely fine, fetters, flogging, retaliation in kind, civil disgrace, banishment, death and slavery.]

7

Pliny: In the Twelve Tables only sun risen and ‘set’ are mentioned as such; only after a number of years was the term ‘midday,’ or ‘noon,’ also added.b

8

Gaius: By the use of the double negatives the Law really permits and did not prohibit.

9

Gaius: ‘Detestatum,’c having renounced under oath, means ‘having renounced by solemn attestation.’

  • csetting’; but, in analogy with ‘sol occasus’ (see pp. 430–1), I conclude that the Tables used the expression ‘sol ortus,’ and translate ‘ortus’ and ‘occasus’ here as participles.
