You will like to know that our friend Curio has done very nicely with his veto on the provinces. When the question about the veto was put (this was done in accordance with the Senate’s decree) and M. Marcellus’ proposal to the effect that representations should be made2 to the Tribunes was the first pronounced, the House voted it down in large numbers. Pompey the Great’s digestion is now in such a poor way that he has trouble finding anything to suit him!3 They have come round to accept that a person should be allowed to stand for office without handing over his army and provinces. How Pompey is going to take this I’ll tell you when I know. As for what is to happen if he either resists in arms or just lets it go, you rich old gentlemen can worry!

As I write, Q. Hortensius is at death’s door.

95 (II.12) CICERO TO CAELIUS RUFUS

Camp on the River Pyramus, ca. 26 June 50

From M. Cicero, Imperator, to M. Caelius, Curule Aedile, greetings.
