So you don’t need that quotation from Trabea.2 The ‘miss’ was rather mine. But tell me now, how do you find me as a letter writer? Don’t I deal with you in colloquial style? The fact is that one’s style has to vary. A letter is one thing, a court of law or a public meeting quite another. Even for the courts we don’t have just one style. In pleading civil cases, unimportant ones, we put on no frills, whereas cases involving status or reputation naturally get something more elaborate. As for letters, we weave them out of the language of everyday.

Be that as it may, my dear Paetus, what possessed you to say that all the Papirii without exception were plebeians? They were in fact patricians, one of the Lesser Clans.3 The roll begins with L. Papirius Mugillanus, who was Censor along with L. Sempronius Atratinus, having previously been his colleague in the Consulship in the year 312 after the foundation of Rome.4 But in those days you used to be called Papisii.
