Our friend Cassius has pitched camp with ten legions, twenty auxiliary cohorts, and 4,000 horse about twenty miles away at Paltus. He reckons to win a bloodless victory, for the price of grain with Dolabella has gone up to twelve drachmae. Unless he brings in a supply in Laodicean ships, he must starve to death before long; and Cassius’ very sizable fleet, commanded by Sextilius Rufus, and the three others which I myself, Turullius, and Patiscus have brought to join him, will have no trouble in preventing Dolabella from importing. So you may be of good cheer, confident that, following your example in Italy, we for our part shall soon succeed in solving the commonwealth’s problems.

Good-bye.

Dispatched Ides of June, from Crommyacris, Cyprus.

420 (XI.25) CICERO TO D. BRUTUS

Rome, 18 June 43

From M. Cicero to D. Brutus greetings.

I have been waiting to hear from you every day. All of a sudden our friend Lupus has given me notice to write to you if I have anything I want to say. In point of fact I haven’t—I know that the news of the day is sent to you, and am told that empty chat in correspondence is not to your liking—but I did not want to write nothing at all. But I study to be brief, as your pupil.
