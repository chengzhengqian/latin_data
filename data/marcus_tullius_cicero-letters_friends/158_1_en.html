About politics I have nothing to tell you. Marcellus’ initiatives have subsided, not from lack of energy, but, in my opinion, from policy. The consular elections are anybody’s guess. I have got one nobleman to contend with and one acting-nobleman6—M. Octavius, son of Gnaeus, and C. Hirrus are standing with me. I am telling you this because I know how eagerly you will wait for the result of our elections on Hirrus’ account.7 As soon as you hear I am designate, please see to the matter of the panthers.8 I recommend Sittius’ bond to your kind attention.9 I gave my first Abstract of Affairs in Rome to L. Castrinius Paetus, my second to the bearer of this letter.

79 (VIII.3) CAELIUS RUFUS TO CICERO

Rome, ca. 13 June 51
