Apocolocyntosis

And you above all, who get rich quick By the rattle of dice and the three card trick.”1

Claudius was charmed to hear his own praises sung, and would have stayed longer to see the show. But the Talthybius2 of the gods laid a hand on him, and led him across the Campus Martius, first wrapping his head up close that no one might know him, until betwixt Tiber and the Subway3 he went down to the lower regions. His freedman Narcissus4 had gone down before him by a short cut, ready to welcome his master. Out he comes to meet him, smooth and shining (he had just left the bath), and says he: “What make the gods among mortals?” “Look alive,” says Mercury, “go and tell them we are coming.” Away he flew, quicker than tongue can tell. It is easy going by that road, all down hill. So although he had a touch of the gout, in a trice he arrived at Dis’s door. There lay Cerberus,5 or, as Horace puts it, the hundred-headed monster.Odes ii, 13, 35 Narcissus was a trifle perturbed (it was an off-white bitch6 he used to keep for a pet) when he spied this black shag-haired hound, not at all the kind of thing you could wish to meet you in the dark. In a loud voice he cried, “Claudius is coming!” All marched before him with clapping of hands and singing, “The lost is found, O let us rejoice together!”7 Here were found C.   • 1Claudius was fond of playing with dice.
  • 2Agamemnon’s herald in the Trojan war. Here Mercury is meant.
  • 3The “Via Tecta” “Covered Way” was at the northern limit of the Campus Martius at Rome.
  • 4The powerful freedman Narcissus, Claudius’s secretary, died shortly after Claudius.
  • 5On Dis, see above. Cerberus is the three-headed dog which guarded the gates of Hades.
  • 6B. J. Harrison thinks this refers to Claudius.
  • 7With a slight change, this is a cry used in the worship of Isis and Osiris.
